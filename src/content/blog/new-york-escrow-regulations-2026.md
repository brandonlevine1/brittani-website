## Why New York Escrow Regulations 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Mishandling escrow in New York does not result in a slap on the wrist. It results in license revocation, civil liability, and—in egregious cases—criminal prosecution under Penal Law Section 155. The New York escrow regulations 2026 framework builds on statutes that have tripped up agents for decades, and the Department of State has signaled stricter enforcement cycles this year.

New York is one of the few states where attorneys routinely hold contract deposits rather than brokerages. That split creates confusion for agents who move between upstate and downstate markets, or who relocate from states where title companies manage everything. Understanding where the money sits—and whose license is on the line—is not optional knowledge.

**This article covers the statutes, the forms, the penalties, and the specific audit steps brokers must take to keep their shops clean.** If you handle residential transactions anywhere in the five boroughs, on Long Island, or in the Hudson Valley, the rules below apply to you.

## The Statutory Foundation: Real Property Law and 19 NYCRR Part 175

New York's escrow obligations for real estate brokers originate in [Real Property Law (RPL) Article 12-A](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/RPP/A12-A) and are further detailed in Title 19 of the New York Codes, Rules and Regulations (NYCRR) Part 175. RPL Section 441 outlines the fiduciary duties of licensees, while 19 NYCRR 175.1 specifically addresses the handling of trust funds.

Under 19 NYCRR 175.1, every broker who receives funds on behalf of others must deposit those funds into a segregated escrow or trust account at a New York State–chartered bank or a national bank with a New York branch. The regulation prohibits commingling personal or business operating funds with escrow deposits. Even a temporary shortfall caused by mixing funds triggers a violation.

RPL Section 441(1) makes it a ground for license suspension or revocation if a broker "demonstrates untrustworthiness or incompetency" in handling other people's money. The Department of State's Division of Licensing Services treats escrow violations as among the most serious infractions a licensee can commit.

## Attorney-Held vs. Broker-Held Escrow: The New York Split

**The distinction between attorney-held and broker-held escrow is the single most misunderstood element of New York residential practice.** In New York City, Nassau, Suffolk, and Westchester counties, the prevailing custom is for the seller's attorney to hold the contract deposit. Upstate—in markets like Albany, Syracuse, Rochester, and Buffalo—listing brokers more commonly serve as escrow holders.

| Factor | Attorney-Held (Downstate) | Broker-Held (Upstate) |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Who holds deposit | Seller's attorney | Listing broker |
| Governing rules | Judiciary Law, RPL 441 | 19 NYCRR 175.1, RPL 441 |
| Account type | Attorney trust (IOLA eligible) | Broker escrow account |
| Interest treatment | May be IOLA or interest-bearing per agreement | Non-interest-bearing unless both parties consent |
| Release authority | Attorney upon contract terms | Broker upon written consent or court order |

Agents must confirm at contract signing who will hold the deposit. The standard Residential Contract of Sale used downstate (often based on the New York City Bar Association or REBNY form) specifies the escrow holder by name and address. Upstate contracts frequently reference the listing broker's escrow account in the offer-to-purchase form.

Regardless of who holds the money, the agent's obligation to ensure timely deposit remains. If the contract states the deposit must be delivered within three business days and the agent holds the check for a week, both the agent and the supervising broker face exposure. Timely handoff matters—something many agents learn the hard way when [deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) over procedural failures.

## Forms and Documentation Agents Must Know

New York does not have a single state-mandated escrow receipt form in the way that some other states do. However, multiple documents govern escrow handling in practice.

The NYSCAR (New York State Association of REALTORS) Purchase and Sale Agreement includes an escrow clause in Section 5 that specifies the deposit amount, the escrow holder, and the conditions of release. Agents using this form should verify that all blanks are completed—leaving the escrow holder field empty is a surprisingly common error that generates disputes.

For downstate transactions, the standard Contract of Sale (sometimes called the "Blumberg Form 125" for residential deals, or the Bar Association/REBNY forms) contains a rider section where attorneys specify escrow terms. The Escrow Rider typically addresses deposit timelines, interest allocation, and dispute resolution mechanisms.

Brokers are also required under 19 NYCRR 175.2 to maintain an escrow ledger—either paper or electronic—that records every deposit, disbursement, and running balance for each transaction. The Department of State can demand production of these records during an audit or investigation. Failure to produce them creates a rebuttable presumption of commingling.

Keeping your [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) updated with escrow receipt confirmations protects you if a dispute arises months after closing.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, License Actions, and Deal Death

The penalties for escrow violations in New York operate on three levels: regulatory, civil, and criminal.

At the regulatory level, the Department of State may suspend or revoke a broker's license under RPL Section 441. The Division of Licensing Services can also impose fines of up to $1,000 per violation and require restitution. Salespersons face the same disciplinary exposure, plus the added risk that their sponsoring broker terminates them to protect the brokerage license.

Civil liability exposes the agent and broker to lawsuits from buyers, sellers, or both. A buyer whose deposit is not timely delivered to the escrow holder can claim breach of contract and seek damages. Courts have awarded consequential damages—including the price differential when a buyer loses a property due to a failed deposit. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) often exceeds the commission the agent stood to earn.

Criminal liability arises when mishandling crosses into theft. Under New York Penal Law Section 155.05, a broker who converts escrow funds to personal use commits larceny. Depending on the amount, this can be charged as grand larceny in the third or second degree—a Class D or C felony carrying up to seven or fifteen years in prison, respectively.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Escrow in New York

**Five recurring errors account for the majority of escrow complaints filed with the Department of State.** Recognizing them is the first step toward avoiding them.

The first mistake is holding the deposit check instead of delivering it promptly. Some agents keep checks in their desk drawer over a weekend or longer, waiting for the attorney or broker to "confirm" something. The contract controls the timeline—not the agent's convenience. Every day of delay increases liability.

The second mistake is failing to get written authorization before releasing funds. When a deal collapses, agents sometimes release the deposit to one party based on a phone call or verbal agreement. 19 NYCRR 175.1 requires written consent from all parties or a court order before disbursement. Without it, the releasing broker is exposed to claims from the other side.

Third, agents confuse "holding" with "depositing." Receiving a check and placing it in a locked file is not the same as depositing it into an escrow account. The statute requires actual deposit into a qualifying account. A check in a file cabinet is not protected, not earning interest where applicable, and not compliant.

Fourth, agents fail to disclose interest earned on escrow deposits. Where a deposit is held in an interest-bearing account, the contract must specify who receives the interest. Agents who pocket interest—even small amounts—commit conversion.

Fifth, agents neglect to update escrow instructions when contract amendments change the deposit amount or timeline. A modification to the purchase price that increases the deposit requires a corresponding update to the escrow agreement. Relying on the original terms when the deal has changed creates a gap that either party can exploit.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry personal liability for every escrow violation committed by their sponsored agents. Under RPL Section 442-c, the supervising broker is responsible for the actions of salespersons conducted within the scope of their employment. A broker who fails to supervise escrow handling cannot claim ignorance as a defense.

| Audit Item | Frequency | What to Check |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Escrow ledger reconciliation | Monthly | Balances match bank statements; no unexplained variances |
| Deposit timing | Per transaction | Check deposited within contract-specified window |
| Written authorizations | Per disbursement | Both parties' signed consent or court order on file |
| Account signatories | Quarterly | Only authorized persons listed on bank account |
| Interest allocation records | Annually | Interest distributed per contract terms |

Brokers should conduct unannounced spot audits at least quarterly. The Department of State's own audit protocol involves reviewing three months of escrow records without advance notice. If your books cannot survive that test, your license is at risk.

A written office policy on escrow handling—distributed to every agent and acknowledged in writing—creates an affirmative defense if a rogue agent violates protocol. The policy should specify deposit deadlines, permissible account institutions, ledger maintenance requirements, and the disciplinary consequences for violations.

## Escrow Disputes and Interpleader: The Broker's Exit Ramp

When buyer and seller both claim the escrow deposit after a failed transaction, the broker is caught in the middle. New York law does not require the broker to adjudicate the dispute. Instead, the broker's safest course is to file an interpleader action under CPLR Section 1006.

An interpleader allows the broker to deposit the disputed funds with the court and ask a judge to determine rightful ownership. Filing costs are minimal relative to the liability of releasing funds to the wrong party. The broker can also seek reimbursement of legal fees from the interpleaded funds, though courts grant this discretionarily.

Until the interpleader is resolved or written release is obtained, the broker must hold the funds. Releasing to either party without authorization—even under pressure from an angry client—violates 19 NYCRR 175.1 and invites both a Department of State complaint and a civil suit from the other side.

## Staying Compliant Under New York Escrow Regulations 2026

The rules have not changed dramatically from prior years, but enforcement has tightened. The Department of State increased its audit staff in 2025, and complaint resolution timelines have shortened. Agents who operated on informal practices—verbal agreements, delayed deposits, sloppy ledgers—face higher odds of getting caught in 2026 than at any point in the past decade.

**Build escrow compliance into your transaction workflow from day one, not as an afterthought when something goes wrong.** Document every deposit date, every written authorization, every ledger entry. If your brokerage does not have a formal escrow policy, draft one this week—or push your managing broker to do so. The New York escrow regulations 2026 framework rewards agents and brokers who treat other people's money with the gravity it deserves, and punishes those who treat it as an administrative footnote.
