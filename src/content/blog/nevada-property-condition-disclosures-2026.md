## Why Nevada Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026 Demand Your Attention

Every transaction cycle brings subtle shifts in enforcement and form language, and the rules governing Nevada property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 are no exception. The Nevada Real Estate Division (NRED) under the Department of Business and Industry continues to hold licensees to strict disclosure standards rooted in NRS Chapter 113 and NRS Chapter 645. Agents who treat these forms as a box-checking exercise rather than a legal shield expose themselves—and their brokers—to rescission claims, commission clawbacks, and disciplinary action.

The state's disclosure framework serves a dual purpose: it protects buyers from hidden defects and insulates sellers (and their agents) from post-closing lawsuits when disclosures are complete and accurate. If you listed a property this week in Clark, Washoe, or any other county, the obligations remain identical statewide. Understanding the statutory structure, the correct forms, and the precise timing requirements is not optional knowledge for a licensed professional in Nevada.

## The Statutory Framework: NRS 113 and NRS 645

**Nevada's disclosure obligations live primarily in two chapters of the Nevada Revised Statutes.** NRS 113.100 through 113.150 address the seller's duty to disclose the condition of residential property, while NRS 645.252 and 645.253 impose separate duties on licensees regarding material facts and known defects. These two frameworks operate in parallel, meaning an agent can violate licensee duties even when the seller technically completes the required form.

NRS 113.130 mandates that sellers of residential property containing one to four units provide buyers with a written disclosure statement. The form must describe the condition of structural components, mechanical systems, environmental hazards, and neighborhood nuisances to the best of the seller's actual knowledge. The seller is not obligated to perform inspections or hire consultants—but what they know, they must disclose.

Under NRS 645.252, every licensee owes a duty to disclose material and relevant facts to all parties, regardless of whom they represent. This statute creates independent liability for the agent. If you know about polybutylene pipes, a radon test result, or a former meth lab remediation, you cannot stay silent just because the seller omitted it from the form.

## The Forms: What Nevada Agents Actually Use

The standard form for residential transactions is the **Seller's Real Property Disclosure Form** prescribed under NRS 113.130. NRED does not publish a single mandated template the way some states do; instead, brokerages typically adopt the form produced by the Nevada REALTORS association or their franchise's proprietary version—so long as it meets the statutory minimum content requirements.

| Form / Document | Governing Statute | Who Completes It | Delivery Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seller's Real Property Disclosure Form | NRS 113.130 | Seller | Before or with offer acceptance |
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (pre-1978 homes) | 42 U.S.C. 4852d / 24 CFR 35 | Seller and agent | Before contract ratification |
| Residential Disclosure Guide (common ownership communities) | NRS 116.4103 | HOA / Management Company | Within 10 days of request |
| Environmental hazard addenda (asbestos, meth, radon) | NRS 113.130(3) | Seller | Concurrent with disclosure form |

**The lead-based paint disclosure is federally mandated and applies to every pre-1978 dwelling in Nevada regardless of local custom.** Agents representing sellers of these properties must deliver EPA's "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" pamphlet, collect signatures on the lead disclosure form, and retain records for three years. Failure to comply subjects agents to fines up to $19,507 per violation under 24 CFR 35.86. ## Environmental Disclosures: Beyond Mold and Asbestos

Nevada's desert geography creates environmental disclosure scenarios that agents in temperate states rarely face. Historic mining activity, perchlorate contamination near former military sites, and naturally occurring arsenic in groundwater all trigger potential disclosure issues in specific counties. The seller's form asks about known environmental conditions, but it also requires disclosure of any government notices received about the property's environmental status.

NRS 113.130(3) specifically requires the seller to disclose knowledge of hazardous materials, substances, or waste that have been stored, released, or found on the property. This covers former methamphetamine labs—a persistent issue in parts of northern Nevada—as well as underground storage tanks, asbestos insulation, and soil vapor intrusion. The Nevada Division of Environmental Protection (NDEP) maintains a list of contaminated sites, and agents would be wise to check it during listing intake.

Radon disclosure remains a gray area in Nevada. The state does not mandate radon testing, but the EPA designates portions of Clark and Washoe counties as Zone 2 (moderate potential). If a seller has test results showing elevated radon levels and conceals them, both the seller and any agent with knowledge of the results face liability under NRS 113.130 and NRS 645.252 respectively.

## What Happens When You Get It Wrong: Liability and Penalties

**Noncompliance with Nevada's disclosure laws triggers three distinct consequence tracks—buyer rescission, civil liability, and regulatory discipline.** Understanding all three is what separates agents who survive audits from those who don't.

Under NRS 113.150, a buyer who does not receive the required disclosure form may cancel the purchase agreement at any time prior to closing without forfeiting the earnest money deposit. This right is absolute—it does not require the buyer to demonstrate harm. In practice, this means a missed disclosure form gives a disgruntled buyer an easy exit weeks into escrow, killing your commission and wasting your seller's time.

Civil liability under NRS 113.130 and NRS 40.770 allows buyers to sue for damages caused by undisclosed defects. Nevada courts have awarded repair costs, diminished property value, and in egregious cases, punitive damages where fraud is established. For the agent, NRS 645.252 violations can result in NRED discipline ranging from a letter of reprimand to license revocation, plus fines of up to $10,000 per violation.

> "Each licensee shall disclose to each party to a real estate transaction as soon as practicable any material and relevant facts, data or information which the licensee knows, or which by the exercise of reasonable care and diligence should have known, relating to the property." — NRS 645.252(1)

The standard of "reasonable care and diligence" means agents cannot plead ignorance when red flags exist. If you notice water staining on a ceiling during a listing appointment and the seller marks "no" next to water intrusion on the form, you have an independent obligation to address that discrepancy.

## Common Mistakes Nevada Agents Make

Five recurring errors show up in NRED complaint files and civil litigation involving disclosure failures. Recognizing them now saves you from learning them in a deposition.

**First, agents routinely deliver the disclosure form after contract ratification rather than before or concurrent with the offer.** NRS 113.130 contemplates delivery at or before the time the buyer makes an offer. When the form arrives three days after mutual acceptance, the buyer gains a fresh rescission window that can destabilize an otherwise solid transaction. Train your sellers to complete the form during listing intake.

Second, listing agents sometimes "help" sellers fill out the form by suggesting answers or downplaying issues. This converts a seller's failure to disclose into an agent's affirmative misrepresentation. Your role is to explain what each section asks—never to supply the answers.

Third, agents neglect to deliver the separate federal lead-based paint disclosure for pre-1978 homes, assuming the state form covers it. It does not. The federal requirement stands alone, demands its own signed acknowledgment, and carries its own penalty structure. Agents handling older inventory in downtown Reno or historic Henderson neighborhoods must treat this as a non-negotiable step. The [Virginia property condition disclosure process](/blog/virginia-property-condition-disclosures-2026) provides a useful comparison of how states layer federal requirements on top of state forms.

Fourth, buyer's agents fail to confirm receipt in writing. NRS 113.140 gives buyers five days from receipt to rescind—but without a documented delivery date, disputes over when the clock started become costly.

Fifth, agents ignore updated disclosures. When a seller discovers a new defect between listing and closing—say, a sewer scope reveals a collapsed lateral line—an amended disclosure must reach the buyer immediately. Sitting on new information until after closing is a textbook NRS 645.252 violation.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under NRS 645.630 for the acts of their licensees. A single missed disclosure form in your office's transaction files can become a pattern allegation if NRED opens an investigation. Proactive auditing is the only reliable defense.

| Audit Point | What to Verify | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure form in file | Signed, dated, delivered before or at offer | Every transaction |
| Lead-based paint form (pre-1978) | Seller and buyer signatures, pamphlet delivery noted | Every qualifying property |
| Delivery timestamp | Written acknowledgment of receipt with date | Every transaction |
| Amended disclosures | Filed when new defects arise mid-transaction | As triggered |
| Agent knowledge documentation | Notes confirming agent addressed red flags with seller | Random quarterly review |

Brokers should require listing agents to submit the completed disclosure form as a condition of entering a listing into the MLS. This front-loads compliance rather than chasing it during escrow. Additionally, transaction coordinators reviewing files should flag any property built before 1978 that lacks a lead-based paint disclosure—automated [deal tracking systems](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) can catch this gap before it becomes a regulatory problem.

Quarterly file audits should pull a random sample of closed and active transactions, checking for completeness, proper signatures, and delivery documentation. When deficiencies surface, the corrective action should be documented in the agent's file—not handled with a verbal reminder that leaves no paper trail.
