## Understanding New York Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

Every residential sale of a pre-1978 property in New York triggers a mandatory lead paint disclosure obligation that sits on both the seller and the listing agent. The **New York lead paint disclosure requirements 2026** have not changed substantively from prior years in terms of the federal baseline, but New York City's Local Law 31 amendments and heightened enforcement by the Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD) mean agents face a more aggressive compliance environment than their counterparts in most other states. Failing to execute the right forms at the right time exposes you to federal fines up to $19,507 per violation, potential rescission of the sale, and personal civil liability.

The federal backbone here is the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018), enforced through 24 CFR Part 35 and 40 CFR Part 745. New York layers its own requirements through Real Property Law Section 462 and, within the five boroughs, NYC Administrative Code Title 27, Article 14. Agents who treat lead paint disclosure as a simple checkbox are the ones who end up in front of the New York Department of State's Division of Licensing Services explaining why they skipped steps.

## The Federal Framework and How New York Adds to It

At the federal level, every seller and lessor of pre-1978 housing must disclose known lead-based paint or hazards, provide the EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home," and give buyers a 10-day inspection period. The form most agents use is the standard EPA/HUD "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form, which has no official federal form number but is universally referenced by its title.

**In New York, the disclosure obligation attaches at contract signing, not at closing.** This distinction matters because the buyer's 10-day inspection window runs from receipt of the disclosure, and if you deliver it late, you have effectively extended their due diligence timeline or, worse, given them grounds to void the contract. The New York State Association of Realtors (NYSAR) publishes its own lead paint disclosure rider as part of its standard contract package, and most MLS boards in New York require it as an upload before a listing goes active.

New York City adds a further layer through [Local Law 31 of 2020](https://www.nyc.gov/site/hpd/services-and-information/lead-based-paint.page), which expanded inspection and remediation requirements for multifamily buildings. While Local Law 31 primarily targets landlords, it affects sales transactions because any existing HPD lead violation on the property becomes a known hazard that the seller must disclose. Agents listing in the five boroughs should pull HPD violation history as part of their pre-listing routine.

## Forms and Documents Agents Must Assemble

The paperwork side of lead paint disclosure in New York involves multiple documents working together. Here is a breakdown of what you need in the file before the buyer signs the contract:

| Document | Source | When Delivered | Who Signs |
|----------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Form | EPA/HUD standard or NYSAR rider | At or before contract execution | Seller, Buyer, both agents |
| EPA Pamphlet ("Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home") | EPA website or printed copy | With disclosure form | Buyer acknowledges receipt |
| 10-Day Inspection Contingency or Waiver | Written into contract or separate addendum | At contract execution | Buyer |
| Known Hazard Reports (if any) | Seller's records, HPD, or prior inspection reports | At or before contract execution | Seller attests to accuracy |

The NYSAR form is typically referenced as the "Lead Paint Disclosure Rider" and is included in the NYSAR Forms Library accessible through TransactionDesk or zipLogix. In New York City transactions, attorneys often draft a bespoke lead paint rider that incorporates Local Law 31 compliance language, particularly for co-ops and condos in pre-war buildings.

Agents sometimes ask whether the Property Condition Disclosure Statement (PCDS) under Real Property Law Section 462 covers lead paint. It does include a lead paint question, but completing the PCDS does not satisfy the separate federal lead paint disclosure requirement. These are two distinct obligations. Many sellers in New York opt to pay the $500 PCDS credit rather than complete the form, but even if they choose that path, the federal lead paint disclosure is still mandatory and cannot be bought out.

## Liability When Agents Get It Wrong

**The penalties for non-compliance operate on two tracks: federal enforcement and state-level disciplinary action.** The EPA can impose civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation under 40 CFR 745.118. The Department of Justice can pursue treble damages in private actions brought by buyers who discover undisclosed lead hazards after closing.

At the state level, the New York Department of State can suspend or revoke your license for failing to ensure proper disclosures in a transaction you brokered. Under Real Property Law Section 442-c, agents have an affirmative duty to supervise the completion of all legally required disclosures. A buyer who was not given the disclosure can also seek rescission of the contract—meaning the deal unwinds entirely—plus attorneys' fees and costs.

Beyond regulatory action, errors and omissions insurance carriers have increasingly added lead paint disclosure exclusions or sub-limits to their policies. If your E&O policy excludes claims arising from failure to deliver mandated disclosures, you are personally exposed. Check your declarations page now rather than after a claim lands.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosure

Five recurring errors show up in disciplinary actions and arbitration cases involving New York agents. Each one is entirely preventable.

**First, agents deliver the disclosure at closing instead of at contract execution.** By the time the parties are at the closing table, the buyer's 10-day inspection window should have long since expired or been waived. Delivering late does not cure the violation—it confirms it. The federal rule is clear that the disclosure must be provided before the purchaser is obligated under a contract.

Second, agents fail to confirm that the seller checked the correct box. The disclosure form asks whether the seller has actual knowledge of lead-based paint or hazards. Many sellers check "unknown" reflexively, but if the property has HPD violations on record or prior inspection reports the seller received, "unknown" is inaccurate. Agents who do not ask follow-up questions about prior renovations, abatement work, or city violations are setting up a future misrepresentation claim.

Third, agents omit the EPA pamphlet entirely or provide an outdated version. The current version was last revised in 2021, and earlier printings are technically non-compliant. A missing pamphlet means the buyer's acknowledgment of receipt is invalid, which means the entire disclosure package is deficient. Similar pitfalls arise with [tracking deadlines across multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), where volume causes documentation gaps.

Fourth, agents assume co-op and condo sales are exempt. They are not. If the unit is in a building constructed before 1978, the disclosure applies regardless of ownership structure. The only residential exemptions under federal law are zero-bedroom units (studios used exclusively as sleeping quarters without separate living spaces), housing for the elderly or disabled (unless children reside there), and properties that have been certified lead-free by a licensed inspector.

Fifth, the listing agent and buyer's agent both fail to sign the form. The federal rule requires signatures from the seller, buyer, and each agent involved in the transaction. A missing agent signature does not void the sale, but it does expose that agent to individual liability if the EPA or a private plaintiff pursues enforcement.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under 19 NYCRR 175.21, which requires that they maintain a system of oversight sufficient to ensure agents comply with all applicable laws. A quarterly audit of closed transaction files should specifically check for the following lead paint compliance items.

| Audit Item | Compliant | Non-Compliant Indicator |
|------------|-----------|------------------------|
| Disclosure form present in file | Signed by all four parties, dated before or at contract execution | Missing signatures, dated at closing, or absent entirely |
| EPA pamphlet acknowledgment | Buyer's initials or signature confirming receipt | No acknowledgment or outdated pamphlet version |
| 10-day inspection clause | Buyer elected inspection or signed written waiver | No mention of inspection right in contract or addenda |
| Known hazard documentation | Seller's attestation matches HPD records and property history | Seller checked "unknown" despite existing violation records |
| Date sequencing | Disclosure date precedes or matches contract date | Disclosure dated after contract execution |

Brokers should also confirm that their agents are not relying solely on the transaction attorney to handle lead paint disclosure. In New York's attorney-state practice, it is tempting to assume the lawyer will catch it. But the regulatory obligation runs to the licensee, not to counsel. If the attorney fails to include the disclosure and the agent does not independently verify, the agent—and by extension the broker—is liable.

For brokerages managing high transaction volumes, building an [automated compliance check into your deal-tracking workflow](/blog/where-deals-break-down) eliminates the human error that causes most of these failures. The point of intervention is before contract execution, not during file review after closing.

## Special Considerations for New York City Transactions

New York City's lead paint enforcement landscape is materially different from the rest of the state. HPD actively issues violations under Local Law 1 of 2004 and Local Law 31 of 2020, and these violations attach to the property regardless of ownership changes. A buyer who inherits open HPD lead violations may face mandatory abatement costs exceeding $20,000 per unit.

**Listing agents in the five boroughs should pull the HPD Online system for any property built before 1978 to check for open or closed lead violations.** This is not legally required of the agent, but it is a best practice that protects both the seller's disclosure accuracy and the agent's own liability position. If a violation exists and the seller fails to disclose it, the buyer's attorney will name the listing agent in any subsequent claim.

For co-op transactions, the managing agent may hold inspection records or abatement certifications that the individual shareholder never received. Listing agents should request lead paint documentation from the managing agent as part of the standard co-op package, alongside financials, minutes, and the recognition agreement. This is analogous to the [seller disclosure coordination challenges](/blog/rhode-island-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) seen in other states with layered requirements.

## Staying Compliant When Deadlines Compress

The practical challenge for most New York agents is not ignorance of the rules—it is timing. In a hot market with multiple offers and compressed attorney review periods, the lead paint disclosure can slip through the cracks if it is not part of your standard pre-contract checklist. The 10-day inspection window also creates scheduling pressure; if the buyer requests an inspection, you need a certified lead inspector on short notice, and availability in metro New York can be tight during peak season.

Build the disclosure delivery into your listing launch process, not your contract process. The moment a property goes active, the disclosure should be pre-signed by the seller and ready for immediate delivery to any prospective buyer. This eliminates the scramble at offer acceptance and ensures the 10-day clock starts as early as possible.
