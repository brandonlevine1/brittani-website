## Tennessee Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026: The Rules That Actually Bind

The question of Tennessee who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is not merely a negotiation talking point. It is a compliance obligation governed by state statute, contractual language in Tennessee REALTORS standard forms, and oversight from the Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC). Agents who treat cost allocation as an afterthought expose themselves and their brokers to disciplinary action, deal collapse, and civil liability.

Tennessee does not have a single statute that dictates exactly who pays every closing cost. Instead, the framework relies on a combination of statutory mandates for specific fees, contractual freedom for everything else, and longstanding county-level customs that vary between Memphis, Nashville, Knoxville, and Chattanooga. Your job as a licensee is to know which costs are fixed by law, which are negotiable, and where to document the agreement so there is no ambiguity at the closing table.

## Statutory Framework: What Tennessee Law Actually Requires

**Tennessee Code Annotated Title 67, Chapter 4, Part 4 governs transfer taxes, and this is the one closing cost with a clear statutory default.** The real estate transfer tax under T.C.A. 67-4-409 is imposed at $0.37 per $100 of the purchase price. While the statute places the tax liability on the grantor (seller), the parties can contractually shift it.

Recording fees fall under T.C.A. 8-21-1001 and are set by the county register of deeds. These fees attach to the document being recorded, meaning the deed recording fee is customarily the buyer's cost because the buyer is the party receiving the recorded instrument. Mortgage recording fees are always the buyer's responsibility since the buyer is the borrowing party.

The mortgage tax under T.C.A. 67-4-409(b) is assessed at $0.115 per $100 of indebtedness and is the borrower's obligation by statute. There is no customary or contractual workaround here — if your buyer is financing, they pay the mortgage tax. ## The Standard Cost Split: What Custom Dictates in 2026

Beyond the statutory mandates, Tennessee closing cost allocation follows regional custom unless the contract states otherwise. The table below reflects the prevailing split across most Middle and East Tennessee counties.

| Closing Cost Category | Customary Payor | Statutory Mandate? |
|---|---|---|
| Owner's title insurance | Seller | No — negotiable |
| Lender's title insurance | Buyer | No — negotiable |
| Transfer tax ($0.37/$100) | Seller | Yes — T.C.A. 67-4-409 |
| Mortgage tax ($0.115/$100) | Buyer | Yes — T.C.A. 67-4-409(b) |
| Recording fee (deed) | Buyer | No — custom |
| Attorney/closing fee | Split or buyer | No — negotiable |
| Survey | Buyer | No — negotiable |
| Home warranty | Seller | No — negotiable |
| HOA transfer/estoppel fee | Seller | No — negotiable |

West Tennessee, particularly Shelby County, has some variations. The seller in Memphis often pays for the title search in addition to the owner's policy, while in Nashville the buyer more frequently absorbs that cost. Agents must not assume statewide uniformity.

## Forms and Documentation: Where Agents Must Get It Right

The Tennessee REALTORS Form RF401 (Residential Purchase and Sale Agreement) is the primary document governing cost allocation. Section 12 of RF401 contains explicit blanks and checkboxes for designating which party pays title insurance, survey costs, home warranty premiums, and closing/attorney fees. Leaving these fields blank is not a neutral act — it creates ambiguity that can torpedo a deal at the closing table.

**Form RF451 (Counter Offer) is where cost allocation most frequently changes hands during negotiation.** If your buyer initially asks the seller to pay all closing costs and the seller counters with a partial contribution, that counter must clearly specify the dollar amount or category limitation. A vague counter like "seller will contribute to buyer's costs" without a cap is a compliance failure waiting to happen.

Form RF301 (Listing Agreement) also matters here. Paragraph 8 of the listing agreement addresses seller expenses, and agents should discuss transfer tax, title insurance, and potential buyer-requested credits with the seller at the listing stage. Waiting until an offer arrives to explain that the seller customarily pays for the owner's title policy is a disservice that TREC views dimly. ## Liability Risks: What Happens When Agents Get It Wrong

TREC has authority under T.C.A. 62-13-312 to discipline licensees for misrepresentation, failure to disclose material facts, and incompetence. An agent who tells a buyer "the seller always pays that" when the contract is silent on a particular cost has made a misrepresentation. Penalties include formal reprimand, mandatory continuing education hours, fines of up to $1,000 per violation, and license suspension or revocation in egregious cases.

Civil liability is the larger financial exposure. If a buyer arrives at closing and discovers $4,000 in unexpected costs because the agent failed to properly document who was paying what, the buyer can sue the agent for negligent misrepresentation. Tennessee courts have upheld such claims when the agent's statements created reasonable reliance. The broker's errors and omissions insurance may cover the damages, but the deductible, increased premiums, and reputational harm are real.

> "Any licensee who fails to exercise reasonable skill and care in the performance of licensed activities shall be subject to disciplinary proceedings." — T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(15)

Deal cancellation is the most common immediate consequence. When a buyer's lender pulls the Closing Disclosure three days before closing and the numbers do not match what the buyer expected, the buyer may exercise their right to walk — especially if the discrepancy relates to costs they believed the seller was covering. The three-day TRID review period under federal law compounds this risk because there is no time to fix contractual ambiguity at the last minute.

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Agents Make

**First, agents routinely fail to complete Section 12 of RF401 in full.** They fill in the purchase price, the earnest money amount, and the closing date, then leave cost allocation checkboxes blank assuming "we'll work it out later." Later never comes cleanly. TREC auditors reviewing transaction files flag incomplete forms as evidence of incompetence under [T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(15)](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/trec.html).

Second, agents conflate seller concessions with seller-paid closing costs. A seller concession is a credit toward the buyer's costs, capped by the buyer's loan program (typically 3% for conventional with less than 10% down, 6% for FHA). Seller-paid closing costs are specific line items the seller contractually agrees to pay directly. Writing "seller to pay 3% of buyer's closing costs" on a contract that also has the seller paying the owner's title policy can push the total concession beyond lender limits, triggering loan denial.

Third, agents in attorney-closing counties (most of Tennessee) forget that the closing attorney represents one party or neither — not both. When the contract says "buyer to pay closing attorney fee," the buyer's agent must confirm whether the attorney is representing the buyer, the seller, or acting as a neutral closing agent. Misidentifying who the attorney represents creates conflicts of interest and potential malpractice exposure.

Fourth, listing agents fail to disclose HOA transfer fees in the listing or during negotiations. Many Tennessee HOAs charge $200 to $500 for transfer documentation and estoppel letters. If the seller's agent does not flag this cost and the contract is silent on it, the seller gets blindsided at closing. This is a disclosure failure under TREC Rule 1260-02-.12.

Fifth, agents do not verify whether the county charges a separate land transfer tax. While the state transfer tax is uniform, certain Tennessee municipalities historically assessed additional local fees. Agents must confirm current county-level charges with the register of deeds rather than relying on outdated closing cost worksheets.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers have supervisory obligations under T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(7) and TREC Rule 1260-02-.01. A broker who fails to review transaction files for complete cost allocation documentation shares liability with the agent. Here is what a broker audit should cover for every closed file.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| RF401 Section 12 | All checkboxes completed | Blank fields or "TBD" notations |
| Seller concession math | Total credits vs. loan program cap | Credit exceeds 3%/6% limit |
| Counter offer clarity | Dollar amounts specified | Vague language like "some costs" |
| Transfer tax allocation | Contract matches HUD/CD | Discrepancy between contract and CD |
| Attorney fee designation | Clear party assignment | No designation or dual representation |

Brokers should run quarterly file audits specifically focused on closing cost documentation. A single TREC complaint from a disgruntled buyer who claims they were surprised at closing can trigger an office-wide audit. The cost of prevention — a 10-minute file review per transaction — is trivial compared to defending a formal complaint.
