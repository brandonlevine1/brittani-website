## Understanding Florida Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026

The question of Florida who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is not answered by a single statute or blanket rule. It is determined by a combination of county custom, contract language, and lender requirements that shift based on the deal structure. Agents who treat this as a set-it-and-forget-it checkbox are exposing themselves — and their brokers — to liability.

Florida closing costs typically range from 6% to 10% of the purchase price when you combine both sides of the transaction. That is real money, often $20,000 to $50,000 on a median-priced Florida home, and misallocating even a single line item can crater a deal days before the closing date. This post breaks down the statutory framework, the exact contract forms that control allocation, and the mistakes that get agents dragged in front of the Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC).

## The Statutory Framework Governing Cost Allocation

**Florida does not have a statute that mandates which party pays specific closing costs.** Unlike some states that legislate seller responsibility for transfer taxes or title charges, Florida leaves allocation almost entirely to the contract. The one exception is documentary stamp tax on the deed, governed by Florida Statute Section 201.02, which imposes the tax on the grantor (seller) unless the parties agree otherwise in writing.

Section 475.25(1)(b) of the Florida Statutes makes it a disciplinary offense for a licensee to be "guilty of fraud, misrepresentation, concealment, false promises, false pretenses, dishonest dealing by trick, scheme, or device" — and that includes misrepresenting who owes what at closing. The FREC has historically treated sloppy cost allocation as negligence falling under this provision.

County customs layer on top of statute. In Miami-Dade, Broward, Sarasota, and Collier counties, the seller customarily selects the title/closing agent and pays for the owner's title insurance policy. In most of the remaining 63 counties, the buyer bears that cost. None of this is legally binding unless memorialized in the contract, which is why your form selection matters enormously.

## The Forms That Control Everything

The two primary residential contracts used by Florida agents are the FAR/BAR AS IS Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (FR/BAR-ASIS-6) and the standard FAR/BAR Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (FR/BAR-6). Both contain a dedicated section — typically Paragraph 9 — that addresses closing costs, title evidence, and who selects the closing agent.

Paragraph 9 of these contracts lets parties check a box for the county where closing will occur, triggering the applicable custom for title insurance responsibility. Agents must also address documentary stamps on the deed, intangible tax on any new mortgage, and prorations of taxes and HOA fees within the contract body. The FAR/BAR Comprehensive Rider provides additional checkboxes for seller-paid closing cost credits, repair allowances, and home warranty allocations.

| Cost Item | Typical Buyer Responsibility | Typical Seller Responsibility |
|-----------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Owner's title insurance (most counties) | Yes | No |
| Owner's title insurance (Miami-Dade, Broward, Sarasota, Collier) | No | Yes |
| Documentary stamps on deed (F.S. 201.02) | No | Yes (unless contract states otherwise) |
| Intangible tax on new mortgage | Yes | No |
| Lender's title insurance | Yes | No |
| Recording fees — deed | No | Yes |
| Recording fees — mortgage | Yes | No |
| Survey | Negotiable (often buyer) | Negotiable |
| HOA estoppel letter | No | Yes (per F.S. 720.30851) |

When agents need to add seller concessions — say, the buyer requests the seller contribute 3% toward closing — they write that dollar amount or percentage into the Additional Terms section or attach a Comprehensive Rider addendum. Failing to tie concessions to a specific cap leaves the seller exposed to open-ended liability.

## What Agents Get Wrong: Five Common Mistakes

**Mistake one: assuming county custom is law.** An agent in Palm Beach County tells a seller they "have to" pay for owner's title insurance because "that's how it works here." The seller pushes back, the buyer's agent disagrees, and the deal stalls. County custom is a default in the FAR/BAR form — it is not a statutory mandate. Either party can negotiate a different allocation by modifying Paragraph 9.

Mistake two: ignoring the TRID Closing Disclosure timeline. Under federal TRID rules, the buyer must receive the Closing Disclosure at least three business days before closing. If cost allocation changes after that form is issued, closing gets pushed. Agents who renegotiate cost splits at the last minute without understanding this federal overlay [cause deals to blow up entirely](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Mistake three: failing to disclose a seller concession to the lender. Seller credits must appear on both the contract and the Closing Disclosure. If an agent negotiates a side agreement for the seller to pay a buyer's cost outside the contract, the lender may treat it as an undisclosed inducement. This triggers loan denial, and the agent may face FREC discipline under F.S. 475.25(1)(b) for concealment.

Mistake four: not updating the contract when the closing agent changes. If a buyer switches lenders or a seller changes title companies mid-transaction, the party responsible for title insurance costs may shift. The original Paragraph 9 election may no longer match the new closing agent's fee structure. Agents must execute an amendment to reflect the change.

Mistake five: overlooking Florida's estoppel fee statute. Under F.S. 720.30851, the HOA must deliver an estoppel letter within a specific timeframe, and the cost is borne by the requesting party — almost always the seller. Agents who forget to budget this $250 to $500 charge create a surprise at the closing table that the seller never agreed to absorb.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokerage compliance depends on catching allocation errors before they reach the closing table. Brokers should build a review checkpoint into their [transaction handoff process](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) that specifically flags cost allocation in Paragraph 9 and any attached riders.

**Every executed contract should be cross-referenced against the buyer's pre-approval letter to confirm the lender will allow the negotiated seller concession amount.** FHA loans cap seller contributions at 6% of the purchase price. Conventional loans cap at 3% for borrowers putting less than 10% down, 6% for 10-25% down, and 9% for over 25% down. VA loans cap at 4% (with certain exclusions). A contract promising a 5% seller credit on an FHA loan is fine, but a 5% credit on a conventional loan with 5% down will get rejected by underwriting.

Brokers should also audit whether agents are using outdated form versions. FAR/BAR updates forms periodically, and using an expired revision can create enforceability problems. The [FREC administrative complaint process](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/intentions2.asp?chession=&catid=5&divid=25) does not require a consumer complaint to initiate an investigation — a broker's own audit failure can trigger regulatory scrutiny during a routine office inspection.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Risk if Missed |
|-------------------|---------------|----------------|
| Paragraph 9 completion | County selection, title insurance allocation | Deal cancellation, buyer/seller dispute |
| Seller concession cap vs. loan type | FHA 6%, Conv 3-9%, VA 4% | Loan denial, delayed closing |
| Form version currency | FR/BAR-ASIS-6 or FR/BAR-6, current revision | Enforceability challenge |
| Estoppel fee allocation | Written in contract or rider | Seller surprise at closing |
| TRID timeline compliance | Three-business-day rule observed | Closing postponement |

Brokers who rely solely on agents to self-police these items are playing a dangerous game. A single FREC complaint that reveals systemic noncompliance across multiple transactions can result in a brokerage-level fine or license suspension under F.S. 475.25(1)(k) for failure to properly supervise.

## How Post-NAR-Settlement Rules Affect Cost Allocation in 2026

The 2024 NAR settlement reshaped how commission costs interact with closing cost allocation. Buyer-broker compensation agreements — now mandatory before showing properties — must clearly state who pays the buyer's agent. If a seller offers compensation through the listing, that amount appears on the Closing Disclosure as a seller expense. If the buyer pays their agent directly, it becomes a buyer closing cost.

This distinction matters for concession math. A seller who agrees to a 3% buyer credit and also offers 2.5% to the buyer's agent is contributing 5.5% total — which may exceed the lender's allowable seller contribution limit depending on loan type. Agents who [miss these intersecting deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) or fail to reconcile the numbers risk triggering a last-minute loan denial.

Florida agents should also be aware that the FAR Buyer Broker Agreement form (FAR-BBA) introduced post-settlement explicitly addresses whether the buyer or seller will fund the cooperating broker's fee. If the contract does not match the buyer-broker agreement, the agent may be forced to absorb the difference or face a breach claim from their own client.

## Liability When Agents Get It Wrong

The FREC has broad disciplinary authority under Chapter 475, Florida Statutes. An agent who misallocates closing costs — whether through negligence or intentional misrepresentation — faces potential outcomes ranging from a letter of reprimand to license revocation. Fines under F.S. 475.25 can reach $5,000 per count per violation.

Beyond regulatory action, agents face civil liability. A buyer who discovers at closing that they owe $8,000 more than expected can sue the listing agent, the buyer's agent, or both for negligent misrepresentation. Florida courts have consistently held that real estate licensees owe a duty of competence in preparing contract documents, and botched cost allocation is one of the most common grounds for claims against E&O policies.

**Deal cancellation is the most immediate consequence.** When a buyer cannot close because costs were misallocated, the seller may claim the deposit under the contract's default provisions. The buyer then looks to their agent for reimbursement. These disputes frequently end up before the Florida Association of Realtors mediation program before escalating to arbitration or litigation.
