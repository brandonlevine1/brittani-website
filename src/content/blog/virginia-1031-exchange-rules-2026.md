## Virginia 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: The State-Level Details Agents Miss

Most Virginia agents know the broad strokes of a 1031 exchange—sell investment property, reinvest the proceeds, defer the capital gains tax. What catches agents off guard is the state-level tax machinery that operates alongside the federal deferral. Understanding Virginia 1031 exchange state tax implications 2026 is not optional if you represent investors in the Commonwealth.

Virginia conforms to Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code through its rolling conformity provision in Virginia Code Section 58.1-301. That conformity means the state generally follows the federal treatment, but "generally" hides a number of compliance traps. Agents who fail to flag these issues early risk blowing deals, exposing clients to unexpected tax bills, and drawing liability back to their own license.

## How Virginia Conformity Actually Works

**Virginia's rolling conformity to the IRC means the state automatically adopts federal tax code changes unless the General Assembly decouples.** As of 2026, no decoupling from Section 1031 has occurred. The Virginia Department of Taxation treats a properly structured like-kind exchange as fully tax-deferred at the state level, mirroring the federal outcome.

This matters because some states—Maryland and California among them—impose additional reporting requirements or partial non-conformity for exchanges involving out-of-state property. Virginia does not. A Virginia investor exchanging a Richmond duplex for a Norfolk warehouse receives identical deferral treatment at both the federal and state level, provided the exchange meets all IRC Section 1031 timing and structural requirements.

The conformity applies to Virginia's individual income tax (reported on Form 760), corporate income tax (Form 500), and pass-through entity taxes. Agents should verify that their clients' CPAs understand this alignment because confusion between states with different conformity stances is one of the most common sources of bad advice in multi-state transactions.

## Withholding Requirements for Nonresident Sellers

Here is where the state-level complexity spikes. Under Virginia Code Section 58.1-1816, the purchaser or settlement agent in a Virginia real estate transaction must withhold state income tax from nonresident sellers. The withholding amount is the greater of 2% of the sales price or the seller's anticipated gain.

| Scenario | Withholding Required? | Amount | Exception Available? |
|----------|----------------------|--------|---------------------|
| Nonresident selling investment property (no exchange) | Yes | Greater of 2% of price or gain | No |
| Nonresident completing a valid 1031 exchange | Potentially exempt | $0 if exemption filed | Yes—via affidavit |
| Virginia resident selling investment property | No | N/A | N/A |
| Nonresident selling primary residence | Exempt | $0 | Yes—statutory exemption |

The exemption for 1031 exchanges requires the nonresident seller to provide a signed affidavit to the settlement agent confirming the transaction qualifies under IRC Section 1031. Without that affidavit, the settlement agent must withhold. This is reported on Virginia Form VA-6 and remitted to the Virginia Department of Taxation within 30 days of closing.

**Agents representing nonresident investors must surface this requirement before the settlement statement is prepared.** A last-minute scramble to produce an affidavit—or worse, discovering at the closing table that withholding will deplete the exchange funds—can derail the 45-day identification window and collapse the entire exchange.

## Critical Deadlines and Their Virginia-Specific Impact

Virginia does not impose its own exchange deadlines beyond the federal ones. The 45-day identification period and 180-day closing period apply identically. However, Virginia's tax filing calendar creates an indirect deadline trap that agents rarely discuss with clients.

If a taxpayer's Virginia income tax return (Form 760 or Form 500) is due before the 180-day exchange period expires, the exchange must close by the earlier return due date—not the 180th day. For calendar-year taxpayers selling property in the first quarter, this compressed timeline can shave weeks off the replacement period. Extensions of the Virginia return (filed via Form 760IP) extend this deadline, but only if actually filed before the original due date.

Agents should document a written reminder to their investor clients no later than 15 days post-closing to confirm the extension has been filed. This is not tax advice—it is transaction coordination that protects the deal you brokered. Tracking these parallel deadlines is the kind of detail that [transaction coordinators handle systematically](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) rather than leaving it to memory.

## Common Mistakes Virginia Agents Make on 1031 Exchanges

**The errors below are not hypothetical—they reflect patterns from actual Virginia Department of Taxation audits and DPOR complaint files.**

The first mistake is failing to identify the nonresident withholding issue before listing. Agents who take investment property listings from out-of-state owners without asking about exchange intent create a disclosure gap that surfaces at settlement. By then, the qualified intermediary may not have time to structure the exchange properly.

The second mistake is advising on tax treatment. Virginia Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR) rules prohibit licensees from providing tax advice. Agents who tell clients "you don't need to worry about Virginia taxes because it's a 1031" are making a legal conclusion they are not licensed to make. The correct move is to inform the client that Virginia conforms to federal 1031 rules and direct them to a CPA or tax attorney for confirmation.

Third, agents frequently confuse the role of the qualified intermediary with the settlement agent. In Virginia, the settlement agent handles [title and closing procedures](/blog/virginia-title-and-closing-procedures-2026) and withholding compliance. The QI holds exchange funds and enforces the exchange structure. Mixing these roles—or assuming one party covers the other's obligations—is how withholding gets missed.

Fourth, agents forget that boot (non-like-kind property or cash received in the exchange) is taxable at the Virginia level even when the rest of the exchange qualifies for deferral. If your client pulls $40,000 in cash from an exchange, that amount hits their Virginia return as recognized gain.

Fifth, listing agents sometimes market properties as "1031 exchange eligible" without understanding that eligibility depends on the investor's holding period and intent, not on the property itself. This language in MLS remarks can create implied representations that expose the agent to liability if the buyer's exchange fails for qualification reasons.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers supervising Virginia agents involved in investment transactions carry specific oversight obligations under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2130 and the Virginia Real Estate Board regulations in 18 VAC 135-20. These obligations intensify when exchange transactions are involved because the compliance surface area expands.

| Broker Audit Point | What to Verify | Risk If Missed |
|-------------------|----------------|----------------|
| Nonresident seller identification | File contains residency status documentation | Withholding liability falls to settlement agent; agent reputation damage |
| QI engagement letter | Signed QI agreement in file before contract ratification | Exchange may be invalid; client tax liability; potential E&O claim |
| Tax advice boundaries | No written or email communications providing tax conclusions | DPOR complaint; license disciplinary action |
| Boot calculations documented | Settlement statement reflects exchange structure accurately | Client underpays state taxes; audit triggers penalties |
| Affidavit of exchange on file | Signed affidavit provided to settlement agent before closing | Unnecessary 2% withholding depletes exchange funds |

**Brokers should implement a file review checkpoint specifically for any transaction flagged as a 1031 exchange.** This checkpoint should verify that the agent has not crossed into tax advisory territory, that the QI was engaged before contract execution, and that withholding exemption documentation is complete. Without this, the brokerage absorbs downstream risk when agents handle these transactions on autopilot.

Review your firm's [commission structure documentation](/blog/virginia-commission-structure-changes-2026) as well—exchange transactions sometimes involve deferred closings or failed exchanges that change how and when commissions are earned.

## Liability Exposure When Compliance Fails

The consequences of botching the Virginia state tax side of a 1031 exchange extend beyond the client's tax bill. Settlement agents who fail to withhold from nonresident sellers face direct assessment of the tax owed plus a penalty of up to 100% of the amount that should have been withheld, under Virginia Code Section 58.1-1816(D).

For agents, the exposure is indirect but real. If an agent's failure to identify a nonresident seller or surface the exchange documentation requirement leads to a blown exchange, the client faces an immediate capital gains tax event at both the federal and Virginia level. On a $500,000 gain, that combined hit can exceed $150,000. Agents have faced civil suits and DPOR complaints in these scenarios even when they did not provide explicit tax advice—the allegation is typically that the agent failed to recommend professional consultation or failed to coordinate with the settlement agent on known exchange requirements.

The [agency disclosure framework](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) in Virginia requires agents to act in their client's best interest, which courts have interpreted to include flagging foreseeable tax consequences of transaction structures the agent is facilitating. Not knowing is not a defense when the transaction file shows the agent knew the property was held for investment.
