## Understanding Pennsylvania Escrow Regulations 2026

Pennsylvania escrow regulations 2026 remain one of the most audited areas of real estate practice in the Commonwealth, and violations carry consequences that range from license suspension to civil liability. The State Real Estate Commission (SREC), operating under the Department of State's Bureau of Professional and Occupational Affairs, enforces escrow rules codified primarily in Title 49, Chapter 35 of the Pennsylvania Code. If you hold a Pennsylvania license and touch earnest money at any point during a transaction, the rules below apply to you directly.

The statutory backbone sits in the Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. Sections 455.101–455.902. RELRA grants the SREC authority to discipline licensees who mishandle trust funds, commingle personal and escrow monies, or fail to maintain proper records. These are not suggestions—they are enforceable regulations that carry fines up to $10,000 per violation and can end a career.

## Deposit Timelines and Account Requirements

**The clock starts the moment a licensee receives a deposit check or wire confirmation.** Under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.327(a), brokers must deposit escrow funds into a federally insured escrow or trust account by the end of the next business day following receipt. The only exception: when the parties' written agreement explicitly states a different deposit timeline.

That account must be clearly designated as a trust or escrow account at a federally insured bank, savings association, or credit union operating in Pennsylvania. The account title must include the word "escrow" or "trust" and the name of the brokerage. Personal accounts, business operating accounts, and out-of-state accounts that are not federally insured all violate SREC rules.

Interest-bearing escrow accounts are permitted under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.327(c), but only when all parties to the transaction provide written consent specifying who will receive the interest. Without that written consent, the broker must use a non-interest-bearing account.

## The Forms That Matter

Pennsylvania agents work primarily within the PAR (Pennsylvania Association of Realtors) form ecosystem. The Standard Agreement for the Sale of Real Estate (PAR Form ASR) contains the escrow provisions most agents encounter daily, including the deposit amount, the timeline for delivery, and the identity of the escrow holder.

| Form | Purpose | Escrow Relevance |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| PAR Form ASR | Agreement of Sale (Residential) | Specifies deposit amount, holder, and deposit deadline |
| PAR Form EFD | Escrow Fund Disbursement | Authorizes release of escrow funds at or before settlement |
| PAR Form EMD | Earnest Money Dispute | Addresses disbursement when parties disagree on entitlement |
| PAR Form RR | Release of All Claims | Often accompanies escrow release after termination |
| SREC Form REL-4 | Broker Audit Compliance | Used during SREC escrow account audits |

When disputes arise, Form EMD becomes the document of record. It outlines the escrow holder's limited options: hold the funds pending mutual written release, hold pending a court order, or interplead the funds into court. Agents who advise escrow holders to release funds without proper authorization from all parties expose themselves to liability claims.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

SREC enforcement is not theoretical. The Commission publishes disciplinary actions monthly, and escrow violations consistently appear among the top reasons for sanctions. Penalties escalate based on severity and intent.

A first-time late-deposit violation with no client harm typically results in a formal reprimand and a civil penalty between $1,000 and $3,000. Commingling—mixing escrow funds with personal or business operating funds—triggers mandatory suspension proceedings. Conversion of escrow funds (using client deposits for personal expenses) results in license revocation and criminal referral to the Attorney General's office under 18 Pa.C.S. Section 3927 (theft by failure to make required disposition).

**Beyond SREC action, agents face civil exposure from both buyers and sellers.** A mishandled deposit can torpedo a deal entirely, exposing the agent and brokerage to damages for the buyer's lost inspection costs, appraisal fees, and rate-lock expenses. Brokers carry vicarious liability for their agents' escrow failures under RELRA Section 604, which is why [understanding where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) should be a standing agenda item at every brokerage meeting.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Escrow in Pennsylvania

Five recurring errors account for the majority of SREC complaints filed against Pennsylvania licensees. None of them are complex—they are procedural failures that happen under time pressure.

**First: holding checks "until the seller signs."** Some listing agents instruct buyers' agents to hold deposit checks until the seller has accepted the offer. This violates 49 Pa. Code Section 35.327 unless the Agreement of Sale explicitly authorizes delayed deposit. The deposit clock begins upon receipt, not upon acceptance.

Second: failing to document the date and time of receipt. SREC auditors look for a written record showing exactly when the brokerage received the deposit. Without it, there is no way to prove compliance with the next-business-day rule. A simple intake log with date, time, source, amount, and receiving agent's initials satisfies this requirement.

Third: releasing funds on verbal authorization. Pennsylvania law requires written consent from all parties before escrow funds can be disbursed. A phone call from one party's attorney saying "go ahead and release" is not sufficient. Agents who [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) under pressure often skip this step and pay for it later.

Fourth: depositing into the wrong account during brokerage transitions. When agents switch brokerages or when a brokerage changes banks, deposits occasionally land in the old account. The broker of record must ensure all active escrow funds transfer properly and that the new account meets SREC titling requirements.

Fifth: ignoring the PAR Form EMD process during disputes. When a deal falls apart and both parties claim entitlement to the deposit, some agents attempt informal resolution by holding funds indefinitely. Pennsylvania courts have held that unreasonable delay in interpleading disputed funds can constitute a breach of fiduciary duty.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear ultimate responsibility for escrow compliance under RELRA Section 604(a)(15). The SREC expects brokers to maintain systems—not just policies—that prevent violations before they occur.

| Broker Obligation | Frequency | Regulatory Basis |
|-------------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Reconcile escrow account | Monthly (minimum) | 49 Pa. Code 35.327(b) |
| Review deposit intake logs | Weekly (best practice) | SREC Audit Standards |
| Verify account titling | Upon opening and annually | 49 Pa. Code 35.327 |
| Document interest-bearing consent | Per transaction | 49 Pa. Code 35.327(c) |
| Maintain records for 3 years | Ongoing | RELRA Section 604(a)(18) |

Monthly reconciliation is the bare minimum. The reconciliation must compare the bank statement balance against the ledger of all individual deposits being held. Any discrepancy—even one dollar—must be investigated and documented within 48 hours. Brokers who delegate this to an unlicensed bookkeeper without personal review are still personally liable for errors.

SREC can initiate a random audit at any time, and the [Pennsylvania State Real Estate Commission](https://www.dos.pa.gov/ProfessionalLicensing/BoardsCommissions/RealEstateCommission/Pages/default.aspx) has increased audit frequency since 2024. During an audit, investigators will request the escrow account bank statements, the individual transaction ledgers, deposit receipts, and all disbursement authorizations for a specified period. Brokers who maintain [organized transaction handoff procedures](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) are materially better positioned to survive an audit without findings.

## Escrow and Wire Fraud: Pennsylvania's Growing Concern

Wire fraud targeting real estate escrow accounts has prompted the SREC to issue advisories reinforcing agent obligations. While Pennsylvania has not yet codified specific wire verification procedures into Chapter 35, the Commission's 2025 guidance bulletin recommended that all brokerages implement callback verification protocols before wiring escrow funds.

The practical implication: if your brokerage receives wiring instructions by email and disburses funds without verbal verification to a known phone number, you may face both civil liability and SREC discipline if the funds are lost to fraud. This is a fiduciary duty issue, not merely a best-practice suggestion. Agents who handle closings without [understanding how transaction coordination protects against these gaps](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) are operating without a safety net.
