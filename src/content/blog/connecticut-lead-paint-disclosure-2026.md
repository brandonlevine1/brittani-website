## Understanding Connecticut Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

Connecticut's housing stock is among the oldest in the nation. More than 70 percent of residential properties in the state were built before 1978, the year the federal government banned lead-based paint for residential use. That reality makes Connecticut lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 an everyday concern for agents—not a niche issue that surfaces once a quarter.

The disclosure framework operating in Connecticut sits on two layers: federal law under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and state-level statutes codified primarily in Connecticut General Statutes Section 20-327b through 20-327e. Agents who treat this as a simple checkbox exercise risk fines, deal cancellations, and personal liability that their E&O policy may not cover.

This post breaks down the specific forms, deadlines, penalties, and audit points that licensed agents and brokers need to have locked down heading into 2026 transactions.

## The Federal Foundation: EPA Rules That Apply in Every Connecticut Transaction

**Federal law requires that sellers and their agents disclose known lead-based paint and lead-based paint hazards before a buyer becomes obligated under a purchase contract.** This requirement comes from 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d and is enforced by the EPA under 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F.

Three things must happen before the contract is binding. First, the seller must disclose any known lead-based paint or hazards and provide available records or reports. Second, the buyer must receive the EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." Third, the buyer must be given a 10-day opportunity to conduct a lead inspection or risk assessment (unless waived in writing).

The form agents use nationally is the EPA's "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form, commonly embedded in or attached to state-approved purchase contracts. In Connecticut, this form is typically included as a rider or addendum alongside the Connecticut Residential Property Condition Disclosure Report.

## Connecticut-Specific Statutes and Forms Agents Must Know

Connecticut General Statutes Section 20-327b requires sellers of residential property (one to four units) to complete the Residential Property Condition Disclosure Report. Question 10 on that form directly addresses lead paint, asking sellers whether they have knowledge of lead-based paint on the property. This is separate from—and in addition to—the federal EPA disclosure form.

| Requirement | Federal (EPA) | Connecticut State |
|---|---|---|
| Governing Law | 42 U.S.C. 4852d; 40 CFR 745 | CGS 20-327b through 20-327e |
| Form Used | EPA Lead Paint Disclosure | CT Residential Property Condition Disclosure Report |
| Applies to | Pre-1978 residential housing | All residential sales (1-4 units) |
| Inspection Period | 10 days (waivable) | Not separately required beyond federal |
| Pamphlet Required | "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" | Not a separate state pamphlet |
| Penalty for Non-Compliance | Up to $19,507/violation (2024 adjusted) | Rescission rights, civil liability |

The Connecticut Department of Public Health (CT DPH) also maintains regulations under CGS Section 19a-111 through 19a-111g regarding lead poisoning prevention, which can become relevant when a property has been subject to a lead abatement order. Agents should verify whether a property has any outstanding DPH orders before listing, as those orders create additional disclosure obligations beyond the standard forms.

## The 10-Day Inspection Window and Contract Timing

**Agents frequently mishandle the buyer's right to a lead inspection, either by failing to include the contingency language or by inadvertently shortening the window.** Under federal rules, the buyer gets 10 calendar days to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment. The parties can agree to a different period, but the buyer cannot be forced to accept fewer than zero days—meaning an outright denial of the opportunity is a violation.

In Connecticut practice, this contingency language appears within the EPA disclosure addendum. If a buyer elects to waive the inspection, that waiver must be documented on the disclosure form itself. Simply omitting the contingency from the purchase contract does not satisfy the requirement—agents must ensure the buyer affirmatively acknowledges the waiver in writing on the federal form.

The timing matters for [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) because the 10-day clock starts when the buyer receives all disclosures and the signed contract, not when the offer is accepted verbally. Backdating or failing to document the delivery date exposes the transaction to rescission claims later.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences for non-compliance split into federal and state tracks. On the federal side, HUD and the EPA can impose civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2024). Treble damages are available in private lawsuits, meaning a buyer who discovers undisclosed lead hazards after closing can sue for three times their actual damages plus attorney fees.

On the state side, Connecticut General Statutes Section 20-327b(d) provides buyers with a right of rescission if the seller fails to deliver the property condition disclosure report before the buyer signs the contract. A buyer can void the contract and recover deposits. More critically for agents, CGS Section 20-327e states that a real estate licensee who fails to inform the seller of their disclosure obligations can face disciplinary action by the Connecticut Real Estate Commission.

Brokers should also note that errors here can trigger claims under the licensee's E&O policy. Many carriers have begun adding lead paint disclosure exclusions or sub-limits, making compliance failures a direct financial exposure for the brokerage. The pattern mirrors [where deals commonly break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) due to documentation gaps.

## Common Mistakes Connecticut Agents Make

Five errors show up repeatedly in Connecticut lead paint transactions, and each one is preventable with basic process discipline.

**First, agents confuse the state property condition disclosure with the federal lead paint disclosure.** These are two separate obligations. Completing Question 10 on the CT Residential Property Condition Disclosure Report does not satisfy the federal EPA requirement. Both must be executed independently.

Second, agents forget that the disclosure obligation applies to the seller's agent—not just the seller. Under 40 CFR 745.115(a), the seller's agent must ensure compliance even if the seller is uncooperative. Claiming ignorance because the seller did not mention lead paint does not absolve the listing agent.

Third, some agents allow the 10-day inspection period to be removed entirely from the EPA addendum rather than having the buyer formally waive it. There is a legal difference between a buyer checking the "waive" box and the form simply not including the inspection language. The former is compliant; the latter is a violation.

Fourth, agents fail to retain copies of the signed disclosure for the required three-year federal retention period. 40 CFR 745.113(b)(5) requires that acknowledgment records be kept for not less than three years from the completion date of the transaction. If an agent changes brokerages, those records can easily be lost.

Fifth, agents operating in Connecticut's active rental market forget that lead paint disclosures apply to leasing transactions as well—not just sales. Every new lease or lease renewal for a pre-1978 dwelling requires the same EPA disclosure and pamphlet delivery.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Connecticut law. CGS Section 20-312 holds designated brokers responsible for the acts of their licensees in the course of real estate transactions. A systemic failure to enforce lead paint disclosure compliance across a brokerage creates regulatory and financial exposure that goes beyond any single deal.

| Audit Point | Frequency | Documentation |
|---|---|---|
| EPA disclosure form signed by all parties | Every pre-1978 transaction | Copy in transaction file |
| CT Property Condition Report completed (Question 10) | Every residential sale | Copy in transaction file |
| Buyer pamphlet delivery confirmed | Every pre-1978 transaction | Signed acknowledgment |
| 10-day inspection waiver or election documented | Every pre-1978 transaction | On EPA form |
| Three-year record retention confirmed | Annual audit | Brokerage filing system |

Brokers should build a checklist into their transaction management workflow that flags any property with a construction date before 1978. That flag should trigger automatic verification of both the federal and state disclosure forms before the file can be marked compliant. If your brokerage still relies on agents self-reporting compliance, you have a gap that a single disgruntled buyer could exploit.

The [Connecticut Real Estate Commission](https://portal.ct.gov/dcp/license-services-division/real-estate-commission) publishes disciplinary actions that increasingly include disclosure violations. Reviewing those published actions quarterly gives brokers a concrete sense of where enforcement attention is focused.

## Rental Transactions and the Overlooked Obligation

Connecticut's rental market, particularly in cities like New Haven, Hartford, and Bridgeport, involves a massive inventory of pre-1978 housing. Agents who handle lease transactions must deliver the same EPA disclosure and pamphlet to tenants before the lease is signed. This obligation applies at every lease renewal—not just the initial lease execution.

CGS Section 47a-54f adds a state-specific layer for rental properties by requiring landlords to provide tenants with information about lead hazards and the availability of testing. The [training protocols for new agents](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) at Connecticut brokerages should dedicate specific attention to rental lead paint obligations, since many newly licensed agents handle leasing first and assume these rules only apply to purchases.

Failure to comply in a rental context carries the same federal penalties—up to $19,507 per violation—and exposes the property management side of a brokerage to tenant lawsuits that can include relocation costs, medical expenses, and punitive damages if a child is lead-poisoned.
