## Understanding Massachusetts Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026

The rules governing buyer representation in Massachusetts shifted significantly after the 2024 NAR settlement took effect, and the state's Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons has since issued additional guidance that tightens compliance expectations heading into 2026. If you hold a Massachusetts license and represent buyers, the Massachusetts buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 are not optional—they are a precondition to performing substantive brokerage services. Failing to comply does not just create an ethical problem; it creates a financial one.

Massachusetts operates under a licensee-disclosure framework governed by M.G.L. Chapter 112, Sections 87PP through 87DDD, and further detailed by 254 CMR 2.00 (the Board's administrative regulations). These statutes have always required agents to disclose their agency relationship, but the post-settlement environment now demands a signed, written agreement specifying compensation terms before you accompany a buyer on a single showing. The days of informal handshake representation are over.

## The Legal Foundation: What the Statute Actually Says

**Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 112, Section 87AAA3/4, requires that any licensee acting as a buyer's agent enter into a written agreement disclosing the nature, scope, and duration of the agency relationship.** The Board of Registration reinforced this in its 2025 Advisory Bulletin 25-02, which clarified that verbal agreements do not satisfy the written-agreement requirement regardless of how long the agent has worked with the buyer.

The statute does not prescribe a specific state-authored form. Instead, it sets minimum content requirements: the agreement must identify the parties, define the geographic or property-type scope, state the duration, and disclose how and by whom the buyer's agent will be compensated. The agreement must also include a clear statement that commission rates are negotiable and not set by law.

254 CMR 2.00, Section 12(b) adds that the agreement must be provided to the buyer before the licensee performs any act that could be construed as representation—touring, writing offers, or negotiating on behalf of the buyer all qualify. If you are attending an open house as a visitor, no agreement is needed; the moment you advise a buyer at that open house, you are representing them.

## Which Forms Massachusetts Agents Use

Most practitioners rely on forms published by the Massachusetts Association of Realtors (MAR). The two primary buyer agency forms are MAR Form 511 (Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement) and MAR Form 512 (Non-Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement). Both were revised in late 2025 to incorporate post-settlement language mandated by NAR's revised Participant Rules.

| Form | Type | Key Feature | Duration Default | Compensation Disclosure |
|------|------|-------------|------------------|------------------------|
| MAR Form 511 | Exclusive | Buyer works only with one agent/brokerage | Negotiable (commonly 90 days) | Must state specific amount or percentage |
| MAR Form 512 | Non-Exclusive | Buyer may work with multiple agents | Negotiable (commonly 30-60 days) | Must state specific amount or percentage |
| Custom Brokerage Form | Varies | Drafted by brokerage counsel | Varies | Must meet 254 CMR 2.00 minimums |

**If your brokerage uses a proprietary form instead of MAR 511 or 512, your broker must verify that it meets every element required by 254 CMR 2.00, Section 12(b).** The Board has signaled in recent enforcement actions that missing even one required disclosure—particularly the compensation negotiability statement—can result in a formal complaint proceeding.

Some agents also pair these forms with the MAR Form 801 (Consent to Dual Agency), which must be signed separately if the situation arises later in the transaction. The buyer agency agreement itself cannot pre-authorize dual agency in Massachusetts; consent must be transaction-specific under Section 87AAA3/4(d).

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of operating without a signed buyer agency agreement fall into three categories: regulatory, financial, and transactional.

On the regulatory side, the Board of Registration can initiate disciplinary proceedings under 254 CMR 7.00. Penalties range from a formal reprimand to license suspension or revocation. Fines under the Board's authority can reach $1,000 per violation, and each showing or act of representation without a signed agreement may constitute a separate violation.

Financially, an agent without a signed buyer agency agreement has no enforceable right to compensation. If a dispute arises—say the buyer terminates the relationship after the agent invested weeks of work—the agent has no contract to sue on. Massachusetts courts have consistently held that oral brokerage agreements are unenforceable under the Statute of Frauds (M.G.L. Chapter 259, Section 7), which requires real estate brokerage agreements to be in writing.

Transactionally, a missing buyer agency agreement can [cause deals to break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) when the listing agent or seller's attorney discovers the deficiency during closing preparation. Attorneys in Massachusetts closings routinely audit the buyer-side paperwork, and a gap in documentation can delay or kill a transaction.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Buyer Agreements

**Error one: backdating the agreement.** Some agents, realizing they forgot to secure a signature before showing a property, attempt to backdate the form to cover their tracks. This is fraud. The Board has prosecuted backdating under 254 CMR 7.00(3)(a) as a dishonest act, and it can independently trigger license revocation.

Error two: using a duration term that is unreasonably long without buyer consent. While Massachusetts law does not cap duration, the [NAR settlement practice changes](https://www.nar.realtor/the-facts/nar-settlement-faqs) discourage open-ended terms. Agents who lock buyers into 12-month exclusive agreements without clearly explaining the term and renewal provisions face consumer complaints and potential Board scrutiny for overreaching conduct.

Error three: failing to specify the exact compensation amount. Post-settlement rules require that the buyer agency agreement state a definite amount or percentage the buyer's agent will receive. Language like "whatever the seller offers" or "to be determined" does not satisfy the requirement. MAR's revised Form 511 includes a blank that must be filled with a specific figure—leaving it blank renders the agreement non-compliant.

Error four: not providing a signed copy to the buyer. Under 254 CMR 2.00, Section 12(c), the buyer must receive a copy of the fully executed agreement at the time of signing. Agents who collect signatures on a tablet and fail to email or hand over a copy are technically in violation, even if the agreement itself is properly completed.

Error five: neglecting to terminate or renew expired agreements. If your 90-day agreement lapses and you continue representing the buyer, you are once again operating without a valid contract. The cure is simple—get a new agreement signed before resuming services—but [agents who miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) on this point expose themselves to the same liability as agents who never had an agreement at all.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry independent liability under M.G.L. Chapter 112, Section 87AAA, for the acts of their affiliated licensees. A broker who fails to implement a system for verifying buyer agency agreements can face Board discipline even if the broker personally committed no violation.

| Broker Responsibility | How to Verify | Frequency |
|----------------------|---------------|-----------|
| Agreement signed before first showing | Timestamped copy in transaction file | Per new buyer engagement |
| Compensation amount specified | Review blank fields on Form 511/512 | Per agreement |
| Buyer received signed copy | Delivery confirmation (email receipt, signed acknowledgment) | Per agreement |
| Agreement not expired | Cross-check duration term against active showing dates | Monthly audit |
| Dual agency consent separate from buyer agreement | Verify MAR Form 801 is standalone | Per applicable transaction |

Brokers should conduct file audits at least quarterly, with spot checks whenever a new buyer relationship begins. The Board's 2025 Advisory Bulletin explicitly stated that "a culture of compliance starts with broker oversight" and that ignorance of an affiliated licensee's failure will not shield the broker from accountability.

Building a [checklist-driven audit system](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) into your brokerage workflow is no longer a best practice—it is a regulatory expectation. Documenting that you conducted the audit matters almost as much as the audit itself if the Board ever opens an investigation.

## Compensation Transparency Under the New Framework

The post-settlement environment fundamentally changed how compensation flows in Massachusetts transactions. Buyer's agents can no longer rely on MLS-published cooperative compensation offers as the default mechanism for getting paid. Instead, the buyer agency agreement is now the primary document establishing the agent's right to a fee.

This means the agreement must address several scenarios: what happens if the seller offers compensation that matches or exceeds the agreed amount, what happens if the seller offers less, and what happens if the seller offers nothing. MAR Form 511 now includes conditional language allowing the buyer to authorize the agent to seek compensation from the seller while establishing the buyer's backstop obligation if the seller refuses.

**Agents who gloss over this section during the signing conversation are setting up future disputes.** Buyers who do not understand their potential financial obligation will feel blindsided at closing, and that surprise is exactly the kind of consumer harm the Board investigates. Spend the extra five minutes explaining the compensation mechanics—your license may depend on it.
