# Florida Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026: What Every Agent and Broker Must Get Right

If you handle any pre-1978 residential listings in Florida, the **Florida lead paint disclosure requirements 2026** should be etched into your transaction checklist. Failing to deliver the correct forms at the right time is one of the fastest ways to blow up a deal, trigger a lawsuit, or invite enforcement action from HUD. This is not a gray-area compliance issue — it is a binary pass-fail obligation backed by federal statute and enforced with real financial teeth.

Florida does not have its own standalone state lead paint disclosure statute layered on top of the federal rule. Instead, the requirement flows directly from the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulations at 24 CFR Part 35 and 40 CFR Part 745. That means agents in Florida follow the federal framework without additional state-specific forms — but "federal only" does not mean "simple."

## Who Must Comply and When the Obligation Triggers

The disclosure requirement applies to the sale or lease of any target housing, defined as residential property constructed before 1978. It does not matter whether anyone has ever tested the home or whether the seller believes the paint is fine. The duty exists regardless of actual knowledge.

Exemptions are narrow. Housing for the elderly or disabled where children under six are not expected to reside, zero-bedroom dwellings, property sold at foreclosure, and short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or fewer fall outside the rule. If your listing does not fit squarely into one of those carve-outs, you must comply.

**The obligation triggers before the buyer becomes obligated under the contract.** In a typical Florida Realtors/Florida Bar "AS IS" Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (FR/Bar ASIS-6 or the standard FR/Bar-6), that means the disclosure must be delivered and acknowledged before or at the time the buyer signs the purchase agreement.

## The Required Forms and Documents in Florida Transactions

Because Florida does not mandate a state-specific lead paint form, agents use the federally prescribed "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form. The EPA publishes the template, and most Florida MLS associations and transaction management platforms include it in their standard disclosure packages.

In practice, Florida Realtors provides a version titled "Lead-Based Paint Seller's Disclosure" within its forms library. Agents using the FR/Bar contracts will find a lead paint rider or addendum integrated into the contract package. The critical elements the form must contain are: the seller's disclosure of any known lead-based paint or hazards, a list of any available records or reports, confirmation that the buyer received the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home," and the buyer's acknowledgment of the 10-day inspection opportunity.

All parties — seller, buyer, and their respective agents — must sign the disclosure. This is not a formality. Each signature creates an independent compliance record, and a missing agent signature can be treated as a violation by HUD's Office of Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity.

## The 10-Day Inspection Period: What Florida Agents Get Wrong

Under 24 CFR 35.90, the buyer must receive an opportunity of not less than 10 days to conduct a risk assessment or inspection for lead-based paint. The parties can mutually agree in writing to a different time period — longer or shorter — or the buyer can waive the opportunity entirely. But the waiver must be affirmative and documented.

Many Florida agents assume that because the contract already has a general inspection period (typically 15 days under the AS IS contract), the lead paint inspection window is automatically satisfied. That assumption is dangerous. The lead paint inspection opportunity is a separate, federally mandated right. If your contract's inspection period is only 7 days and you have not expressly addressed the lead paint inspection window, you may have a compliance gap.

The safest practice is to check the box on the disclosure form confirming the buyer received the 10-day opportunity, and if your contract inspection window is shorter, to either extend it or include a clear written acknowledgment that the buyer waives or modifies the lead paint inspection period specifically.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: The Numbers That Should Concern You

**Agents who skip or botch the lead paint disclosure face a penalty structure that escalates quickly.** HUD can impose civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act). The EPA can pursue separate penalties of up to $46,517 per day for ongoing violations.

Beyond agency enforcement, buyers have a private right of action. Under Section 1018(b)(3) of Title X, a buyer can recover treble damages — three times the amount of actual damages — plus reasonable attorney's fees and court costs. And the contract itself may be voidable at the buyer's election if the disclosure was never properly made.

| Consequence | Federal Standard | Practical Impact in Florida |
|---|---|---|
| Civil penalty per violation (HUD) | Up to $19,507 | Applies per transaction, per party |
| EPA penalty (per day) | Up to $46,517 | Rare in residential sales but possible |
| Private lawsuit damages | Treble (3x actual) | Buyer can sue agent and seller jointly |
| Contract voidability | Buyer may rescind | Deal dies; agent loses commission |
| Criminal penalty | Up to $10,000 + imprisonment | Reserved for knowing/willful violations |

Florida's Real Estate Commission (FREC) can also treat a lead paint disclosure failure as a violation of Section 475.25(1)(b), Florida Statutes — fraud, misrepresentation, or failure to disclose a material fact. That opens the door to license discipline independent of federal enforcement. ## Common Mistakes Florida Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosures

After reviewing thousands of transaction files, certain errors recur with alarming frequency. Here are the five most common.

**First, agents forget to include the EPA pamphlet.** The disclosure form alone is not sufficient. The seller (through the listing agent) must provide the buyer with the current version of "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." A signed form without pamphlet delivery is incomplete compliance. Many agents assume the buyer can find it online — that does not satisfy the requirement.

Second, the listing agent fails to sign the disclosure form. The regulation at 40 CFR 745.113(b)(4) explicitly requires the agent's signature as certification that the agent informed the seller of obligations and ensured compliance. A form with only seller and buyer signatures leaves the agent exposed.

Third, agents treat post-1977 properties carelessly. The cutoff is January 1, 1978. A home with a building permit issued in late 1977 but completed in 1978 still requires disclosure if any portion of construction or the permit predates the cutoff. When the build date is ambiguous, disclose — it costs nothing and eliminates risk.

Fourth, agents confuse the lead paint disclosure with the general seller's disclosure. Florida's standard [seller's property disclosure](/blog/pennsylvania-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) concept does not substitute for the specific federally mandated lead paint form. These are separate obligations with separate form requirements.

Fifth, agents fail to retain the signed disclosure for three years. Under 24 CFR 35.92, the seller and agent must keep the signed acknowledgment for not less than three years from the completion date of the transaction. If your file management system purges records sooner, you are out of compliance.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Florida Statute 475.25(1)(u) for failure to maintain proper oversight of their agents' transactions. A systematic lead paint disclosure failure across multiple agents in a brokerage is not just a federal enforcement risk — it is a license-threatening pattern.

**Brokers should implement a quarterly audit of all pre-1978 transaction files to verify three elements: signed disclosure form (all parties including agent), documented pamphlet delivery, and documented 10-day inspection opportunity or waiver.** These three checkpoints catch the vast majority of compliance failures before they escalate.

| Audit Checkpoint | What to Verify | Red Flag if Missing |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure form completeness | All signatures present, all boxes checked | Unsigned agent line, blank disclosure section |
| EPA pamphlet delivery | Buyer acknowledgment of receipt | No pamphlet reference or acknowledgment |
| 10-day inspection opportunity | Written record of period offered or waived | Contract inspection period under 10 days with no separate lead paint waiver |
| Records retention | File retained minimum 3 years | File archived or destroyed before 3-year mark |
| Property age verification | Tax records confirming pre-1978 build | No documentation of build date in file |

Brokers who rely solely on their agents' self-reporting are asking for trouble. Consider assigning a transaction coordinator or compliance officer to flag every listing where the property appraiser's records show a pre-1978 effective year built. This single automation eliminates the most common point of failure: the agent who simply forgets.

## Florida-Specific Considerations: Climate, Housing Stock, and Risk

Florida's housing stock presents a somewhat unusual profile for lead paint compliance. Unlike northeastern states with dense concentrations of pre-1920 housing, Florida's residential building boom occurred primarily from the 1950s onward. Still, major metros like Miami, Tampa, Jacksonville, and St. Petersburg contain significant pockets of pre-1978 homes — particularly in historic districts, midcentury neighborhoods, and older condominium complexes.

The subtropical climate accelerates paint deterioration. Humidity, salt air, and intense UV exposure cause exterior paint to fail faster than in temperate climates, increasing the likelihood that lead-based paint — if present — is in a deteriorated condition. This makes the hazard disclosure more than a paperwork exercise; it has genuine health implications, particularly for properties with young children in residence.

Agents working in areas like Coconut Grove, Hyde Park, Riverside-Avondale, or Old Northeast should treat lead paint disclosure as a reflex for nearly every listing. The property appraiser's effective year built is your first data point, but it is not always accurate for renovated or expanded homes. When in doubt, disclose.
