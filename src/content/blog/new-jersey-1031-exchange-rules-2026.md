## Why New Jersey Treats 1031 Exchanges Differently Than the IRS

Real estate agents across the state routinely assume that a properly structured 1031 exchange automatically eliminates all tax obligations at closing. That assumption is wrong in New Jersey, and understanding the **New Jersey 1031 exchange state tax implications 2026** landscape is non-negotiable if you want to avoid derailing a deal. While IRC Section 1031 defers federal capital gains tax, New Jersey's Gross Income Tax (GIT) Act imposes its own withholding rules that apply regardless of federal deferral status.

New Jersey is one of a handful of states that does not automatically conform to the federal 1031 deferral. Under N.J.S.A. 54A:8-8.1, the state requires an estimated income tax payment at closing for nonresident sellers—and the twist is that even resident sellers doing exchanges must address withholding through specific exemption filings. The New Jersey Division of Taxation administers this process, and agents who ignore it risk liability exposure for their clients and themselves.

The practical effect is straightforward: your seller cannot simply hand over exchange documents and expect closing to proceed without state-level paperwork. A qualified intermediary handles the federal mechanics, but New Jersey's forms sit on your side of the table.

## The GIT/REP Form System: What Agents Must Know

**New Jersey's withholding system for real property transfers revolves around the GIT/REP series of forms, and the specific form used depends on the seller's residency status and claimed exemption.** The primary forms agents encounter in 1031 exchange transactions are GIT/REP-1, GIT/REP-2, and GIT/REP-3.

GIT/REP-1 is the Seller's Residency Certification, filed when a seller claims New Jersey residency and therefore exemption from nonresident withholding. GIT/REP-2 is the form for nonresident sellers who do not qualify for any exemption—it calculates the estimated tax owed. GIT/REP-3 is the critical form for 1031 exchanges: it is the Seller's Exemption from GIT Withholding based on a like-kind exchange under IRC Section 1031.

| Form | Purpose | Who Files | When to Use in 1031 Context |
|------|---------|-----------|----------------------------|
| GIT/REP-1 | Residency certification/exemption | Resident seller | Resident seller doing exchange still files this |
| GIT/REP-2 | Nonresident withholding calculation | Nonresident seller | Only if no exemption applies |
| GIT/REP-3 | Like-kind exchange exemption | Seller (any residency) | Primary 1031 exemption form |
| GIT/REP-4 | Buyer's withholding remittance | Buyer or buyer's attorney | Filed when withholding is due |

The GIT/REP-3 must be completed and submitted to the Division of Taxation, and it requires the seller to certify under penalty of law that the transaction qualifies as a like-kind exchange. This form does not automatically get approved—agents need to understand that filing it is a declaration, not a guarantee.

## New Jersey 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: The Withholding Mechanics

The default withholding amount under N.J.S.A. 54A:8-8.1 is the greater of 2% of the total consideration paid or the estimated tax due on the gain. For a $1.2 million commercial property sale with $400,000 in gain, that means a minimum of $24,000 withheld at closing unless a valid exemption is in place. New Jersey's top GIT rate on capital gains for 2026 remains 10.75% for income over $1 million, which could push the estimated tax well above the 2% floor.

When a seller files GIT/REP-3 claiming a 1031 exchange exemption, the withholding obligation can be reduced to zero—but only if the form is properly executed and the exchange actually closes. If the exchange subsequently fails (the seller misses the 45-day identification window or the 180-day acquisition deadline), the exemption is retroactively invalid. At that point, the seller owes the full tax plus interest, and the Division of Taxation may assess penalties.

**The buyer's side bears statutory responsibility for remitting the withholding.** Under the statute, if the buyer fails to withhold when required, the buyer becomes personally liable for the tax. This means your buyer clients have skin in this game too, even when they are not the ones doing the exchange.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in New Jersey 1031 Exchange Transactions

The first and most frequent error is assuming that a qualified intermediary's involvement means the state tax issue is handled. QIs manage the exchange structure and hold funds for federal deferral purposes, but they do not file GIT/REP forms and they do not interface with the New Jersey Division of Taxation on the agent's or seller's behalf. Agents who conflate these two systems create gaps that surface at the closing table.

The second mistake is failing to flag the 1031 exchange early enough in the transaction timeline for the seller's attorney to prepare and submit GIT/REP-3. This form should be addressed during the contract phase, not three days before closing. When it arrives late, title companies and closing attorneys will delay settlement rather than proceed without proper documentation—and [that delay costs everyone money](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Third, agents frequently misadvise clients that New Jersey "follows" federal tax treatment on exchanges. It does not fully conform. New Jersey allows deferral on like-kind exchanges, but only when the replacement property is also located in New Jersey or when the seller properly reports out-of-state replacement property on their NJ-1040 return. A seller exchanging a Newark warehouse for a Phoenix industrial building may still owe New Jersey GIT on the deferred gain when the replacement property is eventually sold.

Fourth, agents neglect to confirm whether the seller is classified as a resident or nonresident for GIT purposes. A seller who relocated to Florida last year but still owns New Jersey investment property is a nonresident seller—triggering the stricter withholding requirements under GIT/REP-2 unless GIT/REP-3 applies.

Fifth, listing agents sometimes fail to communicate the 1031 exchange to the buyer's side early enough, leaving the buyer's attorney unable to confirm whether they need to withhold. This breakdown in [transaction transparency](/blog/transaction-transparency-for-clients) creates last-minute chaos that jeopardizes closings.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

**Brokers have a supervisory obligation under N.J.A.C. 11:5-1 (the New Jersey Real Estate Commission's administrative code) to ensure their agents operate within the bounds of competence.** While brokers are not tax advisors, they must verify that agents in their office are not providing tax advice beyond their license, and that proper referrals to attorneys and CPAs are being made on every 1031 exchange transaction.

A broker's compliance checklist for 1031 exchange deals should verify three things at minimum: that the listing file contains evidence the seller was advised to consult a tax professional, that the applicable GIT/REP form number is noted in the transaction file, and that the agent did not draft or complete tax forms on the client's behalf. Agents are not licensed to prepare tax documents, and doing so exposes the brokerage to liability under both the New Jersey Real Estate License Act (N.J.S.A. 45:15-1 et seq.) and potential unauthorized practice of law claims.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Look For | Risk If Missing |
|-------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Tax advisor referral documentation | Written recommendation in file | Agent liability for tax advice |
| GIT/REP form identification | Correct form number noted | Withholding errors at closing |
| QI engagement confirmation | Letter or contract from intermediary | Exchange structure failure |
| Timeline tracking for 45/180-day deadlines | Calendar entries or system alerts | Missed deadlines, failed exchange |
| Communication to buyer's side | Email or letter confirming exchange | Buyer's withholding liability |

Brokers should also audit whether agents are [tracking these deadlines across multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals). A single missed 45-day identification deadline does not just cost the seller their tax deferral—it creates a cascade of withholding obligations that the buyer's side may not have budgeted for at closing.

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When Agents Get This Wrong

The consequences of noncompliance split into three categories: client financial harm, regulatory discipline, and deal failure. On the financial side, a seller who loses their 1031 deferral because an agent failed to coordinate GIT/REP-3 filing faces an immediate and unexpected tax bill that can reach six figures on high-value commercial properties. That seller's attorney will look for someone to blame.

The [New Jersey Real Estate Commission](https://www.nj.gov/dobi/division_rec/) can discipline agents under N.J.A.C. 11:5-6.1 for demonstrating incompetence or negligence in a real estate transaction. While the Commission does not typically adjudicate tax disputes directly, an agent who repeatedly mishandles exchange-related paperwork—or worse, provides incorrect tax guidance—faces potential license suspension or mandatory continuing education requirements.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk. Title companies in New Jersey will not issue clear-to-close without confirming that GIT withholding requirements are satisfied. If the seller's GIT/REP-3 is incomplete, unsigned, or missing, the title company halts the transaction. For agents working on commission, a delayed or cancelled closing is direct income loss, and understanding [why deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) at this stage is essential to preventing it.

Third, communicate the exchange to the buyer's side within the first week of contract.
