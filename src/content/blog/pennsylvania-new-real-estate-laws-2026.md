## Pennsylvania New Real Estate Laws 2026: The Changes That Hit Hardest

The phrase **Pennsylvania new real estate laws 2026** is already generating anxiety in brokerages from Pittsburgh to the Poconos, and for good reason. Between revised buyer representation requirements under 68 Pa.C.S. Chapter 56, overhauled PAR standard forms, and tighter enforcement mechanisms from the Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission (PREC), agents who coast on old habits will find themselves exposed. This is not a minor administrative update—it is a structural shift in how licensees document relationships, disclose material facts, and protect themselves from liability.

The regulatory overhaul traces back to both the NAR settlement fallout and Pennsylvania-specific legislative sessions in late 2025. Governor Shapiro signed amendments to the Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA) that took effect on January 1, 2026, while the Pennsylvania Association of Realtors released updated standard forms in March 2026. Agents now operate under a dual compliance framework: state statute and association-mandated documentation.

## Buyer Agency Agreements: No More Verbal Understandings

**The single most consequential change for buyer's agents is the mandatory written buyer agency agreement before any property showing.** This requirement, codified in the 2026 amendments to 68 Pa.C.S. Section 5608, eliminates the gray area that previously allowed agents to show properties under informal or implied agency. The agreement must specify compensation terms, duration, geographic scope, and the buyer's right to terminate.

PAR Form BAA-2026 (Buyer Agency Agreement) replaces the older Form ABR. The new form includes a mandatory addendum section where agents must itemize any compensation they expect to receive from listing brokers versus what the buyer agrees to pay directly. Leaving this section blank is not an option—doing so renders the form incomplete under PREC guidelines.

Agents who show property without an executed BAA-2026 face disciplinary action under Section 604 of RELRA, which authorizes fines up to $5,000 per violation. Repeat offenses can trigger license suspension proceedings. Beyond regulatory risk, any commission claim arising from an unsigned buyer agreement will be virtually unenforceable in Pennsylvania civil court.

## Updated Seller Disclosure: Form SPD Gets a Major Revision

Pennsylvania's Seller's Property Disclosure Statement (Form SPD) has been revised to include three new disclosure categories effective March 2026. Sellers must now affirmatively disclose: prior insurance claims related to water intrusion or mold within the past ten years, any known PFAS contamination in private water sources, and the presence of knob-and-tube wiring that remains energized.

The expanded SPD form went from 12 pages to 16 pages. Every "unknown" response now requires the seller to initial a separate acknowledgment confirming they made a reasonable effort to investigate before selecting that answer. This change alone will slow down listing appointments and require agents to coach sellers more carefully during the disclosure process.

**Listing agents who allow sellers to rush through the SPD with blanket "unknown" answers are exposing both parties to post-closing litigation.** Under the 2026 amendments to 68 Pa.C.S. Section 7304, a pattern of "unknown" responses—particularly on items the seller demonstrably lived with for years—can be treated as constructive fraud if a court determines the seller deliberately avoided inquiry.

## Compensation Transparency: What the New Forms Require

The Pennsylvania Association of Realtors rolled out a suite of compensation-related forms in March 2026 that directly respond to the NAR settlement's cooperative compensation rules. The most significant additions are Form CDD (Compensation Disclosure to Buyer) and Form COS (Confirmation of Showing Agreement).

| Form Number | Purpose | When Required | Who Signs |
|---|---|---|---|
| BAA-2026 | Buyer Agency Agreement | Before any showing | Buyer + Agent |
| CDD-2026 | Compensation Disclosure to Buyer | Before offer submission | Buyer + Agent |
| COS-2026 | Confirmation of Showing Agreement | Before property access | Buyer + Agent |
| SPD-2026 | Seller Property Disclosure (revised) | Before agreement of sale execution | Seller |
| ADS-2026 | Agency Disclosure Statement (revised) | At first substantive contact | All parties |

Form CDD-2026 requires the buyer's agent to disclose, in writing, the exact dollar amount or percentage of compensation they will receive from any source—including the listing broker, the buyer directly, or a combination. This form must be signed before the buyer's agent submits an offer on the buyer's behalf. Submitting an offer without an executed CDD is grounds for PREC complaint.

Form ADS-2026 (Agency Disclosure Statement) replaces the prior version and now includes a mandatory section explaining that listing broker compensation offers are negotiable and not set by any trade association, MLS, or government entity. The language is prescribed by PREC and cannot be modified by brokerages.

## Common Mistakes Agents Are Already Making

Even though these rules have been in effect for months, PREC complaint data from Q1 2026 shows patterns of non-compliance that repeat across brokerages statewide. Here are the five most frequent errors.

**First, agents are using outdated form versions.** Some agents still have the pre-2026 ABR form loaded in their transaction management software. Title companies have started rejecting files that contain superseded forms, causing closings to stall while replacements are executed. Every form has a revision date in the lower-left footer—check it before every transaction.

Second, agents are obtaining buyer agency agreements with open-ended durations. The 2026 amendments require a specific termination date on Form BAA-2026. PREC has stated that agreements exceeding 90 days without a written extension clause will be scrutinized during audits. An indefinite agreement is treated as non-compliant.

Third, listing agents are failing to deliver the updated SPD within the statutory timeframe. Under Section 7305, the seller disclosure must be provided to the buyer or buyer's agent before execution of the agreement of sale—not at the same time, and not after. Delivery means actual receipt, not merely sending.

Fourth, agents are combining the CDD and BAA into a single signing event and backdating the CDD to match. These forms serve different purposes at different transaction stages. PREC investigators have already flagged backdated CDD forms as potential violations during routine audits.

Fifth, dual agents are not properly documenting informed consent under the revised dual agency provisions of Section 5606. The 2026 revision requires a standalone consent form—PAR Form DAC-2026—separate from the ADS. Embedding dual agency consent within the agreement of sale is no longer sufficient.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under RELRA Section 603(a), which makes the broker of record personally responsible for ensuring affiliated licensees comply with all statutory requirements. The 2026 changes create specific audit obligations that brokers cannot delegate to office managers without maintaining oversight.

| Audit Area | Frequency | Documentation Required | Risk if Missed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Buyer agency agreements | Every transaction | Executed BAA-2026 in file before first showing date | Fine up to $5,000; license suspension |
| Form version control | Quarterly | Confirmation that all templates are current PAR versions | Transaction rejection by title; PREC citation |
| SPD delivery timing | Every listing transaction | Timestamped proof of delivery before agreement execution | Rescission right extended; potential fraud claim |
| Compensation disclosure | Every buyer transaction | Executed CDD-2026 before offer date | Commission dispute; PREC complaint |
| Dual agency consent | Every dual agency transaction | Standalone DAC-2026 signed by all parties | Voidable agency; disciplinary action |

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-audit are gambling with their own license.** The PREC has increased random audit frequency by 40 percent in 2026, focusing specifically on buyer representation files and compensation documentation. A single missing BAA-2026 in an audited file can trigger a full brokerage review.

Brokerages should implement a mandatory file review at three checkpoints: after buyer agency execution, before offer submission, and before closing. ## Liability Exposure: What Happens When You Get It Wrong

The consequences of non-compliance range from inconvenient to career-ending. At the lowest level, title companies and lenders will reject files with missing or outdated forms, causing closing delays. Title insurance underwriters in Pennsylvania have issued internal bulletins requiring 2026-version forms as a condition of issuing policies.

At the mid-level, buyers who never received a properly executed CDD-2026 can dispute commission payments post-closing. Without the form, the buyer's agent has no enforceable documentation of the agreed compensation structure. Arbitration panels at local Realtor associations have already sided with buyers in early 2026 disputes where the CDD was absent.

At the highest level, PREC can impose fines, require remedial education, suspend licenses for up to three years, or revoke licenses permanently under RELRA Section 604. The Commission's [enforcement actions page](https://www.dos.pa.gov/ProfessionalLicensing/BoardsCommissions/RealEstateCommission/Pages/Enforcement.aspx) publishes all disciplinary orders—your name will appear there publicly.
