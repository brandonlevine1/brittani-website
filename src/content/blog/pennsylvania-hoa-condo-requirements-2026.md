## Why Pennsylvania HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

**If you represent a buyer or seller in any association-governed community in Pennsylvania, two statutes control your disclosure obligations—and the penalties for ignoring them are deal-killing.** The Pennsylvania hoa & condo disclosure rules 2026 framework rests on the Uniform Condominium Act (68 Pa.C.S. Chapter 34) and the Uniform Planned Community Act (68 Pa.C.S. Chapter 54). Both were originally enacted in the 1980s but remain the active statutory authority governing resale disclosure packets in 2026.

The rules affect roughly 2.5 million Pennsylvania homeowners who live in condominiums, planned communities, or cooperatives with mandatory association membership. For agents, this means almost every suburban and many urban transactions will trigger disclosure requirements that carry statutory cancellation rights for buyers. Understanding the mechanics—who orders what, when it must arrive, and what happens when it doesn't—separates a smooth closing from a blown deal.

## The Two Statutes That Control Everything

Pennsylvania splits its HOA and condo disclosure obligations across two parallel but distinct statutes. Agents who conflate them risk applying the wrong deadline or form to the wrong property type.

The Uniform Condominium Act, codified at [68 Pa.C.S. § 3407](https://www.legis.state.pa.us/cfdocs/legis/LI/consCheck.cfm?txtType=HTM&ttl=68&div=0&chpt=34&sctn=7&subsctn=0), governs condominium resales. The Uniform Planned Community Act, at 68 Pa.C.S. § 5407, governs planned communities with mandatory membership associations—think townhome developments, single-family HOA neighborhoods, and mixed-use planned communities. Both require the seller to furnish a resale certificate to the buyer, and both grant the buyer a five-day right of cancellation after receiving it.

| Requirement | Condominiums (§ 3407) | Planned Communities (§ 5407) |
|---|---|---|
| Document required | Resale certificate | Resale certificate |
| Who prepares it | Condo association | Community association |
| Buyer cancellation period | 5 days after receipt | 5 days after receipt |
| Delivery deadline | Before or at agreement signing | Before or at agreement signing |
| Statutory fee cap | None specified | None specified |
| Right waivable? | No | No |

The five-day cancellation right is absolute. It cannot be shortened by contract, waived in an addendum, or bargained away. If your buyer receives the resale certificate on day three after signing, their cancellation window runs through day eight.

## What the Resale Certificate Must Contain

The resale certificate is not a casual summary. Both statutes enumerate mandatory contents, and an incomplete certificate does not start the buyer's five-day clock.

**For condominiums under § 3407, the certificate must include:** the declaration, bylaws, rules, current operating budget, reserve fund balance, any pending capital expenditures, existing or anticipated special assessments, insurance coverage summaries, and any pending lawsuits involving the association. It must also state whether the unit owner is current on assessments and identify any right of first refusal or transfer fees.

For planned communities under § 5407, the requirements mirror the condo statute almost identically. The certificate must disclose the same budgetary, legal, and governance information. The association must deliver the certificate within ten days of the request, though many Pennsylvania associations now use third-party vendors like CondoCerts or HOA Management that promise faster turnaround for premium fees.

Agents should confirm that every required element is present. A certificate missing the reserve study disclosure or pending litigation notice is legally deficient, meaning the buyer's cancellation window arguably never starts. This has been litigated in Pennsylvania courts, and buyers have successfully unwound transactions weeks after closing based on incomplete certificates.

## Pennsylvania Association of Realtors Forms You Must Know

The Pennsylvania Association of Realtors (PAR) produces standardized forms that most brokerages adopt. Two forms directly address HOA and condo disclosures in the transaction workflow.

**PAR Form ASR (Agreement of Sale—Residential)** contains a specific section addressing condominium and planned community resale certificates. Paragraphs within the agreement reference the buyer's right to receive the certificate and the five-day cancellation provision. Agents filling out this form must correctly identify whether the property is a condominium, planned community, or cooperative—each triggers different statutory sections.

PAR Form COND (Condominium Resale Certificate Addendum) provides additional terms when the certificate has not yet been received at the time of signing. This form sets out the process for delivery, the consequences of non-delivery, and how the cancellation period interacts with other agreement timelines like the inspection contingency. If your brokerage uses PAR forms and you skip the COND addendum on a condo sale where the certificate is pending, you have created an ambiguity that favors the buyer in any dispute.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of disclosure failures in Pennsylvania's HOA and condo context are not theoretical. They fall into three categories: statutory cancellation, civil liability, and licensing discipline.

Statutory cancellation is the most immediate risk. If a buyer never receives the resale certificate, they can cancel the agreement of sale at any time before closing and recover their deposit in full. This is not a breach—it is an exercise of a statutory right. The seller has no claim for damages, and the deal simply dies.

Civil liability arises when an agent's negligence causes a party financial harm. If a listing agent tells a buyer's agent that "there's no HOA" when one exists, and the buyer closes without receiving the certificate, the buyer may have a claim against the listing agent and their brokerage for damages including undisclosed assessments or transfer fees. Pennsylvania courts have held that real estate licensees owe a duty of honest dealing to all parties, not just their client.

The State Real Estate Commission, housed within the Bureau of Professional and Occupational Affairs, can impose disciplinary action under 63 P.S. § 455.604 for conduct demonstrating incompetency or untrustworthiness. Repeated failures to disclose association status or deliver required documents could trigger a commission investigation, especially if a consumer files a formal complaint. Sanctions range from fines to license suspension.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With HOA Disclosures

Five errors recur with frustrating regularity in Pennsylvania HOA and condo transactions. Each one is preventable.

**First, agents misidentify the property type.** A townhome in a planned community is governed by § 5407, not § 3407. A converted row home registered as a condominium falls under the condo statute even if it looks nothing like a high-rise. The declaration recorded against the property—not the building's appearance—determines which statute applies. Pulling the declaration from the county recorder's office before listing eliminates this confusion.

Second, agents assume the seller's verbal representation about assessments substitutes for the official resale certificate. It does not. The statute requires the association to prepare and deliver the certificate. A seller saying "dues are $200 a month and there are no special assessments" carries zero statutory weight and does not start any cancellation clock.

Third, agents fail to calendar the five-day cancellation window accurately. The five days begin the day after the buyer receives the certificate—not the day it was mailed, not the day it was emailed to the buyer's agent. Delivery to the buyer's agent counts as delivery to the buyer under most PAR form language, so agents must document the exact date they forwarded the certificate to their client. Missing this detail creates [the kind of deadline failure](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) that unravels closings.

Fourth, agents neglect to review the certificate for completeness before passing it to the buyer. An incomplete certificate does not trigger the cancellation window, which means your closing timeline is built on sand. Agents should compare the certificate's contents against the statutory checklist before marking it "received."

Fifth, listing agents forget to order the certificate early enough. Many associations take the full ten statutory days to respond, and third-party certificate vendors can take longer during peak spring markets. Ordering the certificate at listing—not after an accepted offer—prevents timeline compression that threatens closing dates.

## The Interplay With Other Pennsylvania Disclosures

HOA and condo disclosures do not exist in isolation. They layer on top of the Seller's Property Disclosure Statement (PAR Form SPD), lead paint disclosures for pre-1978 properties, and any local municipal point-of-sale requirements. Agents must track all of these concurrently without letting one overshadow the others.

The resale certificate often contains information that contradicts or supplements the seller's property disclosure. For example, the seller may disclose no knowledge of water intrusion, but the association's certificate reveals pending litigation over building envelope failures. These contradictions create legal exposure for agents who fail to flag them.

HOA disclosure gaps rank among the top reasons Pennsylvania transactions fall apart between ratification and closing, particularly in condominium conversions and newer planned communities where associations are still establishing governance procedures.
