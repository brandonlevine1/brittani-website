## Tennessee Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026: The Ground Rules Have Shifted

The Tennessee commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 represent the most significant operational change Tennessee agents have faced since the state adopted mandatory agency disclosure in 1995. If you hold a license governed by the Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) and you have not restructured your compensation practices, you are already exposed to regulatory risk. This is not a theoretical warning—TREC began issuing citations in Q1 2026 for non-compliant compensation arrangements tied to MLS-originating transactions.

The settlement, finalized nationally in late 2024, eliminated the long-standing MLS cooperative compensation model. Tennessee's implementation layer sits on top of those federal terms, filtered through Tennessee Code Annotated Title 62, Chapter 13 and the rules promulgated by TREC under Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. 1260-02. Agents who treat this as a minor paperwork update are making a career-threatening mistake.

## How Tennessee Specifically Implements the Settlement Terms

TREC released Formal Opinion 2025-03 in March 2025 clarifying how the settlement interacts with existing Tennessee licensure law. The opinion confirmed that Tennessee Code Annotated Section 62-13-312, which governs licensee conduct and grounds for discipline, now encompasses failure to obtain written compensation agreements prior to performing brokerage services for a buyer.

**Tennessee REALTORS (the state association) updated its standard forms in August 2025 to reflect the new requirements.** The two primary forms agents must use are Form RF401 (Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement) and Form RF402 (Non-Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement). Both require a specific dollar amount or percentage for compensation—not a vague "to be determined" placeholder—and must be executed before touring any property.

The [Tennessee Real Estate Commission's official rulemaking page](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/trec.html) provides the most current guidance. Agents should check quarterly for rule amendments, as TREC has signaled additional rulemaking through 2026.

## What Changed Versus What Stayed the Same

Not everything is different. Tennessee already required written agency disclosure under T.C.A. 62-13-403, and listing agreements have always specified seller-side compensation. The fundamental shift is on the buyer side and on MLS display rules.

| Element | Pre-Settlement (Before Aug 2024) | Post-Settlement (2025-2026) |
|---------|----------------------------------|----------------------------|
| Buyer agent compensation on MLS | Displayed as cooperative offer | Prohibited from MLS display |
| Buyer representation agreement | Recommended but not mandatory before showings | Mandatory before any touring |
| Compensation specificity | Could reference "customary" or "MLS offer" | Must state exact amount or percentage |
| Seller concessions for buyer agent pay | Negotiated at contract | Still permitted, but off-MLS only |
| Dual agency compensation | Governed by T.C.A. 62-13-404 | Unchanged, but disclosure form RF301 updated |

This table simplifies the comparison, but the nuance matters. A seller in Franklin can still agree to contribute toward the buyer's agent fee—the mechanism just changed from a pre-listed MLS field to a negotiated term within the purchase agreement or a separate concession addendum (Tennessee REALTORS Form RF141).

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Agents Are Making Right Now

After reviewing TREC complaint filings from the first half of 2026 and speaking with managing brokers across the state, five errors appear repeatedly.

**First, agents are using outdated RF401 forms from before the August 2025 revision.** The pre-revision version contained a clause referencing MLS cooperative compensation as a potential payment source, which is no longer compliant. If your form still references "compensation offered through the MLS," you are using the wrong version.

Second, some buyer agents are signing representation agreements with compensation listed as "$0" or "seller-paid" without specifying the actual amount the buyer would owe if no seller concession materializes. TREC's Formal Opinion 2025-03 explicitly states that compensation must be a "definite and ascertainable" figure. A contingent-only reference to seller payment does not satisfy this standard.

Third, agents touring properties at open houses without a signed agreement are technically in violation once they begin providing brokerage services beyond casual conversation. The line between "attending an open house" and "receiving brokerage services" is thinner than most agents assume—pointing out features, discussing price, or advising on offer strategy crosses it.

Fourth, listing agents are verbally communicating "we'll pay 2.5% to your buyer's agent" in ways that amount to off-MLS advertising of cooperative compensation without proper documentation. If you make that representation, it must appear in writing—either in a seller concession addendum or a separate agreement—before contract execution.

Fifth, teams are having unlicensed assistants present buyer representation agreements. Under T.C.A. 62-13-104, only a licensed affiliate broker or broker may discuss compensation terms with a client. An unlicensed team member handing someone a form and saying "sign here" creates a licensing violation independent of the commission structure rules.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in Tennessee carry personal liability for supervisory failures under T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(14), which allows TREC to discipline a broker for "failing to exercise adequate supervision over the activities of licensed affiliate brokers." The settlement did not change this statute, but it dramatically expanded what "adequate supervision" means in practice.

**Every Tennessee brokerage should audit three elements monthly.** First, confirm that every active buyer file contains a signed RF401 or RF402 dated before the first showing occurred. Second, verify that the compensation figure in the buyer agreement matches or is reconciled with what the buyer actually paid at closing. Third, check that no agent in the office is advertising cooperative compensation through social media, email blasts, or verbal promises at listing presentations without corresponding written documentation.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Risk if Missed |
|------------|-----------|----------------|
| Buyer agreement execution date vs. first showing date | Every file at contract | TREC complaint, deal rescission |
| Compensation specificity in RF401/RF402 | Weekly file review | Civil liability, TREC fine up to $1,000 per violation |
| MLS listing compliance (no coop comp field) | At listing entry | MLS fine, potential suspension |
| Seller concession documentation (RF141) | At contract review | Closing delay, lender rejection |
| Team member licensing verification | Quarterly | Unauthorized practice, broker license risk |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-police these requirements are gambling with their own license. The TREC disciplinary process under Tenn. Comp. R. & Regs. 1260-02-.09 provides for fines up to $1,000 per violation, license suspension, and in egregious cases, revocation. A pattern of supervisory neglect across multiple transactions amplifies the penalty.

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When You Get It Wrong

The consequences of non-compliance fall into three categories: regulatory, contractual, and civil.

On the regulatory side, TREC can initiate a complaint investigation based on a consumer grievance, another licensee's report, or its own audit. If TREC finds a violation of T.C.A. 62-13-312, the disciplinary options range from a formal reprimand to license revocation. The commission has historically favored consent orders with monetary penalties for first-time paperwork violations, but the settlement-related infractions are drawing stiffer responses because of the national scrutiny.

Contractually, a buyer who signed a representation agreement that fails to meet the "definite and ascertainable" compensation standard may have grounds to void the agreement entirely. Tennessee contract law requires mutual assent to material terms—if the compensation term is ambiguous, there may be no enforceable agreement at all. That means you performed work without a valid contract and have no legal basis to collect a fee.

**Civil liability enters when a buyer claims they were never informed they could negotiate compensation or that alternatives existed.** The Tennessee Consumer Protection Act (T.C.A. 47-18-104) prohibits unfair or deceptive acts in trade. An agent who obscures, minimizes, or fails to disclose the buyer's right to negotiate agent compensation could face a private cause of action with treble damages under that statute. This is the scenario plaintiff's attorneys are actively building cases around in 2026.

## How the Compensation Conversation Should Actually Work

The mechanical process looks different depending on whether you represent buyers or sellers—or both in a designated agency arrangement under T.C.A. 62-13-404.

For buyer agents, the workflow is: initial consultation, presentation of RF401 or RF402, discussion of your fee (flat fee, percentage, hourly—whatever your model), signature, and then touring begins. If a buyer wants to attend an open house before signing, they can do so unrepresented. The moment they ask you to accompany them or provide analysis, the agreement must exist.

For listing agents, the workflow includes a conversation with sellers about whether they want to offer a buyer agent concession. If yes, that offer gets documented in the listing agreement and communicated off-MLS—through agent-to-agent outreach, the listing agent's website, or during showing coordination. The MLS cooperative compensation field no longer exists in Tennessee's three major MLS systems (RealTracs, East Tennessee REALTORS MLS, and Chattanooga Association of REALTORS MLS).

If you handle transactions in both [buyer agency](/blog/why-agents-hate-paperwork) and listing capacities, maintaining clean file separation becomes even more critical. Your brokerage's transaction management system—or your [transaction coordinator](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator)—must flag any file where the buyer agreement compensation and the closing disbursement do not match.
