## The State of Washington Teams & Supervision Rules 2026

Washington's real estate team structures have exploded in popularity over the past five years, but the regulatory framework governing them has tightened in parallel. Understanding Washington teams & supervision rules 2026 is no longer optional knowledge—it is a compliance baseline that every licensee operating within a team must meet or face real consequences. The Department of Licensing (DOL) has made it clear through enforcement actions that team-related violations rank among the most common triggers for disciplinary proceedings.

The rules governing teams in Washington sit primarily within RCW 18.85 (the Real Estate Brokerage Relationships Act) and the Washington Administrative Code sections WAC 308-124C-125 through WAC 308-124C-140. These are not suggestions. They carry the force of law, and the DOL actively audits brokerages for compliance.

## How Washington Defines a "Team" Under Current Law

**Washington does not have a single statutory definition of "team" that mirrors what agents casually use in marketing.** Instead, the DOL treats any group of licensees who market collectively under a shared name—other than the brokerage name itself—as operating under a "team name" that triggers specific registration and advertising requirements under WAC 308-124C-125.

A team in Washington can consist of brokers, managing brokers, and unlicensed assistants. Every licensed member must hold an active license under the same designated broker. You cannot form a cross-brokerage team in Washington—period. If two agents at different firms want to co-market, they must either transfer to the same brokerage or operate as separate entities with proper referral agreements.

The critical distinction that trips up many agents: a team is not its own legal entity for licensing purposes. The designated broker's firm license covers the team, and all team activities fall under that firm's supervision umbrella. This means the [agency disclosure requirements](/blog/washington-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) that apply to individual agents apply identically to team members, with no special carve-outs.

## Registration, Advertising, and Name Compliance

Every team name must be registered with the Washington DOL before it appears in any advertising, signage, or marketing material. The registration process requires submission through the firm's designated broker, who certifies that the team name does not mislead the public into believing the team is a standalone brokerage.

Here is where the advertising rules get specific. WAC 308-124C-125 mandates that all team advertising include the licensed firm name in a manner that is "conspicuous and reasonably prominent." The firm name cannot be buried in fine print while the team name dominates.

| Requirement | Team Name | Firm Name |
|---|---|---|
| Placement on signs | Permitted prominently | Must appear conspicuously |
| Font size relative minimum | No restriction | Cannot be less than half the size of team name |
| Social media profiles | Permitted in handle | Must appear in profile or linked page |
| Email signatures | Permitted | Required in every communication |
| Business cards | Permitted | Required with equal or greater prominence |

The DOL has issued guidance clarifying that "conspicuous" means a reasonable consumer could identify the licensed firm without effort. An Instagram bio reading "The Martinez Group" without any reference to the associated brokerage violates this standard.

## Supervision Duties: What Managing Brokers Must Actually Do

**RCW 18.85.275 places direct supervisory responsibility on managing brokers, and this responsibility intensifies when teams operate under their oversight.** A managing broker cannot simply sign off on a team's existence and walk away. The statute requires active, documented supervision.

The supervisory obligations break down into several categories. Managing brokers must review transaction files for completeness and compliance, ensure proper agency disclosures are delivered and signed, verify that [earnest money](/blog/washington-earnest-money-rules-2026) is handled according to trust account rules, and confirm that all advertising meets DOL standards. For teams specifically, managing brokers bear additional responsibility for monitoring unlicensed assistant activities.

WAC 308-124C-110 requires that the designated broker maintain a "reasonable system of supervision." For teams, this has been interpreted through DOL enforcement actions to mean documented policies addressing who reviews files, how often reviews occur, and what triggers escalation. A brokerage that cannot produce written supervisory policies during an audit is presumed non-compliant.

The distinction between a designated broker and a managing broker matters here. The designated broker holds ultimate firm-level responsibility. Managing brokers handle day-to-day supervision. Within teams, the team lead—if they hold a managing broker license—may perform some supervisory functions, but only if the designated broker has formally delegated that authority in writing.

## Unlicensed Assistants: The Minefield Within Teams

Teams frequently employ unlicensed assistants for transaction coordination, marketing, and administrative support. Washington law strictly limits what unlicensed individuals can do. Under RCW 18.85.011 and DOL interpretive guidance, unlicensed assistants may not negotiate terms, advise clients on pricing strategy, show property independently, or host open houses without a licensed agent present.

| Activity | Licensed Agent Required? | Unlicensed Assistant Permitted? |
|---|---|---|
| Scheduling showings | No | Yes |
| Hosting open houses alone | Yes | No |
| Drafting contract terms | Yes | No |
| Delivering pre-filled forms | No | Yes (no explanation of terms) |
| Discussing pricing with clients | Yes | No |
| Processing paperwork post-signature | No | Yes |
| Conducting CMAs | Yes | No |

When an unlicensed assistant crosses these lines, liability falls on the supervising managing broker and the designated broker—not on the assistant. The assistant cannot be disciplined through the DOL because they hold no license. The agents and brokers who allowed the activity bear full responsibility.

## Common Mistakes That Trigger DOL Enforcement

Five specific errors appear repeatedly in DOL disciplinary actions involving teams. These are not hypothetical risks—they are patterns drawn from published enforcement orders.

**First, team leads acting as managing brokers without holding the proper license.** A broker-level licensee cannot supervise other licensees. Only managing brokers can supervise. When a team lead holds a standard broker license but reviews files, approves advertising, and oversees transactions for junior team members, they are practicing outside their license level. The DOL treats this as a supervision failure chargeable to the designated broker.

Second, failing to disclose which team member represents which party. In transactions where one team member represents the buyer and another represents the seller, each party must receive proper agency disclosure forms. Washington's Form 21 (Residential Purchase and Sale Agreement) and the mandatory Agency Disclosure (Form 25A) require clear identification of the representing licensee. Team structures do not excuse vague attributions like "The Smith Team represents both parties."

Third, commingling team funds with personal accounts. Some teams operate informal "team accounts" for marketing expenses or lead generation costs that inadvertently receive client funds. Any account that holds client money—even temporarily—must comply with WAC 308-124C-145 trust account requirements.

Fourth, advertising team production numbers without proper attribution. When a team advertises "$50M sold," the DOL requires that such claims not mislead consumers about any individual licensee's experience or production level. Post-NAR settlement, this has gained additional scrutiny as [commission structure transparency](/blog/washington-commission-structure-changes-2026) becomes a focal point.

Fifth, failing to update team rosters with the DOL when members join or leave. The firm's licensing records must accurately reflect current team composition at all times.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carrying teams within their firm should maintain an audit framework that addresses seven specific areas. Without documentation in each category, a DOL audit can result in findings against the brokerage even if no consumer harm occurred.

Start with advertising compliance. Pull every active team's social media profiles, websites, signs, and printed materials quarterly. Verify firm name placement meets WAC 308-124C-125 standards. Document your review with dates and screenshots.

**Second, verify that every team member's license status is active and properly affiliated.** The DOL's online license lookup should match your internal roster. When agents transfer in or out, the firm has five business days to update records under WAC 308-124C-135. Teams with high turnover create particular risk here.

Third, audit trust account access. No team should have independent access to client funds without designated broker oversight. Review who holds check-signing authority and whether any team-specific accounts exist outside the firm's official trust account structure.

Fourth, review agency disclosures in completed transaction files. Every file should contain a signed Form 25A identifying the specific licensee—not just the team name—who provided agency services. The DOL has assessed fines in cases where team branding obscured individual licensee accountability.

Fifth, confirm that unlicensed assistant activities are documented and bounded. Written job descriptions that specify permitted and prohibited activities, signed by the assistant, create a defensible record during audits. The [Washington DOL's licensing page](https://dol.wa.gov/professional-licenses/real-estate-professionals) provides current guidance on activity boundaries.

Sixth, maintain written supervisory policies that address team-specific scenarios. These policies should cover file review frequency, advertising approval workflows, and escalation procedures when compliance issues arise.

Seventh, document delegation of supervisory authority. If a team lead who holds a managing broker license performs supervision, the designated broker must have a written delegation agreement specifying scope and limitations.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

The consequences for violating Washington's team supervision rules range from monetary fines to license revocation. Under RCW 18.85.361, the DOL can impose fines up to $5,000 per violation. Multiple violations within a single transaction—improper advertising plus missing agency disclosure, for instance—stack.

Beyond fines, the DOL can suspend or revoke licenses. Managing brokers who fail supervision duties face personal disciplinary action separate from any firm-level consequences. In egregious cases, the DOL refers matters to the Attorney General's office for civil enforcement.

Consumer remedies add another layer of exposure. A buyer or seller who was not properly informed of agency relationships due to team confusion may have grounds to rescind a transaction or pursue damages. The team structure that was supposed to provide better service becomes the liability that unravels the deal.
