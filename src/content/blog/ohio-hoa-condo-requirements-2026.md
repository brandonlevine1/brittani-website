## Why Ohio HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Getting a deal to the finish line in a condominium or HOA community in Ohio requires more than the standard residential disclosure packet. The **Ohio hoa & condo disclosure rules 2026** impose specific statutory obligations on sellers—and by extension on the agents who guide them—that differ materially from a fee-simple transaction. Miss a deadline or omit a required document, and you hand the buyer an indefinite right to walk away.

Ohio's condominium statute, Ohio Revised Code Chapter 5311, has been in place for decades, but agents continue to trip over the same procedural wires year after year. The planned-community provisions under ORC Chapter 5312 layer on additional requirements for non-condo HOA properties. Both chapters interact with the Ohio Association of Realtors (OAR) standardized forms that most brokerages mandate, making it essential to understand where statutory law ends and contract-based obligations begin.

## The Statutory Framework: ORC 5311 and ORC 5312

**Ohio Revised Code Section 5311.26 is the backbone of condominium resale disclosure in the state.** It requires that a seller of a condo unit furnish the buyer with a "certificate of resale" before the buyer is bound by the purchase agreement. The certificate must contain specific financial and governance data about the association, including current assessments, pending special assessments, capital reserve balances, and any pending litigation involving the association.

ORC 5312, the Ohio Planned Community Act, governs subdivisions and developments with mandatory membership associations that are not structured as condominiums. While 5312 does not contain a resale-certificate mandate as explicit as 5311.26, it does require developers to furnish a public offering statement, and many purchase agreements contractually incorporate HOA disclosure packets as a condition of closing. Agents listing properties in planned communities should treat these disclosures with the same urgency as condo resale certificates.

The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, housed within the Ohio Department of Commerce, oversees agent conduct. A failure to advise clients about statutory disclosure obligations can trigger a license-law complaint under [ORC 4735.18(A)(6)](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-4735.18), which prohibits dishonest or incompetent dealing.

## What the Certificate of Resale Must Include

The certificate of resale is not a single checkbox form the agent fills out. It originates from the condominium association itself and must include, at minimum, the items prescribed by ORC 5311.26(A). The following table breaks down the required disclosures versus items that are commonly included but not statutorily mandated:

| Disclosure Item | Statutorily Required (ORC 5311.26) | Commonly Included |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly assessment amount | Yes | Yes |
| Pending special assessments | Yes | Yes |
| Capital reserve fund balance | Yes | Yes |
| Outstanding association liens on unit | Yes | Yes |
| Current operating budget | Yes | Yes |
| Pending litigation involving association | Yes | Yes |
| Rules and regulations booklet | No | Yes |
| Insurance certificate summary | No | Yes |
| Minutes from last board meeting | No | Often requested |

Agents who assume the association will automatically include everything a buyer needs are making a dangerous bet. Your job is to verify that every statutory line item appears in the returned certificate before forwarding it to the buyer's side.

## Key Forms Ohio Agents Must Know

The Ohio Association of Realtors publishes the standardized forms most agents encounter daily. For HOA and condo transactions, the relevant forms include OAR Form 1062 (Condominium Resale Certificate Request), which the listing agent or seller sends to the association to initiate the disclosure process. OAR Form 1061 (Homeowners Association/Condominium Addendum) attaches to the purchase agreement and establishes contractual timelines for delivery and buyer review.

**Form 1061 is where many agents unknowingly create liability for themselves.** The addendum specifies the number of days within which the seller must deliver the HOA or condo documents and the number of days the buyer has to review them and potentially terminate. If you leave the blanks empty or insert unrealistic timelines, you set the stage for a blown closing.

Ohio Real Title's standard closing checklists and most title company requirements also reference the OAR Residential Property Disclosure Form (Form 5302), which includes a section on homeowner association membership. This is a seller-completed form, distinct from the certificate of resale, and failing to complete the HOA-related questions on 5302 does not satisfy the ORC 5311.26 obligation—a distinction many newer agents miss. ## The Buyer's Rescission Right and Timing

Under ORC 5311.26(B), once the buyer receives the certificate of resale, a five-business-day rescission period begins. During that window, the buyer may cancel the contract for any reason—or no reason at all—without forfeiting earnest money. The rescission must be delivered in writing.

Here is the critical timing point: the five-day clock does not start until delivery is actually made. If the seller never delivers the certificate, the buyer's right to rescind remains open indefinitely, right up until closing. That means a buyer who discovers an unfavorable special assessment the morning of closing can walk, and the seller has no recourse.

| Scenario | Rescission Clock Status | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| Certificate delivered Day 3 of contract | Clock starts Day 3, expires Day 8 | Low |
| Certificate delivered Day 25 of 30-day contract | Clock starts Day 25, may extend past closing date | High |
| Certificate never delivered | Buyer can rescind anytime before transfer | Critical |
| Certificate delivered but missing required items | Arguably not compliant delivery—clock may not start | High |

Agents on both sides should calendar the delivery date and count forward. If you represent the buyer, do not let your client waive this right casually.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Ohio HOA and Condo Disclosures

**First, agents frequently confuse the seller's property disclosure form with the association's resale certificate.** Completing the HOA section on OAR Form 5302 does not discharge the seller's obligation under ORC 5311.26. These are two separate documents serving different legal purposes.

Second, listing agents often delegate the certificate request entirely to the seller without follow-up. Sellers do not understand the statutory implications of late delivery, and many associations take 10 to 14 business days to process requests. If nobody tracks the timeline, the certificate arrives after the buyer's contingency deadline—or not at all.

Third, agents fail to review the returned certificate for completeness. Associations sometimes omit pending litigation or reserve-fund data, either through negligence or intentional avoidance. Delivering an incomplete certificate to the buyer may not trigger the five-day rescission clock, leaving the deal in legal limbo.

Fourth, buyer's agents neglect to advise their clients about the rescission right in writing. When a dispute later arises, the buyer may claim they were never told they could cancel, creating an agency-liability exposure. Document your advice in an email or within your transaction management system.

Fifth, agents in planned-community transactions assume ORC 5311 applies when it does not. If the property is governed by ORC 5312 rather than 5311, the statutory resale-certificate mechanism is different, and the disclosure obligations flow primarily from the purchase agreement terms rather than from a specific code section. Misapplying the wrong statute undermines your credibility if the matter reaches mediation or arbitration. Tracking these nuances across multiple active deals is exactly the type of deadline management that separates [agents who close predictably from those who scramble](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under ORC 4735.18(A)(6) and (A)(9) for the acts of their affiliated agents. A pattern of missed HOA disclosures across a brokerage signals a systemic training failure, and the Ohio Division of Real Estate has historically treated systemic failures more harshly than isolated mistakes.

**Brokers should audit three specific checkpoints in every condo or HOA file.** First, confirm that the appropriate OAR addendum (Form 1061) was attached to the purchase agreement with completed timeline blanks. Second, verify that the resale certificate or HOA disclosure packet appears in the transaction file with a date-stamped delivery confirmation. Third, check that the five-day rescission period elapsed or was affirmatively waived before the transaction proceeded to closing.

Quarterly file reviews are the minimum standard. Brokers operating teams of 10 or more agents should consider implementing automated compliance checks that flag any condo or HOA transaction missing the certificate at the 15-day mark. Training sessions at least once per year specifically addressing ORC 5311 and 5312 obligations will reduce exposure. ## Liability Consequences for Non-Compliance

The consequences of failing to deliver required HOA or condo disclosures in Ohio fall into three categories: contractual, regulatory, and civil.

Contractually, the buyer retains the right to rescind at any point before closing if the certificate was never delivered. Even after closing, courts have entertained claims that the buyer was denied material information, though post-closing rescission is far more difficult to obtain.

Regulatorily, the Ohio Division of Real Estate can impose sanctions ranging from a formal reprimand to license suspension. Under ORC 4735.18, "incompetency" includes repeated failure to advise clients of statutory obligations. Fines associated with disciplinary actions vary, but license suspension—even temporarily—carries economic consequences far exceeding any single commission.

Civil liability may arise if a buyer suffers financial harm—say, a $30,000 special assessment they were never told about—and sues the seller and the listing agent for failure to disclose. Ohio courts apply a "knew or should have known" standard to agents, meaning ignorance of the association's financial condition is not an automatic defense if the information was available through the resale certificate process.
