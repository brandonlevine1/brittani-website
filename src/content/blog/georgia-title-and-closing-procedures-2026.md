## Why Georgia Title & Closing Procedures 2026 Demand Agent Attention

Georgia remains one of a handful of states that requires an attorney to conduct residential closings. That single fact shapes every deadline, every form, and every compliance obligation you face as a licensed agent working under the Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC). Understanding Georgia title & closing procedures 2026 is not optional knowledge—it is the difference between a smooth settlement and a blown deal that triggers liability.

The regulatory framework has not relaxed. GREC continues to enforce Rules 520-1-.08 and 520-1-.09, which govern trust account handling and the duty of agents to ensure proper documentation reaches the closing attorney on time. Agents who treat closing as "the attorney's problem" expose themselves and their brokerage to disciplinary action, financial loss, and malpractice claims.

This post breaks down the statutes, the forms, the pitfalls, and the broker-level audit requirements that matter right now. If you have [multiple deals running simultaneously](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), the margin for error shrinks with every file you add.

## Georgia's Attorney-Close Requirement and What It Means for Agents

**Georgia law treats the preparation of deeds, security deeds, and title opinions as the practice of law—period.** Under O.C.G.A. § 15-19-50 and the Georgia Supreme Court's Formal Advisory Opinions, only a member of the State Bar of Georgia may conduct a closing, disburse funds from a real estate escrow account tied to settlement, or render a title opinion. This is not a convention; it is a statutory mandate.

What does that mean for you as an agent? You cannot prepare closing documents, offer title interpretations to clients, or disburse earnest money at settlement. Your role is to facilitate the flow of information between parties, ensure contractual deadlines are met, and confirm that the closing attorney has every document required under the purchase agreement.

Violating the unauthorized practice of law prohibition carries consequences beyond GREC discipline. The State Bar can pursue injunctive relief, and a court may void documents prepared by an unlicensed person—killing the deal entirely and leaving your client without title insurance coverage.

## Key Forms and Contracts Agents Must Manage

The Georgia Association of Realtors (GAR) publishes the standard forms most agents use. Knowing the form numbers and their closing-related provisions is baseline competency, not extra credit.

| Form Number | Name | Closing Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| GAR F20 | Purchase and Sale Agreement | Defines closing date, cost allocation, and title objection deadlines |
| GAR F101 | Exhibit to Address Closing | Specifies attorney selection, closing location, and document delivery |
| GAR F205 | Amendment to Address Concerns | Used to modify closing date or title cure provisions post-binding |
| GAR CB20 | New Construction Purchase Agreement | Contains unique title provisions for subdivisions with recorded plats |
| GAR F702 | Seller's Property Disclosure | Must be delivered before closing; failure triggers rescission rights |

**The F20 is where most closing-related disputes originate.** Section 7 addresses title and survey matters, requiring the seller to convey marketable title by warranty deed. Section 10 sets the closing date and specifies that time is of the essence once a date is established. If you miss the interplay between these sections and the title objection cure period, you hand opposing counsel an easy argument for contract termination.

Agents should also be aware of the [ALTA Settlement Statement](https://www.alta.org/) (which replaced the HUD-1 for most transactions) and Georgia's specific requirements around its preparation by the closing attorney rather than a title company employee without attorney supervision.

## The Title Examination Process: Timeline and Agent Responsibilities

A standard Georgia title examination covers 50 years of chain of title, per industry practice aligned with the Georgia Title Standards published by the Real Property Law Section of the State Bar. The closing attorney or their designated title abstractor searches the county Superior Court records, tax records, and any applicable federal lien filings.

Agents do not perform title work. However, you are responsible for ensuring the buyer's due diligence period under the GAR F20 aligns with the realistic timeline for title delivery. A title search in a metro Atlanta county with digital records might return in five business days. A search in a rural county that still relies on physical deed books could take two to three weeks.

**Failing to calendar the title objection deadline is one of the fastest ways to lose a deal.** Under Section 7(b) of the GAR F20, the buyer must raise title objections within the specified number of days after the binding agreement date. If the buyer misses that window, they accept title as-is—regardless of defects discovered later. Your job is to coordinate with the closing attorney so the title commitment arrives with enough runway for the buyer to review and object.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Georgia Closings

Three to five errors show up repeatedly in GREC disciplinary files and closing attorney complaints. These are not theoretical—they result in real enforcement actions.

**First: Agents disbursing earnest money without written agreement or court order.** GREC Rule 520-1-.08 requires that earnest money held in a broker's trust account be disbursed only upon written agreement of all parties, a court order, or through interpleader. Agents who release funds to a seller before closing—or refund them to a buyer without mutual consent—face trust account violations carrying fines up to $5,000 per occurrence and potential license revocation.

Second: Communicating wire instructions without verification. Wire fraud targeting real estate closings has intensified in Georgia, and GREC has made clear that agents who forward wiring details received via email—without independent verification through a known phone number—may be held liable under the duty of care standards in Rule 520-1-.09. The [wire instructions verification process](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) is no longer a best practice; it is a compliance requirement in practice.

Third: Missing the distinction between "closing" and "funding." Georgia attorneys close the transaction at the table, but funding—the actual disbursement of loan proceeds—may not occur simultaneously on every deal. Agents who tell sellers they can access proceeds on closing day, when the lender has a 24- to 48-hour funding lag, create client disputes that escalate to GREC complaints.

Fourth: Failing to deliver the Seller's Property Disclosure (GAR F702) before closing. Under O.C.G.A. § 44-1-16, the seller must provide the disclosure statement to the buyer. If an agent allows the transaction to close without confirmed delivery, the buyer retains post-closing rescission rights that can unwind the entire deal.

Fifth: Scheduling closing without confirming the lender's clear-to-close and the attorney's document receipt. This logistical failure wastes everyone's time and, when it results in a missed contractual closing date, can give the non-defaulting party grounds to terminate under the "time is of the essence" clause.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry personal liability for the acts of their affiliated licensees under O.C.G.A. § 43-40-18. That statute does not offer a "I didn't know" defense. If your agents are mishandling trust funds, missing disclosure deadlines, or practicing law by preparing closing documents, GREC holds you accountable.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Trust Account Reconciliation | Three-way reconciliation per GREC Rule 520-1-.08 | Monthly (minimum) |
| Disclosure Delivery Confirmation | Signed receipt of F702 in every file | Per transaction |
| Wire Instruction Protocols | Agent callback verification logs | Per transaction |
| Closing Date Calendar Compliance | Binding agreement date vs. title objection deadline | Weekly review |
| Attorney Engagement Confirmation | Closing attorney identified and engaged within 5 days of binding | Per transaction |

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance are asking for a GREC audit finding.** Build systems that pull this data automatically. Review your transaction files weekly—not at closing, not after closing, but during the contract-to-close pipeline when corrections are still possible. A [weekly pipeline review system](/blog/weekly-pipeline-review-system) catches these gaps before they become violations.

GREC conducts both complaint-driven and random audits of brokerage trust accounts. The penalty structure is tiered: first offenses for minor recordkeeping deficiencies may result in a citation and education requirement, while repeated or egregious trust account violations lead to license suspension or revocation for both the agent and the supervising broker.

## Title Insurance: Who Pays, Who Benefits, and What Agents Get Wrong

Georgia does not regulate title insurance premium rates through a state-mandated schedule the way some states do. Premiums are set by the title insurance underwriter, though competitive market forces in metro areas keep them relatively consistent. The GAR F20 allows parties to negotiate who pays for the owner's title insurance policy versus the lender's policy.

In most Georgia transactions, the seller pays for the owner's title insurance policy and the buyer pays for the lender's policy. However, this is contractual—not statutory. Agents must read Section 10 of their specific purchase agreement rather than assuming the "customary" split applies.

One persistent error: agents telling buyers they do not need an owner's policy because the lender's policy "covers them." It does not. The lender's policy protects only the lender's interest in the property, and its coverage decreases as the loan is paid down. Agents have a duty under GREC's standards of practice to avoid misleading statements about title insurance coverage, even when the misstatement is born of ignorance rather than intent.

## Closing Day: Sequence of Events and Agent Conduct Rules

The closing attorney runs the table. Your role on closing day is to ensure your client arrives prepared, that all contractual obligations have been met, and that no last-minute surprises derail settlement. Georgia attorneys typically follow this sequence: verify identification of all parties, review the settlement statement line by line, execute the warranty deed and security deed, collect funds (certified check or confirmed wire), and record documents with the county Superior Court clerk.

> "The closing attorney owes a duty to the transaction, not exclusively to the party who retained them. Agents must understand that the attorney's neutral role means they will halt closing if title defects, funding gaps, or document deficiencies exist—regardless of which party is disadvantaged by the delay." — Georgia Real Property Law Section, Practice Advisory 2024-03

Agents should never pressure a closing attorney to proceed when an unresolved title issue exists. Doing so creates potential liability under O.C.G.A. § 43-40-25(a)(22), which authorizes GREC discipline for conduct that demonstrates incompetency or untrustworthiness. If closing must be delayed, use GAR Form F205 to formally amend the closing date rather than relying on oral extensions that may not be enforceable.
