## Understanding Arizona Buyer Contingency Deadlines 2026

Missing a single contingency deadline can collapse a transaction, expose an agent to liability claims, and trigger a complaint to the Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE). Arizona buyer contingency deadlines 2026 remain governed by the same statutory framework agents have worked within for years—but the consequences of non-compliance have grown sharper as litigation increases and ADRE audits intensify. This post breaks down exactly what licensed agents and their brokers need to know about tracking, enforcing, and documenting buyer contingency deadlines under the current Arizona Association of REALTORS (AAR) contract forms.

The AAR Residential Resale Real Estate Purchase Contract is the standard form used in the overwhelming majority of Arizona residential transactions. Every contingency deadline in that contract is a ticking clock, and the agent who loses track of it is the one who ends up answering questions at a hearing.

## The AAR Contract Framework and Key Contingency Periods

Arizona does not impose contingency deadlines by statute. Instead, the deadlines are contractual—negotiated between the parties and memorialized on the AAR Residential Resale Purchase Contract (last revised 2024, currently in active use through 2026). This distinction matters because it means agents bear the responsibility of ensuring these deadlines are properly inserted, calculated, and met.

**The following contingencies carry explicit deadline fields in the AAR contract and require affirmative action by the buyer's agent:**

Section 6b establishes the Inspection Period, during which the buyer must deliver the Buyer's Inspection Notice and Seller's Response (BINR, Form No. BINSR) before the deadline expires. Section 2l addresses the appraisal contingency. Section 4d governs the loan contingency, with the Loan Status Update (LSU) due on the date specified in Section 2i, and the buyer's obligation to deliver a cure period notice if the loan is denied.

| Contingency | AAR Contract Section | Key Form | Consequence of Missing Deadline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inspection | Section 6b | BINSR | Buyer accepts property as-is |
| Appraisal | Section 2l | Buyer's Notice (custom) | Contingency waived |
| Loan | Section 4d / 2i | LSU (Loan Status Update) | Potential default, earnest money at risk |
| Due Diligence (HOA) | Section 6h | Homeowners Association Addendum | Buyer waives right to disapprove HOA docs |
| Title/Survey | Section 3c | Cure Period Notice | Buyer accepts title as delivered |

Each deadline is stated in calendar days from Contract Acceptance, as defined in Section 8a. The contract specifies that if the final day falls on a weekend or legal holiday, the deadline extends to the next business day—but every other calendar day counts, including Saturdays and Sundays.

## How Deadline Failures Create Agent Liability

Arizona Revised Statutes [A.R.S. 32-2153(A)](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/32/02153.htm) outlines the duties of a real estate licensee, including the obligation to exercise reasonable care and skill, to act in good faith, and to disclose material information to the client. When an agent fails to track a contingency deadline and the buyer loses a contractual right—say, the ability to request repairs or cancel over an appraisal shortfall—the agent has arguably breached that duty.

**ADRE can impose discipline ranging from a letter of concern to license revocation under A.R.S. 32-2153 and 32-2158.** Civil liability compounds the problem. If a buyer loses their earnest money deposit because their agent missed the loan contingency cure deadline, that buyer has standing to sue for damages. Errors and omissions insurance will cover many claims, but repeated violations can make an agent uninsurable.

The Consent-to-Act form (AAR Form No. CTA) does not relieve agents of deadline-tracking responsibilities. It merely clarifies that the agent is authorized to act; it does not shift the duty of competence away from the licensee.

## Common Mistakes Arizona Agents Make with Contingency Deadlines

Three to five errors account for the vast majority of deadline-related complaints filed with ADRE and brokerage compliance departments. Understanding these patterns is the fastest path to avoiding them.

**First: Miscalculating Day One.** Contract Acceptance under the AAR form occurs when the last party signs and delivery is made. Agents routinely count the acceptance date itself as Day 1, but the AAR contract's timing provisions under Section 8a start the clock the day after acceptance. A single day of miscalculation can result in a late BINSR delivery and a waived inspection contingency.

Second: Failing to deliver the BINSR in writing before the deadline. Verbal communication does not satisfy the delivery requirement. Section 8b of the AAR contract requires delivery by hand, mail, fax, email, or other method agreed upon in writing. An agent who verbally conveys the inspection results to the listing agent on the deadline day—without delivering the actual BINSR form—has missed the deadline.

Third: Confusing the Loan Status Update (LSU) deadline with the Loan Contingency Period itself. The LSU is due on the date specified in Section 2i; it is a progress report, not the final determination. If the buyer cannot obtain financing, a separate cure period and cancellation process under Section 4d applies. Agents who conflate these two deadlines often miss one or both, [which is why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) at an alarming rate even after years in the business.

Fourth: Ignoring the HOA document review period. Under Section 6h and the Homeowners Association Addendum, the buyer typically has five days after receipt of HOA documents to disapprove. But "receipt" is not the same as the date the documents were ordered. Agents who start counting from the wrong triggering event expose their clients to waiver.

Fifth: Relying on memory instead of a documented tracking system. This is not a legal error per se, but it is the root cause of nearly every missed deadline. Arizona brokerages that do not enforce systematic deadline tracking are building liability exposure with every file they open. As research on [why checklists beat memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) confirms, human recall is insufficient for managing ten or more concurrent contingency timelines.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Arizona carry supervisory responsibility under A.R.S. 32-2151.01. That statute requires the designated broker to adopt and enforce written policies for the supervision of licensees—including policies that ensure transactional compliance. Contingency deadline tracking falls squarely within this obligation.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Deadline calculation accuracy | Compare contract acceptance date to logged deadlines | Every new file within 48 hours |
| BINSR delivery confirmation | Written proof of delivery (email timestamp, fax confirmation) | Before inspection deadline |
| LSU delivery | Proof that LSU was sent to listing agent by Section 2i date | On LSU due date |
| HOA document receipt logging | Date HOA docs received vs. review period start date | Within 24 hours of receipt |
| Cure Period Notice tracking | Confirm delivery if loan denial or title objection arises | As triggered |

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance are inviting problems.** A quarterly file audit is insufficient for active offices. Best practice is real-time monitoring—either through a dedicated transaction coordinator or an automated compliance system that flags approaching and past-due deadlines.

The ADRE's enforcement actions over the past two years show a pattern: brokerages that cannot produce documentation of their supervisory systems face harsher sanctions. Having a written policy that exists only in a handbook no one reads does not satisfy the standard. Brokers must demonstrate that the policy is operationalized—meaning it generates logs, triggers alerts, or otherwise creates a paper trail showing the policy was followed.

## The Interplay Between Contingency Deadlines and Earnest Money

Arizona earnest money disputes almost always trace back to a missed or disputed contingency deadline. Under the AAR contract, the buyer's right to a refund of earnest money is contingent on proper and timely cancellation within the applicable contingency period. If the buyer cancels after the deadline passes—without having secured a written extension—the seller may claim the deposit as liquidated damages under Section 7.

**This makes deadline compliance a financial protection issue, not merely a procedural one.** Agents who advise their buyers that "we can always work it out" after a missed deadline are giving advice that contradicts the plain language of the contract. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) most often reinforces the point: the contingency-to-close period is the highest-risk window.

The Release of Earnest Money form (AAR Form No. REM) requires both parties' signatures to release the deposit. If a dispute arises because a deadline was missed, the funds sit in escrow indefinitely—or until a court orders their release. Neither outcome is acceptable for the client or the agent's reputation.

## Extensions, Amendments, and How to Properly Modify Deadlines

When a contingency deadline cannot be met, the correct remedy is a written extension executed before the deadline expires. The AAR Contract Amendment / Supplement (Form No. CASM) is the standard form for modifying any contract term, including contingency deadlines.

Agents sometimes attempt to extend deadlines through email exchanges or text messages. While Arizona courts have recognized electronic communications as satisfying the Statute of Frauds under A.R.S. 44-7001 (the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act), the safer and more defensible practice is to use the CASM form with signatures from all parties. An unsigned email exchange is far easier to dispute than an executed amendment.

The timing of the extension request matters enormously. If the buyer's agent requests an extension on the inspection deadline after it has already passed, the seller has no obligation to grant it—and the buyer has already lost the contingency. Proactive communication, ideally two or more days before a deadline, gives the parties time to negotiate and execute the amendment properly.
