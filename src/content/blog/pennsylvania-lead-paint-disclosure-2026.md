## Why Pennsylvania Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026 Still Trip Up Experienced Agents

Pennsylvania has roughly 3.8 million housing units built before 1978, making lead paint disclosure one of the most frequently triggered compliance obligations in the state. Understanding Pennsylvania lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 is not optional — it is a federal mandate enforced with penalties that can exceed $19,000 per violation. Yet agents and brokers continue to make avoidable errors that expose them to lawsuits, deal cancellations, and disciplinary action from the Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission (PREC).

The core obligation comes from federal law — specifically the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulation at 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F. Pennsylvania does not have a separate state-level lead paint disclosure statute that overrides or supplements the federal requirement. Instead, Pennsylvania agents operate under the federal framework while simultaneously meeting their duties under the Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. Section 455.101 et seq., which holds licensees to standards of competence and honest dealing.

## The Federal Framework That Governs Pennsylvania Transactions

**Every residential sale or lease of a property built before 1978 triggers the lead paint disclosure requirement — no exceptions based on property condition, renovation history, or seller representations.** The rule applies equally to single-family homes, condominiums, townhouses, and multi-unit buildings when individual units are being sold or leased.

The disclosure has three components mandated by EPA and HUD. First, the seller must disclose any known lead-based paint or hazards present in the property. Second, the seller must provide any available records, reports, or test results related to lead-based paint. Third, the buyer must receive a copy of the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home."

Agents on both sides of the transaction share a distinct legal obligation. The listing agent must ensure the seller completes the disclosure and that the buyer receives the pamphlet. The buyer's agent must ensure their client acknowledges receipt and understands their right to a 10-day inspection period. This is not a task you can delegate away and forget — [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) means tracking each one's disclosure status individually.

## Forms Pennsylvania Agents Must Use

The standard form used in most Pennsylvania transactions is the **PAR Form LPD (Lead-Based Paint Disclosure)**, published by the Pennsylvania Association of Realtors. This form satisfies the requirements of 40 CFR 745.113 and includes signature blocks for the seller, buyer, listing agent, and buyer's agent. All four signatures are required before or at the time of contract execution.

| Form Component | Who Completes It | When It Must Be Signed |
|---|---|---|
| Seller's Disclosure of Known Hazards | Seller | Before buyer signs purchase agreement |
| Buyer's Acknowledgment of Receipt | Buyer | At or before contract execution |
| Listing Agent Certification | Listing agent | At or before contract execution |
| Buyer's Agent Certification | Buyer's agent | At or before contract execution |
| Buyer's Election (inspect or waive) | Buyer | Within 10 days of contract or as negotiated |

Some brokerages supplement the PAR Form LPD with the EPA's own sample disclosure form found in 40 CFR Part 745, Appendix A. Either version satisfies the regulation as long as it contains all required elements. The critical point is that using an outdated version of any form — one that omits required language about the buyer's inspection rights — creates an incomplete disclosure that will not protect you in litigation.

For agents working with the [EPA's official guidance on lead paint disclosure](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-based-paint-disclosure-rule), the agency maintains updated copies of required pamphlets and sample forms that satisfy 40 CFR 745.

## Penalties and Liability for Non-Compliance

**The financial exposure for failing to comply with lead paint disclosure requirements is severe and personal — it attaches to individual licensees, not just the brokerage.** Under 40 CFR 745.118, civil penalties can reach $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2024). Criminal penalties for knowing and willful violations can include fines up to $19,507 per violation and imprisonment.

Beyond federal enforcement, Pennsylvania buyers who discover undisclosed lead hazards after closing have a private right of action. They can pursue treble damages (three times actual damages) under the federal statute. Actual damages in lead paint cases often include remediation costs ($10,000 to $30,000 for a typical Philadelphia rowhome), medical costs if children test positive for elevated blood lead levels, and diminished property value.

| Consequence | Source of Authority | Potential Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Civil fine per violation | 40 CFR 745.118 | Up to $19,507 |
| Criminal fine (knowing violation) | 42 USC 4852d | Up to $19,507 + imprisonment |
| Treble damages (buyer lawsuit) | 42 USC 4852d(b)(3) | 3x actual damages |
| License discipline | PREC under 63 P.S. 455.604 | Suspension, revocation, additional fines |
| Contract rescission | Common law / buyer's election | Deal cancellation + return of deposits |

The Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission can also pursue independent disciplinary action under Section 604 of RELRA if it determines that an agent's failure to ensure proper disclosure constitutes incompetence, untrustworthiness, or dishonest dealing. This is separate from any federal penalty and can result in license suspension or revocation.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosure in Pennsylvania

Thousands of transactions close properly every month in Pennsylvania. But audits and litigation reveal the same handful of errors appearing repeatedly. Here are five specific mistakes that create real liability.

**First, agents present the disclosure form after contract execution rather than before.** The regulation at 40 CFR 745.113(b) requires that the buyer receive the disclosure before becoming obligated under the contract. Handing it over at the settlement table — or even the day after mutual execution — violates the timing requirement. This is one of the reasons [agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) so frequently; the lead paint form needs to be part of the initial offer package, not an afterthought.

Second, listing agents accept a seller's blanket "unknown" response without any follow-up conversation. While sellers can legally disclose that they have no knowledge of lead-based paint, the listing agent has a duty under RELRA to ask reasonable questions. If the seller received a lead inspection report at their own purchase and fails to disclose it, the listing agent who never asked may share liability.

Third, agents fail to document the buyer's election regarding the 10-day inspection period. The buyer must affirmatively check a box or sign a statement either electing the inspection period, negotiating a different period, or waiving the right entirely. A blank field on this section renders the form incomplete and potentially voids the disclosure.

Fourth, agents working with non-English-speaking clients fail to ensure the parties actually understand the disclosure content. While there is no regulatory requirement to provide translated forms, an agent who knows a client cannot read English and does nothing to facilitate understanding may face claims of inadequate disclosure.

Fifth, agents in lease transactions ignore the requirement entirely. The lead paint disclosure applies to rentals of pre-1978 housing, not just sales. Property management divisions and agents handling lease transactions must follow the same protocol, and many do not.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under RELRA Section 604(a)(14) for the actions of their affiliated licensees. A broker who fails to implement systems for lead paint disclosure compliance exposes the entire brokerage to regulatory action.

**At minimum, every Pennsylvania brokerage should audit the following on a quarterly basis.** First, pull a random sample of closed files for pre-1978 properties and verify that the LPD form is present, complete, and signed by all four parties. Second, confirm that the form was signed on or before the date of contract execution — compare timestamps. Third, verify that a copy of the EPA pamphlet delivery is documented somewhere in the file, whether as a separate acknowledgment or as part of the LPD form itself.

Brokers should also confirm that their agents are using the current version of the PAR Form LPD. The Pennsylvania Association of Realtors periodically updates its forms, and an outdated version may lack language required by regulatory amendments. A firm-wide policy requiring agents to download forms only from the PAR library — never from personal archives — eliminates this risk.

Training is the other enforcement mechanism brokers control. New agent onboarding should include a specific module on lead paint disclosure timing and form completion, not just a passing mention during a general compliance overview. The [training timeline you build for new agents](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) should flag lead paint compliance as a day-one competency, not something they absorb by osmosis six months into production.

## Exemptions That Apply (and Do Not Apply) in Pennsylvania

Not every transaction triggers the disclosure obligation. Properties built in 1978 or later are exempt. Zero-bedroom units such as lofts, efficiencies, and dormitories are exempt. Housing designated exclusively for elderly or disabled persons (where children under six are not expected to reside) is exempt. Foreclosure sales are exempt. Short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or fewer are exempt.

However, several situations that agents commonly assume are exempt actually are not. Estate sales of pre-1978 properties still require disclosure — the executor or administrator stands in the shoes of the seller. Properties sold "as-is" still require lead paint disclosure; the as-is clause does not override a federal mandate. And properties that have been "completely renovated" still require disclosure unless the seller can produce a certified inspection report showing no lead-based paint is present.
