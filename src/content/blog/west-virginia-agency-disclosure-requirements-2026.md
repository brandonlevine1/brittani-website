## Understanding West Virginia Agency Disclosure Requirements 2026

The West Virginia agency disclosure requirements 2026 remain one of the most common compliance triggers that trip up otherwise competent agents. Unlike states where a single signature page handles everything, West Virginia demands timely, relationship-specific disclosures governed by the West Virginia Real Estate License Act (W. Va. Code Chapter 30, Article 40) and the rules issued by the West Virginia Real Estate Commission (WVREC) under 174 CSR 1. Missing the mark does not just invite a slap on the wrist—it can unwind a deal or cost you your license.

This post breaks down the specific statutory requirements, the forms involved, the penalties for noncompliance, and the operational checkpoints that brokers need to build into their transaction management workflows. If you have already reviewed our piece on [seller disclosure obligations in West Virginia](/blog/west-virginia-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026), consider this the companion guide focused exclusively on the agency relationship side.

## The Statutory Framework: W. Va. Code 30-40-19 and 174 CSR 1

**West Virginia's agency disclosure obligation is codified in W. Va. Code Section 30-40-19, which requires licensees to disclose their agency relationship to all parties in a transaction at the earliest practicable time.** The statute does not define "earliest practicable time" with clock-and-calendar precision. Instead, the WVREC's interpretive guidance—published through advisory opinions and enforcement actions—holds that disclosure must precede any exchange of confidential information or any discussion of transaction terms.

The Commission's [Legislative Rule 174 CSR 1](https://www.wvsos.com/csr/verify.asp?TitleSeries=174-01) fleshes out the procedural details. It prescribes the form language, establishes that licensees must use the Commission-approved Disclosure of Agency form, and sets out the dual agency consent procedure. Agents cannot substitute their brokerage's proprietary form unless it contains, at minimum, every element of the Commission-prescribed version.

## Who Gets What—and When

Timing is everything. The statute envisions three distinct agency relationships—seller's agent, buyer's agent, and dual agent—and each carries its own disclosure cadence.

| Relationship | When to Disclose | Who Signs | Additional Consent Needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seller's Agent | At listing appointment or first substantive contact with seller | Seller | No |
| Buyer's Agent | At first substantive contact with prospective buyer | Buyer | No |
| Dual Agent | Before dual agency arises (often at offer presentation) | Both buyer and seller | Yes—separate written dual agency consent |
| Transaction Broker | Not recognized in WV | N/A | N/A |

West Virginia does not authorize a "transaction broker" or "facilitator" status like Colorado or Florida. You are either an agent for one party, an agent for both parties with dual agency consent, or you are violating the statute. That binary framework means the Disclosure of Agency form must clearly identify which hat you are wearing before you take any substantive step in the transaction.

## The Form Itself: What It Must Contain

The WVREC's Disclosure of Agency form is not a mere checkbox sheet. It includes an explanation of seller agency, buyer agency, and dual agency written in plain language so consumers understand what they are consenting to. Agents must present the form, allow the consumer to read it, and secure a signature. If the consumer refuses to sign, you note the refusal on the form with the date and your own signature—then keep the form in the file.

**Retaining the completed form is not optional.** Under 174 CSR 1, all transaction records—including agency disclosures—must be kept for a minimum of three years from the date of closing or, if the transaction does not close, from the date of the last substantive activity. The WVREC can audit files at any time during that window without prior notice.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with West Virginia Agency Disclosure

Five errors show up repeatedly in WVREC disciplinary actions and informal complaint files.

**First, disclosing too late.** Agents who wait until the offer is drafted have already blown past the statutory trigger. If you discussed price expectations, property condition issues, or negotiation strategy before handing over the form, you were already functioning as an agent without disclosure. The WVREC treats this as a violation regardless of whether the consumer was actually harmed.

Second, using outdated forms. The Commission periodically updates form language to reflect statutory amendments or interpretive changes. Running off copies from 2019 will not cut it if the current prescribed version contains different language. Agents should verify form currency against the WVREC website at least once per calendar year.

Third, failing to secure dual agency consent from both parties. Some agents obtain the seller's consent at listing and forget to circle back with the buyer when the agent's own buyer client writes an offer on that listing. Dual agency does not activate until both parties have signed.

Fourth, not documenting refusal to sign. A consumer can refuse to acknowledge the disclosure, but the agent still has the obligation to present it and memorialize the refusal. An unsigned form with no refusal notation looks identical to a form that was never presented—and the Commission will draw the negative inference.

Fifth, treating teams as a workaround. Two agents on the same team, one representing the buyer and one representing the seller, does not eliminate dual agency obligations if both agents are licensed under the same broker. The firm-level agency relationship controls.

## Penalties for Noncompliance: Fines, License Actions, and Deal Rescission

The WVREC has statutory authority under W. Va. Code 30-40-26 to impose civil penalties of up to $5,000 per violation. Repeat violations or willful misconduct can result in license suspension or revocation. The Commission can also require additional continuing education hours as a remedial condition.

| Penalty Type | Authority | Maximum Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Civil fine | W. Va. Code 30-40-26 | $5,000 per violation |
| License suspension | W. Va. Code 30-40-26(a)(1) | Indefinite until conditions met |
| License revocation | W. Va. Code 30-40-26(a)(2) | Permanent bar |
| Transaction rescission | Common law + W. Va. Code 30-40-19 | Full unwinding of sale |
| Private civil liability | Tort/contract claims | Compensatory and potentially punitive damages |

Beyond regulatory action, an aggrieved party can seek rescission of the transaction in circuit court. If a buyer can demonstrate that they would not have entered the contract had they understood the agent's true loyalties, courts have granted rescission and ordered return of earnest money. Private lawsuits may also seek compensatory damages for any financial harm flowing from the undisclosed conflict. Agents carry E&O insurance for good reason, but no policy covers intentional regulatory violations.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under W. Va. Code 30-40-20. The statute places an affirmative duty on the qualifying broker to ensure that all licensees under their supervision comply with disclosure requirements. Ignorance is not a defense—if your agent blew the disclosure and you never checked, the Commission can sanction you alongside the agent.

**Build a file review checkpoint that triggers within 48 hours of a new listing or buyer agreement.** That checkpoint should confirm the presence of a signed Disclosure of Agency form dated on or before the first substantive contact. If the form is missing or undated, the file should not advance to the next stage of your workflow.

Brokers should also conduct quarterly random audits of closed and pending files. Pull five to ten files per quarter and verify form presence, correct version, proper signatures, and compliant dating. Document the audit itself—date, files reviewed, findings—so you can demonstrate supervisory diligence if the WVREC ever investigates.

For brokerages managing high transaction volume, connecting your compliance audits with [deadline-tracking systems](/blog/real-cost-of-missed-deadlines) reduces the chance that a single overlooked form becomes a regulatory nightmare six months later.

## Dual Agency: The Highest-Risk Disclosure Scenario

Dual agency in West Virginia is legal but heavily scrutinized. The Disclosure of Agency form includes a dual agency section, but the statute requires more than a signature on a pre-printed paragraph. Both parties must provide informed, written consent after receiving an explanation of how dual agency limits the agent's ability to advocate for either side.

The practical risk is highest in small markets where one brokerage dominates listings. An agent who shows a buyer three properties—all listed by their own brokerage—triggers potential dual agency on each one. The disclosure and consent process must repeat for every property where dual agency arises, not just the one that eventually goes under contract.

Agents sometimes argue they disclosed dual agency "at the beginning of the relationship." That is insufficient under West Virginia law. Dual agency is transaction-specific, not relationship-wide. Each new dual agency scenario requires fresh consent.
