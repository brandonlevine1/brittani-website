## Indiana Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026: The Current Rules

The question of **Indiana who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026** remains one of the most misunderstood areas of transaction practice in the state. Unlike states with rigid statutory mandates assigning every fee to a specific party, Indiana relies heavily on contractual allocation — meaning the purchase agreement your clients sign is the operative document. Agents who treat closing cost allocation as "just how it's always done in our market" expose themselves and their brokers to real liability.

Indiana Code Title 25, Article 34.1, governs real estate licensee conduct, and the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC) enforces compliance. The Indiana Association of Realtors (IAR) publishes standard forms that most agents use statewide, but those forms only work if filled out correctly. A blank or ambiguous closing-cost field does not default to local custom — it creates a dispute.

## Standard Allocation: Who Traditionally Pays What

Indiana custom assigns certain costs to buyers and others to sellers, but these defaults are not codified in statute. They exist as market practice, reinforced by lender requirements and title company procedures. The table below reflects the prevailing allocation for a typical residential resale in 2026.

| Cost Item | Typically Paid By | Negotiable? |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|
| Owner's title insurance policy | Seller | Yes |
| Lender's title insurance policy | Buyer | Yes |
| Recording fees (deed) | Seller | Yes |
| Recording fees (mortgage) | Buyer | Yes |
| Transfer tax (not applicable in IN) | N/A | N/A |
| Home warranty (if offered) | Seller | Yes |
| Appraisal fee | Buyer | Rarely |
| Attorney/settlement fee | Split or buyer | Yes |

**Indiana does not impose a state-level real estate transfer tax.** This is a meaningful distinction from neighboring states like Illinois and Ohio. Agents moving into Indiana practice from those states sometimes mistakenly advise clients that a transfer tax will appear on the settlement statement — it will not, unless a local ordinance applies (currently none do in 2026).

The absence of a transfer tax simplifies the seller's side of the ledger, but it does not reduce the importance of documenting every cost allocation in the contract. The IAR Purchase Agreement (Form 46) includes specific sections where agents must itemize who pays for what, including title evidence, inspections, and surveys.

## Key Forms and Where Cost Allocation Lives

The [IAR Form 46 (Purchase Agreement)](https://www.indianarealtors.com) is the backbone of most residential deals in Indiana. Section 10 of Form 46 addresses closing costs and title evidence. Agents must complete this section in full — leaving it blank or writing "per custom" invites post-acceptance disputes that can delay closing or kill deals.

Form 46, Section 10 specifically requires the parties to designate who provides title evidence (abstract or commitment), who pays for the owner's title policy, and who covers survey costs. The 2025-2026 revision added clarifying language about concession caps tied to lender guidelines, reflecting FHFA updates that took effect in late 2025.

IAR Form 50 (Seller's Disclosure) does not address closing costs directly, but agents sometimes conflate disclosure obligations with cost allocation. These are separate issues. A seller's failure to disclose a material defect is governed by IC 32-21-5 (the Indiana Residential Real Estate Sales Disclosure statute), while cost disputes fall under breach of contract or licensee misconduct under IC 25-34.1-10-10.

## Seller Concessions: What Agents Get Wrong

Seller concessions — where the seller agrees to pay a portion of the buyer's closing costs — remain the single most litigated closing-cost issue in Indiana residential transactions. The problem is not that concessions are complicated. The problem is that agents routinely write them incorrectly.

**A concession written as "seller to pay up to 3% of buyer's closing costs" means nothing if the purchase price and the specific costs are not reconciled on the Closing Disclosure.** Agents must confirm the concession amount does not exceed the lender's maximum allowable contribution. For conventional loans, Fannie Mae caps range from 3% to 9% depending on down payment and occupancy type. FHA allows up to 6%. VA allows up to 4% plus all reasonable closing costs.

When an agent writes a concession exceeding the lender cap, the deal does not simply proceed with the lower figure. The Closing Disclosure will reflect the actual amount applied, but if the buyer expected a higher credit, they may lack funds to close. This is a failure in agent diligence, and IREC has disciplined licensees for it under the "competency" standard in 876 IAC 5-1-13.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make on Indiana Closing Costs

**First, agents fail to specify who pays the settlement or closing fee.** In Indiana, many title companies charge a flat settlement fee (typically $250-$500) that gets split between buyer and seller by default. But "by default" is not "by contract." If Form 46 does not address it, a dispute arises when the preliminary Closing Disclosure shows the fee assigned to one party who did not expect it.

Second, agents conflate the cost of the home inspection with "closing costs." Home inspection fees are paid out of pocket by the buyer at the time of inspection — they do not appear on the Closing Disclosure. Writing "seller to pay buyer's inspection costs" as part of a closing-cost concession creates confusion because lenders typically will not allow that expense to be credited at closing since it was already paid.

Third, agents neglect to update cost allocations when contract amendments change the purchase price. If the original agreement includes a 3% seller concession and the price drops $15,000 after an inspection amendment (IAR Form 56, Counter Offer/Amendment), the dollar amount of the concession changes. Failing to recalculate — or failing to explicitly state whether the concession is a fixed dollar amount or a percentage — causes CD revisions and potential closing delays.

Fourth, agents in counties near state borders (Dearborn, Clark, Floyd) sometimes apply Ohio or Kentucky customs to Indiana transactions. Indiana has no transfer tax. Indiana does not require attorneys at closing. Indiana uses IAR forms, not Cincinnati Area Board or Greater Louisville Association forms. Applying out-of-state assumptions to an Indiana transaction violates the licensee's duty of competence.

Fifth, agents omit proration details from the agreement. Real property taxes in Indiana are paid in arrears — a May closing means the seller owes taxes from January through May, but those taxes are not billed until the following year. If the agent does not correctly select the proration method in Form 46 Section 11, the title company may prorate using a method neither party expected. This commonly generates disputes of $1,500-$4,000 on properties in Marion, Hamilton, and Allen counties.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Broker responsibility under IC 25-34.1-10-3.5 includes supervisory duty over all licensee transactions. When it comes to closing costs, that duty translates into concrete audit steps.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Risk if Missed |
|------------|---------------|----------------|
| Form 46 Section 10 completion | All blanks filled; no "per custom" language | Contract voidability; E&O claim |
| Concession vs. lender cap | Dollar amount within program limits | Buyer funding shortfall; delayed closing |
| Amendment consistency | Price changes reflected in concession math | CD revision; 3-day re-disclosure delay |
| Proration method selection | Section 11 marked clearly | Tax proration dispute; post-closing claim |
| Dual-fee disclosure | Settlement fee allocation stated in writing | IREC complaint; IC 25-34.1-10-10 violation |

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-audit these items are accepting risk they do not need to accept.** A quarterly file review is insufficient given the speed of most Indiana residential pipelines. Real-time transaction oversight — whether through a dedicated TC, a compliance platform, or automated checklists — is the standard that IREC increasingly expects when evaluating broker supervision complaints.

## Liability and Discipline: What Happens When It Goes Wrong

IC 25-34.1-10-10 lists conduct that constitutes grounds for license discipline. Subsection (a)(2) covers "incompetency" and subsection (a)(4) covers "failing to account for or to remit any moneys or documents coming into the licensee's possession which belong to others." A closing-cost miscommunication that results in a buyer receiving fewer credits than contractually promised falls squarely within these provisions.

IREC penalties include written reprimand, mandatory continuing education, license suspension, fines up to $1,000 per violation, and license revocation in repeated or egregious cases. Beyond administrative action, agents face civil liability. Indiana courts have upheld breach of fiduciary duty claims where agents failed to properly document or communicate closing cost obligations — see, e.g., decisions citing IC 25-34.1-10-11 regarding the agent's duty to exercise reasonable care.

Deal cancellation is also a real outcome. If a buyer cannot close because the seller concession was written above lender limits and the buyer lacks the difference, the transaction dies. The listing agent loses their commission. The buyer's agent faces a potential [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) post-mortem with an unhappy client. And both agents risk complaints to IREC.

## How 2026 Market Conditions Affect Cost Negotiations

The 2026 Indiana market has shifted toward greater buyer leverage in many metro areas, particularly Indianapolis MSA, Fort Wayne, and South Bend. Rising inventory means sellers are more frequently agreeing to pay a larger share of closing costs to attract offers. Agents representing sellers must counsel their clients that agreeing to a 3% concession on a $350,000 home is a $10,500 reduction in net proceeds — not a trivial marketing gesture.

On the buyer side, agents must understand that requesting excessive concessions can weaken an offer even in a buyer's market. A seller comparing two offers at the same price — one with no concession request and one with a $12,000 concession — will net $12,000 more from the first. Agents who reflexively request maximum concessions without analyzing the competitive landscape of that specific listing are not serving their clients' interests.

## Final Guidance on Indiana Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026

The answer to **Indiana who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026** is: whatever the purchase agreement says, documented correctly on the proper IAR forms, within lender guidelines, and supervised by the managing broker. There is no statutory default. There is only what the contract states and whether the agent documented it with professional precision.

Agents who treat closing cost allocation as an afterthought — or worse, as something the title company will "figure out" — are gambling with their license and their clients' money. Build the habit of completing every cost-related section of Form 46 before presenting offers, verifying concession limits with the lender before drafting terms, and flagging discrepancies the moment a Closing Disclosure arrives. The discipline required is not extraordinary. It is simply the minimum standard of competent practice in Indiana.
