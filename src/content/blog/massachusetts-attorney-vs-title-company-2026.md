## Massachusetts Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Rule Agents Get Wrong

The question of Massachusetts attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 trips up even experienced agents because the answer is not found in a single clean statute. Massachusetts operates as an "attorney state," but that designation comes from case law, bar opinions, and regulatory practice rather than a single line in the General Laws saying "an attorney shall be present." The practical result is that virtually every residential closing in the Commonwealth involves a licensed attorney — and agents who assume a title company alone can handle the table are exposing themselves and their clients to serious risk.

Understanding this distinction matters because [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) often traces back to confusion about who holds responsibility for document preparation, title certification, and funds disbursement. Massachusetts draws sharper lines than most states, and the consequences of crossing them fall on everyone at the table — including the listing and buyer's agents.

## The Legal Framework: Why Massachusetts Is an "Attorney State"

**Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 221, Section 46A defines the unauthorized practice of law and makes it a criminal offense.** The statute prohibits any person not admitted to the Massachusetts bar from preparing legal instruments affecting real property — including deeds, mortgages, and title certifications. The Supreme Judicial Court reinforced this in its landmark opinion *Real Estate Bar Association for Massachusetts v. National Real Estate Information Services* (REBA v. NREIS), which held that title examination and the issuance of title certifications constitute the practice of law.

SJC Rule 3:07, the Massachusetts Rules of Professional Conduct, further clarifies attorney obligations when supervising closings. An attorney who lends their name to a title company closing without meaningfully reviewing documents violates Rule 5.5 and risks disciplinary action. The Division of Professional Licensure, which oversees the Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons, has also issued guidance that agents should not perform acts constituting legal practice — meaning you cannot fill in deed blanks, draft riders, or interpret title issues on behalf of clients.

The [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau's TRID rules](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/rules-policy/regulations/1026/) layer federal requirements on top of state law, requiring that Closing Disclosures be delivered accurately and on time. In Massachusetts, the attorney — not a title company employee acting alone — bears responsibility for ensuring the CD matches the settlement figures and that the lender's instructions are satisfied.

## Attorney vs. Title Company: What Each Party Actually Does

The confusion arises because title companies and attorneys often work together, and agents sometimes cannot tell where one role ends and the other begins. Here is the functional breakdown for a standard Massachusetts residential closing in 2026:

| Function | Attorney | Title Company |
|----------|----------|---------------|
| Title examination and certification | Required — must be performed by or under direct supervision of MA-licensed attorney | Cannot independently examine or certify title |
| Deed preparation | Attorney drafts and reviews | Cannot prepare without attorney involvement |
| Title insurance issuance | Attorney often acts as title insurance agent | Issues policy but relies on attorney's title certification |
| Funds disbursement | Controls IOLTA account, disburses at closing | May hold escrow funds but attorney authorizes release |
| Closing Disclosure review | Reviews and certifies accuracy | May prepare draft for attorney review |
| Recording of documents | Attorney or designee records at Registry of Deeds | May handle ministerial recording if attorney authorizes |

A title company in Massachusetts typically functions as an underwriter or administrative coordinator. It does not replace the attorney. When agents tell buyers "the title company handles everything," they are misstating the legal reality and potentially steering clients away from necessary legal representation.

## Forms and Documents Agents Must Know

Massachusetts residential transactions rely on a specific set of forms that agents encounter at every closing. The Greater Boston Real Estate Board Standard Purchase and Sale Agreement (Form 501 in its current iteration) contains attorney-review provisions and contingency language that agents must understand but cannot modify without attorney input.

**The Massachusetts Mandatory Licensee-Consumer Relationship Disclosure form, required under 254 CMR 2.05, must be delivered to buyers and sellers at first substantive contact.** This form does not address closing representation, but it establishes the framework within which the agent operates — and that framework excludes legal advice. Agents who stray into document interpretation at closing are exceeding the scope established by their own disclosure.

The HUD-1 replacement — the TRID Closing Disclosure (CD) — must reconcile with the Loan Estimate within tolerances set by Regulation Z. In Massachusetts, the closing attorney prepares and certifies the final CD. The title company's role is limited to providing payoff figures, title insurance premiums, and recording fees for the attorney to incorporate. Agents should review the CD for obvious commission errors but should not attempt to resolve discrepancies between lender figures and settlement amounts — that is the attorney's job.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make at Massachusetts Closings

**The first and most dangerous mistake is assuming the title company's presence eliminates the need for attorney involvement.** This happens when agents work with national title companies that operate differently in non-attorney states. In Massachusetts, even if the title company has an office and staff, a licensed Massachusetts attorney must review and certify title, prepare the deed, and oversee the closing.

The second mistake is filling in blanks on legal documents. Agents sometimes complete assignment clauses, draft addenda to the purchase and sale agreement, or modify deed language at the closing table. Under M.G.L. Chapter 221, Section 46A, this constitutes unauthorized practice of law. The Board of Registration can suspend or revoke an agent's license for this conduct, and the transaction itself may be challenged.

Third, agents fail to confirm that the "closing attorney" actually holds a current Massachusetts law license. Some title companies use the term loosely, referring to a paralegal or notary as the "closer." Agents should verify bar status through the Massachusetts Board of Bar Overseers' online directory before the closing date. A closing conducted by an unlicensed person is not merely improper — it may render the deed voidable.

Fourth, agents neglect to advise clients — particularly buyers — to retain independent counsel. The closing attorney typically represents the lender's interests or operates as a neutral party. Buyers and sellers each benefit from separate representation, and agents who discourage this to "keep things simple" risk complaints alleging that they prioritized speed over client protection.

Fifth, agents provide legal opinions about title defects. When a title search reveals an old lien, an encroachment, or a boundary dispute, agents sometimes offer reassurances like "that won't be a problem" or "the title company will just insure over it." These statements constitute legal advice, and if the defect later causes the buyer harm, the agent faces liability for negligent misrepresentation.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under 254 CMR 2.00, the regulations governing real estate practice issued by the Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons. If an agent in your office facilitates a closing without proper attorney involvement, the broker's license is at risk — not just the agent's.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Consequence of Non-Compliance |
|------------|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Verify attorney engagement letter in file | Every transaction | Potential Board complaint, E&O claim |
| Confirm attorney MA bar status | Every transaction | Closing may be voidable |
| Review agent communications for legal advice | Quarterly | License suspension risk |
| Check that agents are not drafting deed language | Quarterly | UPL violation, criminal exposure |
| Ensure Closing Disclosure reviewed by attorney before signing | Every transaction | TRID violation, lender repurchase demand |

**Brokers should establish a written office policy stating that no transaction may proceed to closing without documented confirmation of attorney involvement.** This policy should specify that the file must contain the attorney's name, bar number, and written engagement before the closing date is set. Agents who cannot produce this documentation should have their closing delayed until compliance is confirmed.

The Division of Professional Licensure conducts random audits of brokerage files. A pattern of closings without attorney documentation can trigger a formal investigation, resulting in fines up to $5,000 per violation and potential license revocation for the supervising broker. These are not theoretical risks — the Board has issued consent agreements and disciplinary actions against brokerages for exactly this pattern.

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When Agents Get This Wrong

When an agent facilitates a closing that later turns out to have been conducted improperly — no attorney review, defective deed, unauthorized document preparation — the liability chain extends in multiple directions. The buyer may sue the agent for negligent misrepresentation. The lender may demand repurchase or refuse to honor the mortgage. The title insurer may deny coverage based on the exclusion for unauthorized practice of law.

Massachusetts courts have held that agents owe a duty of care that includes ensuring clients understand the legal framework of the transaction. This does not mean the agent must provide legal advice — it means the agent must not obstruct or circumvent the legal protections built into the system. An agent who discourages attorney involvement, or who tells a client that "the title company is enough," has arguably breached this duty.

E&O insurance policies typically exclude coverage for claims arising from unauthorized practice of law. If an agent's conduct crosses that line — even inadvertently — the insurer may deny the claim entirely, leaving the agent personally liable for damages that can reach six figures on a standard residential transaction.
