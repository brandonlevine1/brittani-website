## Why Indiana Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026 Demand Your Attention

Indiana's disclosure framework has not undergone a major statutory overhaul in recent years, but that stability makes complacency the real threat. Agents who treat Indiana property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 as a formality—rather than a liability checkpoint—leave themselves, their brokers, and their clients exposed. The Indiana Real Estate Commission continues to audit files, and the penalties for sloppy or missing disclosures remain sharp enough to end a career.

The governing statute is Indiana Code Title 32, Article 21, Chapter 5, commonly cited as IC 32-21-5. It establishes the mandatory seller's disclosure form, outlines exemptions, and specifies buyer remedies when the form is missing or fraudulent. Every agent practicing in Indiana should know this chapter the way a surgeon knows anatomy.

This post breaks down the exact forms, the environmental obligations layered on top, the liability consequences of non-compliance, and the internal controls brokers should enforce right now.

## The Indiana Residential Real Estate Sales Disclosure Form

**Indiana mandates a single statutory disclosure form: the Indiana Residential Real Estate Sales Disclosure (State Form 46234).** This is not an optional "best practice" document. IC 32-21-5-2 requires every seller of residential real estate containing one to four dwelling units to complete and deliver this form to the prospective buyer before an offer is accepted—or, at the latest, before closing.

State Form 46234 covers structural systems, mechanical systems, water and sewer, environmental hazards, land characteristics, and property condition history. The form asks yes/no/unknown questions across roughly 80 items. Sellers initial each section, sign at the bottom, and date it.

Agents do not fill this form out for the seller, but they are expected to confirm it has been completed and delivered. The listing agent's role is ministerial and advisory—never authorial.

## Environmental Disclosures: What Goes Beyond the Standard Form

State Form 46234 includes a section on environmental concerns—underground storage tanks, soil contamination, hazardous waste, radon, and lead-based paint awareness. But two additional federal and state obligations sit on top of that form and operate independently.

First, the federal Lead-Based Paint Disclosure rule under 42 U.S.C. 4852d and EPA/HUD regulations at 24 CFR Part 35 requires sellers of any pre-1978 residential property to provide buyers with a lead-paint disclosure, the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home," and a 10-day inspection opportunity. Indiana does not have a separate state lead-paint statute, so agents rely on the federal framework. Failure to comply can trigger penalties of up to $19,507 per violation under the [Toxic Substances Control Act penalty schedule](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-based-paint-disclosure-rule).

Second, Indiana's Responsible Property Transfer Law (IC 13-25-3) applies to transfers of property where hazardous substances or petroleum have been stored, released, or disposed of. This statute primarily targets commercial and industrial sites, but mixed-use properties and residential parcels with documented contamination histories fall within its scope. When applicable, the seller must complete a Disclosure Document under IC 13-25-3-7 describing environmental conditions, regulatory actions, and remediation history.

| Disclosure Obligation | Governing Law | Form/Document | Applies To |
|---|---|---|---|
| Residential Sales Disclosure | IC 32-21-5 | State Form 46234 | 1-4 unit residential sales |
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | 42 U.S.C. 4852d / 24 CFR 35 | EPA Lead Disclosure Form | Pre-1978 residential |
| Responsible Property Transfer | IC 13-25-3 | Environmental Disclosure Document | Properties with known hazardous substance history |

Agents listing properties with known contamination or adjacent to Superfund sites should flag the Responsible Property Transfer Law early. Waiting until buyer due diligence surfaces the issue creates a negotiation disadvantage and potential rescission risk.

## Exemptions Every Indiana Agent Should Know Cold

IC 32-21-5-7 carves out specific transaction types from the seller's disclosure requirement. The most common exemptions include transfers by personal representatives or trustees of an estate, court-ordered sales (foreclosures, sheriff's sales), transfers between co-owners or spouses, sales of new construction where the buyer receives a warranty, and transfers by a relocation company.

**Exempt does not mean invisible.** Even when State Form 46234 is not required, the federal lead-paint disclosure still applies to pre-1978 properties regardless of exemption status. And agents still owe duties of honest dealing and material fact disclosure under Indiana's license law (IC 25-34.1-10-10). If an agent knows about a material defect in an exempt transaction, silence is not an option.

The exemption list does not include "as-is" sales between private parties. A seller who markets a property "as-is" still must complete and deliver the disclosure form. The as-is label affects the buyer's remedy expectations, not the seller's disclosure obligation. This distinction trips up agents regularly, as we see in comparable states like [Virginia](/blog/virginia-property-condition-disclosures-2026).

## What Happens When Agents or Sellers Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance in Indiana stack up across three channels: buyer remedies, Commission discipline, and civil liability.

Under IC 32-21-5-10, a buyer who never receives the required disclosure may rescind the purchase agreement at any time before closing. Post-closing, the buyer's remedy shifts to civil action for actual damages caused by the seller's knowing omission or misrepresentation. Indiana courts have upheld claims where sellers checked "no" on known defects, and juries have awarded repair costs, diminished value, and in some cases attorney fees.

For the agent, the Indiana Real Estate Commission (operating under the Indiana Professional Licensing Agency) can impose discipline ranging from a letter of reprimand to license revocation under 876 IAC 6-4-4. Failing to ensure disclosures are delivered, assisting a seller in concealing defects, or completing disclosure forms on behalf of a seller all constitute violations. Fines under IREC disciplinary proceedings vary, but suspensions of 30 to 90 days are common for first-time paperwork failures that result in buyer harm.

Brokers face vicarious liability under IC 25-34.1-10-10.5 when their agents violate disclosure requirements. E&O insurance may cover defense costs, but policy exclusions for known omissions or fraud leave brokers personally exposed.

| Consequence | Who Bears It | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-closing rescission | Seller (deal loss) | Failure to deliver State Form 46234 |
| Post-closing damages | Seller + potentially agent | Knowing misrepresentation or omission |
| IREC discipline | Agent | Failure to ensure delivery or assisting concealment |
| Vicarious liability | Broker | Agent's violation under broker's supervision |
| Federal penalty up to $19,507 | Seller + agent | Lead-paint disclosure violation |

## Common Mistakes Indiana Agents Make

**The first and most frequent error is allowing the disclosure form to be delivered after contract acceptance.** IC 32-21-5-2 states the form should be completed and signed before the buyer makes an offer. Agents who wait until the inspection period to collect the signed disclosure have created a rescission window that did not need to exist.

The second mistake is treating "unknown" as a safe harbor for sellers who clearly have knowledge. When a seller marks "unknown" on a question about basement water intrusion, but the agent has seen the sump pump and staining during the listing appointment, the agent has a duty to inquire further. Remaining silent in the face of obvious contradictions is not neutral—it is potentially actionable negligence.

Third, agents routinely forget to update the disclosure when conditions change between listing and closing. A pipe burst in February that gets repaired before the April closing still requires an amended disclosure. IC 32-21-5-4 requires the seller to amend the form if conditions change materially.

Fourth, listing agents fail to retain proof of delivery. Without a signed receipt or timestamp showing the buyer received the disclosure, the agent cannot defend against a rescission claim. Digital delivery with read receipts solves this, but only if the record is preserved in the transaction file. If you are [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), building delivery verification into your workflow is non-negotiable.

Fifth, agents conflate state disclosure requirements with MLS data entry. Entering square footage and room counts in the MLS does not satisfy IC 32-21-5. The statutory form is a standalone legal document, and no MLS field replaces it.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry direct supervisory responsibility under Indiana license law. A broker who discovers—or should have discovered—that an agent skipped disclosures cannot plead ignorance.

**Every brokerage operating in Indiana should conduct quarterly file audits specifically checking for three items: a signed and dated State Form 46234, a lead-paint disclosure in every pre-1978 transaction, and documented proof of delivery to the buyer.** Missing any one of these in a closed file is a ticking liability.

Brokers should also enforce a policy requiring agents to submit disclosure forms for review before the listing goes active. This catches errors in completion—blank sections, missing initials, contradictory answers—before they reach the buyer's side. The [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) concept applies here: systematic verification at each stage eliminates the gaps that create claims.

Training is the other enforcement lever. Annual continuing education covers disclosure obligations at a surface level, but brokers should run scenario-based workshops internally. What does an agent do when a seller refuses to disclose a known defect? When an environmental report surfaces contamination at a neighboring property? When a buyer's inspector finds evidence contradicting the seller's disclosure? These situations require judgment calls, and judgment improves with practice, not just reading statutes.

> "The owner or a person acting on behalf of the owner shall complete and sign a disclosure form... and shall deliver the disclosure form to the prospective buyer before an offer is made." — IC 32-21-5-2

## Staying Compliant Without Slowing Down

Disclosure compliance does not require agents to become environmental engineers or attorneys. It requires discipline in process: deliver early, verify receipt, retain records, and escalate ambiguity. The agents who get disciplined are almost never the ones who made honest mistakes—they are the ones who skipped steps because they were busy.
