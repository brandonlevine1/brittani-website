## Understanding Ohio Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

Ohio's housing stock is old. According to the U.S. Census Bureau, roughly 70 percent of the state's residential units were built before 1978—the year the federal government banned lead-based paint in housing. That means the majority of listings an Ohio agent handles in a given quarter will trigger **Ohio lead paint disclosure requirements 2026** obligations under both federal and state law.

The governing federal statute is the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992, codified at 42 U.S.C. sections 4851–4856 and enforced through EPA regulations at 40 CFR Part 745. Ohio layers additional enforcement authority through the Ohio Department of Health under Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Chapter 3742 and Ohio Administrative Code (OAC) 3701-32. Agents who treat this as a minor paperwork checkbox are underestimating the risk.

Failure rates on lead disclosure are higher than you might expect. Between incomplete forms, missing signatures, and agents who never hand over the EPA pamphlet, this remains one of the top compliance gaps that [brokers should audit during file reviews](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist).

## Which Properties Trigger the Requirement

Not every transaction demands a lead paint disclosure, but the exemptions are narrow. Any "target housing" built before 1978 triggers the obligation. Target housing means any residential dwelling except housing for the elderly or persons with disabilities (unless children under six reside there), zero-bedroom dwellings like studio apartments, and properties sold at foreclosure.

Short-term leases of 100 days or fewer are also exempt, but that rarely applies to a standard sale transaction. If you are listing a pre-1978 condo, single-family home, duplex, or multifamily building, the disclosure requirement applies regardless of whether the seller actually knows about lead hazards.

**A common misconception is that a property renovated after 1978 is exempt.** It is not. The exemption is based solely on original construction date, not renovation history. A gut-rehabbed 1920s Colonial still requires full disclosure.

## The Forms Ohio Agents Must Use

The primary form is the federal Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards, published by the EPA and HUD. This two-page document requires the seller to indicate whether they have knowledge of lead-based paint or hazards, disclose any available records or reports, and acknowledge that the buyer received the EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home."

In practice, most Ohio brokerages also attach Ohio Association of Realtors (OAR) Form 1028, which serves as a state-specific lead paint addendum. Form 1028 pairs with the federal form and includes signature lines for both the buyer and seller, confirmation of the 10-day inspection opportunity, and agent certification language required by 40 CFR 745.113.

| Form | Purpose | Who Signs | When in Transaction |
|------|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| EPA/HUD Federal Disclosure | Disclose known lead paint/hazards | Seller, Buyer, Both Agents | Before buyer is obligated |
| OAR Form 1028 | Ohio-specific addendum with inspection waiver/election | Seller, Buyer, Both Agents | Attached to purchase agreement |
| EPA Pamphlet (delivery confirmation) | Educational requirement | Buyer acknowledges receipt | Before or at contract signing |

Both agents—listing and buyer's—must sign the disclosure certifying they informed their client of their obligations. This is not optional. The regulation at 40 CFR 745.113(b)(3) explicitly names the agent as a responsible party.

## The 10-Day Inspection Window and How It Works

**Buyers must receive a minimum of 10 calendar days to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment before becoming obligated under the contract.** The parties can agree to a different time period, or the buyer can waive the opportunity entirely, but the right must be offered in writing.

In Ohio, this election typically appears on OAR Form 1028. The buyer checks one of two boxes: they either elect to conduct an inspection within the specified period, or they waive the right. If the buyer elects to inspect and discovers lead hazards, they can renegotiate or withdraw from the contract depending on the terms of the purchase agreement.

Agents sometimes assume the standard inspection contingency covers this requirement. It does not. The lead inspection opportunity is a separate, federally mandated right under 40 CFR 745.110(a). Even if the buyer waives their general inspection contingency, the lead inspection right still applies unless independently waived on the proper form.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The penalties for noncompliance are not hypothetical. The EPA can impose civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act). In a multi-unit building where the agent failed to provide disclosure to multiple buyers, each unit sale constitutes a separate violation.

Beyond federal fines, Ohio agents face these consequences:

The buyer can void the contract. Under 42 U.S.C. section 4852d(b)(4), a buyer who was not provided required disclosures may rescind the transaction. If the sale has already closed, the buyer can bring a civil action for damages, including treble damages—three times the actual harm suffered.

The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, housed under the Ohio Department of Commerce, can take disciplinary action against an agent's license for failure to comply with federal disclosure mandates. ORC 4735.18(A)(6) authorizes discipline for "any conduct of a character likely to mislead, deceive, or defraud," which regulators have applied to disclosure failures.

| Consequence | Source of Authority | Maximum Exposure |
|-------------|-------------------|------------------|
| Federal civil penalty | 40 CFR 745.118 | $19,507 per violation |
| Contract rescission | 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(4) | Full unwinding of sale |
| Treble damages (civil suit) | 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3) | 3x actual damages + costs |
| License discipline | ORC 4735.18(A)(6) | Suspension or revocation |
| Criminal penalties (knowing violations) | 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(5) | Up to $10,000 fine |

Brokers carry vicarious liability for their agents' failures. If a listing agent under your license consistently omits lead disclosures, the brokerage is exposed to both regulatory and civil claims. This makes compliance tracking a broker-level responsibility, not just an individual agent concern. Those managing [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) are particularly vulnerable to missed steps.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make

**Mistake 1: Delivering the pamphlet but not the disclosure form.** Handing the buyer the EPA's "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" brochure satisfies one half of the requirement. The seller's signed disclosure form—with specific representations about known hazards—is a separate document. Both are required.

**Mistake 2: Having only the buyer sign.** The federal regulation requires signatures from the seller, the buyer, and both agents. Files that are missing the listing agent's certification or the seller's signature on the disclosure form are incomplete and noncompliant.

**Mistake 3: Failing to attach known lead inspection reports.** ORC 3742.40 and the federal rule both require sellers to provide buyers with any available lead inspection reports, risk assessments, or abatement records. Sellers who have this documentation must hand it over—not merely disclose its existence. Agents who do not specifically ask sellers whether such records exist are exposing themselves to liability.

**Mistake 4: Using an outdated pamphlet version.** The EPA periodically updates its lead paint pamphlet. As of 2026, agents must provide the current version. Distributing a pamphlet from 2010 or earlier is technically noncompliant, even if the content is substantively similar.

**Mistake 5: Assuming new construction additions exempt the property.** A 1955 ranch with a 2015 addition still requires full lead paint disclosure for the entire property. The original construction date controls.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers have a supervisory obligation under ORC 4735.18(H) and should treat lead paint compliance with the same rigor they apply to agency disclosures. A quarterly file audit should verify three things: the disclosure form is signed by all four parties, the buyer's election or waiver of the 10-day inspection period is documented, and the EPA pamphlet delivery is confirmed in writing.

Build a simple checklist into your transaction management workflow. Flag every listing with an original construction date before 1978 at the intake stage—before marketing begins. If you rely on agents to self-report, you will miss files. The most effective brokerages automate this check at listing creation by pulling construction year from public records.

Training matters here. New agents often receive minimal education on lead paint obligations during pre-licensing coursework. A 15-minute compliance brief during onboarding—covering the specific forms, the 10-day rule, and the penalty structure—prevents the kind of errors that generate lawsuits years after closing. For brokerages [onboarding new agents](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines), lead paint compliance should be a non-negotiable module.

Retention of signed disclosure forms must follow federal guidelines: three years from the date of sale. Ohio brokerages should keep them longer as a defensive measure, given that lead exposure claims can surface years after a child is harmed.

## How Ohio Compares to Other States

Ohio follows federal baseline requirements without adding a separate state-mandated lead disclosure form—unlike states such as Illinois or Pennsylvania that layer additional state-specific forms on top of the federal requirement. However, Ohio's enforcement infrastructure through the Ohio Department of Health and local health departments (particularly in Cleveland, Columbus, and Cincinnati) is more aggressive than many peer states when it comes to enforcement actions against properties with known hazards.

If you work across state lines, be aware that [Pennsylvania's lead paint disclosure process](/blog/pennsylvania-lead-paint-disclosure-2026) includes additional state-level obligations that do not apply in Ohio. The federal form remains the backbone in both states, but the procedural details differ enough to matter.

Ohio's ORC 3742 also governs lead abatement contractor licensing and clearance testing—provisions that do not directly affect agents but become relevant when a buyer's lead inspection reveals hazards and the seller agrees to remediation as a condition of sale.

## Keeping Your Files Compliant Through the Rest of 2026

The simplest way to avoid violations is to treat lead paint disclosure as a mandatory step that triggers at listing intake, not at contract drafting. By the time a purchase agreement is being signed, the seller should have already completed the disclosure form, and the pamphlet should already be in the buyer's hands. Waiting until the offer stage compresses timelines and increases error rates.
