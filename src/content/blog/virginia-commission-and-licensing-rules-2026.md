## Understanding Virginia Commission & Licensing Rules 2026

The Virginia commission & licensing rules 2026 framework governs how every active licensee earns, discloses, and collects compensation in real estate transactions across the Commonwealth. These rules are enforced by the Virginia Real Estate Board (VREB) under the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), and violations carry penalties ranging from monetary fines to license revocation. If you hold an active Virginia license, the statutes codified in Title 54.1, Chapter 21 of the Code of Virginia dictate your obligations.

This year's regulatory environment reflects the post-settlement reality where commission transparency is not optional. Written buyer agency agreements are mandatory before property tours, and the way agents document compensation has shifted significantly from even two years ago. Brokers who fail to update their internal compliance systems are exposing their firms to liability that compounds with every closed transaction.

## The Statutory Framework: Where Virginia Law Stands

Virginia Code Section 54.1-2130 through 54.1-2135 establishes the legal foundation for agency relationships, including how compensation is structured and disclosed. Section 54.1-2131 specifically requires that any brokerage relationship be established through a written agreement before the licensee can represent a client. This is not a suggestion — it is a statutory prerequisite to earning a commission.

**The Virginia Real Estate Board has the authority to sanction any licensee who performs compensable acts without a written agreement in place.** Sanctions are governed by 18 VAC 135-20-10 et seq., which outlines the standards of conduct and practice for licensees. The Board can impose fines up to $2,500 per violation, require additional education, place a license on probation, or revoke it entirely.

Compensation itself must flow through the broker. Under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2106, a salesperson may not receive compensation directly from a consumer or another brokerage. All commission payments must pass through the agent's supervising broker. This rule remains unchanged, but its enforcement has tightened as DPOR audits have increased in frequency since mid-2025.

## Written Agreements and Required Forms

Virginia agents work with several mandatory forms when establishing compensation terms. The [buyer agency agreement requirements](/blog/virginia-buyer-agency-agreement-requirements-2026) in the Commonwealth specify that agents must use a written buyer representation agreement before conducting any showing or property tour activity.

The Virginia REALTORS association provides standardized forms including Form 460 (Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement) and Form 400 (Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement). These forms contain the specific fields where commission rates, flat fees, or alternative compensation structures must be documented. Agents working outside the Virginia REALTORS forms system must still meet the statutory minimums outlined in Section 54.1-2130.

| Form | Purpose | When Required | Key Commission Field |
|------|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| Form 460 | Exclusive Buyer Representation | Before first showing | Section 7: Compensation terms |
| Form 400 | Exclusive Right to Sell | Before marketing property | Section 6: Broker compensation |
| Form 440 | Non-Exclusive Buyer Agreement | Before first showing | Section 5: Fee arrangement |
| Form 401 | Exclusive Agency Listing | Before marketing property | Section 6: Commission structure |

Each form requires the compensation amount to be stated as either a percentage, a flat dollar figure, or a formula. Leaving the compensation field blank or writing "TBD" does not satisfy the statutory requirement for a written agreement.

## How Commission Disclosure Intersects with Agency Disclosure

Virginia's [agency disclosure requirements](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) directly impact commission conversations. Under Section 54.1-2138, agents must provide the "Disclosure of Brokerage Relationship" form at the earliest practical time. This disclosure must occur before any substantive discussion of specific property terms — including compensation.

**The agency disclosure form is distinct from the brokerage agreement, and agents frequently conflate the two.** The disclosure informs the consumer about the types of representation available. The brokerage agreement establishes the actual terms, including compensation. Both are required, and they serve different legal functions.

When working with unrepresented parties, agents must disclose their role and cannot negotiate commission terms with a party they do not represent. The [dual agency rules in Virginia](/blog/virginia-dual-agency-rules-2026) further complicate compensation because both sides' fees must be documented and consented to in writing before the dual agency arrangement commences.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Commission and Licensing Compliance

Mistakes in this area are not theoretical. DPOR disciplinary actions published quarterly reveal patterns that repeat across brokerages statewide. Here are five errors that appear most frequently in enforcement proceedings.

**First, agents perform showings before a buyer agency agreement is signed.** Post-settlement rules make this a clear violation. The written agreement must be executed before the agent provides brokerage services, which includes touring properties. DPOR considers this an unlicensed-activity-adjacent violation because the statutory prerequisite for compensable service has not been met.

Second, agents allow their licenses to lapse during an active transaction. Virginia licenses expire on a two-year cycle tied to the licensee's birth month. If a license expires before closing, the agent has performed compensable acts while technically unlicensed for the final portion of the transaction. DPOR can fine the agent and the broker separately for this oversight.

Third, agents verbally agree to commission splits with cooperating agents without documenting them. Virginia law requires all compensation to flow through the broker, and verbal side agreements between agents at different firms create liability exposure that neither broker has authorized. These informal arrangements violate Section 54.1-2106 and potentially constitute an unauthorized practice.

Fourth, agents fail to disclose who is paying their commission to the consumer. Virginia Code Section 54.1-2137 requires that clients and customers both understand the source of the agent's compensation. Omitting this disclosure — particularly in buyer transactions where the seller may be contributing to buyer-agent fees — creates grounds for a Board complaint.

Fifth, agents operating under a team name that has not been registered with DPOR as an assumed name. Under 18 VAC 135-20-180, any name used in advertising that is not the licensee's legal name or the brokerage's registered name must be filed with the Board. Unregistered team names on commission checks or agreements create documentation problems during audits.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers carry the statutory weight of compliance enforcement within their firms. Under Section 54.1-2103, the principal broker is responsible for the activities of every agent operating under their license. This is not passive oversight — it requires active auditing systems.

| Audit Area | Frequency | What to Check | Consequence of Failure |
|------------|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| Buyer agency agreements | Every transaction | Signed before first showing, compensation terms complete | Fine up to $2,500 per violation |
| License status | Monthly | Active status, CE completion, renewal dates | Broker and agent both sanctioned |
| Commission disbursement records | Quarterly | All payments through escrow, no side agreements | License suspension possible |
| Advertising compliance | Monthly | Team names registered, license numbers displayed | DPOR warning escalating to fine |
| Agency disclosure timing | Every transaction | Provided before substantive discussions | Transaction voidability risk |

Brokers should implement a file review checklist that flags missing documents before closing. The [why checklists beat memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) principle applies with particular force here because a single missing form can trigger Board action months after closing when a consumer files a complaint.

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance gaps are already exposed.** DPOR's investigative process typically begins with a consumer complaint, at which point the broker's entire file is subject to review. If the agency disclosure is missing, the buyer agreement is undated, or the commission terms are ambiguous, the broker shares in the liability regardless of whether they personally handled the transaction.

## Penalties and Liability: What Non-Compliance Actually Costs

The penalty structure under Virginia regulations is tiered but can stack quickly. A single transaction with multiple violations can generate separate sanctions for each deficiency. DPOR publishes its [disciplinary actions](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov/Boards/Real-Estate) publicly, and the reputational damage often exceeds the monetary fines.

First-offense violations for documentation failures typically result in fines between $500 and $1,000 plus mandatory continuing education. Repeat violations escalate to probation, mandatory supervision periods, or license suspension. Practicing without a license — including performing compensable acts during a lapsed period — carries fines up to $1,000 per violation under Section 54.1-111.

Beyond DPOR sanctions, civil liability exists independently. A buyer or seller who discovers their agent was unlicensed at any point during the transaction can seek rescission of the contract or damages. Commission disputes that reach court become significantly more expensive than the commission itself, particularly when attorneys' fees are factored in.

Transaction cancellation is also a real outcome. If a buyer discovers post-ratification that no valid buyer agency agreement existed at the time of the first showing, they may have grounds to void the agreement and any related obligations. This risk cascades — the listing agent's seller may then sue for lost time on market.
