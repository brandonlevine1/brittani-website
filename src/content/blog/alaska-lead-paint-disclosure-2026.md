## Alaska Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026: The Federal Rule That Bites Locally

Alaska does not have a standalone state statute imposing lead paint disclosure obligations beyond what federal law requires. That fact lulls many agents into complacency. The Alaska lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 are still governed primarily by the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X) and its implementing regulations at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H — and the penalties for non-compliance have only grown steeper.

Every residential property built before 1978 triggers this obligation, whether it sits on a hillside in Juneau or in a 1960s subdivision off Muldoon Road. The Alaska Real Estate Commission (AREC), housed under the Division of Corporations, Business and Professional Licensing, does not maintain a separate lead paint disclosure form. Instead, agents rely on the EPA's standard disclosure form and the pamphlet titled *Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home*. Getting this wrong does not just risk a federal fine — it can unwind a closed deal months after settlement.

## Who Must Disclose and When

**The disclosure duty falls on three parties simultaneously: the seller, the listing agent, and the buyer's agent.** Under [24 CFR 35.86](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-24/subtitle-A/part-35/subpart-H), each has a distinct responsibility. The seller must disclose known lead-based paint or hazards. The listing agent must ensure the seller completes the disclosure and that the buyer receives the federally mandated pamphlet. The buyer's agent must ensure the buyer receives the 10-day inspection opportunity and signs the acknowledgment.

This three-pronged structure means that a buyer's agent cannot simply assume the listing side handled it. If the transaction closes without proper documentation, all three parties share potential liability. AREC has historically treated disclosure failures as violations of AS 08.88.391, which requires licensees to conduct business with competence and in accordance with applicable law.

## The Forms Alaska Agents Actually Use

Alaska does not publish a state-specific lead paint disclosure form the way some states do. The standard practice is to use the EPA/HUD disclosure form, which most MLS purchase agreements incorporate as an addendum. The Alaska Association of Realtors (AAR) Purchase and Sale Agreement includes a lead paint disclosure section, but many brokerages also attach the standalone federal form for clarity and documentation purposes.

Here is how the form landscape breaks down for Alaska transactions:

| Document | Source | When Required | Who Signs |
|----------|--------|---------------|-----------|
| EPA Lead Paint Disclosure Form | EPA/HUD (24 CFR 35, Subpart H) | All pre-1978 residential sales | Seller, Buyer, Both Agents |
| *Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home* pamphlet | EPA | Must be delivered to buyer before obligation to purchase | Buyer acknowledges receipt |
| AAR Purchase and Sale Agreement (lead paint addendum section) | Alaska Association of Realtors | When using AAR forms for pre-1978 homes | All parties |
| Seller's Disclosure Statement (AS 34.70.010) | State of Alaska | All residential sales (covers known defects including lead paint knowledge) | Seller |

The Alaska Residential Real Property Transfer Disclosure Statement required under AS 34.70.010 asks sellers to disclose known material defects. If a seller knows about lead paint, that knowledge must appear both on the state disclosure and on the federal lead paint form. Omitting it from either creates a gap that plaintiffs' attorneys love to exploit.

## The 10-Day Inspection Window: Non-Negotiable Unless Waived in Writing

Buyers must receive a minimum 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment before becoming obligated under the contract. This requirement exists under 24 CFR 35.86(b). The parties can agree to a different time period, but the buyer must affirmatively waive the opportunity in writing if they choose not to inspect.

**Agents frequently confuse this with the general inspection contingency period.** They are not the same thing. Even if a purchase agreement sets a 7-day inspection contingency, the lead paint inspection opportunity must be separately documented as either exercised, waived, or extended. The waiver language must be explicit — a general inspection contingency waiver does not satisfy the federal requirement.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance in Alaska

The penalty structure for lead paint disclosure violations operates on multiple levels. Federal enforcement comes through HUD and the EPA. AREC disciplinary action comes through the state licensing board. And private civil liability comes through buyer lawsuits.

| Violation Type | Enforcing Body | Potential Consequence |
|----------------|---------------|---------------------|
| Failure to disclose known lead paint | HUD/EPA | Fine up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation) |
| Failure to provide pamphlet | HUD/EPA | Fine up to $19,507 per violation |
| Failure to provide 10-day inspection opportunity | HUD/EPA | Contract voidable by buyer |
| Pattern of violations | DOJ (criminal referral) | Up to $19,507 per violation plus treble damages in civil suit |
| License law violation (AS 08.88.391) | AREC | Reprimand, fine, suspension, or revocation |
| Buyer civil suit | Superior Court | Rescission, actual damages, attorney fees |

**The treble damages provision is what should keep agents up at night.** Under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3), a court may award the buyer three times the amount of damages if the violation was willful or the result of a failure to exercise due care. An agent who "forgot" to include the lead paint disclosure in a $400,000 transaction could face six-figure personal liability.

AREC has the authority under AS 08.88.071 to take disciplinary action against any licensee who violates a law related to real estate practice. A federal lead paint violation qualifies. Disciplinary outcomes range from a letter of reprimand and mandatory continuing education to license suspension or revocation, depending on the severity and whether prior violations exist.

## Common Mistakes Alaska Agents Make

After reviewing enforcement actions and transaction audit findings, five errors appear repeatedly in Alaska lead paint compliance failures.

**First, agents assume the property is exempt because it "looks new."** Renovated kitchens and fresh paint do not change a home's construction date. If the structure was originally built before 1978, the disclosure applies regardless of subsequent renovations. Only complete demolition and new construction on the same lot creates an exemption.

Second, agents attach the disclosure form but fail to get all four signatures. The federal form requires the seller's signature, the buyer's signature, the listing agent's signature, and the buyer's agent's signature. A missing agent signature does not technically void the disclosure, but it creates an evidentiary problem if the transaction is later challenged. AREC auditors flag incomplete signature blocks during brokerage reviews.

Third, listing agents accept a seller's claim of "no knowledge" at face value without documenting it properly. The form includes a checkbox for sellers to indicate they have no knowledge of lead-based paint. But if the seller previously received an inspection report identifying lead paint — even from a prior transaction — claiming "no knowledge" is fraudulent. Listing agents should ask pointed questions and document the conversation in their file notes.

Fourth, agents deliver the EPA pamphlet electronically without confirming receipt. HUD guidance permits electronic delivery, but the buyer must acknowledge receipt in writing. Sending a PDF via email without a signed acknowledgment form does not satisfy the requirement. Many agents operating in remote Alaska communities rely heavily on electronic document delivery, making this error particularly common.

Fifth, buyer's agents waive the 10-day inspection period on the buyer's behalf without explicit written authorization. The waiver must come from the buyer directly, not the agent. An agent checking the waiver box without buyer instruction creates both a federal compliance issue and a potential breach of fiduciary duty under AS 08.88.391.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Alaska carry responsibility under AS 08.88.395 for supervising their associated licensees. This means a brokerage's compliance failure on lead paint disclosure can land directly on the broker's license. Passive oversight is not a defense.

Brokers should build a lead paint compliance check into every pre-1978 transaction file review. The audit should confirm four elements: the signed disclosure form with all four signatures, the buyer's written acknowledgment of pamphlet receipt, documentation of the 10-day inspection opportunity (exercised or waived in writing by the buyer), and a note confirming the property's construction date was verified — not assumed.

Quarterly file audits catch problems before they become enforcement actions. AREC does not conduct routine brokerage audits on a fixed schedule, but complaints from buyers trigger investigations that can result in full file reviews across a brokerage's transaction history. One sloppy file opens the door to scrutiny of every file in the office.

Brokers should also ensure their agents complete continuing education on federal disclosure requirements. Alaska requires 20 hours of CE for license renewal under 12 AAC 64.410, and brokers have discretion to require specific topic coverage as an internal policy. Mandating at least one hour on lead paint and federal disclosure compliance every renewal cycle is a low-cost risk management strategy.
