## Understanding Kentucky Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026

Kentucky's disclosure framework sits at the intersection of consumer protection and transactional efficiency, and agents who treat it as a mere checkbox exercise are the ones who end up in front of the Kentucky Real Estate Commission (KREC). The rules governing Kentucky property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 haven't undergone dramatic statutory overhaul this cycle, but enforcement patterns and buyer expectations have shifted meaningfully. If your last compliance review was more than twelve months ago, you're overdue.

The foundational statute remains KRS 324.360, which mandates that sellers of residential real property (one-to-four dwelling units) provide a written disclosure of known material defects. This obligation isn't optional, and it isn't something you can let slide because a seller "doesn't want to deal with it." The Kentucky Association of Realtors (KAR) publishes the standardized Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition form—commonly referenced as Form 305—that satisfies the statutory requirement when completed accurately.

## The Core Statutory Framework: KRS 324.360 and Beyond

**KRS 324.360 establishes the legal backbone for property condition disclosures in the Commonwealth.** The statute requires sellers to disclose known defects affecting the physical condition of the property, including structural issues, water intrusion, environmental hazards, and defective systems. Agents must understand that "known" is the operative word—sellers are not required to conduct inspections, but they cannot conceal defects they are actually aware of.

The statute carves out several exemptions worth memorizing. Transfers resulting from foreclosure, transfers by a fiduciary administering an estate, court-ordered transfers, and transfers between co-owners or spouses incident to divorce are all exempt. New construction sold by a builder who provides a warranty is also excluded from the standard disclosure requirement, though environmental disclosures may still apply.

Beyond KRS 324.360, agents must account for federal mandates. The Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (42 U.S.C. 4852d) and its implementing regulation at 24 CFR Part 35 require disclosure of known lead-based paint hazards for any dwelling constructed before 1978. This federal layer operates independently of Kentucky's state disclosure statute and has its own specific forms, timelines, and penalties.

## Forms and Documents: What Goes in Every File

The KAR Form 305—Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition—is the primary instrument. It covers categories including roofing, basement and crawlspace conditions, plumbing, electrical systems, HVAC, environmental concerns (radon, asbestos, underground storage tanks), flood zone status, and property boundary issues. Agents should verify they're using the most current version of Form 305, as KAR periodically updates the language.

For lead-based paint, agents must use the EPA-approved disclosure form and the "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" pamphlet. The disclosure form must be signed by the seller, the buyer, and both agents. A ten-day inspection contingency period for lead testing is required unless the buyer waives it in writing.

| Document | Applicability | Responsible Party | Timing |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|--------|
| KAR Form 305 (Seller's Disclosure) | Residential 1-4 units | Seller completes; listing agent delivers | Before or at contract acceptance |
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | Pre-1978 homes | Seller discloses; both agents ensure compliance | Before contract ratification |
| Radon Disclosure (if known) | All residential | Seller discloses on Form 305 | With property condition disclosure |
| Flood Zone Notification | Properties in FEMA zones | Seller/agent identifies | Before contract acceptance |
| Underground Storage Tank Disclosure | Properties with known USTs | Seller | Before contract acceptance |

Environmental disclosures in Kentucky don't exist as a single standalone form, but they are embedded within Form 305's environmental section. Sellers must disclose known presence of radon, asbestos, mold, underground storage tanks, contaminated soil, or proximity to hazardous waste sites. Similar to how [Virginia handles its property condition disclosures](/blog/virginia-property-condition-disclosures-2026), Kentucky integrates environmental items into the broader property condition form rather than requiring separate filings.

## Liability and Consequences: What Happens When Agents Fail

**Noncompliance with Kentucky disclosure requirements triggers a cascade of consequences that can end careers.** Under KRS 324.160, the Kentucky Real Estate Commission has authority to impose disciplinary action for violations including failure to disclose material facts or failure to ensure proper documentation. Penalties range from formal reprimands to fines of up to $1,000 per violation, license suspension, and outright revocation.

Beyond regulatory discipline, agents face civil liability exposure. Buyers who discover undisclosed material defects post-closing can pursue claims for fraud, negligent misrepresentation, or breach of statutory duty. Kentucky courts have held that agents who knew or should have known of material defects—and failed to disclose—can be held jointly liable with the seller. The case law trend in the Commonwealth shows increasing willingness to pierce the "I didn't know" defense when red flags were visible during property showings.

Contract rescission is another real risk. Under KRS 324.360(3), if a seller refuses to provide the disclosure form, the buyer may terminate within a specified window. But even after closing, material misrepresentations can form the basis for rescission claims, putting the commission, the seller's proceeds, and the agent's errors and omissions coverage all at risk simultaneously.

| Consequence | Trigger | Authority |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| Fine up to $1,000 per violation | Failure to disclose or ensure disclosure | KREC under KRS 324.160 |
| License suspension/revocation | Pattern of noncompliance or fraud | KREC |
| Contract rescission | Material misrepresentation discovered | Circuit court |
| Civil damages | Buyer suffers loss from undisclosed defect | Circuit court |
| Federal penalty up to $19,507 per violation | Lead-based paint disclosure failure | EPA/HUD |

The federal lead-paint penalties deserve special attention. HUD and the EPA can impose civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation), and agents are independently liable—not just the seller. This is not a theoretical risk; enforcement actions do occur, particularly in markets with aging housing stock like many Kentucky communities.

## Common Mistakes Kentucky Agents Make

**The first and most frequent error is treating the disclosure as the seller's sole responsibility.** Yes, the seller completes Form 305. But KRS 324.360 and KREC's administrative regulations place an affirmative obligation on the licensee to ensure the form is provided. "My seller didn't fill it out" is not a defense that will hold at a disciplinary hearing. You must follow up, document your requests, and refuse to proceed without the completed form or a written refusal on file.

The second common mistake is ignoring environmental red flags during property tours. Kentucky has significant legacy coal, petroleum, and agricultural chemical contamination across the state. When a listing agent notices abandoned tanks, staining, unusual odors, or proximity to industrial sites and fails to inquire or disclose, they create personal liability. You are not an inspector—but you are expected to disclose conditions you observe that a reasonable buyer would want to know about.

Third, agents routinely botch the lead-based paint timeline. The ten-day inspection period is a federal requirement, not a suggestion. Agents who pressure buyers to waive this period without proper written documentation, or who fail to provide the EPA pamphlet, expose themselves to federal enforcement. The transaction coordination process here is identical to what we've outlined for [other states with robust lead paint requirements](/blog/west-virginia-lead-paint-disclosure-2026)—the forms must be signed by all parties before ratification.

Fourth, many agents confuse "as-is" sales with disclosure exemptions. Selling a property "as-is" does not eliminate the seller's or agent's obligation to disclose known material defects under Kentucky law. The "as-is" clause affects the buyer's ability to demand repairs, not the seller's duty to reveal what they know. This misunderstanding leads to preventable complaints.

Fifth, agents fail to update disclosures when conditions change between listing and closing. If a seller discovers a new defect—say, a basement leak during a heavy rain after the initial disclosure was signed—a supplemental disclosure must be provided. The original Form 305 is not a one-and-done document.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Kentucky bear supervisory liability under KRS 324.160(4), which means your agents' disclosure failures are your disciplinary exposure. A passive approach to compliance auditing is a ticking clock.

**Start by requiring file review at three critical checkpoints: listing agreement execution, contract ratification, and pre-closing.** At listing, verify that the seller has received a blank Form 305 and that your agent has documented delivery. At contract ratification, confirm the completed disclosure (or written refusal) is in the file alongside any applicable lead-paint forms. Pre-closing, check for supplemental disclosures and any amendments triggered by inspection findings.

Brokers should maintain a disclosure compliance checklist specific to Kentucky transactions. This checklist should flag properties built before 1978, properties in known flood zones, properties with well/septic systems, and properties in areas with documented environmental contamination. The [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) model applies here—structured workflows prevent the kind of oversight that leads to KREC complaints.

Audit frequency matters. Quarterly random file reviews are a minimum standard; monthly is better for brokerages with more than ten active agents. Document your audit process itself—KREC looks favorably on brokers who can demonstrate systematic compliance oversight when isolated agent errors occur.

The [Kentucky Real Estate Commission's website](https://krec.ky.gov) publishes disciplinary orders that reveal common audit failures. Review these quarterly to identify emerging enforcement patterns and adjust your internal training accordingly.

## Environmental Disclosures Specific to Kentucky's Geography

Kentucky's geological and industrial history creates disclosure obligations you won't find in every state. Underground coal mines exist beneath residential properties in eastern Kentucky, and sellers who know about subsidence risk or mine subsidence insurance claims must disclose. Former tobacco-processing and chemical manufacturing sites dot the Bluegrass and along the Ohio River corridor, creating potential soil and groundwater contamination.

Radon levels in Kentucky run higher than the national average in many counties. While Kentucky does not mandate radon testing, sellers who have test results must disclose them on Form 305. Agents representing buyers should strongly recommend testing and document that recommendation in writing. The Kentucky Department for Environmental Protection maintains county-level radon data that agents can reference.

Karst topography—sinkholes—affects significant portions of central Kentucky. Sellers aware of sinkhole activity, springs, or drainage issues related to karst geology must disclose. This is an area where "I didn't know" claims frequently fail, particularly when the property's topography makes karst features visually obvious.
