## Who Actually Has to Sit at the Closing Table in Ohio?

The question of Ohio attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 lands on agent desks every week, and the answer still trips up experienced licensees. Ohio is not an "attorney state" in the way that New York, Georgia, or South Carolina are. No single Ohio statute mandates that a lawyer physically attend every residential closing, which means both attorneys and title companies can legally conduct settlements depending on the county, the lender, and the purchase contract language.

That ambiguity is exactly where agents get into trouble. Knowing who must be present—and who must not overstep—protects your license, your client, and your commission check.

## Ohio's Statutory Framework for Closings

Ohio Revised Code Section 3953 governs title insurance agents and agencies. The Ohio Department of Insurance regulates title companies, while the Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing (under the Department of Commerce) oversees agents and brokers. The Ohio Supreme Court's Board on the Unauthorized Practice of Law adds a third layer: it decides where title work ends and legal practice begins.

**ORC 4735.18 spells out the conduct that can cost an agent their license**, including offering opinions on legal rights or drafting legal instruments. The 2004 Ohio Supreme Court case *Cleveland Bar Ass'n v. CompManagement, Inc.* reinforced that completing certain legal documents without a law license constitutes unauthorized practice of law—even when the person doing it works at a title company.

In practice, most Ohio residential closings are handled by title companies acting as settlement agents. The lender's closing instructions typically name the title company, and the Ohio Association of Realtors (OAR) purchase contract forms reference title and escrow services—not attorney services—as the default.

Ohio falls firmly in the title-company column, but individual transactions can shift toward attorney involvement when the deal includes complex easements, estate sales, probate transfers, or 1031 exchanges. Some Ohio counties—particularly in the northeast—still follow a tradition of attorney-conducted closings even though no statute requires it.

## The OAR Purchase Contract and Attorney-Review Clauses

The standard Ohio Association of Realtors Residential Purchase Agreement (Form 101, revised 2025) includes an optional attorney-review contingency in the addenda section. When the parties check that box, either side can have their attorney review—and potentially void—the contract within a specified number of days after acceptance.

If that contingency is not selected, no attorney review period exists unless the parties negotiate one in the additional-terms section. Agents sometimes forget this detail and tell buyers they can "always have a lawyer look at it later." That statement, while technically true, misleads the buyer into thinking they have a contractual right to terminate based on attorney feedback—which they do not, absent the contingency.

**Form 200 (Counter Offer) and Form 800 (Addendum) are the vehicles agents should use to add or modify an attorney-review clause after initial offer submission.** Never hand-write legal contingency language on the margins of a printed contract; the Ohio Division of Real Estate has disciplined licensees for unauthorized drafting when improvised language later creates ambiguity.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make at or Before Closing

The first mistake is telling a buyer or seller whether they "need" an attorney. Framing legal representation as necessary or unnecessary is itself a legal opinion. The correct approach: inform clients that they have the right to consult an attorney and recommend they do so if they have legal questions. Full stop.

The second mistake is preparing or modifying deed language. Some agents operating in rural counties have been known to fill in legal description blanks on quit-claim or warranty deed templates. ORC 4735.18(A)(21) prohibits agents from engaging in conduct that constitutes the unauthorized practice of law, and deed drafting qualifies. If discovered, the Division can suspend or revoke a license, and the closing itself may face a title challenge later.

The third mistake is assuming the title company will catch every compliance issue. Title companies verify title, resolve liens, and issue insurance—they do not audit the real estate agent's disclosure obligations. If you miss a required lead-paint disclosure or agency disclosure form, the title company has no duty to flag it, and you bear the liability alone. Agents who rely on the title closer as a backstop often learn this [where deals actually break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down).

The fourth mistake involves wire fraud silence. Ohio has seen a sharp increase in closing-related wire fraud. Agents who fail to warn clients about verifying wire instructions—or worse, who email wiring details in the clear—may face negligence claims. The Ohio Division of Real Estate has issued guidance urging licensees to follow written protocols for [wire instructions verification](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process).

The fifth mistake is disappearing after the contract is signed. Without an attorney at the table, agents become the primary point of contact for timeline questions, document requests, and contingency deadlines. Missing a deadline because you lost track of the file doesn't just kill one deal; it exposes you to the kind of liability examined in [the true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

**Brokers carry vicarious liability under ORC 4735.18(H) for the acts of their affiliated agents.** That means a brokerage can lose its license—or face civil suits—if an agent under its roof routinely crosses into unauthorized practice of law territory or fails to meet disclosure requirements.

Broker audit points should include verifying that every closed file contains the correct OAR disclosure forms (Form 305 for agency disclosure, Form 800-series addenda as applicable), confirming that the attorney-review contingency was either properly selected or intentionally omitted, and ensuring no agent-drafted legal language appears in any contract addendum. Quarterly file reviews are the industry standard, but monthly spot-checks reduce the window of exposure.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Consequence of Failure |
|-------------------|---------------|------------------------|
| Agency Disclosure (Form 305) | Signed by all parties before showing property | Fine up to $2,500 per violation; license suspension |
| Attorney-Review Contingency | Properly marked or intentionally omitted | Potential contract rescission; client lawsuit |

| Wire Fraud Protocol | Written verification process documented | Negligence liability; E&O claim |
| Closing Timeline Tracking | Contingency dates met and documented | Deal cancellation; breach of fiduciary duty claim |

Brokers should also confirm that agents are not making representations about whether an attorney or title company "should" be at closing. The brokerage policy manual needs clear language: agents inform clients of their options, period. The [Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing](https://com.ohio.gov/divisions-and-programs/real-estate-and-professional-licensing) publishes its enforcement actions publicly, and brokerage names appear alongside individual licensees in those records.

## When an Attorney Should Be at the Ohio Closing Table

Even though Ohio law does not require attorney presence, several transaction types practically demand it. Probate sales under ORC Chapter 2127 involve court-ordered procedures that a title company cannot oversee. Short sales with deficiency judgment exposure require legal counsel to protect the seller. Commercial transactions with environmental indemnities, zoning variances, or complex entity structures also exceed what a title settlement agent is authorized or competent to handle.

Agents who recognize these triggers and recommend attorney involvement—without offering a legal opinion on the substance—demonstrate the kind of fiduciary care that prevents post-closing complaints. The line is thin but clear: you can say "transactions like this often involve attorneys" without saying "you legally need one."
