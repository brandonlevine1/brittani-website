## Why Texas Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026 Demand Your Attention

Texas property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 carry more weight than many agents realize, particularly because the state's disclosure framework puts significant liability on both sellers and the licensees who guide them. A missed checkbox or a late delivery can unwind a deal, trigger a TREC complaint, or land an agent in civil court. The rules have not changed dramatically from prior years, but enforcement patterns and buyer-side awareness have sharpened—making sloppy compliance a bigger risk than ever.

The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) and the Texas Association of Realtors (TAR) maintain the primary forms agents use. Understanding which form applies to which transaction type—and when the environmental overlay kicks in—separates competent practitioners from those courting a disciplinary action.

## The Statutory Foundation: Section 5.008 and Beyond

The Seller's Disclosure Notice requirement lives in Section 5.008 of the Texas Property Code. This statute mandates that sellers of residential property (one-to-four units) deliver a written notice disclosing all known material conditions affecting the physical state of the property. The obligation is personal to the seller, but agents bear a professional duty to confirm delivery and accuracy.

**Section 5.008 does not require sellers to investigate unknown defects—but it does require honest reporting of everything they know.** This distinction matters because agents sometimes coach sellers to leave fields blank "just in case," which can backfire spectacularly if evidence of knowledge surfaces later. The statute's exemptions cover foreclosures, transfers pursuant to court orders, transfers by fiduciaries (executors, trustees), and several other categories outlined in Section 5.008(e).

Environmental disclosures layer on top of the property condition notice. Federal law under 42 U.S.C. 4852d (the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act) requires disclosure for homes built before 1978. Texas also requires sellers to disclose known presence of hazardous materials, including asbestos, radon, and underground storage tanks, through the Seller's Disclosure Notice itself.

## Forms Every Texas Agent Must Know

Two primary disclosure forms circulate in Texas transactions. TREC's OP-H is the commission-promulgated Seller's Disclosure Notice, while TAR Form 1406 is the association's expanded version used by TAR members. Both satisfy Section 5.008, but TAR 1406 includes additional environmental questions and more granular condition categories.

| Form | Source | Scope | When Required |
|------|--------|-------|---------------|
| TREC OP-H | Texas Real Estate Commission | Basic property condition disclosure | All residential sales (unless exempt) |
| TAR 1406 | Texas Association of Realtors | Expanded property condition and environmental disclosure | TAR member transactions (optional but recommended) |
| EPA Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | U.S. EPA / HUD | Lead paint hazards in pre-1978 homes | Federal mandate on all pre-1978 residential sales |
| TAR 1506 | Texas Association of Realtors | MUD (Municipal Utility District) notice | Properties within a MUD |
| TREC OP-M | Texas Real Estate Commission | Notice of obligation to pay improvement district assessment | Properties in PIDs |

Agents listing properties in Municipal Utility Districts must also deliver the MUD notice (TAR 1506) at or before contract execution. Similarly, properties within Public Improvement Districts require TREC's OP-M notice. Missing these supplemental disclosures is one of the fastest ways to give a buyer a contractual exit after option period expiration.

## Environmental Disclosures: What Triggers Them

**Environmental disclosure obligations in Texas arise from both federal mandates and state-level property code provisions.** The lead-based paint disclosure is the most common environmental form agents handle, but it is far from the only one. The Seller's Disclosure Notice (both TREC OP-H and TAR 1406) includes specific questions about previous or current environmental hazards on the property.

Key environmental conditions sellers must disclose if known include: lead-based paint or lead hazards, asbestos-containing materials, underground storage tanks (active or abandoned), contamination from nearby industrial sites, radon gas testing results, and the presence of Chinese drywall. The Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ) maintains public records of contaminated sites, and while sellers are not required to run a TCEQ search, agents who advise buyers should be aware of the [TCEQ's online contaminated sites database](https://www.tceq.texas.gov/remediation/superfund/sites).

Proximity to a Superfund site or a state voluntary cleanup site does not automatically trigger a statutory disclosure duty beyond what the seller already knows. However, failing to disclose known proximity—especially if it appeared in a prior inspection or appraisal—can constitute fraud.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Non-compliance consequences fall into three buckets: regulatory discipline, civil liability, and deal cancellation.

TREC can sanction licensees who fail to ensure proper delivery of required disclosures. Sanctions range from formal reprimands and mandatory continuing education to suspension or revocation of a license. Administrative penalties under the Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.701 can reach $5,000 per violation per day.

Civil liability is often more expensive than TREC fines. A buyer who discovers an undisclosed material defect post-closing can sue both the seller and the agents involved for damages, including repair costs, diminished value, and attorney fees. Texas courts have consistently held that agents who knew or should have known about a condition—and failed to ensure disclosure—share liability with the seller.

Deal cancellation is the third risk. Under the TREC One to Four Family Residential Contract (Promulgated Form 20-16), failure to timely deliver the Seller's Disclosure Notice allows the buyer to terminate. This termination right persists until the notice is delivered, meaning a forgetful listing agent can hand a buyer an escape hatch weeks into a transaction.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Texas Disclosures

The first mistake is treating blank fields as safe. When a seller leaves a disclosure question unanswered, many agents file the form as-is rather than pressing for a definitive "yes," "no," or "unknown." Blank fields invite buyer suspicion and provide no legal shelter. TREC and courts interpret omissions unfavorably when the seller later claims ignorance.

The second mistake is delivering the Seller's Disclosure Notice after contract execution without documenting the buyer's receipt date. Section 5.008(c) gives the buyer seven days after receiving the notice to terminate if it arrives post-contract. Agents who cannot prove when the buyer received the form lose the ability to enforce that deadline.

The third mistake is ignoring supplemental disclosures for MUDs and PIDs. Agents listing in master-planned communities outside city limits frequently forget the MUD notice (TAR 1506). This is not a minor oversight—it gives the buyer termination rights and exposes the seller to damages for failure to comply with Section 49.452 of the Texas Water Code.

The fourth mistake is conflating the Seller's Disclosure Notice with an inspection report. Some agents tell sellers they do not need to complete the disclosure because "the buyer is getting an inspection anyway." The two serve entirely different legal functions, and one does not excuse the other.

The fifth mistake is failing to update the disclosure when new information arises between listing and closing. If a seller discovers a foundation crack during the option period, the disclosure must be amended. Agents who do not prompt updated disclosures are exposed to claims of concealment.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

**Brokers carry supervisory liability under Section 1101.803 of the Texas Occupations Code, meaning an agent's disclosure failure can become the broker's problem.** A brokerage compliance audit should verify several specific items in every residential file before closing.

First, confirm that the correct form version is in the file. TREC updates promulgated forms periodically, and using an outdated OP-H can create enforceability questions. Second, verify that the Seller's Disclosure Notice bears the seller's signature and a date, and that the buyer's acknowledged receipt is documented with a separate date.

Third, check that supplemental disclosures (MUD, PID, lead-based paint) are present where applicable. A simple checklist tied to property location and construction year catches most gaps. Fourth, review any amendments to the disclosure. If the inspection revealed conditions the seller previously marked "unknown," the file should contain an updated notice.

| Audit Item | What to Verify | Red Flag |
|------------|---------------|----------|
| Seller's Disclosure Notice | Correct form, signed, dated, all fields complete | Blank fields or missing signature |
| Buyer's receipt acknowledgment | Separate date documented | No receipt date or same date as contract |
| Lead-based paint disclosure | Present for pre-1978 homes, signed by all parties | Missing entirely or unsigned |
| MUD / PID notices | Present for applicable properties | Property in MUD/PID with no supplemental notice |
| Disclosure amendments | Updated if new conditions discovered | Inspection report reveals issue not on original disclosure |

Brokers who skip these audits are gambling with their own license. The cost of a compliance review is negligible compared to defending a TREC complaint or a civil suit alleging supervisory negligence.
