## The Question of Virginia Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026

Every Virginia residential transaction ends at the settlement table, and every agent sitting there should know exactly which line item belongs to which party before the Closing Disclosure hits anyone's inbox. The question of Virginia who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is not merely academic — it dictates contract language, negotiation strategy, and compliance risk. Getting it wrong exposes you to board complaints, deal collapse, and personal liability.

Virginia is an attorney-settlement state. Unlike states where title companies run the show, a licensed settlement agent (often an attorney) conducts closing under the Virginia Consumer Real Estate Settlement Protection Act (CRESPA), codified at Virginia Code Sections 6.2-1400 through 6.2-1415. That structure changes the fee landscape and adds a layer of oversight that agents in other states may not face.

## Statutory Framework Governing Cost Allocation

**Virginia law does not mandate a fixed allocation of closing costs between buyer and seller.** The split is governed primarily by the purchase contract and secondarily by local custom. However, certain statutory charges have a default payor built into the code itself.

The grantor's tax under Virginia Code Section 58.1-801 defaults to the seller at a rate of $1.00 per $500 of sale price (effectively 0.2 percent). The congestion relief fee adds another $0.15 per $500 in Northern Virginia localities covered under Section 58.1-802.1. Regional taxes in Hampton Roads and Northern Virginia tack on an additional $0.15 per $500 under Section 58.1-802.

On the buyer side, the recordation tax under Section 58.1-803 applies at $0.25 per $100 of the deed's consideration plus the amount of any liens. These defaults apply unless the contract explicitly shifts responsibility, which is permissible and common in competitive markets.

## Standard Cost Split: Virginia Custom vs. Contract Terms

Local custom in most Virginia markets follows a recognizable pattern, but custom is not law. Agents must confirm every allocation in writing through the purchase agreement.

| Cost Item | Typical Payor | Statutory Reference |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| Grantor's tax | Seller | Va. Code 58.1-801 |
| Regional congestion tax | Seller | Va. Code 58.1-802.1 |
| Recordation tax | Buyer | Va. Code 58.1-803 |
| Owner's title insurance | Buyer | Contract/Custom |
| Lender's title insurance | Buyer | Lender requirement |
| Settlement agent fee | Split or Buyer | Contract/Custom |
| Deed preparation | Seller | Contract/Custom |
| HOA disclosure packet | Seller | Va. Code 55.1-1990 |
| Pest inspection (WDI) | Negotiable | Contract term |

The Virginia REALTORS Form 600 (Residential Contract of Purchase) addresses settlement cost allocation primarily in Paragraph 11 (Settlement) and Paragraph 12 (Costs). Agents fill in specific dollar amounts or percentage caps for seller-paid closing cost contributions in Paragraph 6 (Price and Terms). These fields are not optional decorations — they are the contractual basis for the settlement agent's disbursement instructions.

## How Forms 600 and 700 Handle the Allocation

Form 600 Paragraph 12 enumerates which party pays for specific items including the owner's title policy, lender's policy, settlement fees, recording charges, and transfer taxes. If you leave a blank unfilled, the settlement agent will default to local custom or call you for clarification — neither of which is ideal at the eleventh hour.

For new construction transactions, Virginia REALTORS Form 700 (New Home Contract) modifies several default cost assumptions because builders often negotiate blanket arrangements with affiliated settlement companies. Agents representing buyers in these deals must read the builder's addendum line by line; some shift the grantor's tax to the buyer or require the buyer to pay both settlement agent fees.

**The Virginia Real Estate Board, operating under the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), holds agents accountable for ensuring clients understand their financial obligations before contract ratification.** Regulation 18 VAC 135-20-180 requires licensees to present contracts in a manner that allows informed consent. An agent who glosses over cost allocation language risks a complaint under this regulation.

## What Happens When Agents Get It Wrong

Liability in Virginia closing cost disputes falls into three buckets: regulatory, contractual, and civil.

On the regulatory side, the [Virginia Real Estate Board](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov/Boards/Real-Estate) can sanction agents under 18 VAC 135-20-300 for improper conduct including misrepresentation of material facts. Penalties range from a reprimand to a $2,500 fine per violation, mandatory continuing education, probation, or license suspension. A pattern of closing cost misrepresentations can escalate to revocation.

Contractually, a miscommunicated cost allocation can trigger a default. If a buyer arrives at closing expecting $5,000 in seller-paid costs that the contract doesn't actually provide, the deal may die on the spot. The non-defaulting party can claim the earnest money deposit and, depending on contract language, pursue actual damages. Agents who verbally promised cost coverage without memorializing it in the contract face the worst of both worlds — an angry client and zero contractual protection.

Civil liability attaches when an agent's negligence or misrepresentation causes quantifiable financial harm. Virginia recognizes causes of action for negligent misrepresentation, and the measure of damages is typically the difference between what the client expected to pay and what they actually owed. Errors and omissions insurance covers many of these claims, but deductibles and premium increases make prevention far cheaper than correction. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) is critical to avoiding these last-minute explosions.

## Five Common Mistakes Virginia Agents Make on Closing Costs

**First, agents routinely quote "standard" closing costs verbally without referencing the actual contract language.** Virginia custom varies between Northern Virginia, Hampton Roads, the Richmond metro, and rural markets. What counts as "normal" in Fairfax County may surprise a buyer in Bedford County.

Second, agents forget that the HOA disclosure packet fee — required under the Virginia Property Owners' Association Act (Va. Code 55.1-1990) or the Condominium Act (Va. Code 55.1-1900 et seq.) — can range from $150 to $500 or more. The seller pays for this packet, but agents sometimes fail to warn sellers of the cost or to account for it in net sheet calculations.

Third, agents conflate seller concessions with seller-paid closing costs. A seller concession is a contractual agreement for the seller to contribute toward the buyer's costs, capped by the buyer's lender guidelines. Writing "seller pays all closing costs" without specifying a dollar cap or referencing lender limits creates ambiguity that settlement agents cannot resolve without amendment.

Fourth, agents neglect to update cost estimates when contract amendments change the settlement date, lender, or loan product. A shift from conventional to VA financing, for example, limits seller concessions to 4 percent of the sale price and prohibits the buyer from paying certain fees — fundamentally altering the cost allocation.

Fifth, listing agents fail to review the preliminary Closing Disclosure against the contract terms before the closing date. The three-business-day delivery rule under TRID means errors discovered late require a new disclosure and a delayed closing. Tracking these timelines manually is where [agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) most often.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2130.1 and DPOR regulation 18 VAC 135-20-300(5). If an agent under your license consistently misallocates closing costs or fails to document cost agreements properly, the board can hold you jointly responsible.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Frequency |
|-------------|--------------|-----------|
| Form 600/700 Paragraph 12 completeness | Every blank filled, no TBD entries | Every ratified contract |
| Seller net sheet accuracy | Grantor's tax, HOA packet, deed prep included | Listing intake and price changes |
| Buyer cost estimate alignment | Matches loan estimate and contract terms | Pre-offer and under contract |
| Amendment documentation | Any verbal cost agreement reduced to writing | Within 24 hours of agreement |
| CD review against contract | Line items match Paragraph 12 allocations | Three days before closing |

Brokers should institute a file review protocol that catches unsigned addenda, missing cost disclosures, and discrepancies between the buyer's Loan Estimate and the contract's cost terms. The [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) is a good starting framework for building that internal audit into your workflow.

Training is not a one-time event. Virginia requires 16 hours of continuing education per license renewal cycle (every two years), including required courses on ethics and fair housing. But closing cost compliance falls into the elective bucket, meaning many agents never get formal instruction on it after pre-licensing. Brokers who make cost allocation review part of their [new agent training timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) reduce compliance risk before it compounds.

## VA, FHA, and Conventional Loan Variations That Change the Split

Loan product rules overlay state law and contract terms, creating a third layer of cost allocation that agents must track. VA loans prohibit buyers from paying certain "non-allowable" fees including settlement agent charges, attorney fees, and notary fees — those costs must fall on the seller or be wrapped into the loan. Seller concessions on VA loans cap at 4 percent of the sale price.

FHA loans permit sellers to contribute up to 6 percent of the sale price toward buyer closing costs. Conventional loans set the cap based on the buyer's down payment: 3 percent for down payments under 10 percent, 6 percent for 10-25 percent down, and 9 percent above 25 percent down.

Agents who write contracts with seller concession amounts exceeding these caps create an immediate amendment situation. The settlement agent will flag it, the lender will reject it, and the closing will stall. The fix is simple: know your buyer's loan product before drafting the offer and cap concessions accordingly.

## The 2026 Adjustment: What Changed This Year

The Virginia General Assembly's 2025 session produced HB 1523, which adjusted the congestion relief fee calculation for certain Northern Virginia localities effective January 1, 2026. The rate increased from $0.15 to $0.20 per $500 in jurisdictions within Planning District 8. This seemingly minor change adds roughly $120 to the seller's cost on a $600,000 sale.

Additionally, DPOR updated its guidance on electronic settlement disclosures, confirming that settlement agents may deliver Closing Disclosures via secure electronic portals under the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (Va. Code 59.1-479 et seq.). This does not change who pays what, but it shortens the timeline for agents to review and dispute line items.
