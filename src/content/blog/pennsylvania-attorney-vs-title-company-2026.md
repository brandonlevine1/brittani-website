## Who Actually Needs to Be at Settlement in Pennsylvania?

The question of Pennsylvania attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 confuses even experienced agents because the answer sits in a gray zone between statute, bar association opinion, and local custom. Pennsylvania is not a pure "attorney state" like New York or Massachusetts, nor is it a straightforward "title company state" like California. The result is a patchwork of requirements that varies by county, lender, and transaction type.

Agents who guess wrong expose themselves and their brokers to unauthorized practice of law complaints, deal delays, and potential E&O claims. This post breaks down exactly what the law says, what forms apply, and where agents most commonly stumble.

## What Pennsylvania Law Actually Requires

**Pennsylvania has no statute that explicitly mandates attorney representation at closing for buyers or sellers.** The state's Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. Section 455.101 et seq., governs agent conduct but does not address who must conduct settlement. The Pennsylvania Consolidated Statutes Title 42, Section 2524, addresses unauthorized practice of law — and that is where the friction lives.

The Pennsylvania Supreme Court, not the legislature, retains exclusive authority to define the practice of law. In the landmark case Dauphin County Bar Association v. Mazzacaro (1978), the court held that preparing deeds, mortgages, and conducting title searches constitutes the practice of law when performed by non-attorneys for others. This opinion has never been overruled.

The practical result is a two-track system. Title companies may conduct settlements and issue title insurance, but the underlying title examination and legal document preparation must be performed by or under the supervision of a licensed Pennsylvania attorney. The [Pennsylvania Bar Association's Unauthorized Practice of Law Committee](https://www.pabar.org/site/For-Lawyers/Committees-Commissions/Unauthorized-Practice-of-Law-Committee) has issued multiple formal opinions reinforcing this boundary.

## Title Company vs. Attorney: A Side-by-Side Comparison

| Function | Title Company (Settlement Agent) | Pennsylvania Attorney |
|----------|----------------------------------|----------------------|
| Conduct settlement / manage signing | Yes | Yes |
| Issue title insurance policy | Yes (as licensed agent of underwriter) | Yes (if also a title agent) |
| Perform title search / examination | Only under attorney supervision per Mazzacaro | Yes |
| Draft or modify deed language | No — considered practice of law | Yes |
| Prepare HUD-1 or TRID Closing Disclosure | Yes (ministerial function) | Yes |
| Advise parties on legal rights | No | Yes |
| Record documents with county recorder | Yes | Yes |

This distinction matters because many agents assume that hiring a title company eliminates the need for any attorney involvement. It does not. The title company's underwriting counsel or a supervising attorney must still sign off on the title examination and approve document language.

## Key Pennsylvania Forms Agents Must Know

The Pennsylvania Association of Realtors (PAR) publishes the standard forms most agents use. Several directly affect the closing process and the attorney-versus-title-company question.

PAR Form ASR (Agreement of Sale — Residential) contains Section 24, which addresses settlement. This section identifies who will serve as the settlement agent and provides space to designate either a title company or an attorney's office. Agents who leave this blank create ambiguity that regularly causes last-minute scrambles.

PAR Form SPD (Seller's Property Disclosure Statement) must be delivered before closing, and any material changes require an amendment. PAR Form AOS (Assignment of Settlement) handles changes when the originally designated settlement agent is replaced. Agents who switch settlement providers mid-transaction without executing this form risk breaching the agreement.

**If you regularly handle transactions where the settlement provider changes, track those amendments the same way you would track [any other deal milestone that can derail a closing](/blog/where-deals-break-down).** Missing a single form swap has killed deals in the final week.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make at Pennsylvania Closings

The following errors appear repeatedly in disciplinary actions reviewed by the Pennsylvania State Real Estate Commission (SREC) and in E&O claims data.

**First, agents draft or modify deed language themselves.** Even changing a name spelling on a deed prepared by a title company is considered document modification. Under 63 P.S. Section 455.604, agents practicing law face license suspension and civil liability. SREC has issued formal warnings on this exact point.

Second, agents tell clients they do not need an attorney when the client asks. Agents are prohibited from giving legal advice under RELRA. The proper response is to recommend the client consult with a licensed attorney — full stop. Saying "you don't need one" is itself a legal opinion.

Third, agents fail to verify that the title company's examination was supervised by a Pennsylvania-licensed attorney. If the title company uses an out-of-state abstractor without local attorney review, the title commitment may be challenged. The agent who recommended that company may face an E&O claim for negligent referral.

Fourth, agents schedule closings at locations that lack proper notarization. Pennsylvania's Revised Uniform Law on Notarial Acts (57 Pa.C.S. Chapter 3) requires specific notary procedures. A settlement without a properly commissioned Pennsylvania notary present renders recorded documents defective.

Fifth, agents do not confirm that the settlement agent has proper licensing under the Pennsylvania Insurance Department. Title companies must hold a title insurance agent license. An unlicensed settlement agent conducting closing exposes every party — including the referring agent — to regulatory and civil action.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability under RELRA Section 604(a)(12) for the acts of their affiliated licensees. A single agent conducting unauthorized practice of law or recommending an unlicensed settlement provider can trigger fines up to $10,000 per violation and potential brokerage license revocation.

## Liability When Things Go Wrong

The financial exposure for non-compliance is not theoretical. SREC has the authority under 63 P.S. Section 455.604 to impose fines, require continuing education, suspend licenses, or revoke them entirely. Civil liability compounds the regulatory risk.

If a buyer discovers a title defect post-closing because no attorney supervised the title examination, the chain of liability runs from the title company to the supervising attorney (or lack thereof) and potentially to the agent who selected and recommended that provider. Pennsylvania courts have allowed negligent referral claims to proceed when agents recommended settlement providers without verifying their compliance infrastructure.

Deal cancellation is the more immediate risk. Lenders increasingly require confirmation that a Pennsylvania-licensed attorney has reviewed title before they fund. If a closing is scheduled and the lender's counsel rejects the title work at the eleventh hour, everyone — buyer, seller, agents on both sides — absorbs the cost of delay. The agent who set up the closing bears reputational damage even when the legal fault lies elsewhere. Understanding [the true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) should motivate every agent to verify settlement-agent credentials early rather than hoping it works out at the table.

## How 2026 Changes Affect the Attorney-Title Company Dynamic

The Pennsylvania legislature has not passed new statutes in 2026 that alter the fundamental attorney-supervision requirement. However, SREC's updated guidance memoranda issued in early 2026 emphasize that remote online notarization (RON) closings — increasingly common post-pandemic — still require Pennsylvania-licensed notaries and do not eliminate the attorney-supervision requirement for title examination.

Additionally, the CFPB's continued enforcement of TRID rules means that Closing Disclosure accuracy falls on whoever prepares it. If a title company prepares the CD without proper attorney review of underlying figures (transfer taxes, recording fees specific to Pennsylvania counties), errors trigger TRID tolerance violations. The lender may refuse to fund, and the settlement agent bears primary liability — but the agent who set the timeline too tight for corrections shares blame in the client's eyes.

## The Bottom Line for Pennsylvania Agents in 2026

Pennsylvania's hybrid system means both attorneys and title companies play roles at closing, but they are not interchangeable. Agents must verify attorney supervision of title work, avoid any document drafting, and confirm proper licensing of every settlement provider they recommend.
