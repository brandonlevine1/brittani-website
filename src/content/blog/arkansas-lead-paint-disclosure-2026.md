## Understanding Arkansas Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

Every Arkansas agent listing a pre-1978 home faces the same federal mandate that has tripped up licensees for three decades. The Arkansas lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain rooted in the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulation at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H, and 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F. Arkansas has not enacted a separate state-level lead paint disclosure statute that supersedes federal law, which means compliance lives entirely under EPA and HUD jurisdiction.

The Arkansas Real Estate Commission (AREC) holds licensees accountable for following all applicable federal disclosure laws as part of their duties under Ark. Code Ann. 17-42-313. A violation is not merely a federal issue — it can trigger a license complaint before the Commission. Agents who treat lead paint disclosure as a checkbox instead of a liability shield are the ones who end up defending themselves in front of AREC's disciplinary panel.

## Which Properties Trigger the Disclosure Obligation

**Not every transaction in your pipeline requires a lead paint disclosure, but the threshold is simple: was the home built before 1978?** If yes, disclosure is mandatory for sales and leases of target housing. Target housing means any residential dwelling except housing for the elderly or persons with disabilities (unless children under six reside there) and zero-bedroom dwellings such as studio apartments, dormitories, and military barracks.

The exemption list is narrow. Foreclosure sales, short sales, and estate sales of pre-1978 homes still require full compliance. There is no exemption for distressed property condition, investor purchasers, or cash transactions. Arkansas agents working the Little Rock, Fort Smith, or Hot Springs markets — where pre-war housing stock is dense — will encounter this obligation on a significant percentage of listings.

If you are unsure about a property's construction date and the county assessor records are ambiguous, err on the side of disclosure. The cost of including the form unnecessarily is zero. The cost of omitting it is up to $21,018 per violation under current EPA penalty schedules, adjusted for inflation.

## The Specific Forms Arkansas Agents Must Use

Arkansas does not have a state-specific lead paint disclosure form. Agents rely on the EPA's federally prescribed disclosure form titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards." This form is commonly referred to by its informal label, the "EPA Lead Paint Disclosure Form," and is available through the [EPA's lead paint program page](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-paint-disclosure-rule).

The Arkansas Realtors Association (ARA) distributes the form through its transaction forms library, often packaged with the standard residential sales contract. The form has three sections: seller disclosure, buyer acknowledgment, and agent certification. All three sections must be completed and signed before or at the time the buyer becomes obligated under a purchase contract.

| Form Section | Who Signs | What It Confirms |
|---|---|---|
| Seller Disclosure | Seller(s) | Known lead paint presence, records, or reports provided |
| Buyer Acknowledgment | Buyer(s) | Receipt of disclosure, EPA pamphlet, and inspection opportunity |
| Agent Certification | Listing agent and buyer's agent | That each agent informed their client of obligations |
| EPA Pamphlet Delivery | Documented by agent | Buyer received "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" |

The EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" must be physically or electronically delivered to the buyer before the contract is signed. Digital delivery is acceptable as of 2023 regulatory updates, but agents must retain proof of delivery. A signed acknowledgment line on the disclosure form serves this purpose.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: What Arkansas Agents Actually Face

**Failing to provide the lead paint disclosure exposes the listing agent, the buyer's agent, and the seller to separate federal penalties and private civil liability.** Under 42 U.S.C. 4852d, violations can result in civil penalties up to $21,018 per violation (as adjusted), criminal penalties up to $11,000 per violation plus imprisonment for knowing and willful violations, and treble damages in private lawsuits brought by buyers.

In Arkansas specifically, AREC treats federal disclosure violations as a breach of Ark. Code Ann. 17-42-311(a), which addresses dishonest or incompetent conduct. Disciplinary actions range from formal reprimand to license suspension or revocation. The Commission's enforcement history shows it takes disclosure failures seriously, particularly when a buyer's child later tests positive for elevated blood lead levels.

Buyers also retain the right to rescind the contract. Under 40 CFR 745.118, a buyer who did not receive proper disclosure before becoming obligated under the contract can void the deal. This creates deal-killing exposure for sellers who have already committed to a new purchase, and it puts the listing agent's commission — and reputation — directly at risk.

| Federal civil penalty | EPA enforcement | Up to $21,018 per violation |
| Criminal prosecution | DOJ referral | Fine up to $11,000, imprisonment |
| Private civil suit | Buyer lawsuit | Treble damages plus attorney fees |

## Common Mistakes Arkansas Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosures

Three decades of enforcement have produced a clear pattern of recurring errors. These are not obscure hypotheticals — they are the mistakes that show up in AREC complaints and EPA enforcement actions in the mid-South region.

**First, agents frequently hand the disclosure form to the seller at closing instead of before the buyer signs the purchase contract.** The regulation requires disclosure before the purchaser becomes obligated. A form signed at the closing table is legally meaningless — the buyer was already bound when they executed the contract days or weeks earlier. Timing is not flexible.

Second, agents forget that both the listing agent and the buyer's agent must sign the certification section. A listing agent who completes the form but fails to obtain the cooperating agent's signature has an incomplete disclosure. This gap matters because liability extends to every agent involved in the transaction, and an unsigned certification removes the buyer's agent's documented compliance.

Third, agents fail to deliver the EPA pamphlet separately from the form. The pamphlet is a standalone requirement. Simply attaching the disclosure form without the pamphlet — or referencing it without actual delivery — violates the regulation. Electronic delivery of the pamphlet is permitted, but the agent must document that the buyer actually received it, not merely that it was sent.

Fourth, agents allow buyers to waive the 10-day inspection period verbally rather than in writing. The waiver must be documented on the form itself or in a written addendum. A verbal waiver has no legal effect, and a buyer who later claims they were denied inspection rights will prevail if there is no written waiver on file.

Fifth, agents neglect the three-year retention requirement. After closing, the signed disclosure and all related records must be kept for a minimum of three years from the completion date. Agents who rely solely on the title company's file or the MLS upload — without retaining their own copy — risk being unable to produce documentation when an EPA audit or civil claim surfaces two years post-closing. Building consistent [transaction management habits](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) prevents this kind of gap from becoming a liability event.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under both federal lead paint regulations and Arkansas license law. Ark. Code Ann. 17-42-311(b) places affirmative duties on designated brokers to ensure their agents comply with all disclosure requirements. Passive oversight is not a defense.

**Every brokerage operating in Arkansas should maintain a written policy requiring lead paint disclosure completion before contract execution on any pre-1978 listing.** This policy should specify who reviews the form for completeness, where signed copies are stored, and how the broker verifies pamphlet delivery. Brokerages that treat this as optional are accepting personal liability for every agent in their office.

Brokers should conduct quarterly audits of closed transaction files for pre-1978 properties. The audit checklist is straightforward: Is the form fully signed by all parties? Is the EPA pamphlet delivery documented? Was the form executed before or concurrent with the purchase contract — not after? Is the buyer's inspection period waiver (if applicable) in writing? Is the cooperating agent's signature present?

| Audit Point | Pass Criteria | Common Failure |
|---|---|---|
| Form timing | Signed before or with contract | Signed at closing table |
| All signatures present | Seller, buyer, both agents | Missing cooperating agent |
| Pamphlet delivery documented | Written acknowledgment | No documentation |
| Inspection waiver | Written on form or addendum | Verbal only |
| File retention | Broker retains 3+ years | Reliance on title company alone |

Brokers should also verify that agents are correctly identifying pre-1978 properties at the listing stage. A CMA that pulls county assessor data will typically include the year built. If the listing agent marks "post-1978" incorrectly — whether through carelessness or reliance on a seller's verbal statement — the broker shares in the resulting exposure.

## The Buyer's 10-Day Inspection Right in Practice

Arkansas buyers are entitled to a 10-day window to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense. This period can be shortened, lengthened, or waived — but only by mutual written agreement. The default is 10 days, and it begins when the buyer receives the disclosure and pamphlet.

Agents representing buyers in pre-1978 transactions should advise their clients about this right clearly and early. Failing to inform a buyer of their inspection opportunity is itself a violation, regardless of whether the buyer would have elected to exercise it. The disclosure form includes a checkbox where the buyer either accepts the inspection period, agrees to a different timeframe, or waives the right entirely.

In practice, most Arkansas buyers waive the lead inspection because few certified lead inspectors operate outside the Little Rock and Northwest Arkansas corridors. This scarcity does not eliminate the agent's obligation to present the option. Document the waiver on the form and move on — but never skip the step.

## How This Intersects With Arkansas Seller Disclosure Obligations

Lead paint disclosure operates independently from the Arkansas Property Disclosure Act (Ark. Code Ann. 18-44-104). The state seller disclosure form asks about known material defects, but it does not satisfy the federal lead paint disclosure requirement. These are two separate obligations, and completing one does not excuse the other.

Agents sometimes assume that checking "no known defects" on the state property disclosure covers lead paint. It does not. The federal form requires specific questions about known lead-based paint, known lead-based paint hazards, and any available reports or records. A generic state disclosure form cannot substitute for the EPA-mandated form, period.
