## Minnesota Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026: The Statute-Level Breakdown Agents Need

Getting Minnesota property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 right is not optional — it is a statutory obligation with real teeth. Every residential sale in this state triggers a disclosure framework rooted in Minnesota Statutes Chapter 513, and agents who treat the forms as afterthoughts expose themselves, their sellers, and their brokerages to litigation. The stakes are higher than a delayed closing; they include rescission rights, damage awards, and disciplinary action from the Minnesota Department of Commerce.

This post breaks down the specific forms, statutes, deadlines, and enforcement realities that licensed agents and brokers face when handling property condition and environmental disclosures in Minnesota transactions this year.

## The Statutory Foundation: Minn. Stat. Chapter 513

Minnesota's seller disclosure requirements originate in Minn. Stat. sections 513.52 through 513.60, commonly referred to as the Seller's Disclosure Act. The statute requires that sellers of residential real property (one to four units) provide a written disclosure to prospective buyers before or at the time of the purchase agreement signing. This is not a recommendation — it is a condition precedent to a binding contract in most residential transactions.

**The disclosure must be based on the seller's actual knowledge at the time of signing.** Sellers are not required to conduct independent inspections, but they cannot hide behind willful ignorance either. Minnesota courts have consistently held that a seller who knew or should have known about a defect and failed to disclose it is liable for damages under both the statute and common-law fraud theories.

The Act provides a narrow list of exemptions under Minn. Stat. 513.56, including transfers by court order, transfers by foreclosure, and transfers between co-owners. Standard arms-length sales — even those marketed as-is — do not qualify for exemption.

## Required Forms and Their Role in the Transaction

Minnesota Association of Realtors (MNAR) publishes the forms most agents use in practice. The primary disclosure document is the **Seller's Property Disclosure Statement**, which MNAR designates as form **SPD-1**. This form covers structural components, mechanical systems, water intrusion, pest damage, environmental hazards, and neighborhood conditions.

For properties built before 1978, agents must also deliver the federal **Lead-Based Paint Disclosure** required by 42 U.S.C. section 4852d and EPA/HUD regulations at 24 CFR Part 35. MNAR provides a Minnesota-specific version of this form integrated into their transaction packet. Failure to deliver this federal disclosure triggers a separate enforcement track under federal law — including fines up to $19,507 per violation, adjusted for inflation. Agents dealing with [lead paint disclosure requirements in neighboring Wisconsin](/blog/wisconsin-lead-paint-disclosure-2026) will recognize the parallel federal obligations.

| Form | Governing Authority | Applies To | Timing |
|------|-------------------|-----------|--------|
| SPD-1 (Seller's Property Disclosure) | Minn. Stat. 513.55 | All residential 1-4 units (with limited exemptions) | Before or at purchase agreement signing |
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | 42 U.S.C. 4852d / 24 CFR Part 35 | Pre-1978 residential properties | Before purchase agreement becomes binding |
| Well Disclosure Certificate | Minn. Stat. 103I.235 | Properties with known wells (active or sealed) | Before or at closing |
| Radon Disclosure | Minn. Stat. 144.496 | All residential sales | Before or at purchase agreement signing |
| Individual Sewage Treatment System (ISTS) Disclosure | Minn. Stat. 115.55 | Properties with septic systems | Before closing; compliance inspection required |

The Well Disclosure Certificate is often overlooked in metro transactions, but it applies statewide. If a property ever had a well — even one that has been sealed — the seller must file a disclosure with the Minnesota Department of Health and deliver a copy to the buyer.

## Environmental Disclosures: Radon, Septic, and Beyond

**Minnesota is one of only a handful of states with a standalone radon disclosure statute.** Under Minn. Stat. 144.496, sellers must disclose any known radon test results and provide buyers with the Minnesota Department of Health's radon informational materials. The statute does not require the seller to test, but if testing has occurred, the results must be shared — even if they show elevated levels.

Septic system compliance is another environmental disclosure that catches agents off guard. Under Minn. Stat. 115.55, properties served by individual sewage treatment systems require a compliance inspection prior to sale. The seller must disclose the system's existence on the SPD-1, and many counties require a Certificate of Compliance or a plan for system upgrade as a condition of transfer. County rules vary substantially — Hennepin County's requirements differ from those in St. Louis or Otter Tail counties.

For properties in known contamination zones or near [Superfund sites listed by the MPCA](https://www.pca.state.mn.us/waste/cleanup-sites), agents should also flag potential vapor intrusion or groundwater contamination concerns. While there is no separate statutory form for this, failure to disclose known environmental contamination falls squarely within the material defect disclosure requirements of Chapter 513.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of noncompliance range from deal rescission to personal liability. Under Minn. Stat. 513.58, a buyer who receives a late or amended disclosure has **two business days** to rescind the purchase agreement without penalty. This rescission right is absolute — no justification is required.

Beyond rescission, sellers and agents face civil liability for damages. Minnesota courts have awarded buyers the cost of repairs, diminution in value, and in egregious cases, consequential damages. In Johnson v. Davis-type fact patterns, agents who knew about undisclosed defects have been held personally liable alongside sellers.

The Minnesota Department of Commerce can also take disciplinary action against licensees under Minn. Stat. 82.81. Sanctions include reprimand, license suspension, license revocation, and civil penalties. A pattern of disclosure failures — even unintentional ones — signals to the Department that an agent lacks the competence required for licensure.

| Violation | Potential Consequence | Governing Authority |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Late delivery of SPD-1 | Buyer rescission within 2 business days | Minn. Stat. 513.58 |
| Omission of known material defect | Civil damages (repair costs, diminution in value) | Minn. Stat. 513.55; common law fraud |
| Failure to deliver lead paint disclosure | Federal fines up to $19,507 per violation | 42 U.S.C. 4852d |
| Missing well disclosure certificate | Transfer may be voided; MDH enforcement | Minn. Stat. 103I.235 |
| Agent concealment of known defect | License suspension/revocation; personal liability | Minn. Stat. 82.81 |

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Minnesota Disclosures

The errors below are not hypothetical. They appear repeatedly in complaints filed with the Minnesota Department of Commerce and in civil litigation.

**First, agents conflate "as-is" with "no disclosure required."** This is flatly wrong under Minnesota law. An as-is clause in the purchase agreement limits the seller's obligation to repair — it does not eliminate the statutory duty to disclose known defects. Agents who advise sellers to skip the SPD-1 because the property is being sold as-is are creating liability for everyone involved.

Second, agents deliver the disclosure too late. The statute requires delivery before or at the time the purchase agreement is signed. Delivering the SPD-1 after the buyer has already committed creates an automatic rescission window that the buyer can exercise for any reason. Agents managing [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) are particularly susceptible to this timing error.

Third, listing agents coach sellers on how to answer disclosure questions. Telling a seller to answer "unknown" to a question they clearly know the answer to is not a disclosure strategy — it is a pathway to fraud claims. Agents must explain the form, but they cannot draft the answers or minimize known defects.

Fourth, agents forget the radon disclosure for properties that have been tested. If a radon test was conducted — whether by the seller, a prior owner, or during a previous transaction — and the results are known, they must be disclosed. Agents who simply skip the radon section because "the seller never tested" may be right, but they need to confirm that factual assertion rather than assume it.

Fifth, agents omit the well disclosure certificate on properties where the well was sealed years ago. The statute applies to sealed wells, not just active ones. Many metro-area properties that now connect to municipal water once had private wells — and those wells still require disclosure.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Minn. Stat. 82.81, subdivision 5. A broker who fails to establish systems for disclosure compliance can face disciplinary action even when the individual agent is the one who dropped the ball.

**Every brokerage operating in Minnesota should have a transaction file checklist that separately tracks each required disclosure form.** The SPD-1, lead paint disclosure, radon disclosure, well certificate, and ISTS compliance should each have their own line item with a date of delivery and signature confirmation.

Brokers should conduct quarterly audits of closed files to verify that disclosures were delivered on time and properly executed. A missing signature or an undated form is not a minor administrative gap — it is a potential rescission trigger or a disciplinary exhibit. Understanding [where deals commonly break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) helps brokers identify which agents need additional training on disclosure timing.

Training should explicitly cover the distinction between the agent's duty to disclose material facts they personally know (under common law and Minn. Stat. 82.81) and the seller's independent statutory duty under Chapter 513. These are parallel obligations, not interchangeable ones. An agent who learns about a foundation crack during a showing cannot rely on the seller's SPD-1 to satisfy the agent's own disclosure duty.

Brokers should also maintain a relationship with a real estate attorney who can advise on edge cases — properties in flood zones, properties with known methamphetamine contamination history, or properties subject to MPCA cleanup orders. These situations require more than a standard form.

## Practical Workflow for Staying Compliant

The most reliable way to handle disclosure compliance is to make it a gating step in your transaction workflow rather than a checkbox you confirm after the fact. Before presenting or accepting a purchase agreement, confirm that the SPD-1 is complete, signed, and dated. Before going under contract on a pre-1978 home, verify that the lead paint disclosure has been delivered and the buyer has received the EPA pamphlet.

Build reminders for the well certificate and ISTS compliance early in the listing process — not at closing, when a missing form can delay or derail the transaction.
