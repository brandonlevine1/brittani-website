## Understanding Georgia Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

Georgia earnest money & escrow laws 2026 remain one of the most consequential compliance areas for licensed agents, yet they trip up experienced practitioners every single quarter. The Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) continues to list trust account violations among its top disciplinary actions, and a single misstep can cost you your license. This post breaks down the statutory framework, the exact forms you should be using, and the operational mistakes that generate complaints.

Earnest money in Georgia is governed primarily by O.C.G.A. Title 43, Chapter 40 (the Georgia Real Estate License Law) and GREC Rule 520-1-.10. These aren't suggestions. They carry enforcement power that includes license revocation, monetary penalties, and civil liability—outcomes that no production year can absorb.

## The Statutory Framework: What the Law Actually Says

**O.C.G.A. 43-40-25 grants GREC broad authority to discipline licensees for mishandling trust funds.** The statute authorizes fines of up to $5,000 per violation, license suspension, and permanent revocation. It also opens the door for affected parties to file civil claims seeking actual damages and attorney's fees.

GREC Rule 520-1-.10 sets the operational requirements. Earnest money must be deposited into a trust or escrow account at a federally insured financial institution within five banking days of the binding agreement date. The rule uses the phrase "banking days," not calendar days—a distinction that catches agents off guard around holiday weekends.

The escrow account itself must be maintained in Georgia, carry the broker's name with a "trust" or "escrow" designation, and never be commingled with operating funds. Even temporary commingling—depositing a personal check alongside an earnest money check, for example—constitutes a violation. GREC auditors look for exactly this pattern during routine reviews.

## GAR Forms Every Agent Should Know

The Georgia Association of Realtors (GAR) publishes the standard contract forms that govern most residential transactions in the state. Agents who default to outdated versions or improvised addenda expose themselves to disputes that could have been avoided with the correct paperwork.

| Form Number | Form Name | Relevance to Earnest Money |
|---|---|---|
| GAR F20 | Purchase and Sale Agreement | Paragraph 3 establishes earnest money amount, holder, and deposit deadline |
| GAR F273 | Earnest Money Disbursement Agreement | Used when both parties agree to release earnest money without closing |
| GAR F274 | Earnest Money Change | Modifies holder, amount, or deposit terms after binding agreement |
| GAR F801 | Exhibit to Address Additional Terms | Sometimes used to specify non-standard escrow instructions |

**Paragraph 3 of GAR F20 is where most compliance stories begin.** It names the holder, sets the deposit deadline, and specifies the form of payment. If you leave any field blank or ambiguous, you create a factual dispute that can freeze the funds indefinitely. GREC has made clear in multiple advisory opinions that agents who fail to complete Paragraph 3 fully may be held responsible for resulting damages.

When a transaction falls apart, GAR F273 provides the mutual release mechanism. Both buyer and seller must sign before the holder can disburse. Without that dual signature, the holder faces a choice between sitting on the money or filing an interpleader action under O.C.G.A. 23-3-90—a process that involves court filings and legal fees that no one wants to absorb.

## Liability When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance break into three categories: regulatory, civil, and transactional. Understanding all three is the only way to grasp the full risk profile.

On the regulatory side, GREC investigates complaints and can act unilaterally. A first offense for a deposit-timing violation might result in a citation and fine. Repeat violations or evidence of intentional mishandling escalate rapidly to suspension. [The GREC disciplinary database](https://grec.state.ga.us/) is public, which means your violation becomes part of your searchable professional record.

Civil liability is the financial hammer. A buyer whose earnest money was improperly disbursed can sue the holder and the licensee who facilitated the error. Georgia courts have awarded actual damages, statutory interest, and attorney's fees in cases where agents disbursed funds without proper written authorization from both parties.

Transactionally, a blown escrow situation can kill the deal outright. If a buyer loses confidence that their deposit is being handled properly—or if a seller's attorney discovers the earnest money was never deposited on time—the binding agreement may be voided, and your commission evaporates. Agents tracking [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) are especially vulnerable because a missed five-day window on one file can cascade into credibility problems across others.

## Five Common Mistakes Georgia Agents Make

Mistake number one: treating the five-banking-day deposit rule as flexible. It is not. Agents sometimes hold checks in desk drawers waiting for the seller to countersign an unrelated addendum. That delay violates GREC Rule 520-1-.10, full stop, regardless of the agent's good intentions.

Mistake number two: failing to specify the holder clearly in Paragraph 3 of GAR F20. Writing "TBD" or leaving the field blank creates an immediate compliance gap. GREC expects the holder to be identified at the time of the binding agreement so that deposit timelines can be enforced.

**Mistake number three: disbursing earnest money based on a verbal agreement between the parties.** Georgia law requires written authorization—either a signed GAR F273 or a court order. A phone call from the buyer saying "go ahead and release it to the seller" does not satisfy the statute, and the agent who acts on it assumes full liability.

Mistake number four: not reconciling the escrow account monthly. GREC Rule 520-1-.10(4) requires monthly reconciliation of all trust accounts. Brokers who delegate this task to unlicensed staff without oversight often discover discrepancies only after GREC initiates an audit—by which point, the violation is already on record.

Mistake number five: converting earnest money to a different form without documentation. If a buyer submits a promissory note as initial earnest money and later converts it to cash, the conversion must be documented and deposited within the original timeline. Agents who lose track of this conversion—especially those [who rely on memory instead of checklists](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory)—create audit trails that go cold.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear ultimate responsibility for trust account compliance under O.C.G.A. 43-40-18. The licensee who physically handles the check may face individual discipline, but the qualifying broker's license is on the line for systemic failures.

| Broker Responsibility | Frequency | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | GREC Rule 520-1-.10(4) |
| Verification of deposit within 5 banking days | Per transaction | GREC Rule 520-1-.10(1) |
| Review of holder designation in contracts | Per transaction | GAR F20, Paragraph 3 |
| Training on earnest money handling procedures | Annually (recommended) | GREC Advisory Opinions |
| Audit trail for all disbursements | Per disbursement | O.C.G.A. 43-40-25 |

**Brokers who do not have a written escrow policy are playing defense without a playbook.** GREC expects firms to maintain internal procedures that specify who deposits checks, who reconciles ledgers, who authorizes disbursements, and what documentation is required at each step. The absence of such a policy does not excuse individual violations—it compounds them by demonstrating a pattern of negligence at the firm level.

One area brokers frequently overlook is the [handoff between agents and transaction coordinators](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist). When an agent collects a check and passes it to the TC or office manager for deposit, the chain of custody must be documented. If the deposit lands late because of an internal communication failure, GREC holds the broker accountable, not the TC.

Quarterly internal audits—pulling a random sample of closed and pending files to verify deposit dates, holder designations, and disbursement authorizations—are the single most effective risk-reduction practice a brokerage can implement. The cost of a two-hour quarterly review is insignificant compared to the cost of a GREC investigation.

## Disputed Earnest Money: The Interpleader Option

When buyer and seller disagree about who should receive the earnest money, Georgia law provides a structured resolution path. O.C.G.A. 23-3-90 allows the holder to file an interpleader action, depositing the funds with the court and asking a judge to decide.

This option protects the holder from liability to either party but involves filing fees, potential attorney costs, and time. GREC has issued guidance stating that a holder who fails to act within a reasonable time after a dispute becomes apparent may face discipline for inaction. "Reasonable time" is not defined by statute, but GREC advisory opinions suggest that holding disputed funds for more than 30 days without initiating either a mutual release (GAR F273) or an interpleader is likely to attract scrutiny.

Agents should document every communication related to a dispute. Emails, texts, and written notices all become evidence in a GREC investigation or civil proceeding. The agent who can produce a clean timeline of communications and attempted resolutions is in a far stronger position than the one who operated on verbal exchanges alone.
