# Indiana Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026: What Every Agent Must Do Before Closing

Wire fraud targeting real estate closings has grown into a half-billion-dollar annual problem nationally, and Indiana is not immune. Understanding Indiana wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 is no longer optional knowledge for licensees—it is a baseline expectation from the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC), title companies, and the courts. If you hold an active license in this state, every transaction you touch carries disclosure obligations that did not exist five years ago.

The stakes are not abstract. In 2024, the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center reported that real estate wire fraud losses in Indiana exceeded $18 million across 127 reported incidents. That figure only captures what victims reported, meaning the actual number is almost certainly higher.

## The Regulatory Framework Behind Indiana's Wire Fraud Requirements

Indiana does not have a standalone wire fraud prevention statute the way some states do. Instead, the obligations come from a patchwork of existing law, administrative code, and industry-standard forms that collectively create an enforceable duty.

**The primary legal authority is Indiana Code 25-34.1, which governs real estate broker conduct and establishes the fiduciary duties agents owe their clients.** Under 876 IAC 8-2-4, the Indiana Real Estate Commission interprets those duties to include protecting clients from reasonably foreseeable risks during transactions. Wire fraud in 2026 qualifies as reasonably foreseeable.

The [Indiana Real Estate Commission](https://www.in.gov/pla/professions/indiana-real-estate-commission/) has issued guidance letters in 2024 and 2025 clarifying that agents who fail to warn clients about wire fraud may be found in violation of their duty of care. While the Commission has not yet imposed standalone fines specifically for missing wire fraud disclosures, it has cited the omission as an aggravating factor in disciplinary proceedings related to closing failures.

Indiana Association of Realtors (IAR) Form 58—the Wire Fraud Advisory—serves as the standard disclosure vehicle. It is not technically mandated by statute, but its use has become the de facto standard of care that courts and the Commission evaluate against.

## Which Forms Indiana Agents Must Use and When to Deliver Them

IAR Form 58 is the one-page Wire Fraud Advisory that warns parties about email compromise schemes, spoofed phone numbers, and fraudulent wiring instructions. The form requires signatures from buyers, sellers, and their respective agents acknowledging that they have been warned.

Delivery timing matters more than most agents realize. IAR guidance and IREC interpretive letters both indicate the form should be presented at or before the time of offer acceptance. Delivering it at the closing table defeats its purpose entirely—by then, a compromised wire may have already been sent days earlier.

| Form | Purpose | Delivery Timing | Who Signs |
|------|---------|----------------|-----------|
| IAR Form 58 | Wire Fraud Advisory | At or before offer acceptance | All parties and agents |
| IAR Form 55 | Buyer's Acknowledgments | With purchase agreement | Buyer and buyer's agent |
| Title company wire verification sheet | Confirms legitimate wiring instructions | 24-48 hours before closing | Buyer (wire sender) |

Some brokerages also require agents to deliver a supplemental wire fraud warning via text or secure portal when the title company sends wiring instructions. This is a brokerage-level policy, not a state requirement, but failing to follow your brokerage's internal procedures carries its own liability implications under your independent contractor agreement.

If you are working [wire instructions verification into your transaction process](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process), the timing of Form 58 delivery should be the first item on your pre-closing checklist—not an afterthought.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**The consequences of skipping wire fraud disclosures range from professional discipline to personal financial liability.** Here is what Indiana agents actually face.

The Indiana Real Estate Commission can initiate disciplinary proceedings under IC 25-34.1-11 for any conduct that demonstrates incompetence or neglect in performing licensee duties. A missing Form 58, combined with a client who loses money to a fraudulent wire, creates a textbook disciplinary case. Sanctions include reprimand, mandatory education, suspension, or license revocation.

Civil liability is the bigger financial threat. If a buyer wires $300,000 to a fraudulent account and the agent never provided a wire fraud warning, the buyer's attorney will argue the agent breached their standard of care. Indiana courts look at what a reasonable licensee would have done under the circumstances—and in 2026, a reasonable licensee provides the warning. Errors and omissions insurance carriers have begun including wire fraud disclosure compliance in their coverage conditions, meaning a policy may deny the claim if the agent skipped the form.

Deal cancellation is also on the table. Title companies increasingly require proof of Form 58 delivery before they will insure the transaction. If you cannot produce the signed form, the title company may refuse to close, and the resulting delay can trigger deadline violations under the purchase agreement.

## Common Mistakes Indiana Agents Make With Wire Fraud Compliance

Five recurring errors show up in IREC complaint files and brokerage audits related to wire fraud prevention.

The first mistake is delivering Form 58 at closing instead of at offer acceptance. By the time parties sit down to sign final documents, wire transfers have typically already occurred. The advisory only works as a prevention tool if the parties receive it before they are in a position to wire money.

The second error is assuming the title company handles everything. Title companies do their own verification procedures, but the agent's disclosure obligation is independent. You cannot delegate your fiduciary duty to a third party. The Commission has been explicit about this in its 2025 guidance.

Third, agents frequently fail to obtain all required signatures. Form 58 requires acknowledgment from both buyer and seller, plus both agents. A form signed only by the buyer does not satisfy the standard. Agents on the listing side sometimes assume it is "the buyer's agent's problem," which is incorrect—the listing agent has independent duties to their seller regarding wire fraud risks on proceeds disbursement.

Fourth, some agents provide verbal warnings but skip the written form. Verbal warnings are functionally useless in a disciplinary hearing or lawsuit. Without a signed acknowledgment, you have no proof you did anything. Indiana follows a documentation-first enforcement approach.

Fifth, agents who work across state lines sometimes confuse Indiana's requirements with neighboring states. Illinois, Ohio, and Kentucky each have different disclosure norms. Using an out-of-state form or following another state's timing standards does not satisfy Indiana's expectations. ## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers carry heightened responsibility under IC 25-34.1-10 for the conduct of their affiliated licensees. If an agent under your supervision fails to deliver wire fraud warnings and a client suffers a loss, IREC can hold you accountable for inadequate supervision.

**Every Indiana brokerage should have a written wire fraud prevention policy that specifies exactly when agents must deliver Form 58, how delivery is documented, and what follow-up communication is required.** The policy should be included in the brokerage's policy manual and reviewed during onboarding.

Quarterly audits of transaction files should verify that Form 58 appears in every closed file. Brokers should spot-check at least 10 percent of pending files monthly to catch compliance gaps before closing rather than after.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag |
|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Form 58 present and fully signed | Every file at closing | Missing seller or listing agent signature |
| Delivery date on Form 58 | Every file | Date matches or predates contract execution |
| Brokerage supplemental wire warning | Monthly spot-check | No record in transaction management system |
| Agent completion of annual wire fraud training | Annually | Expired or missing CE certificate |
| Client communication log showing wire verification call | Quarterly sample | No outbound call 24-48 hours before close |

Brokers should also confirm that agents are completing wire fraud prevention training as part of their continuing education. While IREC does not yet require a standalone wire fraud CE module, several approved providers offer them as electives, and brokerages can mandate them internally.

The brokerage's E&O policy may also require evidence of wire fraud compliance programs. Check your policy's cooperation clause and any endorsements related to cyber fraud—failure to maintain documented procedures could void coverage for the entire brokerage, not just the individual agent.

## How Indiana Compares to Other States

Indiana's approach is less prescriptive than some states but carries equivalent enforcement weight through its broad fiduciary duty framework. The practical effect is that agents cannot claim ignorance—IREC expects you to know the standard of care even without a statute that says "you must use Form 58."

Agents who work in border markets should note that [Washington's wire fraud prevention requirements](/blog/washington-wire-fraud-prevention-2026) differ in both form and timing, and that each state evaluates compliance independently. Holding licenses in multiple states means maintaining separate compliance workflows for each.
