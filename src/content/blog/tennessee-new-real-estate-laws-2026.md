The Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) has been busy. Multiple statutory changes and form revisions take effect between mid-2026 and early 2027, and agents who treat them as minor paperwork tweaks are going to find themselves on the wrong end of a complaint. Tennessee new real estate laws 2026 affect disclosure timing, buyer representation agreements, compensation transparency, and flood zone documentation — and the enforcement mechanisms have teeth this time.

This is not a summary you can skim and forget. The changes touch daily workflows, and the liability exposure for non-compliance ranges from deal cancellation to license suspension.

## Revised Agency Disclosure Requirements Under T.C.A. 62-13-403

The Tennessee General Assembly amended T.C.A. 62-13-403 effective July 1, 2026, tightening the timing for agency disclosure delivery. Previously, agents had until the first "substantive contact" to present the agency disclosure form. The revised statute now defines substantive contact explicitly: any discussion of pricing, property condition, or negotiation strategy triggers the obligation.

**TREC's updated Confirmation of Agency Status form (RF201) must now be signed before any showing where pricing or property condition is discussed verbally or in writing.** This means the old practice of handing the form over at the offer stage is dead. Agents who wait until the buyer writes an offer are already in violation.

The penalty structure under the amendment allows TREC to impose fines of up to $1,000 per occurrence and mandate additional continuing education hours. Repeat violations within a 24-month period can trigger a formal hearing with potential license suspension.

## Compensation Transparency and the New Buyer Representation Agreement

Tennessee's response to the NAR settlement framework arrived in the form of Senate Bill 1247, codified as an amendment to T.C.A. 62-13-405. The law requires that all buyer-broker compensation be documented in a signed buyer representation agreement before the agent submits any offer on the buyer's behalf. This mirrors trends in other states, but Tennessee's version includes a unique wrinkle: the agreement must itemize services provided in exchange for the stated compensation.

The new RF141 Buyer Representation Agreement template released by the [Tennessee Real Estate Commission](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regboards/trec.html) in May 2026 includes mandatory fields for compensation amount or percentage, the duration of the agreement, and a termination clause with a maximum 90-day lookback period. Leaving any field blank renders the form non-compliant.

Agents working with buyers who refuse to sign the RF141 face a hard choice: decline to show property or risk submitting an offer without the required documentation. If a listing agent receives an offer without a valid buyer representation agreement on file, the listing agent is now required to notify both brokers within two business days. The deal does not automatically terminate, but the buyer's agent is exposed to a TREC complaint.

## Updated Property Condition Disclosure (RF401) and Flood Zone Addendum (RF305)

**The RF401 Property Condition Disclosure form has been revised for the first time since 2019, and the changes are not cosmetic.** Three new disclosure categories have been added: prior insurance claims related to water damage, known proximity to FEMA-designated flood zones, and the presence of any unpermitted structural modifications. Sellers must now affirmatively check a box indicating they have reviewed their property's flood zone status using FEMA's current maps.

| Form | Previous Version Date | 2026 Version Effective | Key Change |
|------|----------------------|----------------------|------------|
| RF401 | January 2019 | August 1, 2026 | Added water damage claims, flood zone, unpermitted work sections |
| RF305 | March 2021 | August 1, 2026 | Delivery deadline reduced to 3 business days; requires FEMA map reference |
| RF201 | July 2020 | July 1, 2026 | Must be signed before any pricing/condition discussion |
| RF141 | New form | July 1, 2026 | Buyer rep agreement with itemized services and compensation |

The RF305 Flood Zone Addendum previously had a five-business-day delivery window. That has been reduced to three business days from the effective date of the purchase agreement. Failure to deliver on time gives the buyer an automatic five-day extension of the inspection period at the buyer's option — a provision that can throw closing timelines into chaos.

## Common Mistakes Agents Will Make Under the New Laws

Based on TREC's disciplinary records from previous legislative cycles and early compliance audits, these are the errors most likely to generate complaints in the second half of 2026.

**First, agents will continue using outdated forms past the August 1 effective date.** Every cycle produces a wave of agents who either did not update their document libraries or whose transaction management systems still auto-populate old versions. TREC has stated it will treat use of superseded forms after September 1, 2026 as a per-transaction violation — no grace period beyond the initial 30 days.

Second, buyer's agents will fail to obtain the RF141 before showing property. The old workflow of building rapport, touring homes, and then formalizing the relationship does not survive under the new statute. Agents accustomed to informal buyer relationships will face complaints from listing agents who are now obligated to flag missing documentation.

Third, listing agents will neglect to verify that the seller actually reviewed FEMA flood maps before signing the RF401. The form includes a new certification line requiring the seller to confirm map review. An agent who simply hands the form over without directing the seller to the relevant FEMA resource is not meeting their advisory obligation under T.C.A. 62-13-403(b)(2).

Fourth, agents will miscalculate the three-business-day delivery deadline for the RF305 by counting calendar days instead of business days, or by measuring from the wrong starting event. The clock starts on the effective date of the purchase agreement — not the date of acceptance signatures, not the date of MLS status change.

Fifth, agents will assume the 90-day lookback provision in the RF141 is negotiable at closing. It is not. Once the buyer representation agreement is executed, the lookback period is fixed and survives closing unless formally amended in writing by both parties.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce Starting Now

Brokers bear supervisory liability under T.C.A. 62-13-312 for their affiliated licensees' compliance failures. The 2026 amendments did not soften that exposure — if anything, the new compensation transparency requirements increase it. A broker whose agent submits an offer without a valid RF141 on file can face the same disciplinary action as the agent.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Risk If Missed |
|-------------------|-----------|----------------|
| RF141 on file before first offer submission | Every transaction | TREC complaint, fine up to $1,000 |
| RF201 signed before substantive contact | Every buyer interaction | Deal rescission, fine, mandatory CE |
| RF401 version verification | Every listing after August 1 | Per-transaction violation |
| RF305 delivery within 3 business days | Every transaction in or near flood zone | Automatic inspection extension for buyer |
| Compensation documentation matches listing agreement | Every closing | Audit finding, potential commission dispute |

Brokers should implement a file review checkpoint no later than 48 hours after contract execution to verify that the RF141, RF201, and RF305 (where applicable) are all present, current-version, and properly executed. Waiting until closing to audit files is no longer a defensible practice.

The volume of form changes in 2026 makes manual tracking unreliable for brokerages handling more than a few transactions per month.

## Liability Exposure: What Actually Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

TREC's enforcement division has historically been measured in its approach to first-time violations, favoring consent orders with education requirements over immediate suspension. The 2026 legislative session, however, included an appropriations increase for TREC's investigative staff and a directive from the Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance to reduce case resolution timelines.

**Agents should expect faster complaint processing and less willingness to settle with education-only remedies.** The amended T.C.A. 62-13-312(b) now authorizes TREC to impose administrative penalties without a formal hearing for clear-cut documentation failures — meaning a missing RF141 can result in a fine without the agent ever appearing before the commission.

Beyond regulatory penalties, the civil liability exposure is significant. A buyer who discovers undisclosed flood zone proximity after closing has a private right of action under T.C.A. 66-5-202, and the 2026 amendment to the RF401 strengthens the buyer's position by creating a clear paper trail of what the seller certified. If the agent failed to ensure the seller completed the flood zone section, the agent's errors and omissions carrier will want to have a conversation.

Deal cancellation remains the most common practical consequence. Buyers who discover form deficiencies during the inspection period can use them as grounds for termination under the standard Tennessee Association of Realtors purchase agreement, and agents who [understand why deadlines get missed](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) know that a single documentation gap can unravel weeks of negotiation.
