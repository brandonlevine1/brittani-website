## Kentucky Seller Disclosure Requirements 2026: The Statute, the Form, and the Stakes

Kentucky seller disclosure requirements 2026 remain governed by KRS 324.360, a statute that has stayed remarkably stable but still trips up agents who treat it as a formality. The law requires residential property sellers to deliver a written disclosure statement to buyers before or at the time a purchase contract is executed. Failure to comply does not just create awkward conversations—it opens a window for deal rescission and potential disciplinary action by the Kentucky Real Estate Commission (KREC).

Every agent licensed in the Commonwealth should be able to recite the basic framework from memory. The seller must disclose all known material defects. The buyer gets a defined rescission period. The listing agent carries an independent obligation to disclose defects they personally know about, separate from whatever the seller writes on the form.

## The Governing Law: KRS 324.360 and Its Scope

**KRS 324.360 applies to virtually all residential real property transfers in Kentucky, with a short list of exemptions.** The statute mandates that a seller of residential property consisting of one to four dwelling units provide a disclosure of the property's condition. It is not optional, and there is no dollar-value threshold below which it disappears.

The [Kentucky Real Estate Commission](https://krec.ky.gov/) administers and enforces licensing standards tied to this statute. KREC does not publish its own standalone disclosure form with a numbered designation the way some states do, but it references the obligations under KRS 324.360 in its administrative regulations at 201 KAR 11:121. Most brokerages in Kentucky use either the Kentucky Association of REALTORS (KAR) Seller's Disclosure of Property Conditions form or a brokerage-specific version that mirrors the statutory requirements.

The KAR form addresses structural systems, mechanical systems, water and sewage, environmental hazards, and known neighborhood nuisances. Agents should confirm annually that their brokerage's form has been reviewed by legal counsel and tracks any legislative amendments or new KREC guidance.

## Exemptions: Who Does Not Have to Disclose

Not every transaction triggers the disclosure obligation. KRS 324.360(2) carves out specific scenarios where the seller is exempt.

| Exemption Category | Statutory Basis | Common Example |
|---|---|---|
| Court-ordered transfers | KRS 324.360(2)(a) | Foreclosures, judicial sales |
| Transfers by fiduciaries | KRS 324.360(2)(b) | Estate executors, trustees |
| Transfers between co-owners | KRS 324.360(2)(c) | Divorce buyouts |
| New construction with warranty | KRS 324.360(2)(d) | Builder sales with express warranty |
| Transfers to government entities | KRS 324.360(2)(e) | Condemnation proceedings |

Even when an exemption applies, the listing agent's independent duty under KRS 324.160 to disclose known adverse material facts about the property does not evaporate. You cannot hide behind a seller's exempt status if you personally know the basement floods every spring. This distinction catches agents off-guard more often than any other element of the statute.

## The Buyer's Rescission Right and Timing

KRS 324.360(7) gives buyers five calendar days after receipt of the completed disclosure to rescind the purchase agreement without penalty. If the disclosure is delivered before the contract is signed, the rescission period begins at delivery. If it arrives after contract execution, the clock starts on the date the buyer actually receives the form.

**Agents who fail to document when the buyer received the disclosure are setting themselves up for disputes.** The safest practice is to use a signed and dated acknowledgment of receipt—either on the disclosure form itself or as a separate document in the file. Electronic signatures and timestamps from a transaction management system work here, provided they meet Kentucky's UETA standards.

Missing this five-day window matters in both directions. A buyer who discovers undisclosed water damage on day six cannot invoke this specific statutory rescission right, though they may still have common-law fraud claims. And a seller who never delivers the disclosure has effectively left the rescission window open indefinitely, creating perpetual vulnerability through closing and potentially beyond.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Kentucky Disclosures

Five recurring errors account for the majority of KREC complaints and post-closing disputes tied to disclosure failures.

**First, agents let sellers leave sections blank without follow-up.** A blank line on the disclosure is not the same as a "no" answer. It signals either ignorance or evasion, and it does not protect the seller from liability. Agents should instruct sellers to mark every item—yes, no, or unknown—and initial each page.

Second, listing agents fail to disclose defects they personally observed. KRS 324.160 makes clear that a licensee's duty is independent. If you noticed standing water in the crawl space during your listing appointment, you must disclose it regardless of what the seller writes. Hoping the buyer's inspector catches it is not a compliance strategy.

Third, agents deliver the disclosure too late—after both parties have signed the contract—without documenting receipt and informing the buyer of their rescission rights. This error has killed deals at the eleventh hour when a buyer's attorney realizes the timeline was mishandled. Similar timing pitfalls show up in other compliance contexts, as discussed in [why agents miss critical deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

Fourth, agents confuse "as-is" language in the purchase contract with a waiver of disclosure requirements. Selling property "as-is" does not eliminate the seller's statutory obligation to disclose known defects under KRS 324.360. The two concepts operate on different legal tracks. An "as-is" clause may limit the seller's repair obligations, but it does not permit concealment of known material defects.

Fifth, agents recycle last year's disclosure form on relisted properties without having the seller review and re-sign it. Property conditions change. A six-month-old disclosure that does not reflect a new roof leak or a code violation discovered during the prior listing period is stale and potentially misleading.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The consequences of noncompliance stack in three layers: regulatory, contractual, and civil.

At the regulatory level, KREC can pursue disciplinary action under KRS 324.160 for any licensee who fails to disclose material adverse facts or who facilitates a seller's noncompliance. Sanctions range from formal reprimands and mandatory continuing education to license suspension or revocation. Fines imposed by KREC can reach up to $1,000 per violation under 201 KAR 11:250, though in practice they are often paired with probationary conditions.

| Consequence | Triggered By | Potential Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| KREC disciplinary action | Agent's failure to disclose known defects | Fine up to $1,000, CE requirement, suspension |
| Contract rescission | Buyer invoking KRS 324.360(7) | Deal cancellation within 5 days of receipt |
| Civil lawsuit | Fraud, misrepresentation, or negligence | Damages, attorney fees, potential punitive award |

At the contractual level, a buyer who never received the disclosure—or received one that was materially incomplete—can rescind and recover their earnest money. At the civil level, buyers who close and later discover concealed defects can sue both the seller and the listing agent for fraud or negligent misrepresentation. Kentucky courts have upheld six-figure damage awards in residential nondisclosure cases, particularly where the defect involved structural issues or environmental contamination.

The operational cost of getting this wrong extends beyond the single transaction. Agents who have faced KREC complaints often report difficulty obtaining E&O insurance renewals at competitive rates. The [real cost of missed deadlines](/blog/real-cost-of-missed-deadlines) applies here: one overlooked disclosure can cascade into lost referrals, higher insurance premiums, and brokerage-level liability exposure.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability in Kentucky. KRS 324.160(4) holds a principal broker responsible for ensuring that all licensees under their supervision comply with disclosure obligations. This is not a passive duty.

**Brokers should build a disclosure audit into every file review—not just at closing, but at the listing stage.** The checklist is straightforward: confirm the disclosure was completed and signed by all sellers, confirm it was delivered to the buyer with a dated acknowledgment, confirm the listing agent documented any independent observations, and confirm the buyer's rescission period has expired before removing contingencies.

Quarterly file audits should flag any listing that went under contract without a disclosure on file. Monthly compliance reviews—something we discussed in [building a monthly compliance audit routine](/blog/monthly-compliance-audit)—help catch problems before they metastasize into KREC complaints. Brokers operating in multiple states should note that Kentucky's requirements differ in form and timing from neighboring states like [Tennessee](/blog/tennessee-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) and Ohio, making a one-size-fits-all policy inadequate.

Training matters here as well. New agents in particular need explicit instruction on the independent disclosure duty, the difference between "as-is" sales and disclosure obligations, and the mechanics of documenting the buyer's receipt date. An annual refresher for the entire brokerage—ideally before spring listing season—keeps the team aligned.

## Handling Updates and Amended Disclosures

Kentucky law does not explicitly require an amended disclosure when conditions change between initial delivery and closing, but best practice demands it. If the seller discovers a new defect after signing the original disclosure—say, a sewer backup during the inspection period—the seller should complete and deliver an updated disclosure.

The buyer's five-day rescission clock resets upon receipt of any amended disclosure under the logic of KRS 324.360(7). This can create timing pressure near closing, so agents should coach sellers to report new issues immediately rather than waiting until the week before settlement. The sooner the amendment is delivered, the sooner the rescission period expires, and the less likely the closing date will shift.
