## Understanding New York Dual Agency & Designated Agency Rules 2026

New York's agency disclosure framework remains one of the most prescriptive in the country. If you hold a license here, the New York dual agency & designated agency rules 2026 demand your attention every time a transaction involves your brokerage on both sides of the deal. The governing statutes—Article 12-A of the Real Property Law and Title 19 NYCRR Part 175—spell out exactly when disclosure must happen, what form you hand over, and how consent must be documented.

Getting this wrong is not an abstract risk. The New York Department of State (DOS) has revoked and suspended licenses specifically over undisclosed dual agency. Understanding the mechanics protects your commission, your license, and your clients.

## The Statutory Framework: Article 12-A and 19 NYCRR 175

**New York Real Property Law Section 443 mandates that every agent disclose their agency relationship in writing at first substantive contact.** This is not optional and not waivable. The statute defines "substantive contact" broadly enough to include the first conversation where a buyer discusses specific properties or a seller discusses listing terms.

The regulation at 19 NYCRR 175.7 goes further, requiring that dual agency consent be "informed"—meaning the parties must understand what they give up when a single agent or brokerage represents both sides. The DOS interprets this to mean the agent must explain the limitations on confidentiality and undivided loyalty before the client signs.

Section 443(4) of the Real Property Law lists the specific duties owed under each relationship type: seller's agent, buyer's agent, broker's agent, and dual agent. The dual agent section explicitly states that the agent cannot advocate for one party's position over the other on price, terms, or conditions.

## Dual Agency vs. Designated Agency: The Operational Difference

Many agents conflate dual agency and designated agency. They are distinct arrangements with different disclosure obligations and different fiduciary consequences.

| Feature | Dual Agency | Designated Agency |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Who represents both parties | A single licensee | Two different licensees within the same brokerage |
| Fiduciary duty | Limited and equal to both parties | Full fiduciary to each party's assigned agent |
| Confidentiality | Cannot share either party's negotiating position | Each agent keeps their client's information confidential from the other agent |
| Required consent | Written consent from both buyer and seller | Written consent from both parties plus broker's written designation |
| Common scenario | Solo agent has both listing and buyer | Large brokerage where listing agent and buyer's agent share a broker of record |

**Designated agency allows the brokerage to maintain full advocacy for each client, which is why many firms prefer it operationally.** The supervising broker, however, becomes a dual agent by default and must remain neutral. That broker cannot share confidential information between the two designated agents.

In practice, most New York brokerages with more than a handful of agents encounter designated agency situations regularly. The compliance burden falls on the broker of record to properly document the designation and ensure no information leaks between the two sides. ## Required Forms and Timing

The DOS-1736 form—officially titled "Disclosure Regarding Real Estate Agency Relationships"—is the backbone of New York's disclosure system. This form must be presented at first substantive contact, not at contract signing, not at the open house sign-in sheet, and not when the offer is submitted.

Here is the required timing for each form and disclosure step:

| Disclosure Step | When It Must Happen | Form or Document |
|-----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Agency relationship disclosure | First substantive contact | DOS-1736 |
| Dual agency informed consent | Before acting as dual agent | Written consent (no state-mandated form number, but must meet 19 NYCRR 175.7 standards) |
| Designated agency broker designation | Before representation begins on both sides | Written broker designation letter or internal form |
| Confirmation of agency at contract | At or before signing the purchase contract | Agency confirmation within the contract or as a rider |

One point that trips up agents: the DOS-1736 is a disclosure form, not a consent form. It explains the types of agency relationships that exist. Dual agency consent requires a separate acknowledgment where both parties affirmatively agree to the arrangement after understanding its limitations.

The [New York Department of State's Division of Licensing Services](https://dos.ny.gov/licensing-services) publishes the current DOS-1736 form and periodically updates its guidance memos on agency disclosure compliance.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make

**Mistake one: treating the DOS-1736 as a one-time formality instead of a dynamic obligation.** If your relationship with the client changes—say you listed a property and now your buyer client wants to make an offer on it—you must re-disclose and obtain dual agency consent at that moment. The original disclosure does not cover a shift in relationship type.

Mistake two: failing to explain what dual agency actually means in plain language. The DOS expects "informed" consent. Handing someone a form and saying "sign here" without walking through the fact that you cannot advocate for their negotiating position is a violation. The DOS has cited agents specifically for this in disciplinary proceedings.

Mistake three: assuming designated agency eliminates all conflict. The supervising broker still operates as a dual agent. If that broker coaches one designated agent on the other client's motivation or financial position, the brokerage has breached its duty. Brokers who also personally produce deals face heightened exposure here.

Mistake four: not keeping signed copies in the transaction file. If a complaint reaches the DOS and you cannot produce the signed disclosure, the presumption works against you. Many agents rely on memory or verbal acknowledgment—neither holds up in a disciplinary hearing.

Mistake five: disclosing at the wrong time in rental transactions. Section 443-a extends agency disclosure requirements to rental transactions involving properties with four or more units. Agents who only think about agency disclosure in sales contexts miss this entirely and expose themselves to violations on every lease deal.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance operate on three levels: regulatory, civil, and transactional.

At the regulatory level, the DOS can impose fines up to $1,000 per violation, suspend a license for a definite period, or revoke a license entirely. Repeat offenders and those who demonstrate a pattern of non-disclosure face the harshest outcomes. The DOS publishes disciplinary actions, meaning the reputational damage is public and permanent.

At the civil level, an aggrieved party can sue for damages. Courts have ordered commission disgorgement where an agent acted as an undisclosed dual agent. The theory is straightforward: if the client never consented to dual agency, the agent's entitlement to compensation from that client evaporates.

At the transactional level, a party who discovers undisclosed dual agency after contract signing may seek rescission. New York courts have granted rescission where the lack of disclosure materially affected the party's decision-making—particularly on price. This means the deal can be unwound entirely, and the agent loses not just commission but potentially faces liability for the other party's damages from the failed transaction.

The risk calculus is simple. A few minutes of proper disclosure protects months of work. For agents managing [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), building disclosure checkpoints into every file opening is non-negotiable.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers of record carry personal liability for their agents' disclosure failures under New York law. The DOS holds brokers responsible for "adequate supervision," which includes ensuring every transaction file contains the appropriate signed disclosures.

First, brokers should audit every active file quarterly—at minimum—for the presence of a signed DOS-1736 dated on or before first substantive contact. If the date on the form is after the first showing or the first negotiation discussion, the disclosure was late.

Second, brokers must maintain a written policy on dual agency and designated agency that agents sign upon joining the brokerage. This policy should specify exactly how designated agency is triggered internally, who designates, and what information barriers exist between the two sides.

Third, brokers should implement a system that flags any transaction where both the listing agent and the cooperating agent belong to the same brokerage. This flag should automatically trigger designated agency protocols, including the broker's written designation and separate consent from both clients.

Fourth, brokers who personally produce deals must be especially careful. When the broker of record is also the listing agent and another agent in the firm brings a buyer, the broker cannot simultaneously serve as the neutral supervisor and the seller's advocate. Many small brokerages encounter this problem without recognizing it.

Fifth, training cannot be a one-time onboarding event. Annual refreshers on agency disclosure rules—timed to coincide with license renewal cycles—keep agents current and create a documented training record that brokers can point to in the event of a DOS inquiry.
