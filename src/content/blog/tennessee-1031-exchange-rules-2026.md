## Tennessee 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: The Ground Rules

Few topics generate as much confusion at the closing table as Tennessee 1031 exchange state tax implications 2026. Agents routinely assume that because Tennessee eliminated its Hall Income Tax in 2021, there is nothing left to worry about at the state level. That assumption can cost a client thousands of dollars and, in some cases, cost an agent their credibility—or worse.

Tennessee's tax framework interacts with IRC Section 1031 differently than most states, and the distinctions matter most when the exchanging party is a business entity rather than an individual. This post breaks down the statutes, the forms, the pitfalls, and the broker-level oversight that keeps deals clean.

## How Tennessee's Tax Structure Intersects with Section 1031

**Tennessee is one of nine states that levies no broad-based individual income tax, which means individual investors completing a like-kind exchange face zero state income tax on deferred capital gains.** The Hall Income Tax, codified under Tennessee Code Annotated Section 67-2-102, was fully repealed effective January 1, 2021. That repeal eliminated the last vestige of individual investment income taxation in the state.

But here is where agents trip: Tennessee still imposes a franchise and excise tax on most business entities doing business in the state. The excise tax rate is 6.5 percent of net earnings, and the franchise tax is 0.25 percent of the greater of net worth or real and tangible property in Tennessee. These are governed by T.C.A. Section 67-4-2007 and reported on [Tennessee Department of Revenue](https://www.tn.gov/revenue) Form FAE 170.

When an LLC, partnership, S-corporation, or C-corporation executes a 1031 exchange, the gain deferral recognized at the federal level generally flows through to the Tennessee franchise and excise tax return. If the exchange is properly structured and qualifies under IRC 1031, the gain is deferred for excise tax purposes as well. If it fails, the gain hits the entity's Tennessee return immediately.

## The Forms and Filing Obligations Agents Must Understand

Individual exchangers have no Tennessee filing requirement related to their 1031 exchange. There is no state-level equivalent of IRS Form 8824.

| Party Type | Federal Form | Tennessee Form | State Tax Exposure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Individual investor | IRS Form 8824 | None required | None (no state income tax) |
| LLC/Partnership | IRS Form 8824 + Schedule K-1 | FAE 170 (with Schedule J) | 6.5% excise tax if exchange fails |
| S-Corporation | IRS Form 8824 + 1120-S | FAE 170 | 6.5% excise + 0.25% franchise |
| C-Corporation | IRS Form 8824 + 1120 | FAE 170 | 6.5% excise + 0.25% franchise |

Agents do not file these forms themselves, but they need to understand which clients carry state-level exposure. When you list a property owned by a Tennessee LLC—which accounts for a substantial portion of investment real estate in the state—your client's CPA must be looped in early to coordinate the exchange timeline with the FAE 170 filing deadline.

The FAE 170 is due on the 15th day of the fourth month following the entity's fiscal year end. For calendar-year entities, that means April 15. Extensions push the deadline to October 15 but do not extend the time to pay estimated tax.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Flag State-Level Issues

**An agent who ignores the franchise and excise tax dimension of a 1031 exchange risks more than client dissatisfaction—they risk a complaint to the Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC).** Under TREC Rule 1260-02-.12, agents must disclose material facts and exercise reasonable skill and care. Failing to advise a client that their entity structure creates state tax obligations during an exchange could be construed as a competence violation.

The consequences scale from informal to severe. TREC can issue a letter of warning, impose civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation under T.C.A. Section 62-13-313, suspend or revoke a license, or require additional continuing education. Beyond regulatory action, an agent faces potential civil liability if the client incurs unexpected tax because the deal timeline was mismanaged.

Deal cancellation is also on the table. If an exchange fails because the agent missed a deadline or failed to coordinate with the qualified intermediary, the buyer may walk if contingencies allow it. Even without a contingency escape, the transaction economics change so drastically that the seller may refuse to close.

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Agents Make with 1031 Exchanges

The errors below are not hypothetical. They surface repeatedly in transactions across Memphis, Nashville, Knoxville, and Chattanooga.

**First, agents assume all Tennessee sellers are individuals.** A property titled in a single-member LLC is still subject to franchise and excise tax unless it qualifies for a specific exemption under T.C.A. Section 67-4-2008. Agents must check the vesting on the deed before advising a client that "Tennessee has no state tax on this."

Second, agents confuse the 45-day identification period with a Tennessee-specific requirement. Tennessee imposes no additional state timeline beyond the federal 45-day and 180-day windows. However, some agents—particularly those who previously practiced in states like California or New York—incorrectly tell clients that Tennessee requires separate state-level identification notices. This creates unnecessary paperwork and, worse, false urgency that can lead to poor replacement property decisions.

Third, agents fail to verify that the qualified intermediary is properly bonded and insured. Tennessee does not license or regulate qualified intermediaries at the state level. Unlike states such as Washington or Nevada, there is no registration requirement. That means the burden falls entirely on the agent and client to vet the intermediary's financial stability. If the QI goes bankrupt holding exchange funds, no Tennessee regulatory body will intervene.

Fourth, agents neglect to coordinate closing dates between the relinquished property sale and the replacement property purchase. When an agent handles both legs in Tennessee, the [common deadline failures](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) that plague standard transactions become exponentially more damaging in an exchange context.

Fifth, agents forget that Tennessee's transfer tax—known as the recordation tax under T.C.A. Section 67-4-409—still applies to both legs of the exchange. The rate is $0.37 per $100 of consideration. A 1031 exchange does not exempt the transaction from recordation tax, and agents who tell clients otherwise create immediate liability at closing.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under TREC Rule 1260-02-.02 for every transaction their agents touch. When 1031 exchanges are involved, that responsibility intensifies because the downstream consequences of error are larger and more complex.

| Broker Audit Point | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Entity verification | Confirm vesting matches contract; flag LLCs for tax coordination | Every exchange transaction |
| QI documentation | Verify exchange agreement, EIN, and proof of bonding | Before close of relinquished property |
| Timeline tracking | Confirm 45-day ID deadline and 180-day close deadline in file | Weekly during active exchange |
| Recordation tax disclosure | Ensure both legs reflect transfer tax in estimated settlement statements | At contract and at closing |
| TREC disclosure compliance | Verify agent provided material fact disclosures regarding tax implications | File review within 30 days of close |

**Brokers who treat 1031 exchange files like standard resale files are leaving their firms exposed.** A single audit failure—say, a missing exchange agreement in the transaction file—can trigger a TREC audit finding that cascades into additional scrutiny of the entire brokerage.

The smartest brokers in Tennessee are building exchange-specific checklists into their transaction management systems. If your brokerage still relies on memory or generic templates, that gap becomes a liability the moment TREC conducts a routine audit. Understanding [why checklists beat memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) in transaction management is not optional—it is the baseline for professional practice.

## Out-of-State Sellers and Tennessee Withholding

Tennessee does not impose a withholding requirement on out-of-state sellers the way states like California (via Form 593) or Georgia (via Form IT-AFF2) do. There is no Tennessee equivalent of a non-resident withholding obligation at closing. However, if an out-of-state entity sells Tennessee real property and the exchange fails, that entity may owe franchise and excise tax on the gain.

Agents working with out-of-state investors should document in writing—ideally through a simple disclosure email—that the client is responsible for determining their own state tax obligations in both Tennessee and their home state. This does not constitute tax advice; it constitutes material fact disclosure. The [FIRPTA-related withholding considerations](/blog/washington-firpta-withholding-rules-2026) that apply at the federal level are separate from any state obligation but often overlap in the same transaction.

## Practical Steps for Tennessee Agents in 2026

The actionable framework for agents handling exchange transactions comes down to timing, documentation, and referral.

On timing: confirm the 45-day identification deadline and 180-day closing deadline in writing with the client and the QI on the day the relinquished property closes. Put calendar holds in your CRM and follow up at day 30.

On documentation: maintain a separate section in the transaction file for exchange-related documents. This includes the exchange agreement, the QI's written assignment of the purchase contract, identification notices, and proof that the replacement property closed within 180 days.

On referral: do not attempt to answer tax questions beyond your scope. Tennessee agents are not CPAs. Your role is to flag the issue, document the referral, and ensure the transaction timeline supports the exchange structure.
