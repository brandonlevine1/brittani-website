## Understanding Georgia Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Georgia's transfer tax and recording fee structure has remained remarkably stable compared to states that index fees to inflation, but that stability does not excuse agents from knowing the exact numbers. The Georgia transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 affect every residential closing in the state, and a single miscalculation on a settlement statement can delay recording, trigger title defects, or expose your brokerage to liability. Agents who treat these as "the closing attorney's problem" are making a dangerous assumption.

The governing statute for Georgia's real estate transfer tax is O.C.G.A. § 48-6-1, administered by the Georgia Department of Revenue. Recording fees fall under O.C.G.A. § 15-6-77, which grants each county's Clerk of Superior Court the authority to collect fees for filing instruments. Both statutes work in tandem at closing, and both require specific documentation that agents should understand—even if they never personally file the forms.

## The Transfer Tax: Rate, Calculation, and Who Pays

**Georgia imposes a real estate transfer tax of $1.00 per $1,000 of the actual sale price, with no surtax at the county level.** This is sometimes expressed as $0.10 per $100 or one mill per dollar. Unlike states such as Florida or New York, Georgia does not layer additional documentary stamp taxes or mansion taxes on top of the base rate.

The tax applies to the full consideration paid, not the assessed value or the loan amount. For a home selling at $425,000, the transfer tax equals $425.00. The amount is declared on Georgia Department of Revenue Form PT-61 (Real Estate Transfer Tax Declaration), which must be filed with the Clerk of Superior Court in the county where the property is located before or at the time of recording.

By custom and standard GAR contract language, the seller pays the transfer tax in Georgia. However, this allocation is negotiable—and agents should verify contract terms rather than relying on local custom. GAR Form F20 (Purchase and Sale Agreement) includes a section specifying responsibility for closing costs, and any deviation from the default should be reflected there.

## Recording Fees by Document Type

Recording fees in Georgia are set by O.C.G.A. § 15-6-77 and vary slightly by county based on the number of pages filed. The base fee structure applies statewide, but additional pages carry per-page surcharges. Below is the standard fee schedule that agents should reference when estimating buyer and seller costs on a preliminary net sheet.

| Document Type | Base Fee (First Page) | Each Additional Page | Typical Payer |
|---|---|---|---|
| Warranty Deed | $25.00 | $2.00 | Buyer |
| Security Deed (Mortgage) | $25.00 | $2.00 | Buyer |
| Deed to Secure Debt (Refinance) | $25.00 | $2.00 | Borrower |
| Cancellation of Security Deed | $25.00 | $2.00 | Seller |
| Affidavit | $25.00 | $2.00 | Varies |
| Lis Pendens | $25.00 | $2.00 | Filing Party |

Some counties charge an additional intangible tax on new mortgages at a rate of $1.50 per $500 of new debt secured by Georgia real property, per O.C.G.A. § 48-6-61. This is separate from the transfer tax and often catches agents off guard when preparing buyer estimates. On a $340,000 loan, the intangible tax totals $1,020.00—a figure that absolutely must appear on the buyer's Closing Disclosure.

## The PT-61 Form: Filing Requirements and Deadlines

The PT-61 is the single most important form in Georgia's transfer tax process. It requires the property's legal description, sale price, tax map/parcel identification number, and the signatures of both the grantor and grantee (or their attorneys-in-fact). The form is submitted to the Clerk of Superior Court along with the deed and the transfer tax payment.

**Filing the PT-61 is a prerequisite to recording the deed—no PT-61 means no recording.** The Clerk will reject any deed submitted without an accompanying PT-61 and the correct transfer tax amount. Georgia does not impose a separate filing deadline beyond the practical requirement that the deed must be recorded to perfect the buyer's title, but most closing attorneys record within 24 to 72 hours of closing.

Agents should note that the PT-61 has a checkbox for claimed exemptions. Exempt transactions—including foreclosure deeds, transfers between certain family members under O.C.G.A. § 48-6-2, and deeds in lieu of foreclosure—still require a PT-61 to be filed, even though no tax is due. Failing to file the form on exempt transactions is a common oversight that [delays deal closings](/blog/where-deals-break-down) and creates unnecessary title clouds.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Georgia Transfer Tax and Recording Fees

Experienced agents still trip over these errors year after year. Each one carries real financial or timeline consequences.

**First, agents routinely underestimate buyer costs by omitting the intangible tax from preliminary net sheets.** The intangible tax on new financing is not optional and is not waived for first-time buyers. When a buyer sees an unexpected $1,000-plus line item at the closing table, it erodes trust and occasionally kills deals that were already running tight on cash-to-close. Agents who prepare accurate estimates early—factoring both recording fees and intangible tax—protect themselves from last-minute fallout.

Second, agents confuse the transfer tax with the intangible tax and list them as a single line item. These are separate obligations governed by different code sections (O.C.G.A. § 48-6-1 versus O.C.G.A. § 48-6-61), paid by different parties, and calculated on different bases. The transfer tax is based on the sale price; the intangible tax is based on the new loan amount. Conflating them on a net sheet is not just sloppy—it can constitute a misrepresentation of closing costs under GREC Rule 520-1-.09.

Third, listing agents fail to account for cancellation recording fees when estimating seller proceeds. Every existing security deed that must be cancelled requires its own recording fee. Sellers with multiple liens—a first mortgage, a HELOC, and perhaps a mechanics lien release—can face $75 to $150 in cancellation recording fees alone. This matters on tight-equity deals where the seller is counting every dollar.

Fourth, agents on exempt transactions skip the PT-61 entirely, assuming no tax means no form. The Georgia Department of Revenue still requires the declaration for tracking purposes. The Clerk's office will not record the deed without it, and the resulting delay can [blow a closing timeline](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) when the buyer's rate lock or lease termination is time-sensitive.

Fifth, agents working in counties near state borders occasionally apply neighboring states' transfer tax rates or assume Georgia has a county-level surtax. Georgia's rate is uniform statewide at $1.00 per $1,000—no county surcharges exist. Applying a higher rate inflates seller costs unnecessarily and creates discrepancies that must be corrected at the closing table.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under O.C.G.A. § 43-40-18 for the acts of their affiliated licensees. When an agent provides inaccurate closing cost estimates that result in client harm, the broker's license is at stake alongside the agent's. A few targeted audit practices can mitigate this exposure.

Brokers should require that every preliminary net sheet generated by their agents includes a line for Georgia transfer tax (seller side) and separate lines for recording fees and intangible tax (buyer side). Reviewing a random sample of net sheets monthly takes less than an hour and catches systemic errors before they reach closing tables. Any net sheet that combines transfer tax and intangible tax into one line should be flagged and corrected immediately.

**Brokers should also verify that agents are using current GAR forms—specifically the 2026 editions of Form F20 and the associated exhibits—when allocating closing costs in contracts.** Outdated form versions may contain incorrect default language about which party bears transfer tax responsibility, creating ambiguity that opposing counsel will exploit during closing. The [Georgia Association of Realtors](https://www.garealtor.com) publishes form updates annually, and brokerages should maintain a form version log.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Risk if Skipped |
|---|---|---|
| Net sheet accuracy (transfer tax + intangible tax separated) | Monthly sample | Client misrepresentation claims |
| GAR form version verification | Quarterly | Contract ambiguity, renegotiation delays |
| PT-61 filing confirmation on exempt deals | Per transaction | Recording rejection, title cloud |
| Cancellation fee inclusion for multi-lien sellers | Per transaction | Seller shortfall at closing |
| Agent training on O.C.G.A. § 48-6-1 updates | Annually | Systemic compliance failures |

Training new agents on transfer tax mechanics should happen during onboarding, not after their first closing goes sideways. Brokerages that build fee schedule reviews into their [new agent training timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) see fewer GREC complaints and fewer closing delays attributable to cost miscalculations.

## Liability Exposure for Non-Compliance

The Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) has enforcement authority over licensees who misrepresent transaction costs or fail to exercise reasonable care in preparing documents. Under GREC Rule 520-1-.09, providing materially inaccurate cost estimates to a client can result in a formal complaint, investigation, and sanctions ranging from a letter of reprimand to license revocation.

Beyond regulatory exposure, agents face civil liability when inaccurate fee disclosures cause financial harm. A seller who nets $2,000 less than their agent's estimate due to omitted transfer tax has a viable breach-of-fiduciary-duty claim. A buyer who cannot close because the agent failed to disclose intangible tax—pushing cash-to-close above available funds—may pursue damages for lost earnest money, inspection costs, and appraisal fees.

Recording rejections also carry downstream consequences. If a deed is rejected because the PT-61 was not filed or the transfer tax amount was incorrect, the buyer's title insurance policy cannot issue. The lender's closing conditions remain unsatisfied. In competitive markets, even a 48-hour recording delay can cascade into a failed chain of transactions—particularly when the buyer's purchase was contingent on the sale of another property that also needs to record.
