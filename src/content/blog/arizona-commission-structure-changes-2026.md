The Arizona commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 have reshaped how every licensed agent in this state earns, discloses, and documents compensation. If you closed deals last year using pre-settlement habits, you are already behind on compliance obligations that the Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE) and participating MLSs now actively enforce. This post breaks down what the rules actually require, which forms you must use, and what happens when agents or brokers get it wrong.

## How the NAR Settlement Changed Commission Rules in Arizona

The national settlement between the National Association of Realtors and plaintiffs in the Sitzer/Burnett litigation eliminated the cooperative compensation model that had been embedded in MLS systems for decades. For Arizona agents, this meant the Arizona Regional Multiple Listing Service (ARMLS) and all other state MLSs removed the field where listing agents could offer compensation to buyer agents.

That single change triggered a cascade of compliance requirements. Buyer agents must now establish their compensation in a written agreement with their client before providing services. Sellers who want to contribute toward buyer agent fees must do so through a separate, documented negotiation—not through the MLS data field.

The ADRE did not pass new statutes specifically for the settlement, but existing Arizona Revised Statutes governing agency, disclosure, and fiduciary duty now apply with sharper teeth. A.R.S. 32-2153 (grounds for disciplinary action) and A.R.S. 32-2175 (broker supervision requirements) are the two statutory anchors that regulators use when agents fail to follow the new commission disclosure protocols.

## Written Buyer Agreements: The Non-Negotiable Requirement

**Every buyer agent in Arizona must now execute a written buyer representation agreement before touring properties, attending open houses on the buyer's behalf, or submitting offers.** The Arizona Association of Realtors (AAR) updated its Buyer Broker Exclusive Employment Agreement form in late 2024 to reflect settlement requirements. The form explicitly states the buyer agent's compensation amount or rate and identifies who is expected to pay it.

The agreement must include three settlement-mandated elements: a specific and conspicuous disclosure of the amount or rate of compensation, a statement that agent compensation is not set by law and is fully negotiable, and a prohibition on the agent receiving compensation that exceeds the amount stated in the agreement (no earning more from a seller concession than what the buyer agreed to pay). Missing any one of these elements renders the agreement non-compliant.

Arizona agents should be aware that the AAR form satisfies these requirements when filled out completely. Leaving blanks—particularly in the compensation section—does not create a valid agreement and exposes both agent and broker to ADRE disciplinary review.

## Arizona Forms You Must Know and Use Correctly

The paperwork burden increased after the settlement, but the AAR released updated forms to keep agents within compliance boundaries. Here are the primary documents involved in commission documentation:

| Form Name | Form Number / Identifier | Purpose |
|-----------|--------------------------|---------|
| Buyer Broker Exclusive Employment Agreement | AAR Buyer Broker Agreement (revised 2024) | Establishes buyer agent compensation, term, and duties |
| Compensation Disclosure and Consent | AAR Compensation Disclosure | Discloses to buyer who is paying agent and how much |
| Seller Instruction to Cooperating Broker | AAR Seller Instruction Form | Documents seller's agreement to pay buyer agent compensation off-MLS |
| Residential Resale Purchase Contract | AAR Purchase Contract (Section 8) | Contains broker compensation acknowledgment provisions |
| Buyer Advisory (updated) | AAR Buyer Advisory | Advises buyer of right to negotiate compensation terms |

The Seller Instruction to Cooperating Broker form is particularly important because it replaced the function that the MLS compensation field once served. When a seller agrees to pay buyer agent fees, this form documents the specific amount, the conditions, and the seller's informed consent. Without it, there is no enforceable obligation for the seller to pay the buyer's agent.

Section 8 of the AAR Purchase Contract now includes expanded language about compensation. Agents must ensure this section is completed accurately and consistently with both the buyer agreement and any seller instruction form. Contradictions between these documents create liability exposure that [brokers responsible for supervision](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) cannot ignore.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Non-compliance with Arizona commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 carries consequences across three dimensions: regulatory, transactional, and civil.

**From a regulatory standpoint, the ADRE can initiate disciplinary proceedings under A.R.S. 32-2153.** Grounds include failure to disclose material information to a client, acting without proper written authorization, and conduct that demonstrates incompetence or untrustworthiness. Penalties range from a letter of concern to license suspension or revocation, with fines up to $10,000 per violation under the Commissioner's authority.

On the transactional side, a buyer who discovers their agent did not properly disclose compensation terms may have grounds to rescind the purchase contract. Arizona courts have long recognized that undisclosed conflicts of interest involving agent compensation can constitute fraud or breach of fiduciary duty. A rescission unwinds the deal entirely—earnest money returns, and the agent earns nothing.

Civil liability is the third risk. Buyers who paid more than they agreed to—or who were steered toward listings offering higher off-MLS compensation—can sue for damages. The [Arizona Department of Real Estate](https://azre.gov/) has publicly stated it will investigate complaints related to settlement compliance failures, and plaintiff attorneys in Arizona are already advertising for these claims.

## Common Mistakes Arizona Agents Are Making Right Now

After eighteen months of settlement implementation, clear patterns of non-compliance have emerged among Arizona agents. These are not edge cases—they are widespread errors that ADRE investigators and MLS compliance teams are flagging.

**First, agents are showing homes before obtaining a signed buyer agreement.** This violates both the settlement terms and ARMLS rules. The rationalization is usually that the buyer "isn't ready to commit," but the rule contains no exception for casual showings. If you open a lockbox, you need a signed agreement in your file.

Second, agents are using vague compensation language like "up to 3%" or "to be determined" in buyer agreements. The settlement requires a specific amount. An open-ended range does not satisfy the requirement and leaves the agent vulnerable to a claim that the agreement was never properly executed.

Third, listing agents are verbally communicating buyer agent compensation offers to cooperating agents without any written documentation. A phone call saying "my seller will pay your buyer's agent 2.5%" means nothing without the AAR Seller Instruction form signed by the seller. Verbal agreements about commission are unenforceable under Arizona's statute of frauds for real estate transactions.

Fourth, agents are failing to reconcile compensation terms across multiple documents. The buyer agreement says 2.5%, the seller instruction form says 2%, and the purchase contract says nothing. These contradictions create disputes at closing that title companies will not resolve—they simply hold funds in escrow until the parties sort it out or litigate.

Fifth, some agents are structuring seller concessions to disguise buyer agent compensation, labeling it as "closing cost credits" that the buyer then uses to pay their agent. While [creative structuring of closing costs](/blog/where-deals-break-down) is not inherently illegal, doing it to circumvent disclosure requirements violates fiduciary duty rules and the spirit of the settlement.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Arizona bear personal statutory responsibility for the transactions conducted under their license. A.R.S. 32-2175 requires brokers to establish policies and procedures that ensure compliance with all applicable laws and rules. Post-settlement, this means brokers must actively audit commission documentation—not merely trust that agents are handling it correctly.

| Audit Point | What to Verify | Frequency |
|-------------|----------------|-----------|
| Buyer agreement execution | Signed before first showing, specific compensation stated | Every transaction |
| Compensation consistency | Buyer agreement matches purchase contract matches seller instruction | Every transaction |
| MLS compliance | No commission language in listing remarks or agent-only fields | Weekly listing review |
| Disclosure timing | Compensation disclosure provided before offer submission | Every buyer transaction |
| File completeness | All required forms present and properly dated | Quarterly random audit |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance are gambling with their own licenses. The ADRE does not accept "I didn't know my agent was doing that" as a defense when a broker's supervision duty is at issue. Implementing systematic file review—whether through manual checklists or [automated transaction coordination tools](/blog/will-ai-replace-tcs)—is no longer optional for brokerages with more than a handful of agents.
