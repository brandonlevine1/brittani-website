## Why Florida Teams & Supervision Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

The Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) has sharpened its enforcement posture around team structures, and the consequences for non-compliance in 2026 are not theoretical. Florida teams & supervision rules 2026 affect everything from how you advertise a team name to who signs off on transaction documents. The Division of Real Estate under DBPR treats improper supervision as one of the most common grounds for disciplinary proceedings against brokerages statewide.

Florida's real estate team model has exploded over the past five years, but statutory authority has not expanded the rights of sales associates to supervise one another. Chapter 475 of the Florida Statutes still places all supervisory obligations squarely on the shoulders of the qualifying broker or a designated broker associate. That single point of accountability means brokers who allow teams to self-govern are accumulating risk they may not see until a complaint lands on their desk.

This post breaks down the statutes, the forms, the penalties, and the operational mistakes that lead to enforcement actions—so you can run your team or brokerage without a target on your license.

## The Statutory Framework: Chapter 475 and Rule 61J2

**Florida Statute Section 475.01(1)(a) defines a broker as the party who holds ultimate responsibility for all licensees registered under their license.** Sales associates and broker associates cannot operate independently of that supervision structure, regardless of how the team's internal hierarchy is branded to the public. Section 475.25(1)(u) specifically authorizes discipline against brokers who fail to maintain proper supervision over their associated licensees.

Rule 61J2-10.038 of the Florida Administrative Code governs advertising by teams. It requires that any team or group name used in advertising be registered as a fictitious name through the brokerage. The rule further mandates that the brokerage name appear in all advertising in a manner that is at least as prominent as the team name. This is not a suggestion—it is a regulatory requirement enforced through complaint-driven audits and DBPR's own monitoring.

The Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) has published guidance clarifying that a "team leader" who is a sales associate holds no statutory authority to supervise, train, or discipline other licensees. Any arrangement that delegates those functions to a non-broker violates Chapter 475. Brokers who allow this delegation—even informally—are exposed to the same penalties as if they had abandoned supervision entirely.

## Advertising and Name Registration Requirements

Every team operating under a Florida brokerage must register its team name using [Form DBPR RE-54](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/intentions2.asp?chession=), the Application for Registration of Fictitious Name. This form routes through the qualifying broker, who must authorize the use of the name. Operating under an unregistered team name is a violation that can trigger fines and forced cessation of all marketing materials bearing that name.

| Requirement | Team Name | Brokerage Name |
|---|---|---|
| Must appear in all ads | Yes | Yes |
| Font size/prominence | Any size | Equal to or larger than team name |
| Registration form | DBPR RE-54 | Already registered via DBPR RE-13 |
| Who authorizes | Qualifying broker | Owner/qualifying broker |
| Renewal | Per brokerage policy | Biennial license renewal |

The distinction between a team name and a d/b/a for the brokerage itself matters. A brokerage registers its own trade name via Form DBPR RE-13. A team name is subordinate to that registration. If your team's signage, social media profiles, or print ads display the team name without the brokerage name—or display the brokerage name in smaller type—you are out of compliance with Rule 61J2-10.038.

**Agents who run paid digital ads under only a team name are among the most frequently cited violators in DBPR disciplinary reports.** Google Ads, Facebook campaigns, and Instagram profiles all count as advertising under Florida law. The Division does not distinguish between a billboard and a boosted post.

## Supervision Obligations: What Brokers Actually Owe

The qualifying broker's supervision duty under Section 475.25(1)(u) is not satisfied by merely collecting a share of commission. FREC expects active, documented oversight of transaction files, advertising compliance, trust fund handling, and client communications. A broker who delegates file review to a team leader who holds only a sales associate license has not met this standard.

Supervision includes reviewing contracts before execution when the brokerage's policies require it, ensuring proper use of Florida Realtors/Florida Bar forms (such as the FR/Bar "As-Is" Contract, Form FR/BAR-ASIS-6), and confirming that all escrow deposits reach the proper trust account within the timeframes mandated by Section 475.25(1)(d). If a team member mishandles a deposit and the broker had no system in place to catch it, the broker faces discipline—not just the agent.

Brokers who manage multiple teams should build an internal audit cadence that mirrors DBPR's own expectations. Quarterly file reviews, monthly advertising audits, and real-time deposit tracking are the minimum. If you are managing [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) across several teams, the complexity multiplies and so does the risk.

## Common Mistakes That Trigger FREC Disciplinary Action

**The following five errors account for the majority of team-related FREC complaints filed against Florida licensees.** Each one is preventable with proper systems and broker engagement.

First, team leaders acting as de facto supervisors without a broker's license. This happens when a top-producing sales associate hires assistants, brings on buyer's agents, and begins reviewing their files, approving their marketing, and handling escalations. None of those functions are lawful for a sales associate to perform over another licensee. The qualifying broker must retain and exercise that authority.

Second, advertising that omits or minimizes the brokerage name. DBPR's enforcement division has issued citations for team websites where the brokerage name appeared only in the footer, in a font size significantly smaller than the team brand. Social media "about" sections that list the team name without any brokerage reference also trigger violations.

Third, failure to register the team name via Form DBPR RE-54. Some teams operate for months or years under an unregistered name, only discovering the gap during a transaction dispute or license renewal audit. The fix is simple but must be initiated by the qualifying broker.

Fourth, improper escrow deposit handling within teams. When a team's transaction coordinator or unlicensed assistant accepts a deposit check and fails to deliver it to the broker's trust account within the required timeframe, the broker—not the team leader—bears the regulatory consequence under Section 475.25(1)(d).

Fifth, confusion over who communicates with clients on behalf of the brokerage. If a team member provides legal advice, quotes title fees without proper attribution, or misrepresents their role in the transaction, the supervising broker is liable for the misrepresentation under Section 475.25(1)(b). Training new agents on these boundaries is not optional; it is part of the supervision mandate. The [timeline for onboarding new agents](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) should include compliance checkpoints from day one.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers need a documented compliance program—not just good intentions. FREC's disciplinary history shows that "I didn't know my agent was doing that" is never a successful defense. Here is what a defensible audit program includes for team oversight in 2026.

| Audit Area | Frequency | What to Check |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising compliance | Monthly | All digital and print ads include brokerage name at required prominence |
| Team name registration | At team creation + annually | DBPR RE-54 on file and current |
| Escrow deposit tracking | Per transaction | Deposits delivered to trust account within statutory deadline |
| File supervision | Per transaction or weekly | Contracts reviewed, disclosures present, deadlines tracked |
| Unlicensed activity | Quarterly | Assistants not performing licensee-only functions |
| Supervisory delegation | Ongoing | No sales associate exercising broker-level authority |

Brokers should also maintain a written policy manual distributed to every team and signed by each team member acknowledging receipt. This document should specify that the broker—not the team leader—is the final authority on compliance, dispute resolution, and regulatory matters. FREC has referenced the absence of such written policies as an aggravating factor when imposing penalties.

> "A broker's duty of supervision is continuous and cannot be delegated to a non-broker, regardless of the team structure or compensation arrangement." — FREC Declaratory Statement, DS 2019-026

The cost of a failed audit is not abstract. Under Section 475.25, FREC can impose fines of up to $5,000 per count, suspend or revoke licenses, and require mandatory education. In cases involving trust fund violations tied to team mismanagement, the broker's license is at stake—and with it, every agent registered under that license.

## Liability Exposure When Teams Operate Outside the Rules

When a team violates supervision rules, liability cascades upward. The agent who committed the act faces individual discipline. The team leader—if they held themselves out as a supervisor without authority—may face charges of practicing beyond the scope of their license. The qualifying broker faces the most severe consequences because Chapter 475 assigns them the duty that was breached.

Beyond regulatory penalties, civil liability follows. A buyer who suffers damages because a team member provided misleading information without proper broker oversight can name both the agent and the brokerage in a civil suit. Errors and omissions insurance may deny coverage if the policy's cooperation clause required broker supervision that was absent. Deals can be rescinded, deposits returned, and commissions clawed back. Understanding [where deals typically break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) is not just a productivity exercise—it is a risk management function.

Transaction cancellations triggered by compliance failures are particularly painful because they are entirely self-inflicted. A title company that discovers unregistered team advertising or improper agency disclosure may halt closing until the issue is resolved. In competitive markets, a 48-hour delay can kill a deal.
