## Tennessee Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Rule That Trips Up Agents

The question of "Tennessee attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026" keeps surfacing in agent Facebook groups, CE classrooms, and broker compliance meetings — and the confusion is understandable. Tennessee occupies a gray zone: it is neither a pure attorney-close state like Georgia nor a state where title companies operate with zero attorney involvement like many Western states. The answer sits in a patchwork of statutes, Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) rules, and Board of Professional Responsibility opinions that every licensee needs to internalize before their next listing appointment.

Getting this wrong does not merely cause an awkward moment at the closing table. It can expose you to disciplinary action, kill a transaction days before funding, and generate malpractice claims that follow your license for years.

## What Tennessee Law Actually Says About Closing Authority

**Tennessee Code Annotated Section 23-3-101 is the statute that governs who can prepare legal instruments in a real estate transaction.** It classifies the drafting of deeds, deeds of trust, and other conveyancing documents as the practice of law. Only a person licensed to practice law in Tennessee — or someone working directly under an attorney's supervision — may prepare those instruments.

Separately, the Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance regulates title insurance companies under T.C.A. Title 56, Chapter 35. Title companies are authorized to issue title insurance commitments, conduct escrow, disburse funds, and record documents. But the statute draws a hard line: they cannot draft the legal documents that effectuate the transfer unless an attorney is involved.

The practical result is a hybrid system. Most closings in Tennessee involve both a title company (handling escrow, title examination, and insurance) and an attorney (preparing the deed and deed of trust). Some attorneys also serve as the title agent, collapsing both roles into one office. Either arrangement satisfies the law — but the division of duties must be clear.

## How the Roles Break Down: Attorney vs. Title Company

Understanding who handles what prevents agents from accidentally directing clients toward unauthorized services. Here is a side-by-side comparison of permissible functions:

| Function | Licensed Attorney | Title Company (Non-Attorney) |
|----------|:-----------------:|:----------------------------:|
| Draft deed or deed of trust | Yes | No — must be supervised by attorney |
| Conduct title search | Yes | Yes |
| Issue title insurance commitment | Only if also licensed title agent | Yes |
| Hold and disburse escrow funds | Yes (trust account rules apply) | Yes (regulated under T.C.A. 56-35) |
| Record documents at Register's Office | Yes | Yes |
| Advise parties on legal rights | Yes | No — constitutes UPL |
| Prepare closing disclosure (CD) | Yes | Yes |

When an agent tells a buyer "the title company will handle everything," that statement is technically inaccurate in Tennessee unless the title company has in-house or contracted attorney support for document preparation. Training your language to reflect reality protects you.

## The Forms Tennessee Agents Must Know

TREC's standard residential purchase and sale agreement — form RF401 — governs most residential transactions in the state. Section 14 of RF401 addresses the closing process, designating who will serve as the closing agent and how title evidence will be delivered. Agents fill in the blank for the closing entity, but TREC does not mandate that the closing entity be an attorney; it simply must be a party authorized under Tennessee law to perform the required functions.

Form RF201, the exclusive listing agreement, also contains provisions that reference the closing process. Listing agents who pre-select a closing entity in RF201 should confirm that entity can lawfully perform all closing functions — including document preparation — before committing their seller to that choice. A title company without attorney oversight does not qualify.

Additionally, agents should be aware of TREC Rule 1260-02-.12, which prohibits licensees from practicing law or giving legal advice. If you direct a client to a closing entity that engages in unauthorized practice of law, you risk being viewed as a participant. TREC's enforcement division has issued consent orders to agents on this exact issue when clients suffered financial harm from improperly prepared instruments.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Tennessee Closings

**Mistake one: assuming that because a title company is "handling the closing," an attorney is unnecessary.** Many agents in Middle and West Tennessee have closed dozens of deals through title companies without ever seeing an attorney at the table. The attorney may be behind the scenes, reviewing and approving the deed before recording. But when agents do not verify this arrangement, they risk facilitating a closing where no attorney touched the deed — a violation of T.C.A. 23-3-101.

Mistake two: drafting contract addenda or amendment language that goes beyond fill-in-the-blank forms. TREC explicitly limits licensees to completing pre-approved forms. When agents write custom clauses about title defects, closing cost credits, or deed restrictions, they cross into legal practice. The Tennessee Board of Professional Responsibility has issued formal ethics opinions — including [Ethics Opinion 89-F-118](https://www.tbpr.org/) — distinguishing between permissible form completion and unauthorized drafting.

Mistake three: directing clients away from attorney review to "save money" or "speed things up." This constitutes legal advice. If a client later discovers a title defect that an attorney would have caught, the agent's recommendation becomes a liability vector. TREC Rule 1260-02-.12(3)(a) can be cited in a complaint, and E&O insurers have denied coverage when agents actively discouraged attorney involvement.

Mistake four: failing to confirm the closing entity's authority before contract execution. Once RF401 is ratified with a specific closing agent named, changing that entity requires a written amendment signed by all parties. Discovering mid-transaction that the named entity cannot prepare the deed forces a renegotiation — or worse, a missed closing deadline that triggers contractual remedies.

Mistake five: conflating the title insurance commitment with legal review. A title commitment identifies liens and encumbrances, but it does not interpret restrictive covenants, evaluate boundary disputes, or advise on the legal effect of exceptions. Only an attorney can do that. Agents who tell buyers "the title company reviewed everything, you're fine" may be creating implied warranties that expose them to claims.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under T.C.A. Section 62-13-312 and TREC Rule 1260-02-.01. When an agent in your office facilitates a closing that violates the unauthorized practice of law statute, TREC can discipline the broker as well as the agent. Passive ignorance is not a defense.

**Every Tennessee brokerage should maintain a written policy specifying that agents must verify attorney involvement in document preparation before any closing proceeds.** This policy should be part of the office procedures manual referenced in TREC Rule 1260-02-.01(3).

Brokers should audit at least the following elements on a quarterly basis:

First, confirm that every closed transaction file contains evidence that an attorney prepared or supervised preparation of the deed and deed of trust. This can be a letter from the attorney, a notation on the settlement statement, or the attorney's signature block on the recorded instrument.

Second, review any custom language agents have inserted into contracts. If your file reviews reveal addenda with original legal language — rather than text from TREC-approved forms — that is a compliance flag. Provide remedial training and document the correction.

Third, verify that agents are not receiving referral fees, kickbacks, or undisclosed compensation from closing attorneys or title companies. RESPA Section 8 prohibitions apply nationally, and TREC enforces them under state law as well. A broker who discovers undisclosed affiliated business arrangements faces both federal and state exposure.

For brokers managing [large teams or high-volume offices](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory), building an audit cadence into your transaction coordination workflow is the most efficient way to catch problems before they become complaints.

## Liability Consequences When Agents Get This Wrong

The penalties for facilitating unauthorized practice of law in Tennessee are not theoretical. TREC consent orders are public record, and recent enforcement actions have included fines up to $1,000 per violation, mandatory continuing education hours, and license suspensions ranging from 30 days to one year. Repeat offenders face revocation.

Beyond TREC discipline, agents face civil liability. If a defectively prepared deed results in a cloud on title, the buyer can sue the closing entity, the attorney (if one existed), and potentially the agent who selected or recommended the deficient provider. Tennessee courts have recognized negligent referral theories in professional services contexts.

E&O insurance coverage is not guaranteed in these situations. Most policies contain exclusions for claims arising from illegal acts or activities outside the scope of licensed practice. An agent who participated in — or failed to prevent — unauthorized practice of law may find their insurer refusing to defend.

**The deal itself can also unravel.** A deed prepared by an unlicensed person is voidable. While Tennessee courts have not universally voided such instruments, a motivated opposing party in a later dispute (divorce, inheritance, boundary conflict) can challenge the deed's validity. Your client ends up re-litigating what should have been a clean transfer.

## How to Protect Yourself Before Every Closing

Build a verified closing provider list. Before you recommend or name a closing entity in RF401, confirm in writing that the entity either employs a licensed Tennessee attorney or has a documented supervision arrangement. Keep that confirmation in your transaction file.

If you use a [transaction coordinator or compliance system](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) to manage your files, add a checklist item requiring attorney verification at the contract-execution stage — not the week before closing when it is too late to change course.

Communicate clearly with clients. Instead of saying "the title company handles everything," say "the title company will manage escrow and title insurance, and their attorney will prepare the deed." That single sentence shift protects you from misrepresentation claims and educates your client about the process. Agents who [understand where deals commonly break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) know that transparency at the front end prevents fire drills at the back end.
