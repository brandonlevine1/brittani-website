## The New Reality of California Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026

The California commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 represent the most significant operational shift for agents since the state adopted mandatory agency disclosure in 1988. If you hold a California Bureau of Real Estate (DRE) license and have not overhauled your compensation practices, you are already behind. The settlement's provisions, effective since August 2024 and now fully embedded in MLS and state regulatory enforcement, demand documented buyer-broker compensation agreements before touring and prohibit blanket cooperative compensation offers on any MLS feed.

California's Department of Real Estate has not issued new standalone regulations in response to the settlement, but it has signaled through advisory bulletins that existing statutes—particularly Business and Professions Code Sections 10176 and 10177—will be enforced aggressively against agents who fail to disclose compensation clearly or who steer buyers based on commission. The California Association of Realtors (CAR) responded by releasing and revising a suite of forms that now function as the operational backbone of compliant transactions.

## Written Buyer-Broker Agreements: The Non-Negotiable Starting Point

**Before you open a lockbox, you need a signed buyer representation agreement specifying your compensation.** This is no longer aspirational best practice—it is an MLS participation requirement and a DRE compliance expectation under existing fiduciary duty statutes.

The primary form is the CAR Buyer Representation and Broker Compensation Agreement (RLA-B), which replaced the older Buyer Representation Agreement (BRA) in late 2024 and was further revised in early 2026. The RLA-B requires agents to state a specific compensation amount or percentage, identify who pays it, and define the scope and duration of representation. The agreement must be signed before the agent provides any substantive services, including property tours.

Under Business and Professions Code Section 10176(g), a licensee can face discipline for claiming or demanding compensation without a written agreement authorizing it. Agents who operate without the RLA-B are exposed to DRE complaints, buyer rescission claims, and brokerage liability.

## How Seller-Side Compensation Actually Works Now

The old model was simple: listing agents posted a cooperative compensation split on the MLS, buyer's agents could rely on that published offer, and everyone moved forward. That architecture no longer exists in California MLSs, including CRMLS, the largest MLS in the country.

Sellers can still agree to compensate a buyer's agent, but the mechanism has changed entirely. The offer cannot appear in MLS data fields. Instead, it must be negotiated between parties and documented using the CAR Seller Payment to Buyer's Broker Confirmation form (SPBC). This form memorializes the seller's agreement to pay a specific amount toward the buyer's agent compensation and is typically attached as an addendum to the purchase agreement.

The Residential Purchase Agreement (RPA) itself was updated in 2025 and 2026 to include explicit sections addressing broker compensation sourcing. Paragraph 20 of the current RPA now includes fields for documenting whether the buyer, seller, or a combination will fund the buyer-agent's commission. Leaving this section blank or ambiguous is a compliance failure waiting to become a dispute.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: What You Actually Risk

**Failing to follow these rules does not simply trigger a slap on the wrist—it can unravel a closed transaction and end a career.** The consequences fall into four categories: regulatory, contractual, civil, and reputational.

| Risk Category | Specific Consequence | Triggering Violation |
|---|---|---|
| Regulatory (DRE) | License suspension or revocation under B&P Code 10176/10177 | Collecting undisclosed compensation; steering |
| Contractual | Buyer rescinds purchase agreement | Missing or defective RLA-B; undisclosed commission source |
| Civil Liability | Damages lawsuit from buyer or seller | Failure to disclose material facts about compensation |
| MLS Discipline | Fine up to $5,000; MLS access suspension | Publishing cooperative compensation in prohibited fields |
| Brokerage | Termination; E&O insurance denial | Agent operates outside broker's compliance protocols |

CRMLS enacted [enforcement policies](https://www.crmls.org/) that impose escalating fines for agents who attempt to communicate compensation offers through listing remarks, private fields, or coded language. First violations result in a warning and mandatory education; repeat violations carry monetary penalties and potential access suspension.

The DRE's investigative process under Business and Professions Code Section 10177(d) allows for license restriction when an agent engages in conduct that demonstrates incompetence—failing to understand and implement the new compensation framework could fall squarely within that definition.

## Common Mistakes Agents Are Making Right Now

Even agents who believe they are compliant often stumble on details. These are the five most frequent errors appearing in brokerage audits and MLS compliance reviews across California in 2026.

**First, agents are using outdated forms.** The pre-2025 Buyer Representation Agreement (BRA) did not include the compensation specificity required by the settlement. Using it creates a false sense of compliance while leaving both agent and buyer legally exposed. The current operative form is the RLA-B (revised January 2026).

Second, agents describe their compensation in vague terms within the RLA-B. Writing "standard commission" or "up to 3%" without specifying an exact figure or clearly explaining what triggers a higher or lower amount violates the settlement's transparency mandate. The DRE expects definite terms, not ranges masquerading as disclosures.

Third, buyer's agents are still asking listing agents "what's the co-op?" through private messages and expecting that verbal answer to substitute for a written agreement. Any compensation arrangement between the parties must be documented in the RPA or SPBC before closing. Verbal assurances carry zero enforceability and create audit failures.

Fourth, listing agents are embedding compensation signals in listing remarks—phrases like "generous concessions available" or "full buyer agent support"—which violate CRMLS policy prohibiting indirect communication of compensation offers through MLS fields. The intent behind the settlement was to remove commission data from the MLS entirely.

Fifth, agents fail to explain to buyers what happens if the seller declines to pay the buyer-agent's fee. The RLA-B includes a section addressing this scenario. Agents who skip or gloss over it create grounds for a buyer to claim they never understood they might owe their agent directly. This misunderstanding is already generating complaints at the DRE.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Business and Professions Code Section 10159.2, which mandates reasonable oversight of all licensees operating under their license. The post-settlement environment makes this obligation significantly more complex.

**Every brokerage operating in California should be conducting quarterly audits on at least five compliance checkpoints.** These include: verification that every active buyer-client file contains a signed RLA-B dated before the first tour; confirmation that all closed transaction files include documented compensation sourcing in the RPA or via SPBC; review of agent MLS listings for prohibited compensation language; audit of agent communications for off-MLS compensation advertising; and review of agent marketing materials for any implied commission guarantees.

| Audit Checkpoint | What to Look For | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| RLA-B execution date | Signed before first property showing | Every new buyer file |
| RPA Paragraph 20 | Compensation source clearly documented | Every offer/acceptance |
| SPBC attachment | Present when seller pays buyer-agent | Every applicable closing |
| MLS listing remarks | No compensation signals or coded language | Monthly random sample |
| Agent social media/marketing | No "commission paid by seller" guarantees | Quarterly review |

Brokers who discover violations should document the corrective action, require immediate agent retraining, and report repeated offenses to the DRE if the agent refuses to comply. Failure to supervise exposes the broker's own license under Section 10177(h) and creates E&O coverage gaps that insurers are increasingly exploiting to deny claims.

The relationship between [agency disclosure requirements](/blog/washington-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) and compensation transparency is tighter than ever. Agents operating in states with explicit agency disclosure forms—like Washington's—can see how California's framework is converging toward the same mandatory clarity.

## Practical Steps to Stay Compliant Through 2026 and Beyond

Start by ensuring your transaction management system flags any buyer file missing an RLA-B. If you are still tracking deals in spreadsheets or email folders, this is the kind of operational gap where compliance failures hide. Platforms like [Brittani AI](https://brittani.ai/pricing) are built specifically to catch missing forms and flag unsigned agreements before they become audit problems.

Train your buyers on the compensation conversation at first contact—not at the offer stage. Present the RLA-B as a standard professional services agreement, similar to retaining an attorney. Explain the three possible compensation sources (buyer pays directly, seller agrees to pay, or a combination) and document their understanding in writing.

For listing agents, update your listing presentation to include an explanation of why cooperative compensation no longer appears on the MLS and what the seller's options are for attracting buyer-agent participation. The CAR Listing Agreement (RLA) was updated to include advisory language on this point, and your verbal explanation should mirror the written disclosure.

Monitor your MLS compliance bulletins monthly. CRMLS, Bay East, and other California MLSs are issuing guidance letters and policy updates as edge cases emerge. Ignorance of a new policy interpretation is not a defense at a DRE hearing.

## The Intersection of Disclosures and Compensation Compliance

Commission documentation does not exist in a vacuum—it connects directly to your broader disclosure obligations. Agents who already struggle with [managing multiple deal timelines](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) or who [miss critical deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) are the most likely to let a compensation agreement slip through the cracks.

The DRE treats compensation disclosure failures the same way it treats material fact omissions on a Transfer Disclosure Statement. The theory is identical: a buyer or seller made a financial decision without information they were entitled to receive. The remedy is identical too—rescission, damages, or both.

California commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 will continue to evolve as case law develops and the DRE issues formal regulations. Agents who build documentation habits now—systematic RLA-B execution, RPA compensation sourcing, SPBC use, and rigorous file management—will not need to scramble when the next enforcement wave arrives. The agents who treat this as someone else's problem are the ones whose files will end up on a DRE examiner's desk.
