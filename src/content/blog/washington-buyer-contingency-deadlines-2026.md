## Why Washington Buyer Contingency Deadlines 2026 Demand Precision

Missing a contingency deadline in a Washington residential transaction is not a minor administrative hiccup. It can extinguish a buyer's right to terminate, expose earnest money to forfeiture, and generate license-law complaints filed with the Washington Department of Licensing. Understanding Washington buyer contingency deadlines 2026 is foundational knowledge that separates competent practitioners from agents who generate E&O claims.

Washington's framework for contingency deadlines is governed by a combination of state statute (RCW 18.86), administrative code (WAC 308-124C), and the standardized forms published by the Northwest Multiple Listing Service. The interplay between these authorities creates a system that rewards precision and punishes assumptions. Agents who rely on memory or verbal agreements instead of documented timelines are courting liability every single transaction cycle.

The stakes have only increased as buyer representation agreements became standard practice after the 2024 NAR settlement ripple effects. Buyers who sign agency agreements expect their agent to protect deadlines—and Washington's courts have consistently held that agents owe a fiduciary duty of reasonable care that includes calendar management.

## The Forms That Control Washington Contingency Timelines

**Every contingency deadline in a Washington residential transaction traces back to a specific NWMLS form number.** The purchase and sale agreement itself—NWMLS Form 21 (Residential) or Form 25 (Vacant Land)—establishes the framework, but the addenda fill in the operative dates.

NWMLS Form 35 (Inspection Addendum) sets the inspection contingency period, which defaults to 10 calendar days from mutual acceptance. Form 22A (Financing Addendum) establishes the financing contingency deadline. Form 22B (Title Contingency Addendum) governs the buyer's window to object to title exceptions. Form 22D (Calculation of Time/Sincerity of Buyer) addresses how days are counted and what constitutes proper notice.

Form 22D deserves special attention because it dictates the mechanical rules for every other deadline in the file. Calendar days—not business days—are the default counting method. If a deadline expires on a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday recognized under RCW 1.16.050, the deadline extends to the next business day. Agents who confuse this rule with the federal "business day" standard used in TRID disclosures create dangerous timing errors.

| Form | Contingency Governed | Default Period | Counted From |
|------|---------------------|----------------|--------------|
| Form 35 | Inspection | 10 calendar days | Mutual acceptance |
| Form 22A | Financing | Specified by parties | Mutual acceptance |
| Form 22B | Title review | 5 calendar days after receipt | Buyer's receipt of title report |
| Form 22J | Seller disclosure review | 3 business days | Buyer's receipt of Form 17 |
| Form 22T | Sale of buyer's property | Negotiated | Mutual acceptance |

Understanding which form controls which deadline is not optional—it is the baseline for competent practice under [WAC 308-124C-125](https://app.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=308-124C-125), which requires brokers to supervise transactions with reasonable care.

## How Mutual Acceptance Triggers the Clock

Mutual acceptance is the anchor date for nearly every contingency deadline in Washington. Under NWMLS Form 22D, mutual acceptance occurs when the last party signs or initials the final counteroffer and that acceptance is communicated to the other party. The critical word is "communicated"—an acceptance sitting in a DocuSign queue that no one has opened does not start the clock.

**Agents must document the exact date and time of mutual acceptance and communicate it immediately.** A 2023 Washington Court of Appeals decision reaffirmed that the burden of proving mutual acceptance rests on the party asserting it. If your file lacks a clear timestamp showing when acceptance was communicated, you are building your transaction on sand.

Once mutual acceptance is established, the contingency clocks run simultaneously. A buyer facing a 10-day inspection deadline and a 5-day title review window (triggered by receipt of the preliminary commitment) must track these independently. One does not pause the other. Agents who treat contingencies as sequential rather than concurrent [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) with alarming regularity.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The consequences of missed contingency deadlines cascade through three levels: transactional, financial, and regulatory.

At the transactional level, a buyer who fails to deliver a timely contingency response under Form 35 may lose the right to request repairs, renegotiate price, or terminate without forfeiting earnest money. The seller can issue a Form 22EL (Seller's Election Notice) demanding the buyer either waive the contingency or terminate. This is not a theoretical risk—it happens in competitive markets where listing agents are tracking the buyer's deadlines as closely as their own clients.

Financially, earnest money deposits in Washington are governed by RCW 18.85.285 and held in trust by the designated broker or escrow company. If a buyer attempts to terminate after a contingency deadline has expired, the seller may claim the earnest money. Disputes that reach the Interpleader process (Form 22EF) drag on for months and generate legal fees that dwarf the original deposit.

At the regulatory level, the Washington Department of Licensing can investigate complaints alleging negligent transaction management. Under RCW 18.85.361, a licensee who fails to exercise reasonable skill and care faces sanctions ranging from fines to license suspension. The DOL's disciplinary records show multiple cases where agents were sanctioned for failing to properly track and communicate deadlines.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Washington Contingency Deadlines

**The following errors appear repeatedly in E&O claims, DOL complaints, and brokerage audits across Washington.** Each one is preventable with proper systems.

The first common mistake is counting days from the date the offer was written rather than the date of mutual acceptance. Form 22D is explicit: deadlines run from mutual acceptance unless the form specifically states otherwise (as Form 22B does when triggering the title review period from receipt of the title commitment). Agents who draft an offer on Monday, achieve mutual acceptance on Wednesday, and then count the 10-day inspection window from Monday have given their buyer two fewer days than the contract allows—or told the seller the deadline passed when it has not.

The second mistake is failing to deliver contingency responses in writing. Washington's form suite requires that notices, elections, and waivers be delivered in the manner specified in the agreement. Verbal statements like "my buyer is satisfied with the inspection" do not satisfy Form 35's requirement for written notice. An agent who tells the listing agent over the phone that the buyer is waiving the inspection contingency—without following up with the proper written form—leaves the contingency technically unresolved.

The third mistake involves [seller disclosure timelines](/blog/washington-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026). Under Form 22J, the buyer has 3 business days after receipt of the Form 17 (Seller's Disclosure Statement) to approve or rescind. Agents often confuse this business-day calculation with the calendar-day calculation used for inspection and financing contingencies. Three business days and three calendar days can differ by two full days depending on weekends and holidays.

The fourth mistake is assuming that a financing contingency protects the buyer indefinitely. Form 22A requires the buyer to provide written notice of inability to obtain financing by the specified deadline. If the buyer misses this deadline, the contingency may be deemed waived. Agents who wait for the lender's formal denial letter—which often arrives after the contractual deadline—put their buyer's earnest money at risk.

The fifth mistake is neglecting to account for Form 22D's provision regarding electronic delivery. Electronic notices are deemed received when sent (not when opened) if the parties have agreed to electronic delivery in the purchase agreement. Agents who assume their email notification about a missed deadline was not received because the recipient did not respond are misreading the form's delivery rules.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Washington's supervisory requirements under WAC 308-124C-125 place affirmative obligations on designated brokers to ensure their agents manage transactions competently. Contingency deadline tracking is squarely within this supervisory mandate.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency | Risk if Missed |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|----------------|
| Mutual acceptance documentation | Timestamp + communication proof in every file | Every transaction | No verifiable start date for deadlines |
| Contingency response forms | Written Form 35 response, Form 22A notice, Form 22B objection in file | At each deadline | Unresolved contingencies, earnest money disputes |
| Calendar day calculations | Verified against Form 22D rules, including holiday extensions | At file review | Premature or late deadline communications |
| Electronic delivery records | Email timestamps matching Form 22D electronic delivery provisions | Quarterly sample audit | Disputed notice delivery |
| Form 17 receipt documentation | Signed buyer receipt with date for 3-business-day calculation | Every transaction | Rescission right miscalculation |

Brokers who rely solely on agents self-reporting their deadline compliance are failing their supervisory duty. A structured file review process—ideally at mutual acceptance, mid-transaction, and pre-closing—catches errors before they become claims. The brokerage's [checklist systems](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) should include specific verification steps for each contingency deadline in the file.

Designated brokers should also ensure their agents understand the distinction between the contingency period and the closing timeline. A financing contingency that expires on day 30 does not mean the loan must be funded by day 30—it means the buyer must have provided the contractually required notice by that date. This distinction generates more brokerage-level disputes than almost any other timing issue.
