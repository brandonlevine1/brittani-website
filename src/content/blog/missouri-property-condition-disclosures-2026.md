## Missouri Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026: The Statute That Shapes Every Deal

Missouri property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 requirements catch agents off guard more often than any other compliance area in the state. The reason is deceptively simple: Missouri's disclosure statute is shorter and less prescriptive than neighboring states, which leads agents to assume there is less to worry about. That assumption is where liability begins.

**The governing law is RSMo Section 442.606, Missouri's Seller's Disclosure statute, which requires sellers of residential real property to disclose known material defects.** The Missouri Real Estate Commission (MREC) enforces licensee conduct under Chapter 339, and federal environmental regulations layer on additional obligations that no state form alone satisfies. Agents who treat disclosure as a one-form task are already behind.

This post breaks down what the statute actually demands, which forms to use, how liability attaches when things go wrong, and what brokers should be checking in every file.

## What RSMo 442.606 Actually Requires

Missouri's seller disclosure law applies to sales of residential real property containing one to four dwelling units. The seller must disclose, in writing, any "material defects" in the property that are within their actual knowledge. The statute does not require sellers to conduct inspections or discover defects they do not already know about.

That "actual knowledge" standard is narrower than what many agents assume. It protects sellers from claims about latent defects they genuinely never encountered, but it does not shield sellers who were told about a problem and then conveniently forgot. Courts have consistently held that willful ignorance or deliberate concealment still constitutes fraud under Missouri common law.

The disclosure must be delivered to the buyer before the buyer submits an offer or, at the latest, before the execution of the purchase agreement. Timing matters because a buyer who receives a disclosure after signing has stronger grounds to rescind.

## The Forms Missouri Agents Should Be Using

The Missouri Association of REALTORS (MAR) publishes the standard Seller's Disclosure Statement, commonly referenced as MAR Form 2915. This multi-page document covers structural systems, mechanical systems, water and sewer, environmental hazards, zoning issues, and neighborhood conditions. It is not mandated by statute—RSMo 442.606 requires written disclosure but does not prescribe a specific form—yet MAR Form 2915 is the industry standard and the document most Missouri courts expect to see.

For environmental issues, agents must also deliver the federally required lead-based paint disclosure for any residential property built before 1978. This obligation comes from 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d and EPA/HUD regulations at 24 CFR Part 35. The standard form used in Missouri is the Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (often MAR Form 2935 or the equivalent federal form), accompanied by the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home."

| Form | Purpose | Required By | When to Deliver |
|------|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| MAR Form 2915 (Seller's Disclosure) | Property condition defects | RSMo 442.606 | Before offer or contract execution |
| MAR Form 2935 (Lead-Based Paint) | Lead hazard disclosure for pre-1978 homes | 42 U.S.C. 4852d / 24 CFR 35 | Before contract becomes binding |
| Flood Zone Disclosure | FEMA flood zone status | Federal NFIP requirement for financing | At or before contract |
| Meth Contamination Disclosure | Former meth lab status | RSMo 442.606 + MDNR guidance | Before offer or contract execution |

Agents who work with properties that have known environmental contamination—former underground storage tanks, proximity to Superfund sites, or methamphetamine production history—face additional disclosure duties that MAR Form 2915 alone may not fully address. Missouri's Department of Natural Resources (MDNR) maintains a list of known contaminated properties, and sellers with actual knowledge of such issues must disclose them.

## Environmental Red Flags Unique to Missouri

Missouri has a disproportionate share of environmental concerns tied to its mining history. Lead mining in the southeastern part of the state has left soil contamination that extends well beyond the mine sites themselves. The EPA's [Superfund program](https://www.epa.gov/superfund/search-superfund-sites-where-you-live) tracks these sites, and agents listing properties in Washington, Iron, Reynolds, or St. Francois counties should confirm whether the property sits within a known contamination zone.

**Methamphetamine contamination disclosure is another area where Missouri agents face elevated risk.** The state earned national notoriety for meth production rates in the early 2000s, and MDNR maintains a registry of properties with known meth contamination. If a property appears on that registry or the seller has knowledge of prior drug manufacturing on site, that fact must be disclosed. Failure to disclose known meth contamination has resulted in successful buyer lawsuits for rescission and damages.

Radon is present in elevated levels throughout much of Missouri, particularly in the Ozarks region. While radon itself is not a required line item in the disclosure statute, sellers who have conducted radon testing and received high results hold "actual knowledge" of a material defect. Omitting that information from the disclosure creates liability even though no Missouri statute specifically names radon.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Liability for disclosure failures in Missouri runs on two tracks: regulatory discipline and civil litigation. The MREC can take action against a licensee's license under RSMo 339.100 for dishonest dealing, failure to disclose material facts, or conduct that demonstrates incompetence. Penalties range from censure and mandatory education to suspension or revocation of the license.

On the civil side, buyers who discover undisclosed material defects can pursue claims for fraudulent misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, or violation of RSMo 442.606. Remedies include rescission of the purchase contract, compensatory damages for repair costs, and in cases of intentional fraud, punitive damages. Missouri courts have awarded six-figure judgments in disclosure fraud cases involving foundation failures and water intrusion that sellers knew about and concealed.

**Listing agents face personal liability when they have independent knowledge of a defect—from their own observations or from prior inspection reports in their possession—and fail to disclose it.** The "I only know what my seller told me" defense collapses when the MLS photos show obvious water staining or when the agent attended a pre-listing inspection. For buyer's agents, the risk is lower but not zero; failing to advise a buyer to review disclosures carefully or ignoring obvious red flags during showings can constitute a breach of fiduciary duty.

| Violation Type | Regulatory Consequence | Civil Consequence |
|---------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Failure to deliver disclosure form | MREC discipline (RSMo 339.100) | Contract rescission, damages |
| Known defect omitted from form | License suspension/revocation possible | Compensatory + punitive damages |
| Lead paint disclosure not provided | HUD/EPA fine up to $19,507 per violation | Treble damages under federal law |
| Agent conceals independently known defect | MREC action + E&O claim | Personal liability for damages |

The federal lead paint penalties deserve special attention. Under 24 CFR 35.88, violations can result in civil penalties of up to $19,507 per occurrence (adjusted for inflation). Both the seller and the listing agent can be held jointly liable. This is not a theoretical risk—HUD enforces these penalties regularly, and agents who handle pre-1978 homes without delivering the disclosure and pamphlet are gambling with their license and their finances.

## Common Mistakes Missouri Agents Make

The first mistake is treating the disclosure as a seller-only task. Agents hand the form to the seller, tell them to fill it out, and never review the completed document. When a seller writes "unknown" next to a category the agent personally observed—like a crack in the foundation visible during the listing appointment—the agent has a problem. Reviewing the completed form against your own observations is not optional; it is part of your fiduciary duty.

The second mistake is failing to track delivery timing. RSMo 442.606 requires delivery before the offer or contract execution. Agents who stuff the disclosure into the closing package at the title company are too late. If a dispute arises, the buyer's attorney will argue the buyer never had the opportunity to make an informed decision before committing. Keep a signed receipt or email confirmation showing when the buyer actually received the form.

The third mistake involves the lead paint disclosure for pre-1978 homes. Agents routinely skip this form on properties that have been "completely renovated." Renovation does not eliminate the federal requirement. Unless every painted surface in the home has been tested and certified lead-free by a licensed inspector, the disclosure is still mandatory. The 10-day inspection period must also be offered to the buyer unless the buyer voluntarily waives it in writing.

The fourth mistake is ignoring environmental databases. Missouri agents listing rural properties or homes near former industrial sites rarely check the MDNR contamination registry or EPA Superfund database. A five-minute search can reveal that the property sits in a known contamination area, which then becomes information the agent possesses and must disclose.

The fifth mistake is failing to disclose known HOA or neighborhood nuisances. MAR Form 2915 includes a section on neighborhood conditions, and agents who skip it or allow sellers to leave it blank miss disclosures about noise, odors, or pending assessments that buyers later claim were material to their decision.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under RSMo 339.100 and MREC regulations. If an agent in your office consistently fails to deliver disclosures on time or omits the lead paint form from pre-1978 transactions, the broker can face discipline alongside the agent. File audits are not a courtesy—they are a regulatory expectation.

**Every transaction file should contain a signed and dated seller's disclosure form with a delivery receipt, a lead-based paint disclosure for qualifying properties, and documentation of any environmental concerns discussed during the listing period.** Brokers should spot-check at least 10-15% of closed files quarterly and 100% of files where complaints have been received.

Brokers should also maintain a written office policy specifying when disclosures must be delivered, who is responsible for confirming delivery, and what happens internally when a seller refuses to complete the form. That refusal—and the agent's documentation of it—should be in the file.
