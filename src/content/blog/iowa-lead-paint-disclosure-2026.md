## Understanding Iowa Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

The Iowa lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain anchored in federal law but carry state-specific enforcement and procedural details that trip up even experienced agents. Every residential property built before 1978 triggers mandatory disclosure obligations under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X), codified at 42 U.S.C. 4852d and implemented through 24 CFR Part 35 and 40 CFR Part 745. Iowa does not layer additional state-level lead disclosure statutes on top of the federal mandate, but the Iowa Real Estate Commission (IREC) holds licensees accountable for federal compliance through its administrative enforcement authority under Iowa Code Chapter 543B.

**Failing to deliver proper lead paint disclosures in Iowa is not a technicality—it is a violation that can unwind a closed deal and expose you to five-figure penalties.** The EPA adjusted its maximum civil penalty for lead disclosure violations to $19,507 per violation as of January 2024, and that figure continues to increase with inflation adjustments. Agents who treat the lead paint form as just another signature page in the stack are playing a dangerous game with their license and their client's money.

Iowa's housing stock makes this especially relevant. According to U.S. Census data, over 40 percent of owner-occupied homes in Iowa were built before 1978. In cities like Des Moines, Davenport, and Dubuque, that percentage climbs significantly higher in older neighborhoods where agents frequently list properties.

## The Federal Framework That Governs Iowa Transactions

Title X requires three specific actions before a buyer becomes obligated under a purchase contract for pre-1978 housing. First, the seller must disclose any known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards in the property. Second, the seller must provide the buyer with any available records or reports pertaining to lead-based paint in the dwelling. Third, the buyer must receive the EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home."

The [EPA's lead disclosure rule](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-paint-disclosure-rule) also mandates a 10-day opportunity for the buyer to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense. The parties may mutually agree in writing to a different time period, or the buyer may waive the opportunity entirely. Agents cannot pressure buyers to waive this right, and the waiver must be documented on the disclosure form itself.

Iowa does not have a separate state disclosure form for lead paint. The standard federal form—officially titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards"—serves as the operative document. The Iowa Association of Realtors (IAR) integrates lead paint disclosure language into its purchase agreement addendum package, but the substance mirrors the federal form requirements exactly.

## Forms and Documents Iowa Agents Must Use

The paperwork side of lead disclosure in Iowa involves a small set of documents, but each must be handled correctly.

| Document | Source | When Required | Who Signs |
|----------|--------|---------------|-----------|
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Form | EPA / IAR addendum | Before buyer is obligated under contract | Seller, Buyer, and both agents |
| "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" pamphlet | EPA | Delivered to buyer before contract obligation | Delivery confirmed by buyer signature |
| Lead inspection/risk assessment reports (if any exist) | Seller's records | Attached to disclosure form | Acknowledged by buyer |
| 10-day inspection opportunity waiver (if buyer elects) | Federal form or IAR addendum | Before or at contract execution | Buyer only |

The IAR's standard Residential Purchase Agreement includes a lead paint addendum section that satisfies federal requirements when properly completed. Agents using non-IAR forms must ensure their disclosure document contains all elements specified in 40 CFR 745.113. Missing even one required element—such as failing to include the agent's signature confirming compliance—renders the form deficient.

**Both the listing agent and the buyer's agent must sign the disclosure form attesting that they have informed their respective clients of their obligations.** This dual-agent signature requirement is one of the most overlooked elements in Iowa transactions, particularly when cooperating agents from different brokerages handle the deal.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Non-compliance consequences in Iowa arrive from multiple directions. The EPA can assess civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation. HUD can impose its own penalties for transactions involving federally assisted housing. Beyond regulatory fines, the buyer gains a private right of action under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3), which allows treble damages in civil litigation.

At the state level, the Iowa Real Estate Commission can pursue disciplinary action against a licensee's registration under Iowa Code 543B.29, which includes grounds for suspension or revocation when an agent engages in conduct that demonstrates incompetence or untrustworthiness. A pattern of lead disclosure failures would almost certainly qualify.

Transaction-level consequences are equally severe. A buyer who was never given the required disclosure can seek contract rescission. Iowa courts have recognized that material non-disclosure, including failures to provide federally mandated information, can void a purchase agreement. The listing agent's errors and omissions insurance may or may not cover intentional omissions—policies vary, and most require the error to be unintentional for coverage to apply.

If you are [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously, missing a lead disclosure on just one file can cascade into a commission clawback, a complaint to IREC, and malpractice exposure all at once.

## Common Mistakes Iowa Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosures

Five errors appear repeatedly in Iowa disciplinary files and E&O claims related to lead paint disclosures.

**First, agents fail to obtain the disclosure form before the buyer signs the purchase agreement.** The federal rule is clear: the buyer must receive the disclosure and pamphlet before becoming obligated. Presenting both documents at closing—a practice that persists in some offices—violates the timing requirement entirely, even if the buyer signs without objection.

Second, listing agents mark "seller has no knowledge" without actually asking the seller. The form requires the seller to affirmatively disclose known hazards or certify that no known hazards exist. An agent who checks the box without confirming the seller's actual knowledge has manufactured a false disclosure. If the seller later admits they knew about peeling paint or a prior lead test, the listing agent shares liability.

Third, agents skip the 10-day inspection period language. The buyer must be given the option in writing. When agents use custom or out-of-state contract forms that omit this provision, the disclosure is incomplete. The buyer's waiver or acceptance of the 10-day window must appear on the form itself—not in a separate email or text message.

Fourth, cooperating agents neglect to sign the disclosure form. Both agents must sign. The buyer's agent signature confirms they informed the buyer of their rights. When a cooperating agent from another brokerage fails to sign, the form is deficient regardless of what the listing side did correctly.

Fifth, agents treat the pamphlet delivery as optional or assume the buyer "already got one" from a prior transaction. Each transaction requires independent delivery and acknowledgment. There is no carryover from a previous deal, even if the buyer purchased another pre-1978 home six months earlier.

These errors mirror the kinds of deadline-related failures explored in [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines)—the root cause is almost always process breakdown rather than ignorance of the law.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Iowa carry supervisory liability under Iowa Code 543B.34a, which requires designated brokers to maintain policies, procedures, and oversight adequate to ensure compliance by their affiliated licensees. Lead paint disclosure is a prime audit target because violations are binary—either the form is in the file or it is not.

**Every Iowa brokerage should have a file review checklist that flags any pre-1978 property and verifies three things: disclosure form with all signatures, pamphlet delivery acknowledgment, and documentation of the 10-day inspection opportunity.** Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance without independent verification are exposing the brokerage to vicarious liability.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Verify | Red Flag |
|-------------------|---------------|----------|
| Property age check | Year built confirmed via assessor data | No year-built noted in file for older neighborhoods |
| Disclosure form completeness | All four signatures present (seller, buyer, both agents) | Missing cooperating agent signature |
| Pamphlet delivery | Buyer acknowledgment signature with date | Acknowledgment dated same day as closing |
| 10-day inspection language | Waiver or election documented on form | No mention of inspection period anywhere in file |
| Known hazard records | Any existing reports attached or absence noted | Seller marked "no knowledge" but prior inspection report exists in MLS remarks |

Quarterly file audits are the minimum standard. Brokers managing offices with high volumes of older inventory—think Iowa City's near-campus rentals or Cedar Rapids' older residential corridors—should consider monthly spot checks. The cost of a single EPA enforcement action dwarfs the administrative time spent auditing files.

Training is the other half of the equation. Brokers should confirm that every agent in their office can articulate the timing requirement, identify which properties trigger the disclosure obligation, and produce a completed form from memory if asked. ## Exemptions That Apply in Iowa

Not every transaction involving a pre-1978 property requires lead paint disclosure. The federal rule carves out specific exemptions that Iowa agents should know cold.

Foreclosure sales are exempt. Lease transactions of 100 days or fewer are exempt. Sales of housing that has been found to be lead-free by a certified inspector are exempt—but the agent must have documentation of that certification in the file. Zero-bedroom units (studios, lofts, dormitories) are exempt. Housing designated exclusively for elderly or disabled persons is exempt, provided no child under six resides or is expected to reside there.

The foreclosure exemption catches agents off guard most often. A bank-owned property built in 1955 does not require a lead paint disclosure from the selling entity. However, if the prior homeowner provided a disclosure to the bank during the foreclosure process, best practice dictates including it in the buyer's file anyway. The legal exemption protects the seller from penalties but does not immunize the buyer's agent from a negligence claim if they knew about hazards and failed to inform their client.

## Rental Transactions Carry the Same Obligations

Iowa agents who handle leasing for pre-1978 properties face identical disclosure requirements under 40 CFR 745.107. Landlords must disclose known lead hazards, provide the EPA pamphlet, and include specific lead warning language in the lease agreement. The agent facilitating the lease must sign the disclosure form confirming compliance—just as in a sale.

Rental compliance failures generate a disproportionate number of EPA enforcement actions nationally because the volume of lease transactions far exceeds sales, and oversight tends to be looser. Iowa property managers operating older rental portfolios in college towns and historic districts should treat this as a standing compliance obligation, not a per-transaction afterthought.
