## Tennessee Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026: The Rules That Catch Agents Off Guard

Mishandling earnest money is one of the fastest ways to trigger a Tennessee Real Estate Commission investigation, and the consequences in 2026 remain severe. Understanding Tennessee earnest money & escrow laws 2026 is not optional—it is a licensing requirement enforced through audits, complaints, and disciplinary hearings. This post breaks down the statutes, the forms, and the specific errors that lead agents and brokers into trouble every single quarter.

Tennessee treats earnest money as trust funds belonging to neither buyer nor seller until closing or lawful disbursement. The governing framework lives primarily in Tennessee Code Annotated Title 62, Chapter 13 (the Tennessee Real Estate Broker License Act) and the administrative rules promulgated by TREC under Chapter 1260-02. Every licensed agent in the state operates under these provisions whether they have read them or not.

## Statutory Foundation: T.C.A. 62-13-312 and TREC Rule 1260-02-.09

The core escrow obligation appears in [T.C.A. 62-13-312](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/trec.html), which requires that all trust money received by a licensee on behalf of a principal be deposited in a federally insured escrow or trust account maintained by the principal broker. The statute explicitly prohibits commingling personal funds with escrow funds, with one narrow exception: a broker may deposit up to $500 of personal funds to cover bank service charges and prevent the account from falling below minimum balance requirements.

**TREC Rule 1260-02-.09 adds the operational teeth.** It mandates deposit within three business days of contract binding—unless the contract itself sets a different timeline. The rule also requires the principal broker to maintain monthly reconciliation records and to make those records available for TREC audit on demand.

Failure to comply with either provision can result in license revocation, civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation, and personal liability for any funds lost due to negligence. The Commission has the authority to issue a cease and desist order even before a formal hearing concludes.

## Forms That Govern Earnest Money in Tennessee Transactions

Tennessee Association of Realtors (TAR) forms are the standard in most residential transactions, and agents must understand how earnest money provisions thread through multiple documents. The primary form is **RF401 (Purchase and Sale Agreement)**, which contains the earnest money paragraph specifying amount, holder, and delivery deadline.

Form **RF201 (Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement)** does not directly address earnest money but establishes the brokerage relationship that creates fiduciary duties around trust funds. Form **RF651 (Release of Earnest Money)** is the mutual release agents use when both parties agree on disbursement after a failed transaction. Without RF651 signed by both buyer and seller, the escrow agent has no authority to release funds absent a court order or interpleader action.

| Form Number | Name | Earnest Money Function |
|---|---|---|
| RF401 | Purchase and Sale Agreement | Sets amount, holder, and deposit deadline |
| RF651 | Release of Earnest Money | Authorizes disbursement after contract termination |
| RF656 | Notice to Buyer or Seller | Delivers contingency or default notices triggering EM disputes |
| RF301 | Buyer Representation Agreement | Defines agent obligations including trust fund handling |

Agents who rely on custom forms or attorney-drafted contracts still fall under the same TREC escrow rules, but they lose the built-in compliance language that TAR forms provide. If you are working with a non-standard contract, confirm that the escrow paragraph mirrors the requirements in Rule 1260-02-.09.

## Tennessee Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026: Deposit Timing and Account Requirements

The three-business-day deposit rule is the single most audited requirement in Tennessee escrow compliance. TREC auditors count from the date the broker or the broker's agent receives the earnest money—not from the date both parties sign the contract. If an agent receives a check on Monday and the contract does not become binding until Wednesday, the deposit clock starts Monday.

**The escrow account itself must meet specific criteria.** It must be a demand deposit account at a federally insured financial institution located in Tennessee. The account must be designated as a trust or escrow account, and the principal broker's name must appear on the account. Affiliate brokers and sales agents may not hold escrow accounts in their own names under any circumstances.

Interest-bearing escrow accounts are permitted only when the parties to the transaction provide written authorization specifying who receives the interest. Without that written agreement, earned interest belongs to neither party and creates a compliance headache that brokers must resolve before disbursement.

## Common Mistakes That Trigger TREC Enforcement Actions

**Mistake 1: Holding the check "until the contract is fully executed."** Agents frequently delay depositing a buyer's earnest money check because they want to wait for all signatures. TREC does not recognize this as a valid excuse. Once an agent physically possesses the funds, the three-day clock starts regardless of contract status. The proper practice is to deposit immediately and note in the file that the contract is pending execution.

Mistake 2: Using a personal account or a team account not registered with TREC. Some team leaders open separate bank accounts for operational convenience without registering those accounts as principal broker escrow accounts. This constitutes commingling and unauthorized escrow handling—both grounds for disciplinary action under T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(1).

Mistake 3: Releasing earnest money based on a single party's demand without RF651 or a court order. When a deal falls apart and the buyer claims entitlement to the deposit, agents sometimes release funds to avoid conflict. This exposes the broker to liability to the seller and violates escrow holding duties. The correct response is to hold funds and request both parties execute RF651. If either party refuses, the broker must interplead the funds under T.C.A. 66-5-103 or hold them pending resolution.

Mistake 4: Failing to reconcile monthly. TREC requires monthly reconciliation of escrow accounts even when no transactions are pending. Brokers who skip reconciliation during slow months create audit gaps that investigators flag as evidence of poor internal controls.

Mistake 5: Accepting wire transfers without verifying source routing. While not a TREC-specific rule, Tennessee brokers remain liable for funds that arrive via fraudulent wire instructions. Given the [rise in wire fraud schemes targeting real estate transactions](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process), agents must follow their brokerage's verification protocol before acknowledging receipt of electronic earnest money deposits.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Principal brokers carry the ultimate liability for escrow compliance in Tennessee. TREC does not accept delegation as a defense—if an affiliate broker mishandles funds, the principal broker faces the same disciplinary exposure. This means proactive auditing is not a suggestion; it is a self-preservation strategy.

**Brokers should audit three areas quarterly at minimum.** First, verify that every open escrow deposit corresponds to an active contract file with matching dates and amounts. Second, confirm that the monthly bank reconciliation matches the transaction ledger down to the penny. Third, review RF651 forms for closed or terminated transactions to ensure no funds remain in escrow beyond a reasonable disbursement timeline.

| Audit Item | Frequency | TREC Rule Reference | Consequence of Failure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deposit timing (3 business days) | Every transaction | 1260-02-.09(2) | Fine up to $1,000, license suspension |
| Monthly reconciliation | Monthly | 1260-02-.09(5) | Audit finding, mandatory corrective plan |
| Commingling check | Quarterly | T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(1) | License revocation possible |
| RF651 completion for closed files | At file closing | TAR best practice | Escrow disputes, interpleader costs |
| Interest authorization documentation | Per transaction | 1260-02-.09(4) | Unauthorized disbursement liability |

Brokerages with high transaction volume often miss the reconciliation requirement because they assign it to unlicensed staff without oversight. The principal broker must personally review and sign the reconciliation or designate a licensed broker-in-charge who accepts that responsibility in writing. ## Dispute Resolution: Interpleader and the 90-Day Rule

When buyer and seller both claim the earnest money and refuse to sign RF651, Tennessee law provides the interpleader remedy under T.C.A. 66-5-103. The broker files a complaint in the appropriate chancery or circuit court, deposits the funds with the court, and requests discharge from further liability. Court costs for interpleader are typically deducted from the deposited funds.

TREC expects brokers to act within a reasonable time frame once a dispute becomes apparent. While no statute specifies an exact deadline for filing interpleader, the Commission has cited brokers who held disputed funds for more than 90 days without taking action. The practical standard: if neither party has signed RF651 within 30 days of contract termination, begin the interpleader process.

Agents sometimes believe they can resolve disputes informally by brokering a compromise split. This is permissible only if both parties voluntarily agree in writing. Any coercion, misrepresentation of legal rights, or unauthorized legal advice regarding earnest money entitlement can constitute a violation of T.C.A. 62-13-403 (duties of licensees) and may trigger unauthorized practice of law concerns under Tennessee Supreme Court Rule 7.
