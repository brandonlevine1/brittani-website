## Ohio Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026: The Statute Framework Agents Must Know

Mishandling a deposit check can end a career faster than a failed inspection kills a deal. Ohio earnest money & escrow laws 2026 impose specific obligations on licensees that go well beyond "put the check in the account," and the Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing has shown no hesitation in pursuing violations. If you hold an active Ohio license, the rules in Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Chapter 4735 and Ohio Administrative Code (OAC) 1301:5-1-01 apply to every transaction you touch.

The governing body—the Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing under the Ohio Department of Commerce—treats escrow violations as grounds for disciplinary action including license suspension. Agents often assume their broker handles all compliance, but the statute assigns personal responsibility to any licensee who physically receives or directs earnest money. Ignorance of timing rules or deposit procedures is not a recognized defense in administrative hearings.

## Deposit Timing and Trust Account Requirements

**Ohio Administrative Code 1301:5-1-01 requires that all earnest money be deposited into the broker's trust account by the close of the next business day following final contract acceptance.** This is not a suggestion or best practice—it is a regulatory mandate. "Final acceptance" means the moment all parties have signed and the last signature has been communicated, not the day you happen to swing by the office.

The trust account itself must be a demand or checking account at an FDIC-insured institution located in Ohio. Brokers cannot commingle personal or operating funds with escrow deposits, and the account must be designated clearly as a trust account on the bank's records. ORC 4735.18(A)(22) makes commingling a standalone violation separate from any misappropriation.

If the purchase agreement specifies a different deposit deadline—say, five days after acceptance—the contractual term controls. However, the agent must still deliver the funds to the broker immediately upon receipt; it is the broker's deposit into the trust account that follows the contractual timeline. Agents cannot hold checks in desk drawers or car consoles while waiting for a convenient moment.

## Ohio Forms and Contract Provisions That Govern Escrow

Most Ohio residential transactions use the [Ohio REALTORS Standard Forms](https://www.ohiorealtors.org/), specifically the Ohio Residential Purchase Agreement. The earnest money provisions appear in Section 3 of that form, which specifies the deposit amount, the entity holding funds, and the deadline for deposit. Agents working in the Cincinnati area may also encounter the Cincinnati Area Board of Realtors (CABR) forms, which contain parallel escrow language with slight variations in formatting.

The Ohio REALTORS Earnest Money Dispute form becomes relevant when a transaction fails and both parties claim the deposit. Under ORC 4735.24, if a dispute arises, the broker must maintain the funds in trust until receiving either a written release signed by both parties, a court order, or a valid arbitration decision. Releasing funds to one party without proper authorization is a violation regardless of who the agent believes is "right."

Ohio also permits alternative escrow holders. Section 3 of the standard purchase agreement includes a blank for designating a title company or attorney as the escrow agent. When a third-party escrow agent holds funds, the listing broker's trust account obligations do not apply—but the agent's duty to deliver the check promptly to the designated holder remains intact.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Violations fall into three broad categories: late deposit, commingling, and unauthorized release. Each carries distinct consequences that can hit both the individual licensee and the brokerage.

| Violation | Statute | Potential Consequence |
|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| Late deposit into trust account | OAC 1301:5-1-01 | License suspension, formal reprimand, fine up to $2,500 per occurrence |
| Commingling trust and operating funds | ORC 4735.18(A)(22) | License revocation, civil liability for conversion |
| Unauthorized release of disputed funds | ORC 4735.24 | License suspension, personal liability to damaged party |
| Failure to maintain escrow records | OAC 1301:5-1-02 | Audit failure, brokerage fine, mandatory remedial education |
| Conversion or misappropriation | ORC 4735.18(A)(6) | Criminal prosecution, permanent revocation, restitution |

Beyond administrative penalties, agents face civil exposure. A buyer whose earnest money is improperly released can sue the agent and brokerage for breach of fiduciary duty, and Ohio courts have awarded consequential damages including the cost of the lost deal. If you have ever wondered about the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing), an escrow violation can multiply that figure by adding legal fees, regulatory defense costs, and increased E&O premiums.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make With Earnest Money

**Even experienced agents stumble on escrow compliance because the errors often feel like minor administrative oversights rather than legal violations.** Here are the five patterns the Division sees most frequently in enforcement actions.

First, agents hold checks over weekends without informing their broker. If final acceptance occurs on a Friday afternoon, the deposit deadline is Monday's close of business. Agents who wait until Tuesday because "Monday was hectic" have already violated OAC 1301:5-1-01. The fix is simple: deliver the check to your brokerage or its designated lockbox immediately upon receipt and let the broker handle deposit timing.

Second, agents verbally agree to return earnest money without written authorization from both parties. A buyer's agent calls to say the deal is dead and asks for the check back. The listing agent, trying to be helpful, authorizes the release without a signed mutual release form. This violates ORC 4735.24 and exposes the brokerage to a claim from the seller who may have had a legitimate right to those funds.

Third, agents fail to note the escrow holder correctly on the purchase agreement form. If the contract says "listing brokerage" but the agent delivers the check to a title company, there is now a discrepancy between the contract terms and actual practice. This becomes a serious problem if a dispute arises and no one can determine who has legal authority over the funds.

Fourth, agents accept earnest money before a fully executed contract exists. Ohio does not prohibit this, but it creates confusion about when the deposit clock starts. Best practice is to collect the check with the offer but clearly document that deposit will occur upon final acceptance, consistent with OAC timing rules.

Fifth, agents operating on teams or in partnerships assume someone else deposited the check. Shared responsibility often becomes no responsibility. If you physically received the earnest money, you are personally accountable for its chain of custody until it reaches the broker's hands. Tracking deadlines across [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) makes this even more critical—one missed deposit in a busy pipeline can trigger a complaint.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear ultimate responsibility for trust account compliance under ORC 4735.18(A)(22) and OAC 1301:5-1-02. The Division of Real Estate conducts random and complaint-triggered audits of brokerage escrow accounts, and the broker's license is on the line regardless of which agent caused the violation.

**Every Ohio brokerage should maintain a written escrow policy that agents acknowledge in writing at onboarding and annually thereafter.** This policy must cover deposit deadlines, delivery procedures, dispute protocols, and the specific consequences for non-compliance within the brokerage.

Monthly reconciliation is not optional—it is an audit expectation. Brokers must reconcile the trust account balance against individual transaction records to confirm that every dollar on deposit corresponds to an active contract. Unexplained variances, even small ones, will trigger a deeper Division investigation.

| Broker Responsibility | Frequency | Documentation Required |
|----------------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | Bank statement matched to transaction ledger |
| Agent acknowledgment of escrow policy | Annual and at hiring | Signed policy receipt in personnel file |
| Deposit timing verification | Per transaction | Date/time stamp of deposit relative to acceptance |
| Dispute hold confirmation | Per dispute | Copy of written notice to both parties and escrow hold documentation |
| Record retention | 3 years minimum | All escrow records per OAC 1301:5-1-02 |

Brokers should also verify that their agents understand [why deadlines get missed](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) in practice—not because agents are careless by nature, but because systemic gaps in workflow create opportunities for error. Building redundancy into deposit tracking is the broker's job.

## Dispute Resolution: The Interpleader Option

When buyer and seller cannot agree on who gets the earnest money, Ohio brokers face a decision. They can hold the funds indefinitely while the parties negotiate, they can interplead the funds with the court, or they can wait for a signed mutual release. Each path has trade-offs.

Interpleader—filing a court action to deposit the funds with the clerk and let a judge decide—is available under Ohio Rule of Civil Procedure 22. It costs the brokerage a filing fee and attorney time, but it removes the broker from the middle of the dispute and eliminates ongoing liability. Many Ohio brokerages set a 30- or 60-day policy: if no resolution is reached within that window, the broker files interpleader.

The alternative—holding funds indefinitely—technically complies with ORC 4735.24, but it creates an administrative burden and ties up the broker's trust account balance. Brokers carrying multiple unresolved disputes in their accounts invite audit scrutiny, even if each hold is individually justified.
