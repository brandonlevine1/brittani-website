## Why Kansas HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Understanding **Kansas HOA & condo disclosure rules 2026** is not optional for agents handling properties governed by homeowner associations or condominium regimes. Kansas treats condominiums and planned communities under separate but overlapping statutory frameworks, and a misstep on either side can torpedo a deal or invite a complaint to the Kansas Real Estate Commission (KREC). The rules are straightforward once you know where to look, but they punish ignorance with delayed closings and potential license discipline.

Kansas does not have a standalone, unified HOA disclosure statute comparable to states like Virginia or Colorado. Instead, agents must piece together obligations from the Kansas Apartment Ownership Act (K.S.A. 58-3101 et seq.), the Kansas Unit Property Act (K.S.A. 58-4601 et seq.), and common-law contract duties reflected in Kansas Association of REALTORS (KAR) standard forms. This article breaks down what you owe buyers, what sellers must produce, and where deals collapse when agents get lazy.

## The Statutory Framework: Two Acts, Two Property Types

**The Kansas Apartment Ownership Act governs traditional condominium regimes** created before the adoption of the Unit Property Act. K.S.A. 58-3101 through 58-3125 set out requirements for declaration, bylaws, and resale disclosures within these communities. For condominiums created or converted after the Unit Property Act's adoption, K.S.A. 58-4601 et seq. controls.

Both acts require a resale certificate (sometimes called a "statement of unpaid assessments") to be furnished to a prospective purchaser. The certificate must disclose the amount of current monthly assessments, any special assessments approved but not yet billed, outstanding liens against the unit, and the association's financial condition. Under K.S.A. 58-4621, the association must deliver this certificate within 10 business days of a written request.

For non-condo HOAs in planned communities, Kansas has no specific statutory resale certificate mandate. However, the KAR residential purchase contract (Form 100) and its HOA addendum (Form 170) create contractual disclosure obligations between the parties. Agents who fail to attach Form 170 when a property is located within an HOA risk claims that material facts were concealed.

## Forms You Must Know: KAR 100, KAR 170, and the Resale Certificate Request

The Kansas Association of REALTORS publishes the standard forms most brokerages across the state rely on. Here is how the key documents interact with HOA and condo transactions:

| Form | Purpose | When to Use | Who Completes It |
|------|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| KAR Form 100 | Residential Purchase Contract | Every residential sale | Buyer's agent drafts offer |
| KAR Form 170 | HOA/Condo Addendum | Any property subject to an HOA or condo regime | Attached to Form 100 |
| Association Resale Certificate | Discloses assessments, reserves, litigation, rules | Condo sales (statutory); HOA sales (contractual via Form 170) | Association management |
| KAR Form 400 | Seller's Disclosure Statement | Most residential sales | Seller completes |

Form 170 is the linchpin. It establishes who orders the resale certificate, who pays for it, the deadline for delivery, and the buyer's right to cancel if the documents reveal unacceptable conditions. If you list a property in an HOA or condo and omit Form 170, the buyer has no contractual mechanism to receive association documents before closing, and you may have breached your fiduciary duty of disclosure under K.S.A. 58-30,106.

**Agents acting as dual or transaction brokers must be especially cautious** because the limited agency framework in Kansas (K.S.A. 58-30,101 through 58-30,120) still requires disclosure of material facts to all parties, even when the agent does not advocate for either side. An undisclosed special assessment is a material fact, period.

## Buyer's Cancellation Rights and the Clock That Matters

When Form 170 is properly executed, the buyer typically receives a review period (often five business days, though the parties can negotiate a different window) after receiving the resale certificate and governing documents. During that window, the buyer may cancel without penalty if the HOA's financial condition, rules, or pending litigation is unacceptable.

If the association fails to deliver documents within the timeframe specified in Form 170, the buyer may extend the deadline or terminate the contract and recover earnest money. The listing agent should be tracking this deadline as aggressively as the inspection contingency. Many agents treat HOA document delivery as a "soft" deadline that can slip. It cannot. Once the contractual window expires, the buyer's right to cancel based on HOA documents evaporates.

> K.S.A. 58-4621: "The association shall furnish the statement within ten business days after receipt of a written request."

This statutory 10-day clock applies only to condominiums under the Unit Property Act. Non-condo HOAs follow whatever timeline their CC&Rs or management company dictates, which is why your contract language in Form 170 must set an explicit outside date.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

KREC has disciplinary authority over licensees who violate Kansas real estate law or demonstrate incompetence. Under K.S.A. 58-3043 and K.S.A. 58-3050, the Commission can impose civil fines up to $5,000 per violation, suspend or revoke a license, or require additional education. A failure to disclose an HOA's existence or to provide mandated documents can support any of these outcomes.

Beyond regulatory penalties, agents and their brokerages face civil liability. Kansas courts recognize negligence and breach of fiduciary duty claims when agents fail to discover or disclose HOA restrictions that materially affect value or use. A buyer who closes without knowing about a $15,000 pending special assessment has grounds to sue the listing agent, the seller, and potentially the buyer's agent if that agent should have inquired.

Title companies may also refuse to insure clear title if outstanding HOA liens appear at the eleventh hour. Deals that collapse this way generate costs for everyone, and the agent whose negligence caused the failure typically absorbs the blame. ## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Kansas HOA Disclosures

**First, agents forget to ask whether an HOA exists.** This sounds absurd, but it happens most often in rural subdivisions and smaller planned communities where there is no gate, no pool, and no obvious signage. The only clue may be buried in the deed's legal description or a recorded declaration of covenants. Always check the county recorder's office or ask the seller directly.

Second, agents order the resale certificate too late in the transaction. If you wait until 10 days before closing to request documents from a management company, and the association takes its full 10 business days, you have zero margin for buyer review. Best practice: order the resale certificate within 48 hours of mutual acceptance.

Third, agents assume the seller's disclosure statement (Form 400) covers HOA information adequately. Form 400 asks about HOA dues and special assessments, but it relies entirely on the seller's knowledge and honesty. It is not a substitute for the official resale certificate issued by the association, which includes audited financials, reserve studies, and pending litigation.

Fourth, agents fail to verify whether the HOA is in good standing with the Kansas Secretary of State. An HOA that has forfeited its corporate status cannot enforce covenants or collect assessments in the same manner. Buyers need to know this, and listing agents should disclose it.

Fifth, dual agents neglect to explain the buyer's cancellation rights under Form 170 because they are trying to keep the deal together. This violates the agent's duty under Kansas limited agency law and can result in KREC discipline. Tracking these overlapping obligations across multiple deals is exactly the kind of administrative burden that causes [agents to miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under K.S.A. 58-30,111. If an agent in your office routinely skips Form 170 or fails to order resale certificates, KREC can hold you responsible for inadequate supervision. Here is what a broker audit should cover:

| Audit Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|------------|---------------|----------|
| Form 170 attachment | Is it included in every file involving HOA/condo property? | Missing from file; no HOA noted in MLS but deed shows covenants |
| Resale certificate in file | Was it received before closing? | Certificate dated after closing or absent entirely |
| Buyer review period documented | Is there a signed acknowledgment or dated delivery receipt? | No proof buyer received docs within the contractual window |
| Seller disclosure cross-reference | Does Form 400 match the resale certificate on assessments? | Discrepancies in monthly dues or special assessments |
| Timeline tracking | Were deadlines met with margin? | Certificate requested fewer than 15 days before closing |

Brokers should institute a policy requiring agents to upload the resale certificate request confirmation to the transaction file within 72 hours of mutual acceptance. ## Federal Overlay: Lead Paint and Fair Housing Intersections

Kansas HOA transactions involving properties built before 1978 still require compliance with the federal Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Rule (42 U.S.C. 4852d). The HOA addendum does not exempt you from attaching EPA Form "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" and securing the buyer's signed acknowledgment. This is a separate obligation layered on top of the state HOA rules.

Additionally, HOA rules themselves can raise Fair Housing Act concerns. If an association's governing documents contain restrictions that discriminate based on familial status, disability, or other protected classes, the listing agent has an obligation to flag the issue rather than simply passing along the documents. KREC has signaled in continuing education guidance that passively distributing discriminatory covenants without comment does not insulate agents from complaint.

The [Kansas Real Estate Commission's website](https://krec.ks.gov) publishes disciplinary actions and advisory opinions. Reviewing recent enforcement cases involving HOA-related complaints is one of the fastest ways to understand where the Commission draws lines.
