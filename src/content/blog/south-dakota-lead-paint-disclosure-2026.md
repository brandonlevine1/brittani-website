## Understanding South Dakota Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

If you hold a license in South Dakota and sell residential property built before 1978, you are bound by federal lead paint disclosure law whether or not South Dakota has enacted its own parallel statute. The South Dakota lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain anchored to the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulation at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H. South Dakota does not layer additional state-specific lead paint disclosure mandates on top of the federal framework, but that does not reduce your exposure — it concentrates it entirely on your compliance with one set of rules.

The South Dakota Real Estate Commission (SDREC) expects licensees to follow federal disclosure law as part of their fiduciary and statutory duties. Failure to do so can trigger disciplinary action under SDCL 36-21A-71, which governs incompetence and negligence by licensed agents.

## Who Must Disclose and When

**Every seller and landlord of pre-1978 housing must disclose known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards before the buyer or tenant becomes obligated under a contract.** The agent representing the seller shares this duty — you are not merely a messenger passing along paperwork. You have an independent obligation to ensure the disclosure happens correctly and on time.

The law applies to the sale of any residential dwelling built before 1978, including single-family homes, condominiums, duplexes, and apartments offered for sale. Properties exempt from the rule include housing certified as lead-free by an accredited inspector, zero-bedroom dwellings (studios and lofts), housing designated exclusively for elderly or disabled persons where no child under six resides, and short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or less.

Timing matters enormously. The disclosure must be delivered before the buyer is obligated — meaning before the purchase agreement becomes binding. In South Dakota, where many transactions use the South Dakota Association of REALTORS (SDAR) standard forms, this typically means the disclosure must accompany or precede the execution of the purchase agreement.

## The Forms South Dakota Agents Use

South Dakota does not mandate a state-created lead paint disclosure form. Instead, agents rely on the EPA's standard form titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards." This form satisfies the requirements of 24 CFR 35.92 and must be signed by the seller, the buyer, and their respective agents.

| Document | Purpose | Who Signs |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| EPA Lead Paint Disclosure Form | Disclose known hazards, provide pamphlet, confirm 10-day inspection opportunity | Seller, Buyer, Both Agents |
| SDAR Form 801 (Seller's Property Condition Disclosure) | Broader property condition disclosure; may include lead paint section | Seller |
| EPA Pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" | Inform buyer of lead risks | Delivered to Buyer |
| Lead Inspection Waiver (if applicable) | Buyer's written waiver of the 10-day inspection period | Buyer |

Many brokerages in South Dakota integrate the lead paint disclosure into their transaction packets alongside SDAR Form 801, the Seller's Property Condition Disclosure Statement. SDAR Form 801 contains a section referencing lead-based paint, but it does not by itself satisfy the federal requirement. You must still use or attach the standalone EPA form.

## The 10-Day Inspection Period: Non-Negotiable Unless Waived in Writing

Buyers must receive a minimum 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense. This is a federal floor — you can offer more time, but you cannot offer less unless the buyer voluntarily waives the opportunity in writing. A verbal waiver does not count.

**The 10-day clock begins when the buyer receives the disclosure and the EPA pamphlet, not when the purchase agreement is signed.** If you deliver the disclosure late, the inspection window shifts accordingly, which can cascade into extension requests and blown closing dates. Agents who track deadlines manually often lose sight of this distinction when juggling multiple files simultaneously — a problem discussed further in our guide on [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals).

The waiver must be explicit. A clause buried in an addendum that says "buyer acknowledges receipt of lead paint disclosure" is not a waiver of the inspection period. SDAR transactional forms do include a checkbox-style waiver option within the lead paint disclosure form, but both agents should confirm the buyer initialed or signed that specific line.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance in South Dakota

The consequences of failing to comply with lead paint disclosure law are severe and multi-layered. They come from federal enforcement, private litigation, and state licensing discipline.

| Violation Type | Potential Consequence | Authority |
|---------------|----------------------|-----------|
| Failure to disclose known hazards | Treble damages in private lawsuit | 42 U.S.C. 4852d |
| Failure to provide EPA pamphlet | Civil penalty up to $19,507 per violation | HUD/EPA enforcement |
| Failure to provide 10-day inspection opportunity | Contract voidable by buyer | 24 CFR 35.90 |
| Pattern of non-compliance | Criminal penalties up to $19,507/day | 15 U.S.C. 2615 |
| Incompetence or negligence | License suspension or revocation | SDCL 36-21A-71 |

HUD and the EPA have [joint enforcement authority](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-based-paint-disclosure-rule) and have pursued actions against individual agents, not just sellers. In 2024, the per-violation civil penalty cap was adjusted for inflation to $19,507. Courts can also award the buyer's attorney fees and court costs, turning what might seem like a paperwork oversight into a six-figure liability event.

Under South Dakota law, the SDREC can open a disciplinary investigation if a complaint is filed alleging an agent knowingly failed to disclose or facilitated a transaction without proper lead paint documentation. SDCL 36-21A-71 lists "incompetency" and "any conduct which constitutes dishonest dealing" as grounds for discipline ranging from a reprimand to license revocation.

## Common Mistakes South Dakota Agents Make

Even experienced agents trip on lead paint disclosure. These are the five errors that surface most often in South Dakota transactions.

**First, agents assume the seller's verbal denial is sufficient.** The seller must disclose in writing — on the form — whether they have knowledge of lead-based paint or hazards. A casual "no, we never had any issues" during listing intake does not satisfy the regulation. If the form is blank or unsigned, you are exposed.

Second, agents forget to attach available records. Section 1018 requires the seller to provide the buyer with any available records or reports pertaining to lead-based paint in the home. This includes prior inspection reports, abatement records, and even old disclosure forms from when the seller purchased the property. Failing to hand these over, even if the seller says "I lost them," creates liability if the records later surface.

Third, agents deliver the EPA pamphlet late or not at all. The pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" must be provided to the buyer before they become obligated. Handing it over at closing — after the contract is binding — violates the timing requirement and gives the buyer grounds to rescind.

Fourth, agents pressure buyers to waive the 10-day inspection window. Encouraging a buyer to skip the inspection to "keep the deal moving" can be construed as interference with the buyer's federally protected right. If a dispute arises later, that pressure becomes evidence of bad faith.

Fifth, agents fail to retain copies. Federal law requires that you keep a copy of the completed disclosure for at least three years from the date of the sale. Agents who rely on their brokerage's filing system without confirming retention policies risk being unable to produce documentation if HUD or a plaintiff's attorney comes calling years later. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers in South Dakota carry supervisory liability under SDCL 36-21A-69, which requires them to exercise reasonable supervision over the activities of their licensees. If an agent in your office consistently fails to include lead paint disclosures in pre-1978 transactions, the SDREC can hold you accountable.

**A quarterly file audit is the minimum standard most compliance consultants recommend.** During that audit, brokers should verify that every closed file involving a pre-1978 property contains a signed EPA disclosure form, a record that the pamphlet was delivered, and either a signed inspection waiver or documentation that the 10-day period elapsed without issue.

Brokers should also confirm that their agents are dating the disclosure delivery correctly. The date the buyer received the form matters for calculating the inspection window. If agents are backdating or leaving the date field blank, you have a systemic compliance gap.

Training is not optional. New licensees in South Dakota receive minimal instruction on lead paint law during pre-licensing education. Brokers who onboard agents without a dedicated session on federal disclosure obligations — including role-playing the delivery conversation — are building risk into every transaction their team touches.

Finally, brokers should audit their transaction management systems to confirm that lead paint disclosures are flagged as mandatory documents for all pre-1978 listings at the point of file creation, not at closing. If your system allows a file to proceed to contract without a lead paint disclosure attached, your workflow has a design flaw.
