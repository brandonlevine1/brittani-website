## Florida New Real Estate Laws 2026: The Statutory Changes That Hit Your License Hardest

The 2026 Florida legislative session delivered more than talking points. Florida new real estate laws 2026 introduced enforceable changes to disclosure obligations, wire fraud prevention, energy efficiency reporting, and form requirements that directly affect how you handle every residential transaction starting July 1. The Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) has already issued guidance memos, and the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) updated its enforcement protocols in April.

If you have not read the amended text of Florida Statutes Sections 475.25, 475.278, and 689.261, you are operating blind. This post breaks down what changed, what forms you now need, and what happens when agents get it wrong.

## Disclosure Overhaul: Flood, Energy, and Material Facts

**The most consequential change for listing agents is the expanded material fact disclosure requirement under the revised Section 689.261.** Florida previously allowed a relatively narrow interpretation of what constituted a material defect. The 2026 amendment explicitly adds flood history, prior insurance claims for water damage, and current FEMA flood zone designation to the list of facts a seller must disclose — and that a listing agent must confirm was disclosed.

The new Seller's Property Disclosure form (FR/Bar-ASIS-6, revised January 2026) now contains a dedicated flood history section with five specific questions. Agents cannot simply hand over a blank form and hope the seller fills it out correctly. FREC's updated Rule 61J2-14.009 places an affirmative duty on the listing agent to review the disclosure for obvious omissions before submission to the buyer's side.

Separately, Section 475.25(1)(b) was amended to include energy efficiency data. For any property built before 2010, the seller must provide a one-page energy performance summary or check a box acknowledging none is available. The standardized form is FR/Bar-EE-1, released in March 2026 by Florida Realtors in partnership with The Florida Bar.

## Wire Fraud Advisory: A New Mandatory Step

Florida's wire fraud problem is not hypothetical. The FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center reported $446 million in real estate wire fraud losses nationally in 2024, with Florida ranking second behind California. The legislature responded with a 2026 amendment to Section 475.278 requiring a standalone wire fraud advisory disclosure signed by both buyer and seller at or before contract execution.

**The advisory is not optional, and it is not satisfied by boilerplate language buried in a contract addendum.** You must use the new FR/Bar-WF-1 form or a substantially similar document approved by your brokerage's legal counsel. The form must include the phone number of the title company or closing agent handling escrow and a warning that wire instructions should always be verified by phone using a number obtained independently of any email.

Failure to deliver this form triggers a per-transaction fine of up to $5,000 from FREC. If a buyer is actually defrauded and the form was never provided, the agent and brokerage face civil liability under Florida's Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act (FDUTPA), Chapter 501. That exposure dwarfs any commission you earned on the deal.

For agents juggling multiple closings and struggling to keep track of which disclosures have been delivered, [building a reliable system for tracking active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) is no longer a nice-to-have — it is a compliance requirement.

## Updated Forms: What Changed and When to Use Them

The table below compares the key forms revised for 2026 against their prior versions:

| Form Number | Name | Key 2026 Change | Effective Date | Prior Version |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FR/Bar-ASIS-6 | As-Is Residential Contract | Added flood disclosure section, wire fraud acknowledgment clause | July 1, 2026 | FR/Bar-ASIS-5 |
| FR/Bar-11 | Residential Sale and Purchase Contract | Energy disclosure delivery deadline added, updated inspection contingency language | July 1, 2026 | FR/Bar-10 |
| FR/Bar-WF-1 | Wire Fraud Advisory | Entirely new form | July 1, 2026 | N/A |
| FR/Bar-EE-1 | Energy Efficiency Disclosure | Entirely new form | July 1, 2026 | N/A |
| FR/Bar-CRSP-16 | Comprehensive Rider to Sale and Purchase | Revised HOA disclosure timeline from 15 to 10 days | July 1, 2026 | FR/Bar-CRSP-15 |

Using an outdated form after July 1 does not automatically void a contract, but it creates grounds for rescission if the buyer can show they were not informed of a right or obligation that the current form would have disclosed. FREC has historically treated use of superseded forms as evidence of incompetence under Section 475.25(1)(o).

## Common Mistakes Agents Are Already Making

Based on early enforcement actions and FREC complaint filings from Q1 and Q2 2026, five errors are emerging with alarming frequency.

**First, agents are delivering the wire fraud advisory after contract execution rather than at or before.** The statute says "at or before." Sending it with the title commitment two weeks later does not satisfy the requirement. The advisory must be part of the initial contract package or delivered as a standalone document before the buyer signs.

Second, listing agents are failing to review the Seller's Property Disclosure before sending it to the buyer's agent. The seller checks "unknown" on every flood-related question despite the property sitting in a FEMA AE flood zone clearly marked on the survey attached to the listing file. FREC considers this willful blindness, not plausible deniability.

Third, agents are still using FR/Bar-ASIS-5 because their MLS auto-populates the old form. Your MLS template is not a legal defense. You must manually verify you are attaching the correct version of every form. If your brokerage has not updated its document library, that is a conversation you need to have with your broker this week.

Fourth, the energy efficiency disclosure (FR/Bar-EE-1) is being skipped entirely on pre-2010 properties. Some agents assume it only applies to new construction. It does not. The statute applies to any residential property built before 2010 that is being sold.

Fifth, the revised HOA disclosure timeline on FR/Bar-CRSP-16 gives buyers 10 days instead of 15 to cancel after receiving HOA documents. Agents who verbally tell buyers they have 15 days are providing incorrect legal guidance. That five-day difference has already caused at least two reported failed cancellation attempts in Broward County.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Section 475.25(1)(u) for the acts of their sales associates. If your agents are making the mistakes described above, FREC can discipline both the agent and the supervising broker. The 2026 amendments did not change that framework, but they expanded the number of ways agents can trip.

Here is what managing brokers should be auditing immediately:

Every transaction file opened after July 1 must contain a signed FR/Bar-WF-1 dated on or before the contract effective date. A spot audit of 10 percent of active files should reveal whether your agents understand the timing requirement. If even one file is missing the form, assume the problem is systemic.

Your document management system must be updated to include FR/Bar-EE-1 in the default checklist for any property built before 2010. Agents will not voluntarily add forms they have never used before. The brokerage must push this into the workflow, not rely on agent memory. This is precisely the kind of task breakdown that [a structured checklist approach](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) was designed to solve.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Wire fraud advisory (FR/Bar-WF-1) present and dated correctly | Every file | Form dated after contract effective date |
| Seller's Property Disclosure reviewed by listing agent | Every listing file | All flood questions marked "unknown" on a property in a FEMA flood zone |
| Correct form version (2026 revisions) | Every file after July 1 | Any form ending in a pre-2026 revision number |
| Energy disclosure (FR/Bar-EE-1) in file for pre-2010 properties | Every applicable file | Form absent entirely |
| HOA cancellation period communicated accurately | Random audit of buyer communications | Any written or verbal reference to 15-day cancellation period |

Brokers who discover compliance gaps should document the remediation. A written memo to the agent, with a copy retained in the personnel file, creates a record that the brokerage took corrective action — which matters enormously if FREC later opens an investigation.

## Liability Exposure: Fines, License Action, and Deal Collapse

The penalties for non-compliance are tiered but real. FREC can impose fines ranging from $1,000 to $5,000 per violation under its updated penalty guidelines (Rule 61J2-24.001). Repeated violations or a pattern of non-compliance can result in license suspension or revocation.

Beyond regulatory penalties, the civil exposure is substantial. A buyer who loses $300,000 to a wire fraud scheme and was never given the FR/Bar-WF-1 has a straightforward FDUTPA claim against the agent and brokerage. The measure of damages is the actual loss, plus attorney's fees. Your E&O policy may or may not cover a claim rooted in statutory non-compliance — check with your carrier now.

Deal cancellation is the other risk. A buyer who receives a Seller's Property Disclosure with omitted flood history can rescind under the revised Section 689.261(4) within 15 days of discovering the omission. That means a deal you thought was closed can unravel months later if the buyer finds out their property flooded three times in the prior decade and the disclosure said "unknown." The listing agent who failed to review the form shares responsibility with the seller.
