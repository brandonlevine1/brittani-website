## Indiana Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026: The Rules That Changed Everything

The Indiana buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 framework represents the most significant shift in buyer-side practice since Indiana first codified agency relationships in IC 25-34.1. Agents who treated buyer agreements as optional paperwork before August 2024 no longer have that luxury. The combination of state statute, Indiana Administrative Code, and the NAR settlement's practice changes has created an environment where a missing signature can kill a commission, trigger a licensing complaint, or expose a brokerage to civil liability.

This post breaks down exactly what Indiana law demands, which forms satisfy those demands, and where agents and brokers are still getting it wrong heading into the second half of 2026.

## The Statutory and Regulatory Foundation

Indiana's agency framework lives in IC 25-34.1 (the Real Estate Broker Licensing Act) and the rules promulgated under [Indiana Administrative Code Title 876](https://www.in.gov/pla/professions/real-estate/). The Indiana Professional Licensing Agency (IPLA), operating through the Indiana Real Estate Commission, enforces these provisions. Two sections matter most for buyer agency agreements.

**876 IAC 8-2-9 requires that the nature and scope of a licensee's agency relationship be established in writing before providing brokerage services.** This was already on the books, but enforcement tightened dramatically after August 17, 2024, when NAR settlement practice changes went live. The settlement's Participant Duty—requiring a written agreement before touring a home—aligned perfectly with Indiana's existing administrative code, removing any ambiguity agents previously relied on.

IC 25-34.1-10-9.5 addresses agency disclosure forms and the requirement that buyers receive a written explanation of agency types at first substantive contact. This disclosure is separate from the buyer agency agreement itself, but both must exist in the transaction file.

## Which Forms Indiana Agents Must Use

The Indiana Association of REALTORS (IAR) publishes the standard forms that most MLS participants rely on. For buyer agency, two primary form numbers control the conversation.

| Form Number | Form Name | Use Case |
|-------------|-----------|----------|
| IAR Form 44 | Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement | Buyer commits to one agent/brokerage for a defined period |
| IAR Form 44A | Non-Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement | Buyer retains right to work with multiple agents simultaneously |
| IAR Form 44B | Buyer Agency Agreement—Single Property | Engagement limited to one specific property showing or transaction |
| IAR Form 51 | Agency Disclosure | Required at first substantive contact, separate from the agreement |

IAR Form 44 is the workhorse. It specifies the duration of representation, the geographic area (if limited), the compensation structure, and the duties owed to the buyer. Form 44B emerged as a practical response to buyers who refuse to sign an exclusive agreement before a single showing—it satisfies the written-agreement requirement without locking either party into a broader relationship.

Every agent should also understand that compensation terms must be clearly stated in the agreement. Post-settlement rules prohibit referencing MLS cooperative compensation offers as the basis for buyer-agent pay. The amount or rate must be specified in the written agreement, and any request for the seller to cover that amount must flow through the purchase agreement, not through a blanket MLS field.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**Non-compliance carries three categories of risk: regulatory discipline, financial loss, and civil liability.** The Indiana Real Estate Commission can impose fines up to $10,000 per violation under IC 25-1-11-12, suspend or revoke a license, and require additional education. These are not theoretical outcomes—the Commission issued 47 disciplinary actions in fiscal year 2025, several involving documentation failures in buyer representation.

On the financial side, a buyer-agent who cannot produce a signed agreement has no contractual basis to claim compensation. If a dispute arises—say the buyer purchases a property through another agent or directly from a FSBO—the original agent has no enforceable claim without that written contract. Indiana courts have consistently held that oral agreements for real estate brokerage services are unenforceable under the statute of frauds.

Civil liability adds another layer. If an agent provides substantive services without a signed agreement, questions about whom the agent represented can surface during litigation. Sellers have successfully argued that an agent who toured a home with a buyer—without a buyer agreement—was acting as a subagent of the seller, creating disclosure obligations the agent never intended to assume. That kind of confusion is exactly what the written agreement prevents.

For brokers watching their E&O exposure, this matters enormously. An undocumented agency relationship is an invitation for an errors-and-omissions claim, and carriers are increasingly auditing whether agreements were in place before services began. Similar documentation pressures exist across states, as agents in [Washington have also discovered](/blog/washington-buyer-agency-agreement-requirements-2026) with their own tightened requirements.

## Common Mistakes Indiana Agents Still Make

Even eighteen months after the NAR settlement changes took effect, agents in Indiana continue to stumble on the same five errors.

**First, agents present the buyer agency agreement too late.** The rule is clear: before substantive services. That means before the first showing, before sending CMAs, before making phone calls to listing agents on the buyer's behalf. Handing the agreement to a buyer in the car outside a property—or worse, at the offer stage—violates both 876 IAC 8-2-9 and the MLS participation rules that flow from the settlement.

Second, agents leave the compensation section blank or vague. Writing "as per MLS" or "to be determined" does not satisfy the requirement that compensation be specified in the agreement. The buyer must understand, before signing, exactly what they are agreeing to pay (or request the seller to pay). A blank line is an unenforceable term and a regulatory red flag.

Third, agents fail to explain that the buyer agency agreement is negotiable. Indiana law does not set minimum or maximum commission rates, and the agreement's duration, scope, and compensation are all subject to negotiation. Presenting the form as a take-it-or-leave-it document invites consumer complaints to the Commission and potential antitrust scrutiny.

Fourth, agents confuse the IAR Form 51 (Agency Disclosure) with the buyer agency agreement itself. These are separate documents with different legal functions. The disclosure explains the types of agency available; the agreement establishes the actual relationship. Both must be signed, but they are not interchangeable.

Fifth, agents use outdated form versions. IAR updates its forms periodically, and the 2025 revisions to Forms 44 and 44A included material changes to the compensation provisions. Using a pre-settlement version of the form in 2026 creates compliance gaps that auditors and opposing counsel will catch.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear direct supervisory liability under IC 25-34.1-4-11 for the acts of their affiliated licensees. That means a single agent's failure to obtain a proper buyer agreement can generate a complaint against the broker's license, not just the agent's.

**A quarterly audit of active buyer files should verify five specific items.** The following table outlines what brokers should check and the consequence of each gap.

| Audit Item | What to Verify | Risk if Missing |
|------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Signed IAR Form 44, 44A, or 44B | Agreement executed before first showing or substantive service | Commission claim unenforceable; regulatory complaint |
| Compensation terms filled in | Specific dollar amount or percentage stated | Potential antitrust issue; consumer complaint |
| Duration and expiration date | Clear start and end dates, no indefinite terms | Consumer complaint; Commission inquiry |
| Signed IAR Form 51 | Agency Disclosure executed at first substantive contact | 876 IAC 8-2-9 violation; disciplinary action |
| Form version current | 2025 or later revision used | Material omissions; E&O vulnerability |

Beyond file audits, brokers should build systems that prevent gaps rather than just catching them after the fact. Transaction management platforms that flag missing documents before a showing is scheduled—or before an offer is submitted—eliminate the most common compliance failures. Brokers should also provide annual training on form updates and negotiation scripts. Agents who feel confident explaining the purpose of the buyer agreement to skeptical consumers are far less likely to skip the step or defer it to an inappropriate stage in the relationship. The agents who struggle most are those who view the agreement as an obstacle to showing homes rather than as the legal foundation of their right to be compensated.

## Duration, Termination, and Renewal Provisions

Indiana law does not impose a maximum duration on buyer agency agreements, but the NAR settlement's practice standards encourage "reasonable" terms. What counts as reasonable is fact-specific, but the Indiana Real Estate Commission has signaled—through continuing education materials and informal guidance—that agreements exceeding six months without a clear justification invite scrutiny.

**Most Indiana brokerages have settled on 90-day initial terms with written renewal options.** This balances the agent's interest in protecting their investment of time against the buyer's interest in flexibility. The agreement should specify what happens at expiration: does it auto-renew, lapse, or convert to a non-exclusive arrangement?

Termination clauses deserve equal attention. A well-drafted agreement specifies the circumstances under which either party may terminate early, any notice requirements, and whether a protection period survives termination. The protection period—typically 30 to 90 days—ensures the agent receives compensation if the buyer purchases a property the agent previously showed, even after the agreement ends. Agents who skip this clause lose their safety net entirely.

Handling termination disputes requires documentation discipline similar to what agents face with [deadline management in other transaction stages](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines). A timestamped record of when the agreement was signed, when services began, and which properties were shown creates the evidentiary trail needed if a commission dispute reaches mediation or court.
