## Understanding Georgia Continuing Education Requirements 2026

Georgia continuing education requirements 2026 represent one of the most misunderstood compliance obligations in the state's real estate industry. Every year, the Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) processes thousands of lapsed licenses—many of which could have been prevented with basic calendar management and a clear understanding of the rules. The stakes are not abstract: an agent operating on an expired or inactive license faces personal liability, potential deal rescission, and disciplinary action under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-15.

GREC operates under Title 43, Chapter 40 of the Official Code of Georgia Annotated, which governs all real estate licensing activity in the state. The Commission's administrative rules, particularly Rule 520-1-.05, lay out the specific CE requirements that agents must satisfy before renewal. Missing these details does not simply result in a slap on the wrist—it can end a career.

The 2026 renewal cycle follows the same structural framework established in prior years, but agents should not assume nothing has changed. GREC periodically updates approved course topics, provider requirements, and enforcement priorities. Staying current means reading the actual rules, not relying on what your colleague told you at an open house.

## How Many Hours You Actually Need

**Georgia requires 36 hours of continuing education over each four-year license renewal period for salespersons and associate brokers.** This is not an annual requirement—it accumulates across the full cycle. However, first-time renewals operate under a compressed timeline: 25 hours must be completed before the first renewal date, which falls on the licensee's birthday in the year their license is first due for renewal.

Of those 36 hours, a mandatory 3-hour course on Georgia License Law updates is non-negotiable. This course must be approved by GREC and must cover recent amendments to O.C.G.A. Title 43, Chapter 40. The remaining 33 hours can be fulfilled through any GREC-approved elective courses, which range from ethics and fair housing to contract law and risk management.

Brokers holding a broker's license face identical hour requirements. There is no reduced CE track for brokers in Georgia, unlike some states that offer reciprocal credit for supervisory experience.

| Licensee Type | Total CE Hours (4-Year Cycle) | Mandatory License Law Hours | Elective Hours | First Renewal Requirement |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Salesperson | 36 | 3 | 33 | 25 hours before first renewal |
| Associate Broker | 36 | 3 | 33 | 25 hours before first renewal |
| Qualifying Broker | 36 | 3 | 33 | 25 hours before first renewal |

Renewal dates in Georgia are tied to the licensee's date of birth—not a universal calendar deadline. This means every agent in a brokerage may have a different renewal window, which creates a tracking headache for managing brokers.

## What Happens When You Fall Out of Compliance

**Failing to complete CE by your renewal deadline triggers an automatic status change to inactive.** GREC does not issue warning letters or grace periods. On the day after your birthday, if your hours are not logged with an approved provider and reflected in GREC's system, your license goes inactive under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-19.

An inactive licensee cannot list properties, show homes, write offers, collect commissions, or represent themselves as a licensed agent. Any transaction activity conducted while inactive constitutes unlicensed practice, which is a misdemeanor under Georgia law carrying fines up to $1,000 per occurrence. GREC can also impose additional administrative penalties through its disciplinary process outlined in Rule 520-1-.08.

If your license remains inactive for more than four years, reinstatement is no longer an option. You must retake the Georgia licensing examination and submit a new application—effectively starting from scratch. This is not a theoretical risk; [agents who miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) often discover the lapse only after a transaction is underway, creating liability for their broker and jeopardizing their client's deal.

Deals can and do fall apart over licensing issues. A buyer's attorney conducting due diligence on the listing agent's credentials may discover an inactive license and advise their client to rescind the offer. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond the commission check—it damages reputations and invites regulatory scrutiny.

## Common Mistakes Georgia Agents Make With CE

Five recurring errors account for the majority of GREC disciplinary actions related to continuing education. Avoiding them requires attention to detail, not heroic effort.

**First, agents confuse their renewal date with a calendar-year deadline.** Georgia ties renewal to your birthday, not January 1 or a fiscal year. An agent born in March 2026 must have all 36 hours completed before March 2026—not by December 2026.

Second, agents complete courses from providers not approved by GREC. Georgia maintains a list of approved schools and instructors under Rule 520-1-.04. A 6-hour ethics course from a Florida-approved provider carries zero weight in Georgia unless that provider also holds GREC approval. Agents who discover this after the fact cannot retroactively receive credit.

Third, agents fail to retain completion certificates. GREC requires that agents keep documentation of completed CE for at least five years after the renewal cycle. Form REC 3.20, the Application for License Renewal, asks licensees to attest that all CE has been completed—but GREC can audit at any time and demand proof. Without certificates, the attestation becomes a false statement.

Fourth, some agents attempt to complete all 36 hours in the final month before their renewal. While not technically prohibited, many approved courses have enrollment caps, and GREC's electronic reporting from schools can take seven to ten business days to appear in your file. Waiting until the last week is gambling with your license.

Fifth, agents ignore the mandatory 3-hour license law course and assume any three hours of approved credit will satisfy the requirement. The license law course is a specific, designated course—not interchangeable with electives.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Georgia's brokerage structure places significant compliance responsibility on the qualifying broker. Under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-18(d), the broker is accountable for ensuring that all affiliated licensees maintain active, compliant licenses. This is not advisory language—it is statutory obligation.

Brokers should maintain a renewal calendar for every agent in their office. Because Georgia staggers renewals by birthday, a brokerage with 30 agents may have 30 different deadlines throughout the year. A single missed renewal can expose the brokerage to vicarious liability if that agent conducts a transaction while inactive.

| Broker Audit Action | Frequency | Source of Verification | Risk if Skipped |
|---|---|---|---|
| Verify agent CE completion | 60 days before each agent's birthday | GREC online licensee lookup | Agent practices on inactive license |
| Confirm license law course taken | Each renewal cycle | Agent's completion certificate | Agent renewal denied |
| Retain copies of CE certificates | Ongoing (5-year minimum) | Agent submission to brokerage | Cannot prove compliance during GREC audit |
| Review GREC disciplinary bulletins | Quarterly | [GREC website](https://grec.state.ga.us/) | Miss rule changes or enforcement trends |
| Confirm approved provider status | Before recommending courses | GREC approved school list | Agent takes unapproved course, hours rejected |

**Brokers who fail to audit face their own disciplinary exposure.** GREC has sanctioned brokers for supervisory negligence under Rule 520-1-.09 when affiliated agents are found operating without proper CE. The penalties range from formal reprimand to license suspension, depending on severity and pattern.

A sound audit system does not need to be complex. It needs to be consistent. [Building a checklist-based approach](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) to compliance tracking removes the guesswork and catches problems before they become violations.

## Renewal Forms and Filing Procedures

Georgia handles license renewals primarily through its online portal, managed by GREC. The relevant form is the REC 3.20 (Application for License Renewal), which requires the licensee to certify that all CE requirements have been met. Filing this form with false attestation—claiming hours are complete when they are not—is grounds for immediate disciplinary action under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-15(a)(18), which covers fraud and misrepresentation.

The renewal fee for 2026 remains at $125 for salespersons and $150 for brokers, payable through GREC's online system. Late renewals incur additional fees, and reinstatement from inactive status carries a $75 penalty on top of the standard renewal cost. These fees are set by Rule 520-1-.02 and are subject to change by Commission vote.

Agents who need to reinstate from inactive status (within the four-year window) must file Form REC 3.21 (Application for Reinstatement) along with proof of completed CE and all applicable fees. There is no shortcut or waiver process for the CE hours themselves—every hour must be completed through an approved provider regardless of reinstatement circumstances.

## Planning Your 2026 CE Strategy

The smartest approach to Georgia CE is front-loading hours in the first two years of your renewal cycle. This provides a buffer against unexpected schedule conflicts, course availability issues, and reporting delays. An agent who completes 24 hours in years one and two only needs 12 hours across years three and four—a far less stressful pace.

Select courses that serve dual purposes whenever possible. A 6-hour contract law course satisfies CE requirements while also sharpening your actual transaction skills. Ethics courses count toward CE and help agents avoid the kind of procedural errors that [cause deals to break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) in the first place.

Track your hours independently of GREC's system. Maintain a personal spreadsheet or use your brokerage's tracking tool to log course name, provider, date, hours earned, and certificate storage location. If GREC's electronic records show a discrepancy, your personal documentation becomes your defense.
