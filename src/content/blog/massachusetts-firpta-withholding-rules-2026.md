## Understanding Massachusetts FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

Every year, a handful of Massachusetts closings collapse in the final hours because someone on the deal team failed to identify a foreign seller early enough. The Massachusetts firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 have not changed dramatically from prior years at the federal level, but the consequences of ignoring them have compounded as IRS enforcement activity increases and cross-border transactions in Greater Boston remain common. Agents who list or co-broke properties owned by non-U.S. persons need a working knowledge of withholding obligations—not just a vague awareness that "FIRPTA exists."

The Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act, codified at [26 U.S.C. Sections 1445 and 897](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/1445), requires buyers to withhold a percentage of the gross sales price when the seller is a "foreign person" as defined by the IRS. Massachusetts adds its own layer of complexity because the state is an attorney-close state, meaning the closing attorney shoulders significant procedural responsibility. But statutory liability still flows upstream to the buyer, and potentially to agents who knew or should have known about the seller's status.

## Who Qualifies as a "Foreign Person" and Why It Matters

**A "foreign person" under FIRPTA includes nonresident alien individuals, foreign corporations, foreign partnerships, foreign trusts, and foreign estates.** U.S. citizens and resident aliens are exempt. Green card holders are generally treated as U.S. residents for tax purposes, but the analysis gets complicated for dual-status taxpayers and those whose residency is contested.

Massachusetts agents encounter foreign sellers more often than many assume. University-adjacent neighborhoods in Cambridge, Allston, and Amherst attract international investors. Seasonal waterfront properties on Cape Cod and the Islands frequently have non-U.S. ownership structures. An agent who assumes every seller is domestic because they have a Massachusetts mailing address is setting up a compliance failure.

The determination should happen at listing intake—not two days before closing. Ask directly. Document the answer. If the seller says they are not a U.S. person, the withholding analysis begins immediately.

## Federal Withholding Rates and Exemptions in 2026

The default FIRPTA withholding rate is 15% of the gross amount realized (the full sale price, not the net proceeds). Two reduced-rate exceptions apply based on the buyer's intended use and the purchase price.

| Sale Price | Buyer Intends to Use as Residence | Withholding Rate |
|---|---|---|
| $300,000 or less | Yes | 0% |
| $300,001 – $1,000,000 | Yes | 10% |
| Over $1,000,000 | Yes or No | 15% |
| Any amount | No (investment/not residence) | 15% |

These thresholds apply to the amount realized, which includes cash, debt assumed, and other consideration. The "buyer intends to use as residence" exemption requires the buyer or a family member to have definite plans to reside in the property for at least 50% of the days it is in use during each of the first two 12-month periods after closing.

Sellers can apply for a withholding certificate (IRS Form 8288-B) to reduce or eliminate the withholding if their actual tax liability will be less than 15%. The problem: processing times routinely stretch to 90 days or more, and closing must either be delayed or funds escrowed pending IRS approval.

## Massachusetts State-Level Obligations for Nonresident Sellers

**Massachusetts does not have its own FIRPTA-equivalent statute, but nonresident sellers—including foreign persons—owe Massachusetts income tax on gains from the sale of real property located in the Commonwealth.** The applicable rate for capital gains on real estate held more than one year is 5% under M.G.L. Chapter 62, Section 2. Short-term gains are taxed at 8.5%.

The Massachusetts Department of Revenue (DOR) does not mandate automatic percentage withholding the way some states do. Instead, nonresident sellers may need to make estimated tax payments or file Form 1-NR/PY (Nonresident or Part-Year Resident Income Tax Return) to settle their state liability. Closing attorneys in Massachusetts often withhold an estimated amount voluntarily at closing and remit it to DOR as a protective measure, especially when the seller will leave the country post-closing. There is no statutory equivalent to IRS Form 8288 at the state level, but the practical effect is similar: money is held back to satisfy a tax obligation.

Agents should understand the interplay. A foreign seller faces both federal FIRPTA withholding (15% of gross) and potential Massachusetts state tax (5% or 8.5% of gain). On a $2 million sale with a $500,000 gain, the combined hit can exceed $340,000 in withheld or owed taxes. Sellers who do not plan for this will attempt to renegotiate terms or walk away.

## Forms and Filing Procedures Agents Must Know

The primary federal forms in a FIRPTA transaction are:

| Form | Purpose | Filed By | Deadline |
|---|---|---|---|
| IRS Form 8288 | Report and remit withholding | Buyer (transferee) | 20 days after closing |
| IRS Form 8288-A | Statement of withholding on disposition | Buyer (attached to 8288) | 20 days after closing |
| IRS Form 8288-B | Application to reduce withholding | Seller (transferor) | Before or at closing |
| Affidavit of Non-Foreign Status | Certify seller is U.S. person | Seller | At closing |
| MA Form 1-NR/PY | Nonresident state income tax return | Seller | April 15 following sale year |

The Affidavit of Non-Foreign Status (sometimes called a "FIRPTA Affidavit" or "Transferor's Certification") is the document that eliminates withholding when the seller is a U.S. person. It must include the seller's name, U.S. taxpayer identification number, and home address, along with a declaration under penalties of perjury. In Massachusetts closings, the closing attorney typically prepares this affidavit and collects it at the signing table.

If the seller cannot or will not provide the affidavit, the buyer must withhold. There is no discretion here—failure to withhold exposes the buyer to liability equal to the amount that should have been withheld, plus interest and penalties.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with FIRPTA Compliance

The first error is assuming FIRPTA is entirely the closing attorney's problem. Massachusetts agents benefit from the attorney-close model, but [liability under Section 1445](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/1445) can attach to "the transferee's agent" and "the transferor's agent" if they have actual knowledge that a FIRPTA affidavit is false or that withholding is required and not being performed. Ignorance protects you only if you genuinely did not know. Willful blindness does not.

The second mistake is failing to ask about foreign status at listing. Many agents treat FIRPTA as a closing-day issue. By then, a seller who expected full proceeds at closing discovers a six-figure withholding. This creates adversarial dynamics between the parties and puts the deal at risk. The [same pattern plays out in wire fraud situations](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) where last-minute surprises derail transactions.

Third, agents confuse "nonresident" with "foreign person." A U.S. citizen living in London is not a foreign person for FIRPTA purposes. A Canadian citizen with a summer home in Chatham who has no green card is. The distinction is citizenship and immigration status, not mailing address.

Fourth, some agents assume withholding applies only to individuals. Foreign LLCs, foreign trusts, and foreign corporations all trigger FIRPTA. Entity structures popular with international buyers in Boston's luxury market often have foreign ownership layers that require careful analysis—typically by a tax attorney, not the listing agent.

Fifth, agents sometimes allow sellers to provide an affidavit with a mailing address outside the U.S. while claiming U.S. person status. This is not automatically disqualifying, but it should trigger additional diligence. A facially inconsistent affidavit can constitute "knowledge" sufficient to create agent liability if the buyer later fails to withhold.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

**Brokers carry supervisory liability under Massachusetts regulations (254 CMR 2.00 et seq.) and must ensure their agents handle FIRPTA-impacted transactions properly.** This is not optional risk management—it is a regulatory obligation tied to the broker's license through the Massachusetts Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons.

A broker's compliance checklist should verify that every listing file includes documentation of the seller's citizenship status, obtained at the time of listing or shortly after. It should confirm that the transaction file notes whether FIRPTA withholding applies and whether Form 8288-B has been filed to request a reduced withholding amount. Brokers should also verify that agents have referred sellers to qualified tax counsel when foreign status is identified, rather than providing tax advice themselves.

Quarterly file audits are the minimum defensible standard. If a transaction involves a foreign seller and the file contains no FIRPTA-related documentation, the broker has a supervision gap that could become a licensing issue. The [same discipline applies to deadline management across all transaction types](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines)—documentation proves compliance.

Brokers should also maintain a written office policy on FIRPTA procedures, distributed annually and included in agent onboarding. The policy should specify who is responsible for flagging foreign seller status, the firm's protocol for coordinating with closing attorneys, and the documentation standards expected in every file.

## Liability Exposure When Agents Fail to Comply

An agent who knows the seller is foreign and fails to ensure proper withholding faces potential liability under 26 U.S.C. Section 1445(d). The IRS can assess the agent for the tax that should have been withheld, up to the amount of the agent's commission. This is not theoretical—IRS enforcement letters do get sent to agents and brokers.

Beyond federal liability, Massachusetts disciplinary action is possible if the Board determines that an agent's conduct constitutes incompetence, dishonesty, or failure to account for funds properly under M.G.L. Chapter 112, Section 87AAA. License suspension or revocation is the extreme outcome, but formal reprimands and mandatory education orders are more common first responses.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate practical risk. If the buyer's attorney discovers on closing day that FIRPTA withholding was never discussed and the seller refuses to accept reduced proceeds, the transaction falls apart. The [downstream effects mirror what happens when any compliance step is missed at the wrong moment](/blog/where-deals-break-down)—everyone loses time, money, and trust.

## Putting It Into Practice for 2026 Transactions

The agents who handle foreign seller transactions smoothly are the ones who build withholding analysis into their listing workflow rather than treating it as an afterthought. A single question at the listing appointment—"Are you a U.S. citizen or permanent resident?"—sets the entire compliance chain in motion. If the answer raises FIRPTA concerns, the next step is a referral to tax counsel and a heads-up to the closing attorney, not a scramble at the signing table.
