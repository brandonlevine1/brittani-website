# North Dakota Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026: What Every Agent Must Get Right

The North Dakota lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain one of the most straightforward yet frequently botched compliance obligations in residential real estate. Federal law — specifically the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X) and its implementing regulation at 24 CFR Part 35 and 40 CFR Part 745 — applies uniformly across all 50 states, including North Dakota. But the way those requirements interact with North Dakota's own transaction forms, timelines, and brokerage culture creates a distinct set of pitfalls for agents working in Fargo, Bismarck, Grand Forks, and beyond.

This is not a suggestion. It is a mandate with teeth, and the federal government has not stopped enforcing it.

## Why Lead Paint Disclosure Still Matters in North Dakota

North Dakota's housing stock is older than many agents assume. According to U.S. Census data, approximately 45 percent of homes in the state were built before 1978 — the year the federal government banned lead-based paint in residential construction. That means nearly half the homes you list or sell could trigger disclosure obligations.

The North Dakota Real Estate Commission (NDREC), operating under N.D.C.C. Chapter 43-23, does not have a state-specific lead paint statute layered on top of the federal rules. Instead, compliance runs through the federal framework administered by the EPA and HUD. However, NDREC holds licensees accountable for adhering to all applicable laws in the course of a transaction, meaning a lead paint violation can become a licensing issue under N.D.C.C. 43-23-11.1 even without a separate state lead paint statute.

Agents who treat this as a "check the box" exercise often miss critical steps that expose them — and their brokers — to liability.

## The Federal Framework That Governs North Dakota Transactions

The governing statute is [42 U.S.C. 4852d](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/4852d), implemented through EPA regulation 40 CFR 745.100-745.119. The rule applies to the sale of any "target housing" — defined as residential housing built before 1978, with limited exceptions for housing for the elderly, zero-bedroom dwellings, and properties that have been certified lead-free by a licensed inspector.

Under this rule, three things must happen before a buyer becomes obligated under a purchase contract. First, the seller must disclose any known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards. Second, the seller must provide the buyer with any available records or reports regarding lead paint in the property. Third, the buyer must receive the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" and be given a 10-day opportunity to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment.

**The agent's role is not passive.** Under 40 CFR 745.115, agents have an independent duty to ensure the seller complies with these requirements. An agent cannot escape liability by claiming ignorance of the seller's knowledge or by asserting that compliance was the seller's problem.

## Forms and Documents North Dakota Agents Must Use

North Dakota does not have a state-promulgated lead paint disclosure form. Agents rely on the federal disclosure form titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards," which is the standard EPA/HUD form used nationwide. This form requires signatures from the seller, the buyer, and their respective agents — all four parties.

The North Dakota Association of REALTORS (NDAR) integrates lead paint acknowledgment language into its standard residential purchase agreement forms. However, the embedded language in the purchase agreement does not replace the stand-alone federal disclosure form. Both must be completed when the property is pre-1978 housing.

| Document | Who Completes It | When It's Required | Where It Goes |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| EPA/HUD Lead Paint Disclosure Form | Seller, Buyer, Both Agents | Before contract is binding | Retained by all parties for 3 years |
| EPA Pamphlet ("Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home") | Listing agent delivers to buyer | Before or at time of disclosure | Buyer retains |
| NDAR Purchase Agreement lead paint provisions | Buyer and seller | At contract execution | Part of transaction file |
| Any existing lead inspection reports | Seller provides | With disclosure form | Buyer retains copies |

Agents must retain copies of the signed disclosure form for no fewer than three years from the date of completion. This retention requirement applies to the individual agent, not just the brokerage, under 40 CFR 745.113(c).

## Common Mistakes North Dakota Agents Make

Even experienced agents stumble on lead paint disclosure. Here are the errors that show up most frequently in North Dakota transactions and NDREC complaint files.

**Mistake one: Treating the 10-day inspection period as optional or waivable without documentation.** The buyer has the right to a 10-day period to conduct a lead inspection. The buyer can waive this period, but that waiver must be documented in writing on the disclosure form. Agents who simply skip past this section or who verbally tell buyers "you can get an inspection if you want" without memorializing the waiver are out of compliance. The 10-day period can be shortened or lengthened by mutual agreement, but the default is 10 days and the waiver requires the buyer's signature.

Mistake two: Failing to get the listing agent's signature on the form. The disclosure form has four signature lines — seller, buyer, seller's agent, and buyer's agent. Some listing agents fill out the seller's section and hand it off without signing themselves. Under 40 CFR 745.113(b)(3), the agent must certify that they informed the seller of their obligations. A missing agent signature means the form is incomplete.

Mistake three: Confusing "no knowledge" with "no disclosure required." If a seller states they have no knowledge of lead paint or hazards, the disclosure form still must be completed. The seller checks the box indicating no knowledge and no reports, but the form itself is mandatory. Agents who skip the form entirely because the seller says "I don't know of any lead paint" are violating the rule.

Mistake four: Not providing the EPA pamphlet. The pamphlet requirement is separate from the form requirement. Simply handing the buyer a disclosure form without the "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" booklet is a standalone violation. The current version of the pamphlet was last updated by EPA in December 2012, and agents should confirm they are distributing the most recent edition. Similar requirements apply [across other states with older housing stock](/blog/montana-lead-paint-disclosure-2026).

Mistake five: Applying the disclosure to post-1978 homes unnecessarily, then doing it incorrectly. Some agents provide the form for newer homes "just to be safe," but then complete it sloppily because they view it as irrelevant. If you choose to provide the form for a post-1978 home, treat it with the same rigor — incomplete or incorrect forms in the transaction file create audit problems for brokers.

## North Dakota Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026: Penalties and Liability

The consequences for noncompliance are federal, and they are not trivial. Under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(5), violations can result in civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2026). Criminal penalties for knowing and willful violations can reach $19,507 per violation and imprisonment.

Private right of action exists as well. A buyer who was not given proper disclosure can sue for treble damages — meaning three times the amount of actual damages proven. They can also seek attorney's fees and costs. In severe cases, courts have allowed contract rescission, putting the property back in the seller's name and requiring a full refund.

| Consequence | Source | Maximum Exposure |
|-------------|--------|-----------------|
| Civil penalty per violation | 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(5) | $19,507 |
| Criminal penalty (knowing/willful) | 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(4) | $19,507 + imprisonment |
| Private lawsuit — treble damages | 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3) | 3x actual damages + attorney fees |
| Contract rescission | State contract law + federal overlay | Full purchase price refund |
| NDREC license discipline | N.D.C.C. 43-23-11.1 | Suspension, revocation, or fine |

For North Dakota licensees, the NDREC can initiate disciplinary action under N.D.C.C. 43-23-11.1 for violation of any law related to the practice of real estate. A federal lead paint violation documented through a buyer complaint or EPA action could trigger a separate licensing proceeding. Agents who [manage multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously are especially vulnerable to letting one disclosure slip through the cracks.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in North Dakota carry supervisory responsibility for their agents' compliance. Under N.D.C.C. 43-23-08, brokers must exercise reasonable supervision over agents. That obligation extends to lead paint disclosure practices.

**Brokers should conduct quarterly file audits specifically targeting pre-1978 transactions.** The audit should verify four things: the presence of the signed EPA/HUD disclosure form with all four signatures, documentation that the EPA pamphlet was provided, evidence that the 10-day inspection period was honored or properly waived, and copies of any existing lead reports the seller provided. If any of these elements are missing, the broker has a compliance gap.

Training is the other half of the equation. Many brokerages cover lead paint in their onboarding but never revisit it. Annual refresher training — even a 15-minute review during a team meeting — significantly reduces errors. Brokers should also establish a system where transaction coordinators or administrative staff flag pre-1978 properties at listing intake so the disclosure process begins immediately, not at contract execution. Understanding [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) in the first place helps brokers design better systems.

A broker who discovers a past violation should consult legal counsel immediately. Self-reporting and remediation (providing the missing disclosure to the buyer after the fact) may not eliminate liability, but it can reduce the severity of penalties and demonstrates good faith.

## Exemptions and Edge Cases in North Dakota

Not every pre-1978 property requires the disclosure. The regulation carves out specific exemptions: housing exclusively for the elderly or persons with disabilities (unless children under six reside there), zero-bedroom dwellings such as studio apartments or dormitories, short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or fewer, and properties that have been inspected by a certified lead inspector and found to be lead-free with a written determination.

Foreclosure sales also present a gray area. When a lender sells a foreclosed property, the lender steps into the shoes of the seller and must comply with disclosure requirements. However, the lender typically has no knowledge of lead hazards and no inspection reports. The form still must be completed with the "no knowledge" boxes checked. Agents representing lenders in REO transactions sometimes skip this step, assuming institutional sellers are exempt. They are not.

Lease transactions are a separate category. Under 40 CFR 745.107, landlords leasing pre-1978 housing must also provide lead paint disclosure to tenants. North Dakota property managers and agents who handle leasing should apply the same rigor as they would in a sale. The form is identical, and the penalties are the same.

## Staying Compliant Without Slowing Down Your Business

The disclosure itself takes minutes when you build it into your listing workflow from day one. The moment you confirm a listing is pre-1978, pull the EPA/HUD form and the pamphlet. Complete the seller's section at the listing appointment. Have the form ready for the buyer's side at offer presentation. Document the 10-day period or waiver clearly.
