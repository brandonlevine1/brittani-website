## Why Oregon Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026 Still Trip Up Experienced Agents

Oregon's housing stock is old. More than 40 percent of residential structures in the state were built before 1978, which means that nearly half of the listings an agent touches in markets like Portland, Salem, and Eugene trigger federal lead paint disclosure obligations. Understanding Oregon lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 is not optional—it is a license-protection issue that carries real financial teeth.

The federal Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, codified at 42 U.S.C. §§ 4851–4856) sets the floor. Oregon layers additional obligations through ORS 431A.330 and the rules enforced by the Oregon Health Authority's Lead-Based Paint Program. Neither the federal nor the state framework changed dramatically in the past twelve months, but enforcement posture has shifted: EPA Region 10, which covers Oregon, [increased residential enforcement actions](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-laws-and-regulations) in 2025, and the Oregon Real Estate Agency (OREA) flagged lead disclosure gaps in its annual audit findings.

Agents who assume they already know this material are the ones who get caught. The rest of this post breaks down exactly which forms to use, what the penalties look like, where agents most often fail, and what managing brokers should be auditing right now.

## The Federal Framework That Governs Oregon Transactions

**Every pre-1978 residential sale in Oregon triggers duties under 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart F, regardless of whether the property actually contains lead paint.** The seller must disclose any known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards, provide the buyer with any available records or reports, and furnish the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home."

The buyer then receives a minimum 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment. That 10-day window is a default—the parties can agree to a different period or the buyer can waive it entirely, but the opportunity must be offered in writing.

Critically, 24 CFR § 35.86 imposes an independent obligation on the agent. The listing agent must ensure the seller performs each step. The buyer's agent must ensure the buyer receives the pamphlet and the 10-day notice. Neither agent can duck responsibility by claiming the other side handled it.

## Oregon-Specific Layers: ORS 431A and OREA Expectations

Oregon's lead-based paint statutes in ORS Chapter 431A primarily target renovation, repair, and painting activities, but ORS 431A.330 establishes the Oregon Health Authority's authority to enforce lead hazard disclosure in housing. The Oregon Real Estate Agency—the body that licenses agents under ORS Chapter 696—treats lead disclosure failures as potential violations of ORS 696.301(1), which prohibits conduct that is "dishonest or incompetent."

In practical terms, OREA does not maintain a separate lead disclosure form distinct from the federal model. Instead, agents rely on the standard EPA/HUD disclosure form or the Oregon Real Estate Forms (OREF) equivalent. OREF Form 035, the Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards Disclosure Addendum, is the version most commonly attached to purchase agreements. Agents working outside of OREF-based brokerages may use their MLS-provided forms, but the content requirements remain identical.

| Requirement | Source | Agent Duty |
|---|---|---|
| Disclose known lead paint/hazards | 42 U.S.C. § 4852d | Ensure seller completes disclosure |
| Provide EPA pamphlet | 40 CFR § 745.107 | Confirm buyer receipt, retain proof |
| Offer 10-day inspection period | 24 CFR § 35.88 | Include in contract language |
| Retain signed disclosure 3 years | 24 CFR § 35.92 | Both listing and buyer agent |
| Report known hazards to OHA | ORS 431A.330 | Applies to property owners primarily |

This table is the skeleton of your compliance checklist. Miss any row, and you have exposure.

## Penalties: What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

**A lead disclosure violation can unravel a transaction and expose the agent personally.** Under federal law, civil penalties for nondisclosure reach $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation under 40 CFR § 19.4). Criminal penalties for knowing violations can include fines up to $10,000 and imprisonment.

Beyond fines, the buyer can void the contract. Section 1018 of Title X grants the buyer the right to rescind if the disclosure was not properly made. Oregon courts have upheld rescission claims where agents failed to provide the pamphlet or mischaracterized the 10-day inspection period.

Private lawsuits add another layer. Under 42 U.S.C. § 4852d(b)(3), a buyer who suffers damages from nondisclosure may recover treble damages—three times their actual loss—plus reasonable attorney fees and court costs. OREA can also pursue disciplinary action against the agent's license, ranging from a letter of reprimand to suspension or revocation under OAR 863-015-0215.

Agents sometimes assume their errors-and-omissions insurance will absorb these costs. Many E&O policies exclude regulatory fines and intentional nondisclosure, leaving the agent personally liable.

## Common Mistakes Oregon Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosure

Five errors appear repeatedly in OREA complaint files and federal enforcement actions. If you recognize any of these patterns in your own practice, fix them before your next listing appointment.

**First, agents forget to disclose on lease-purchase and lease-option transactions.** The federal rule applies to leases of pre-1978 housing, not just sales. An agent who structures a rent-to-own deal without lead disclosure has violated 24 CFR § 35.80 from day one.

Second, agents deliver the pamphlet but fail to get a signed acknowledgment. Verbal delivery is legally meaningless. The signed disclosure form—the one with the buyer's initials confirming receipt of the pamphlet and the 10-day notice—is the only defensible proof. Without it, you have nothing in a dispute.

Third, agents mark "seller has no knowledge" without actually asking the seller. The disclosure form requires the seller to affirmatively state whether they know of lead-based paint. If the agent pre-checks the "no knowledge" box and hands it to the seller for signature without discussion, the agent has manufactured a false record. When old inspection reports later surface, this becomes evidence of negligence or worse.

Fourth, agents waive the 10-day inspection period on the buyer's behalf. Only the buyer can waive that right. An agent who includes contract language eliminating the inspection window without the buyer's explicit, separate written consent has committed a federal violation.

Fifth, agents fail to retain records for three years. The regulation at 24 CFR § 35.92 requires all parties—including both agents—to keep the signed disclosure for a minimum of three years from the date of sale. Agents who purge files after closing leave themselves without proof of compliance. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in Oregon carry supervisory liability under ORS 696.022. If an agent in your office botches a lead disclosure, OREA can and will examine whether you had adequate systems in place to prevent the error. Brokers who treat lead compliance as "the agent's problem" are accepting unnecessary risk.

| Audit Point | Frequency | What to Check |
|---|---|---|
| Signed OREF Form 035 in file | Every pre-1978 transaction | Buyer, seller, and both agent signatures present |
| EPA pamphlet delivery documented | Every pre-1978 transaction | Date of delivery matches or precedes contract ratification |
| 10-day inspection clause intact | Every pre-1978 transaction | Waiver is buyer-initiated and separately signed |

## Oregon Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026: Edge Cases Worth Knowing

Foreclosure sales present a recurring gray area. HUD has clarified that lender-owned properties (REOs) are not exempt from lead disclosure, but the lender-seller rarely has actual knowledge of lead conditions. The correct approach is to mark "no knowledge" on the disclosure, provide the pamphlet, and offer the 10-day inspection—not to skip the form entirely.

Estate sales create similar confusion. An executor or personal representative who never lived in the property may legitimately lack knowledge, but the disclosure still must be completed and delivered. Agents who skip the form because "the family doesn't know anything about the house" have violated the law.

Short sales and auctions also trigger disclosure. Oregon agents handling auction-format sales should confirm that the auctioneer or platform includes the lead disclosure in pre-bid materials, because the agent of record remains liable if it was omitted.
