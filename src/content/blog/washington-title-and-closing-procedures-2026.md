## Understanding Washington Title & Closing Procedures 2026

Washington operates as an escrow state, meaning a neutral third-party escrow agent handles the mechanics of closing rather than attorneys sitting across a table from each other. If you hold an active license in this state, your fluency with **Washington title & closing procedures 2026** directly affects whether your transactions close on time, whether your clients retain their legal protections, and whether your brokerage avoids regulatory complaints. The closing process here is governed by a patchwork of statutes, administrative codes, and standardized forms that updated heading into this year.

The Washington State Department of Licensing (DOL) regulates agents under RCW 18.85, while the Office of the Insurance Commissioner (OIC) oversees title insurance companies under RCW 48.29. These two regulatory bodies rarely coordinate enforcement, which means an agent can inadvertently violate one while satisfying the other. Your job is to understand where those lines sit.

## The Role of Escrow and Title Companies Under RCW 48.29

Washington's escrow closing model places the escrow officer—not the agents—in control of funds, documents, and recording. Under [RCW 48.29](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=48.29), title insurance companies issue preliminary commitments, underwrite policies, and manage the recording of deeds and deeds of trust with the county auditor. Agents do not disburse funds or record documents, but they are responsible for ensuring the correct forms reach escrow on time.

**The preliminary title commitment is the single most important document agents should review before advising clients on any contingency removal.** It reveals liens, easements, CC&Rs, and exceptions that could derail a transaction. Washington title commitments follow ALTA standards but include state-specific Schedule B exceptions that reflect local recording customs.

Escrow agents in Washington must be licensed under RCW 18.44 or operate through a title insurance company exempt under that chapter. Agents who refer clients to unlicensed escrow operations face potential violation of both RCW 18.44 and RESPA Section 8, which prohibits kickbacks for settlement service referrals.

## Key Forms and Their Role in Washington Closings

The Northwest Multiple Listing Service (NWMLS) publishes the standardized forms that dominate residential transactions across most of the state. Knowing the correct form numbers is not optional—it is the baseline expectation for licensed practice.

| Form Number | Form Name | Purpose in Closing |
|---|---|---|
| Form 21 | Residential Purchase & Sale Agreement | Governs timelines, earnest money, and contingency deadlines that feed into escrow instructions |
| Form 22T | Title Contingency Addendum | Gives buyers the right to object to title exceptions within a stated timeframe |
| Form 22E | Optional Clauses Addendum | Contains provisions for closing date extensions and possession timing |
| Form 35 | Seller Disclosure Statement | Required under RCW 64.06; must be delivered before or at mutual acceptance |
| Form 22K | Closing Agent/Escrow Company Addendum | Identifies the escrow holder and allocation of closing costs |

Form 22T deserves particular attention. It establishes the buyer's window—typically five days from receipt of the preliminary commitment—to disapprove title exceptions. If the buyer's agent misses this deadline, the buyer is deemed to have accepted all disclosed exceptions, including potentially problematic easements or encroachments. This is a hard deadline, not a soft guideline, and [missing it is one of the most common ways deals fall apart](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

## Closing Cost Allocation and Recording Fees

Washington's real estate excise tax (REET) under RCW 82.45 is the largest closing cost unique to this state. As of 2026, REET is graduated based on sale price, with rates ranging from 1.1% on the first $525,000 to 3.0% on amounts exceeding $3,025,000. Local jurisdictions may add an additional 0.25% to 0.50% on top of state rates.

| Cost Item | Typically Paid By | Governing Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Real Estate Excise Tax (REET) | Seller | RCW 82.45 |
| Title Insurance (Owner's Policy) | Seller (negotiable) | OIC / RCW 48.29 |
| Title Insurance (Lender's Policy) | Buyer | OIC / RCW 48.29 |
| Escrow Fee | Split 50/50 (negotiable) | Escrow agreement |
| Recording Fees (Deed) | Buyer | County Auditor per RCW 36.18 |
| Recording Fees (Deed of Trust) | Buyer | County Auditor per RCW 36.18 |

**Agents who tell clients REET is "always" paid by the seller are technically stating custom, not law.** RCW 82.45.080 makes the seller liable for the tax, but the parties can contractually agree to shift this burden. Misrepresenting a negotiable item as fixed exposes agents to misrepresentation claims under WAC 308-124C-137.

Recording fees in Washington increased under the 2023 surcharge for affordable housing programs, and agents should verify current county-specific rates rather than relying on outdated fee schedules. King County, for example, charges differently than Spokane County for the same document types.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make at Closing

Seasoned agents still trip on these errors regularly, and each one carries real consequences ranging from deal cancellation to DOL disciplinary action.

**First: failing to deliver the Form 17 (Seller Disclosure Statement) within the statutory timeframe.** RCW 64.06.020 requires delivery no later than five business days after mutual acceptance if not provided earlier. Agents who treat this as a "we'll get to it" task risk giving the buyer a perpetual right to rescind until the disclosure is actually delivered. The rescission right under RCW 64.06.030 does not expire until three business days after proper delivery.

Second: releasing earnest money without mutual written authorization. Under [Washington's earnest money rules](/blog/washington-earnest-money-rules-2026), the holder cannot disburse disputed funds without either a signed release from both parties, a court order, or interpleader action. Agents who pressure escrow companies to release funds based on one party's verbal instruction expose themselves and their brokerage to liability under RCW 18.85.361.

Third: failing to verify wire instructions independently. Wire fraud targeting Washington closings has increased every year since 2020, and agents who forward wire instructions from email without voice-verifying with the escrow officer risk facilitating fraud. The DOL has made clear in enforcement guidance that agents bear a duty of care when transmitting settlement-related financial information. If you want a deeper dive, review our breakdown of the [wire instructions verification process](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process).

Fourth: not reviewing the closing disclosure against the purchase and sale agreement. Agents are not signatories to the CD, but they owe their clients the duty to flag discrepancies—wrong proration dates, missing credits, or incorrect excise tax calculations—before signing day. Failing to catch a $3,000 error that your client later discovers erodes trust and invites complaints.

Fifth: assuming the closing date in Form 21 is self-executing. If the lender is not ready, the closing date passes, and neither party has signed a Form 22E extension, the transaction exists in legal limbo. The non-delaying party may have grounds to issue a demand for performance or terminate. Agents must proactively manage extension paperwork rather than assuming everyone will "work it out."

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carry personal liability under RCW 18.85.275 for the conduct of their affiliated licensees. WAC 308-124C-125 requires brokers to maintain a system of oversight that includes reviewing transaction files for completeness and compliance. This is not a suggestion—it is an auditable standard that DOL investigators examine during brokerage reviews.

Brokers should audit for timely delivery of the Form 17, proper handling of earnest money receipts, and confirmation that Form 22T deadlines were tracked and communicated to clients. A broker who discovers that agents routinely miss title contingency deadlines without client notification faces potential disciplinary action for inadequate supervision, not just the individual agent.

**File review should happen during the transaction, not after recording.** Post-closing audits catch compliance failures too late to fix them. The WAC requires that brokers maintain records for three years, but the real value of oversight is preventing problems before they generate complaints. Consider whether your current review system flags missed [contingency deadlines](/blog/washington-buyer-contingency-deadlines-2026) in real time or only during quarterly file audits.

Brokers should also verify that agents are not making undisclosed referral arrangements with title companies. RESPA Section 8 violations carry penalties up to $10,000 per occurrence, and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has enforcement authority independent of state regulators. A brokerage policy manual that explicitly prohibits undisclosed affiliated business arrangements—and documents annual agent training on the topic—creates a defensible record.
