## Pennsylvania Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026: The Statutory Framework

The Pennsylvania buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 mark a sharper compliance environment than anything agents faced even two years ago. Between the NAR settlement practice changes that took effect in August 2024 and Pennsylvania-specific statutory mandates under the Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. Section 455.101 et seq., there is no room for agents who wing it. The Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission (PREC) has made clear through enforcement bulletins that written buyer representation agreements are not optional courtesies—they are legal prerequisites to providing brokerage services to a buyer.

RELRA Section 455.606a requires that the nature and scope of the agency relationship be reduced to writing. PREC's regulations under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.331 further specify that licensees must disclose their agency role at the initial interview and obtain the consumer's written acknowledgment. These provisions, combined with the [NAR settlement's buyer agreement mandate](https://www.nar.realtor/the-facts/nar-settlement-faqs), create a dual layer of obligation that Pennsylvania agents must satisfy before accompanying a buyer to a single showing.

## What the Agreement Must Contain

**Pennsylvania law does not leave the contents of a buyer agency agreement to guesswork.** Under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.332 and PREC's published guidance, a valid agreement must include specific elements or it risks being deemed unenforceable—or worse, evidence of a RELRA violation.

The agreement must set forth: the duration of the relationship with a definite expiration date, the geographic area or property types covered, the duties and obligations of both the licensee and the buyer, and the compensation terms. Following the NAR settlement practice changes, the compensation disclosure must be specific and conspicuous—no open-ended language referencing "whatever the seller offers" or "cooperative compensation" without a stated ceiling or alternative.

The Pennsylvania Association of Realtors (PAR) Form BAA is the most widely used template and has been revised multiple times since 2024 to address settlement compliance. Agents using brokerage-drafted alternatives must ensure those forms contain every element required by 49 Pa. Code Chapter 35 and that the compensation language complies with MLS-related practice changes.

| Scope of services | Paragraph 3 | RELRA §455.606a |

| Duties of licensee | Paragraph 4 | 49 Pa. Code §35.331 |
| Buyer's obligations | Paragraph 6 | RELRA §455.606a |

## Timing: When the Agreement Must Be Signed

There is a persistent myth that the agreement can be executed "at some point before the offer." That interpretation died in August 2024. PREC's enforcement position, aligned with the NAR settlement, is that the written buyer agency agreement must be in place before the licensee provides any substantive brokerage services to the buyer. Substantive services include touring properties, preparing CMAs for specific addresses, and negotiating on the buyer's behalf.

Open house attendance remains a gray area. If an agent staffing an open house has a brief conversation with an unrepresented attendee, that single interaction generally does not trigger the agreement requirement. However, the moment the agent offers to show the attendee another property or provide market analysis, the threshold has been crossed. PREC has not issued a formal advisory opinion on this exact scenario for 2026, so the safest course is to execute the agreement at or before the first scheduled activity with the buyer.

Agents who [track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) across various stages should build the buyer agreement execution step into their pipeline as the very first gate—before property searches are shared, before access is provided, before any showing occurs.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make

**Failing to comply usually isn't willful—it stems from sloppy habits dressed up as efficiency.** Here are the five errors PREC disciplinary records and brokerage audits surface most often.

First, agents use an expired form version. PAR updated Form BAA in late 2024 and again in early 2025. Running off copies of the 2023 version or using a PDF that predates the compensation-disclosure revisions means the agreement may lack required language. Always pull the current version from PAR's forms library or your brokerage's approved document management system.

Second, agents leave the compensation field blank or write "TBD." The settlement rules demand a specific, disclosed amount or rate before the buyer tours a property. Ambiguous compensation terms violate both NAR practice requirements and PREC's expectation of clear written agreements. If the amount is genuinely negotiable depending on the property, the agreement must still state the maximum the buyer could owe—not a placeholder.

Third, agents fail to provide a copy of the signed agreement to the buyer at the time of execution. Under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.336, every consumer must receive copies of documents they sign at the time of signing. Handing over the agreement three weeks later at the settlement table does not satisfy this requirement.

Fourth, agents allow the agreement to expire without renewal and continue providing services. An expired agreement means the agent is operating without written authorization—a RELRA violation and a potential bar to collecting compensation. Calendar the expiration date and initiate renewal discussions at least two weeks in advance.

Fifth, agents conflate the Consumer Notice (PREC's mandatory initial interview disclosure) with the buyer agency agreement itself. These are separate documents serving different functions. The Consumer Notice, required by 49 Pa. Code Section 35.336, explains agency options. The buyer agency agreement formalizes the specific relationship. Both must be completed, but neither substitutes for the other.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

PREC has authority under RELRA Section 455.604 to impose civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation, suspend or revoke licenses, and require additional education. In practice, first-time violations involving missing or deficient buyer agency agreements often result in formal citations, fines in the $1,000 to $5,000 range, and mandatory continuing education hours specifically focused on agency law.

Beyond regulatory penalties, the absence of a valid buyer agency agreement exposes the agent to private litigation. A buyer who never signed an agreement can argue they were never the agent's client, potentially disclaiming any obligation to pay a commission. Pennsylvania courts have historically required clear written proof of a brokerage relationship before enforcing commission claims—verbal agreements are not sufficient under RELRA's writing requirements.

|---|---|---|

| License suspension | Repeated violations or fraud | High |

| Transaction rescission risk | Buyer challenges agent's authority to bind | Moderate |

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear direct supervisory liability under RELRA Section 455.604(a)(13) for the acts of their licensees. If an agent under your supervision fails to obtain a proper buyer agency agreement, PREC can—and does—cite the broker alongside the agent. Supervisory negligence is not a defense; it is the violation itself.

**Every brokerage operating in Pennsylvania should have a documented compliance audit process specifically targeting buyer agency agreements.** At minimum, brokers should verify three things within 48 hours of any new buyer engagement: that a current-version agreement has been executed, that compensation terms are specific and not blank, and that the buyer received a signed copy.

Monthly file audits should confirm that no active buyer representation exists under an expired agreement. Transaction management systems can automate expiration alerts, but the broker or their designee must still visually confirm the documents. Delegation to a transaction coordinator is acceptable, but the broker remains liable for the outcome. Understanding [what a transaction coordinator does](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) helps brokers decide how much compliance verification to assign versus retain.

Brokers should also maintain a log of Consumer Notice delivery separate from buyer agency agreements. PREC examiners during audits will look for both, and presenting one without the other raises immediate red flags. Training sessions—held at least quarterly—should walk agents through current form versions, common errors, and recent PREC enforcement actions.
