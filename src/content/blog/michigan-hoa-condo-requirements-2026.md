## Understanding Michigan HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026

Michigan's statutory framework for condominium and HOA disclosures sits primarily in two places: the Michigan Condominium Act (MCL 559.101 et seq.) and the Nonprofit Corporation Act provisions that govern many homeowner associations. If you are listing or selling property governed by either statute, **Michigan hoa & condo disclosure rules 2026** impose specific document delivery timelines, content minimums, and rescission rights that can unwind a deal days before closing.

The Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) oversees real estate licensing, but it does not issue a standardized HOA disclosure form the way some states do. That gap puts the compliance burden squarely on agents and their brokerages. Agents who treat HOA paperwork as an afterthought expose themselves to rescission claims, license complaints, and brokerage E&O hits.

This post breaks down the statutes that apply, the forms you should be tracking, the penalties for non-compliance, and the specific errors that trip up experienced agents year after year.

## The Statutory Framework: MCL 559.184a and Beyond

**Michigan's Condominium Act, specifically MCL 559.184a, is the single most important statute for resale condo transactions.** It requires delivery of a resale disclosure package to the buyer before the purchase agreement becomes binding. The package must contain the declaration, bylaws, rules, current financial statements, the association's budget, any pending special assessments, and insurance coverage details.

For non-condo HOA communities—think planned unit developments governed by deed restrictions and a nonprofit corporate entity—the Condominium Act does not technically apply. Instead, disclosure obligations fall under general seller disclosure law (MCL 565.951–565.966) and the terms negotiated in the purchase agreement. The Michigan Association of Realtors (MAR) addresses this gap with its Homeowners Association Addendum (Form 3125), which requires sellers to disclose HOA fees, transfer fees, and pending litigation.

The distinction matters because agents frequently assume that all "association-governed" properties follow the same statutory path. They do not. Misidentifying the governing structure can mean using the wrong form or missing a required document entirely.

## Required Forms and Documents Agents Must Track

Michigan does not mandate a single state-issued HOA disclosure form, but standard practice across the state relies on several MAR forms and association-generated documents. Here is a comparison of the key documents involved in condo versus non-condo HOA transactions:

| Document | Condo (MCL 559) | Non-Condo HOA | Who Produces It |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Resale Certificate / Disclosure Package | Required by statute | Not statutorily required; contractual | Association or management company |
| MAR Seller Disclosure Statement (Form 2400) | Required | Required | Seller |
| MAR Homeowners Association Addendum (Form 3125) | Recommended | Recommended | Listing agent / Seller |
| Association Bylaws and Rules | Must be included in package | Typically requested by buyer | Association |
| Current Budget and Financial Statements | Must be included | Best practice; not mandated | Association |

Form 2400 is the baseline seller disclosure for all Michigan residential transactions. It includes questions about association membership, monthly dues, and special assessments. Form 3125 goes deeper, covering transfer fees, rental restrictions, pending lawsuits, and reserve fund balances. Neither form replaces the full resale disclosure package required under MCL 559.184a for condominiums—they supplement it.

Agents should also track the [Michigan Condominium Act's full text](https://www.legislature.mi.gov/Laws/MCL?objectName=MCL-Act-59-of-1978) for reference when questions arise about what the association must include in its packet.

## Timelines, Rescission Rights, and Delivery Methods

**The nine-day rescission window under MCL 559.184a is non-negotiable and begins running only upon actual delivery of a complete package.** If any required document is missing—say the insurance summary or the most recent budget—the clock does not start. Buyers can walk away, get their deposit back, and your commission disappears.

Delivery method matters too. The statute contemplates personal delivery or mailing. Electronic delivery is permissible if the buyer has affirmatively consented to receive documents electronically, consistent with Michigan's Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (MCL 450.831 et seq.). A shared Google Drive link without explicit written consent to electronic delivery will not satisfy the statute.

For non-condo HOA properties, there is no statutory rescission window. However, Form 3125 and most purchase agreements include a due-diligence period during which buyers may review association documents and cancel if dissatisfied. Agents representing buyers should negotiate an explicit HOA document review period of at least seven to ten days, particularly in communities with complex covenants or litigation history.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Non-compliance carries three categories of risk: deal cancellation, civil liability, and regulatory discipline. Each one can surface independently or simultaneously, making disclosure failures uniquely dangerous compared to other transaction missteps.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate threat. A buyer who never received a complete condo disclosure package retains rescission rights indefinitely—not just nine days from some arbitrary point. Courts have upheld rescissions weeks after closing when the package was never properly delivered. The listing agent's brokerage ends up returning commissions and potentially covering the seller's damages.

Civil liability extends to negligence claims under Michigan common law. If an agent represented that "all HOA documents have been provided" without verifying completeness, that misrepresentation can support a fraud or negligent misrepresentation claim. Michigan courts apply the economic loss doctrine narrowly in real estate, meaning buyers can pursue tort claims alongside breach of contract. If you have ever wondered about the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing), an HOA disclosure failure is one of the fastest paths there.

LARA can also initiate disciplinary proceedings against a licensee who demonstrates a pattern of negligence in document handling. Sanctions range from formal reprimands to license suspension under MCL 339.2512.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make

Five errors account for the majority of HOA and condo disclosure failures in Michigan transactions. Each one is preventable with proper systems in place.

**First, agents confuse condo and HOA governance structures.** A development may look and feel like a condominium but actually be organized as a site condo with a nonprofit HOA. The master deed and recorded plat—not the marketing materials—determine which statutory regime applies. Pulling the wrong disclosure package creates a compliance gap that neither party notices until a dispute arises.

Second, agents rely on the seller to order the resale certificate without following up. Sellers frequently contact their association, receive a partial response, and assume the obligation is fulfilled. The listing agent has an independent duty to verify that the buyer received a complete package. Passivity here is not a defense.

Third, agents fail to document the delivery date. Without a signed receipt or time-stamped electronic acknowledgment, proving that the nine-day clock started on a particular date becomes a swearing contest. Courts resolve ambiguity in favor of the buyer.

Fourth, agents neglect to check for pending special assessments that were approved after the resale certificate was generated. If the association votes on a new assessment between certificate issuance and closing, the buyer has a legitimate claim that the disclosure was materially incomplete. Listing agents should request an updated certificate or written confirmation from the association within five days of closing.

Fifth, agents treat the MAR Homeowners Association Addendum as optional. Form 3125 is not mandated by statute, but omitting it leaves discoverable gaps that Form 2400 does not cover—particularly around rental caps, pet restrictions, and litigation reserves. When a post-closing dispute emerges over a restriction the buyer did not know about, the absence of Form 3125 becomes exhibit A in the complaint. Tracking deadlines like these across multiple files is exactly [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) without reliable systems.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under MCL 339.2512(1)(m) for failing to adequately oversee agent compliance. That means a disclosure failure by one agent can trigger a LARA investigation of the entire brokerage's practices. The following audit points should appear in every Michigan brokerage's compliance review.

| Audit Item | Frequency | What to Verify |
|------------|-----------|----------------|
| Resale certificate in file | Every condo listing | Complete package with all statutory components present |
| Delivery receipt signed | Every condo transaction | Date-stamped buyer acknowledgment |
| Form 3125 completed | Every association-governed listing | All fields answered; no blanks left as "N/A" without explanation |
| Nine-day rescission tracked | Every condo transaction | Calendar entry confirming rescission window has closed before closing |
| Electronic delivery consent | When applicable | Signed UETA consent form in file |

Brokers should also conduct quarterly spot audits of closed transaction files. A single missing delivery receipt in a random file review signals a systemic training gap. Addressing it proactively—before a buyer's attorney does—is the difference between an internal correction and an E&O claim.

The [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) concept applies here with particular force. When a listing transfers from one agent to another within a brokerage, or when a TC takes over mid-transaction, the HOA disclosure status must be explicitly confirmed during handoff. Assumptions during transitions are where files fall apart.
