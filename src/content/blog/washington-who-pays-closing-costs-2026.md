## Washington Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026: The Statutory and Contractual Framework

The question of Washington who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is not a matter of opinion or local custom alone. It is a hybrid of statutory obligation, contractual agreement, and market convention that shifts with each transaction. Agents who treat the cost split as a boilerplate checkbox expose themselves and their brokers to material liability.

Washington's closing cost framework rests on a handful of statutes — primarily RCW 82.45 (Real Estate Excise Tax), RCW 48.29 (title insurance), and RCW 64.06 (seller disclosures that trigger cost obligations). The rest is negotiated through NWMLS forms, most commonly Form 21 (Residential Purchase and Sale Agreement) and Form 22D (Optional Clauses Addendum). Understanding which costs are fixed by law and which are open to negotiation is the difference between a clean close and a complaint filed with the [Washington Department of Licensing](https://www.dol.wa.gov/business/realestate/).

## Costs the Seller Pays by Statute

**Washington's Real Estate Excise Tax is the single largest seller-side closing cost, and it is imposed by law rather than contract.** Under RCW 82.45.010, the seller is the taxpayer responsible for REET at the point of sale. The 2026 graduated rate structure applies tiers ranging from 1.1% on the first $525,000 of selling price up to 3.0% on amounts exceeding $3,025,000.

The statute allows the parties to contractually shift the economic burden of REET to the buyer, but the Department of Revenue still considers the seller the legal taxpayer. If the buyer defaults on a contractual promise to pay REET, the seller remains on the hook. Agents must explain this distinction clearly; a buyer's agreement to "pay seller's REET" does not release the seller from statutory liability.

Beyond REET, sellers customarily pay for the owner's title insurance policy in most Western Washington counties. This is convention, not statute, but it is baked into NWMLS Form 21 paragraph 12(a), which defaults the owner's policy to the seller unless modified by Form 22D.

## Costs the Buyer Typically Bears

Buyers in Washington are generally responsible for lender-related fees, the lender's title insurance policy, appraisal costs, credit report fees, prepaid taxes and insurance, and recording fees for the deed of trust. Recording fees are set by the county auditor under RCW 36.18.010 and vary by county — King County charges differently from Spokane County, and agents should verify current fee schedules rather than estimating from memory.

**The buyer also pays for any inspection costs, including the structural pest inspection (Form 35P), unless the purchase agreement states otherwise.** Escrow fees are typically split 50/50 per NWMLS Form 21 paragraph 12(d), but this is another negotiable term.

## Negotiation Points That Shift the Default

Every item marked "contractual" in the table above is subject to renegotiation through Form 22D (Optional Clauses Addendum) or Form 22T (Seller Financing Addendum). Agents representing sellers in competitive markets may push some traditionally seller-paid costs onto buyers. Conversely, in a buyer's market, sellers may agree to pay buyer closing costs up to a fixed dollar amount or percentage.

Form 22D, Paragraph E specifically addresses the allocation of closing costs when the parties want to deviate from Form 21 defaults. Agents must use clear, unambiguous language. Vague terms like "seller contributes to closing costs" without a dollar cap or specific line items will create disputes at the settlement table — and those disputes land on the agent who drafted the clause.

The interaction between closing cost credits and [buyer agency agreements](/blog/washington-buyer-agency-agreement-requirements-2026) is another area requiring precision. If a buyer's agent negotiated a seller concession intended to offset the buyer's agent compensation, that must be separately documented and cannot conflict with the compensation terms in the buyer agency agreement.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make

**Mistake one: Assuming REET responsibility transfers when the buyer agrees to pay it.** As discussed, the seller remains the statutory taxpayer. Agents who tell sellers "you won't owe REET because the buyer agreed to pay it" are providing incorrect legal guidance. If the deal falls apart after recording, the Department of Revenue pursues the seller.

Mistake two: Failing to update the cost allocation when switching from Form 21 to a commercial form. NWMLS Form 25 (Commercial and Investment Real Estate Purchase and Sale Agreement) has different default allocations than Form 21. Agents who move between residential and commercial transactions without re-reading the cost paragraphs create mismatches that blow up at closing.

Mistake three: Quoting closing cost estimates without a net sheet disclaimer. Under WAC 308-124C-125, agents cannot provide "settlement statements" — that is the escrow officer's role. Agents who present detailed dollar-figure closing cost breakdowns without clearly labeling them as estimates risk practicing outside their license scope. The liability here is both regulatory (DOL discipline) and civil (negligent misrepresentation claims).

Mistake four: Ignoring the impact of the [earnest money deposit](/blog/washington-earnest-money-rules-2026) on final cost allocation. Earnest money credited at closing reduces the buyer's cash-to-close, but agents sometimes forget to reconcile this with seller concessions, causing double-credits that escrow must unwind under time pressure.

Mistake five: Overlooking the seller disclosure delivery cost. Under RCW 64.06, the seller must provide the [Form 17 Seller Disclosure Statement](/blog/washington-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026), and any costs associated with inspections referenced in that disclosure (such as a sewer scope the seller ordered) must be allocated clearly. Agents who leave this ambiguous create post-inspection disputes about who pays for remediation-related re-inspections.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carry supervisory liability under RCW 18.85.275. When an agent under their supervision misallocates closing costs or provides negligent estimates, the broker's license is at risk alongside the agent's. Here is what managing brokers should be reviewing on every file.

First, brokers should verify that Form 22D is attached whenever the parties deviate from Form 21 default cost allocations. A verbal agreement to split escrow fees differently, memorialized only in an email, is unenforceable and creates E&O exposure. Second, brokers must confirm that any seller concession toward buyer closing costs does not exceed lender limits — conventional loans cap seller contributions at 3% to 9% depending on LTV, and exceeding those caps kills financing.

Third, brokers should audit net sheets prepared by their agents to confirm they carry disclaimer language stating the figures are estimates only and that the official settlement statement will be prepared by escrow. Fourth, brokers must ensure that the REET affidavit and any supplemental statements regarding REET responsibility are completed accurately before recording. A misstatement on the REET affidavit is a misdemeanor under RCW 82.45.090.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

The Washington Department of Licensing can impose sanctions under RCW 18.85.361 for violations including misrepresentation, failure to disclose material facts, and conduct demonstrating incompetence. Penalties range from a letter of reprimand to a $5,000 fine per violation, license suspension, or revocation.

Civil liability adds another layer. If a buyer or seller suffers financial harm because an agent misrepresented who pays a particular cost — and that misrepresentation induced the party to enter the contract — the agent faces a negligent misrepresentation claim. Washington courts have held that real estate agents owe a duty of reasonable care in communicating transaction costs (see *Alejandre v. Bull*, 159 Wn.2d 674). The measure of damages is typically the difference between what the party expected to pay and what they actually paid, plus consequential damages.

Deal cancellation is also on the table. If a material cost allocation error is discovered after mutual acceptance but before closing, the aggrieved party may argue mutual mistake and seek rescission. This wastes everyone's time, kills the commission, and generates exactly the kind of file that ends up on a broker's desk with a demand letter attached.
