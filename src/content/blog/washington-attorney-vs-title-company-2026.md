## Washington Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Definitive Answer

The question of Washington attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 confuses agents more than it should. Washington is an escrow-closing state, meaning the vast majority of residential real estate transactions close through a title or escrow company rather than requiring attorney presence. Yet the legal boundaries around who may perform what function at closing carry real consequences for agents who misunderstand them.

Unlike states such as New York, Georgia, or the Carolinas, Washington has no statutory mandate requiring attorney involvement in residential closings. The Revised Code of Washington (RCW) distributes closing authority across licensed escrow agents and title insurance companies, with attorneys as an optional (though sometimes advisable) participant. The distinction matters for your compliance, your liability, and your clients' protection.

## Who Is Legally Authorized to Close a Transaction in Washington

**Washington law authorizes three categories of professionals to handle settlement and closing functions.** These are governed by separate statutes, each with distinct licensing and regulatory oversight.

First, licensed escrow agents operating under RCW 18.44 may receive, hold, and disburse funds and documents in connection with real estate closings. The Washington State Department of Licensing (DOL) oversees escrow agent licensing, requiring a surety bond and compliance with trust account rules.

Second, title insurance companies and their authorized closers operate under RCW 48.29 (Title Insurance Act), regulated by the Washington State Office of the Insurance Commissioner (OIC). Title companies routinely combine title search, insurance, and closing services into a single transaction workflow. Third, licensed attorneys may perform closing functions as part of their legal practice, though they rarely serve as the primary closing agent in Washington residential deals.

|------|--------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Escrow Agent | RCW 18.44 | WA Dept. of Licensing | Not required, but commonly used |
| Title Insurance Company | RCW 48.29 | Office of Insurance Commissioner | Not required, but commonly used |
| Attorney | WSBA Rules / GR 24 | WA Supreme Court / WSBA | Not required |
| Real Estate Agent | RCW 18.85 | WA Dept. of Licensing | Not required to attend |

The practical reality is that nearly all Washington residential closings go through a title/escrow company. Agents should understand this is a market convention backed by statute, not merely tradition.

## What Agents Cannot Do at Closing — and Why It Matters

**Real estate licensees in Washington hold no statutory authority to conduct closings, prepare legal documents, or disburse settlement funds.** The line between filling in approved transaction forms and practicing law is drawn by Washington General Rule 24 (GR 24), which defines the unauthorized practice of law.

Under GR 24, real estate agents may fill in blanks on approved standardized forms — such as NWMLS Form 21 (Residential Purchase and Sale Agreement) or Form 22A (Financing Addendum) — but they cannot draft deeds, promissory notes, or settlement statements. Agents who prepare closing documents, even informally for a client "to save money," risk disciplinary action from the DOL and potential civil liability for unauthorized practice.

The Washington State Bar Association has issued multiple advisory opinions reinforcing that document preparation beyond approved forms crosses into legal practice. An agent who drafts a quit claim deed for a seller, for example, has committed an act that could trigger a [complaint to the DOL](https://www.dol.wa.gov/business/realestate/), malpractice exposure, and deal cancellation if the document proves defective.

## When Agents Should Recommend Attorney Involvement

Washington may not require attorneys at closing, but certain transaction types practically demand one. Agents who fail to recommend counsel when circumstances warrant it create advisory liability — meaning a court could later find the agent breached their duty under [Washington's agency disclosure requirements](/blog/washington-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) by not suggesting the client seek legal advice.

Situations warranting attorney referral include transactions involving boundary disputes, complex title defects revealed in the preliminary commitment, 1031 exchanges requiring qualified intermediary coordination, commercial-to-residential conversions, and estate sales with unresolved probate issues. NWMLS Form 22K (Attorney Approval Contingency) exists specifically so buyers or sellers can make the deal contingent on attorney review within a stated period.

Agents do not practice law by recommending a client consult an attorney. In fact, WAC 308-124C-135 (the real estate licensing administrative code) explicitly contemplates agents advising clients to seek independent counsel for matters beyond the agent's expertise.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make Around Closing Authority

The gap between what agents think they can do and what Washington law permits creates five recurring errors that invite discipline, litigation, or deal failure.

**First, agents sometimes instruct clients to wire earnest money directly to the listing agent's brokerage trust account rather than the designated escrow holder.** While RCW 18.85.310 permits brokerages to hold earnest money in pooled trust accounts, the purchase and sale agreement (typically NWMLS Form 21, Section 2) specifies who holds the funds. Deviating from the contract's designated holder without written amendment creates a breach and potential [earnest money disputes](/blog/washington-earnest-money-rules-2026).

Second, agents prepare or modify legal instruments they have no authority to draft. This includes handwritten addenda that attempt to create easements, modify legal descriptions, or alter title vesting. These documents are legally deficient and can delay or kill closings when the title company's legal review flags them.

Third, agents select closing agents based solely on marketing relationships rather than confirming proper licensing. An escrow company operating without a current surety bond under RCW 18.44.031 exposes all parties to uninsured loss if funds are mishandled. Agents should verify active licensing through the DOL's online lookup tool before recommending any closing entity.

Fourth, agents attend closing and inadvertently provide interpretive guidance on settlement statement line items, tax prorations, or document language. This crosses from facilitation into legal advice, violating GR 24 boundaries. The safe practice is to direct all document-interpretation questions to the closing officer or the client's attorney.

Fifth, agents fail to confirm their client has received the Closing Disclosure (TRID) at least three business days before closing on financed transactions. While this is technically the lender's obligation under federal Regulation Z (12 CFR 1026.19), agents who calendar and verify delivery protect against last-minute delays and demonstrate the diligence expected under their [buyer agency agreement obligations](/blog/washington-buyer-agency-agreement-requirements-2026).

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers and managing brokers carry supervisory responsibility under RCW 18.85.275 for all licensees operating under their firm. When an agent's closing-related conduct violates law, the broker shares exposure.

**Brokers should audit three specific areas on a quarterly basis at minimum.** First, review a random sample of transaction files to confirm that no agent-prepared documents beyond approved NWMLS forms appear in closing packages. Look for hand-drafted addenda, agent-written letters creating legal obligations, or modified deeds.

Second, verify that every closed transaction file contains confirmation of the escrow holder's active license status at the time of closing. A simple screenshot of the DOL license lookup or a printout from the OIC's company search satisfies this requirement and protects the brokerage if the escrow company later faces regulatory action.

Third, confirm that commission disbursement instructions flow exclusively through the closing agent to the brokerage — never directly to individual agents. RCW 18.85.361 prohibits compensation being paid to a licensee by anyone other than their broker. A closing agent who cuts a commission check directly to an agent (bypassing the broker) creates a statutory violation for both the agent and the closing company.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Source Document | Risk if Missing |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| No agent-drafted legal docs in file | Every transaction | Closing package review | UPL liability, DOL complaint |
| Escrow company license verification | Every transaction | DOL/OIC online lookup | Uninsured fund loss |
| Commission paid through brokerage | Every transaction | Settlement statement | RCW 18.85.361 violation |
| Closing Disclosure delivery confirmed | Every financed deal | Lender communication log | TRID delay, client complaint |
| Attorney referral documented when needed | Complex transactions | File notes / Form 22K | Advisory liability exposure |

Brokers who treat these audits as optional are gambling with their firm's license. The DOL's enforcement division has increased random audit activity in 2025-2026, and failing to demonstrate supervisory compliance under WAC 308-124C-125 can result in fines up to $5,000 per violation or license suspension.

## Liability Exposure When Things Go Wrong

When a closing fails because of role confusion — an agent who overstepped, an unlicensed entity handling funds, or a missing required disclosure — the consequences cascade. The buyer or seller may file a complaint with the DOL, initiating an investigation under RCW 18.85.361. Penalties range from a written warning to license revocation, with fines of up to $5,000 per violation.

Civil liability runs parallel to regulatory action. An agent who provided legal advice that proved incorrect (such as assuring a client they did not need title insurance, or interpreting a deed restriction) faces potential malpractice claims. Washington courts have held that agents owe a duty of reasonable care under RCW 18.86.050, and breaching that duty by exceeding one's professional scope strengthens a plaintiff's case considerably.

> "A licensee shall not provide legal advice or prepare legal documents unless they are also a licensed attorney admitted to practice law in this state." — WAC 308-124C-135

Transaction cancellation is often the immediate practical consequence. Title companies will refuse to close if they discover unauthorized documents in the file or irregularities in fund handling. The deal dies, the client loses the property, and the agent loses the commission — plus faces a potential claim for the client's consequential damages.

## How Washington's Approach Differs from Attorney-Mandatory States

Understanding Washington's position on the spectrum helps agents who work with relocating clients or handle referrals from out-of-state. In attorney-mandatory states like South Carolina, Georgia, or Massachusetts, an attorney must supervise or conduct the closing. Washington sits firmly on the opposite end alongside states like California, Oregon, and Arizona, where title/escrow companies handle settlement without attorney requirement.

This distinction affects client expectations. A buyer relocating from New York may expect their attorney to review every document before closing and be physically present at the table. In Washington, that buyer's attorney is welcome but unnecessary — and the closing will typically proceed through the escrow company's standard process regardless of whether counsel attends. Agents should set these expectations early, ideally during the agency disclosure conversation described in [Washington's disclosure framework](/blog/washington-dual-agency-rules-2026).

## Protecting Your License and Your Clients in 2026

The answer to Washington attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 is straightforward: neither an attorney nor a title company is strictly "required," but a licensed escrow agent or title company closer must handle the settlement mechanics. Attorneys are optional participants, and agents hold no closing authority whatsoever. The practical standard is that a title or escrow company conducts the closing, and agents who stay within their lane — filling approved forms, recommending counsel for complex issues, and verifying the closing entity's credentials — avoid the liability traps that ensnare their less careful peers.
