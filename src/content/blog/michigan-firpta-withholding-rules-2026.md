## Why Michigan Agents Cannot Ignore FIRPTA in 2026

The Michigan firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 affect more transactions than most agents realize. Metro Detroit, Ann Arbor, and the Grand Rapids corridor all attract international investors—from Canadian nationals purchasing rental properties across the border to overseas buyers assembling commercial portfolios. When any of those foreign persons sell U.S. real property, the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act kicks in, and the agent sitting at the closing table carries real exposure.

FIRPTA is a federal statute (Internal Revenue Code Sections 1445 through 1445(e)), but its practical enforcement lands squarely on the parties at the transaction level. Michigan has no separate state-level withholding statute that mirrors FIRPTA, which means agents sometimes assume no action is required on their end. That assumption is wrong—and expensive when the IRS comes calling.

## The Federal Framework Applied to Michigan Closings

**Under IRC Section 1445, the buyer (transferee) must withhold 15% of the gross sale price when purchasing U.S. real property from a foreign person.** The withholding obligation does not fall on the seller; it falls on the buyer, and by extension, the closing agent who disburses funds. In Michigan, that closing agent is almost always a title insurance company operating under the authority of the Michigan Department of Insurance and Financial Services (DIFS).

The IRS treats the "withholding agent" as the person who has control over the payment. Title companies in Michigan therefore act as the de facto withholding agents, but the listing agent and buyer's agent both carry disclosure and verification duties. If no one confirms the seller's foreign status—or everyone assumes someone else handled it—the liability cascades to the buyer personally.

Michigan does not impose a separate state income tax withholding on foreign sellers the way states like California (via FTB Form 593) or Maryland do. However, foreign sellers still owe Michigan income tax on gains from Michigan real property under Michigan Compiled Laws 206.51, and the Michigan Department of Treasury may pursue collection independently.

## Key Forms and Filing Deadlines

The forms involved are federal, not state-specific, but every Michigan agent should know them by number.

| Form | Purpose | Filed By | Deadline |
|------|---------|----------|----------|
| IRS Form 8288 | Transmits withheld tax to IRS | Withholding agent (title company) | 20 days after closing |
| IRS Form 8288-A | Statement of withholding on disposition | Withholding agent | 20 days after closing (attached to 8288) |
| IRS Form 8288-B | Application for reduced/zero withholding | Foreign seller | Before closing (90+ day processing) |
| IRS Form W-7 | ITIN application (if seller lacks one) | Foreign seller | Concurrent with 8288-B or tax return |

The 20-day deadline from the date of transfer is absolute. If the title company misses it, penalties accrue. Agents who pressure closing teams to rush without verifying FIRPTA compliance often trigger the exact scenario where forms are filed late or incorrectly.

## Michigan FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026: Rate Structure

The current withholding rate is 15% of the gross amount realized—not 15% of the gain. That distinction matters enormously. A foreign seller disposing of a $600,000 condo in Birmingham, Michigan will see $90,000 withheld at closing regardless of whether they have any actual profit.

Two exceptions reduce or eliminate the withholding amount. First, if the property will be used by the buyer as a personal residence and the purchase price is between $300,001 and $1,000,000, the withholding drops to 10%. Second, if the buyer will use the property as a residence and the price is $300,000 or less, withholding is zero. These thresholds have not changed for 2026.

**Agents frequently confuse "foreign person" with "non-resident alien individual."** FIRPTA applies to foreign corporations, foreign partnerships, foreign trusts, and foreign estates—not just individuals. A Canadian LLC selling a rental duplex in Kalamazoo triggers the same withholding obligation as a German citizen selling a vacation home in Traverse City.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in Michigan FIRPTA-related disputes, and any one of them can blow a closing or create post-closing liability.

**First, agents accept a verbal statement that the seller is a U.S. person without obtaining a written certification.** The proper procedure is to collect a Non-Foreign Affidavit (sometimes called a FIRPTA Affidavit or Certificate of Non-Foreign Status) signed under penalty of perjury. Without that document, the buyer has no safe harbor from withholding liability.

Second, agents confuse residency with citizenship. A green card holder is not a foreign person under FIRPTA, but a Canadian citizen who owns Michigan property and visits seasonally is. The test is whether the individual is a "United States person" as defined in IRC Section 7701(b), which looks at substantial presence or lawful permanent resident status.

Third, listing agents discover the seller's foreign status too late—sometimes days before closing—leaving no time to file Form 8288-B for a reduced withholding certificate. That form takes the IRS an average of 90 days to process, and [missed deadlines like this are where deals collapse](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

Fourth, agents assume FIRPTA does not apply because the seller lived in the property. Occupancy is irrelevant to the seller's foreign status. A foreign national who lived in a Michigan home for five years but never obtained a green card or met the substantial presence test is still a foreign person.

Fifth, the buyer's agent fails to inform the buyer of their personal liability. If withholding is not properly remitted, the IRS can pursue the buyer for the full 15% plus interest and penalties. That is not a theoretical risk—it is codified in [IRC Section 1445(b)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/1445).

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in Michigan carry supervisory responsibility under the Michigan Occupational Code (MCL 339.2512e) for the conduct of their licensees. When a FIRPTA violation occurs, the broker may face regulatory scrutiny from the Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) even though FIRPTA itself is federal law. LARA can investigate whether the agent's failure to identify a withholding obligation constitutes incompetence or negligence in the practice of real estate.

Brokers should enforce three minimum compliance steps across every listing file. First, require a completed Non-Foreign Affidavit or a documented foreign-seller flag in every transaction file before the listing goes active. Second, mandate that listing agents confirm the seller's FIRPTA status in the MLS agent remarks (not public remarks) so the buyer's side knows immediately. Third, confirm the title company has acknowledged the foreign seller status in writing before the file moves to closing.

| Audit Point | Who Checks | When |
|-------------|-----------|------|
| Non-Foreign Affidavit or foreign-seller flag | Listing agent / TC | At listing intake |
| Buyer notification of withholding obligation | Buyer's agent | At offer acceptance |
| Title company acknowledgment of FIRPTA status | Transaction coordinator | 10+ days before closing |
| Form 8288/8288-A filing confirmation | Title company | Within 20 days post-closing |
| Reduced withholding application (if applicable) | Seller's tax advisor | 90+ days before anticipated closing |

Brokers who skip these audits expose the brokerage to errors-and-omissions claims. A blown FIRPTA obligation can cost a buyer tens of thousands of dollars, and the listing brokerage is the most visible target for a negligence claim. Understanding [the true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) should motivate every managing broker to build FIRPTA checks into their standard file review.

## Liability Exposure for the Agent Personally

FIRPTA liability is not abstract. If the withholding agent fails to withhold, the IRS can assess the tax against the buyer—and the buyer can then pursue the agents and closing company for damages. Michigan courts recognize negligence and breach of fiduciary duty claims in exactly this scenario.

The listing agent who knew or should have known the seller was a foreign person but failed to disclose that fact to the title company has breached their duty under Michigan agency law. The buyer's agent who failed to advise their client about the withholding obligation has similarly breached their fiduciary duty of disclosure. Neither agent remits the tax directly, but both can be held liable for the downstream consequences of their silence.

Fines from the IRS for failure to withhold include the full tax amount plus interest at the federal short-term rate plus three percentage points. There is no cap. In a $2 million commercial sale, that is $300,000 in withholding liability alone—before interest.

## Practical Workflow for Michigan Agents

The smartest approach is to treat FIRPTA status the same way you treat [a transaction handoff checklist item](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist)—it gets verified at intake, not at closing. Ask the seller at the listing appointment: "Are you a U.S. citizen or permanent resident?" Document the answer. If the answer is no, flag the file immediately and loop in a tax advisor.

For buyer's agents, the obligation triggers at contract. Once you know the seller is foreign (or suspect it based on entity structure, out-of-country address, or foreign passport), advise your buyer in writing that withholding applies. Put it in the purchase agreement special provisions if necessary to ensure the title company escrows the correct amount.

If the seller wants to reduce or eliminate the withholding, they must file Form 8288-B with the IRS well in advance of closing. The IRS issues a withholding certificate specifying the reduced amount—but only after review. Sellers who wait until the last minute will either face the full 15% withhold or delay closing indefinitely.
