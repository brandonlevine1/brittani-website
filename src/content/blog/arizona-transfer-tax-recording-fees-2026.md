## Arizona's Unique Position: No Transfer Tax, But Plenty of Fees

Arizona stands apart from the majority of states by imposing no state-level real estate transfer tax. That single fact trips up agents who relocate from states like Washington or California, where excise taxes can run into the tens of thousands. But the absence of a transfer tax does not mean Arizona closings are fee-free, and misunderstanding the **Arizona transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026** can stall a recording, delay possession, or expose you to a complaint with the Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE).

The operative statute is A.R.S. Section 11-1134, which explicitly prohibits counties from levying a documentary transfer tax. This prohibition has been in place since 1971 and remains unchanged for 2026. What Arizona does require is the filing of an Affidavit of Property Value (Form DOR 82162) with every deed transfer unless a statutory exemption applies under A.R.S. 11-1134(A)(1)-(9).

Recording fees, by contrast, are set at the county level under A.R.S. 11-475 and vary modestly between Arizona's fifteen counties. Agents who close transactions across county lines—common in the Phoenix-to-Pinal corridor—need to track these differences or risk short-funding at closing.

The $2 Affidavit of Property Value fee is set by the Arizona Department of Revenue under A.R.S. 11-1133(B). These numbers appear modest, but a typical residential closing involves four to seven recorded documents, pushing total recording costs to $150-$300 depending on page counts.

## Forms and Filings Agents Must Know

The Affidavit of Property Value (Arizona Department of Revenue Form 82162) is required for virtually every transfer of real property in Arizona. The county recorder's office will not accept a deed for recording without it unless the transfer qualifies for one of the nine enumerated exemptions in A.R.S. 11-1134(A). Those exemptions include transfers between spouses incident to divorce, transfers to a trust where the grantor retains beneficial interest, and certain governmental transfers.

**Agents must verify that Form 82162 is fully completed before the deed package reaches the recorder.** Incomplete affidavits—particularly missing legal descriptions or assessor parcel numbers—are the most common rejection reason at Maricopa County Recorder's Office. The AAR Residential Resale Real Estate Purchase Contract (updated annually by the Arizona Association of REALTORS) addresses recording fee allocation in Section 3, Lines 87-95, and agents should confirm the allocation matches what appears on the settlement statement.

For agents handling [title and closing procedures in other states](/blog/washington-title-and-closing-procedures-2026), Arizona's system feels streamlined by comparison—but that simplicity breeds complacency.

## Who Pays What: Allocation Customs and Contract Terms

Arizona follows customs that vary by county, and those customs are not law. They are defaults that the AAR contract permits the parties to modify. The prevailing custom in Maricopa, Pima, and Pinal counties allocates costs as follows:

| Fee Type | Customarily Paid By | Contract Section |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Deed recording | Buyer | AAR Section 3 |
| Deed of Trust recording | Buyer | AAR Section 3 |
| Release of existing lien recording | Seller | AAR Section 3 |
| Affidavit of Property Value ($2) | Seller | AAR Section 3 |
| Title insurance premium (owner's policy) | Seller | AAR Section 3 |
| Title insurance premium (lender's policy) | Buyer | AAR Section 3 |

These customs are not codified in statute. They exist because title companies and escrow officers have followed them for decades. An agent who tells a client that "the seller always pays" for a particular fee without referencing the actual contract language is making a representation that could conflict with the negotiated terms. The ADRE takes misrepresentation seriously under A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(3), which lists making a substantial misrepresentation as grounds for disciplinary action.

When disputes arise about [who pays closing costs](/blog/washington-who-pays-closing-costs-2026), the contract controls—not local custom.

## Common Mistakes Arizona Agents Make With Recording Fees

**The following errors appear repeatedly in ADRE complaint files and title company rejection logs.** Each one is preventable with basic diligence.

First, agents routinely forget to account for the Affidavit of Property Value on the estimated closing statement they provide to clients during the contract period. While $2 is trivial, the document itself must be prepared and signed by the transferee or the transferee's agent, and its absence halts recording entirely. The downstream consequences—missed contractual close of escrow deadlines—are not trivial.

Second, agents miscalculate page counts on multi-page deeds. A legal description that spans three pages turns a $30 recording into a $36 recording. Across a deed, deed of trust, and two affidavits, page-count errors compound into $50-$100 discrepancies on the settlement statement. Escrow officers catch these, but the correction requires an updated HUD-1 or ALTA statement and fresh buyer/seller signatures, which costs time.

Third, agents fail to identify exempt transactions that do not require the Affidavit of Property Value. When an exempt transfer is mischaracterized as non-exempt, clients pay $2 unnecessarily—but more critically, the affidavit they file becomes a public record that may later create a chain-of-title issue if the reported value conflicts with the nature of the transfer.

Fourth, agents representing out-of-state buyers sometimes apply their home-state transfer tax assumptions to Arizona transactions. This leads to inflated buyer cost estimates that undermine the agent's credibility when the actual closing statement arrives.

Fifth, agents neglect to confirm that the title company's recording package matches what the contract specifies. If the contract calls for a Special Warranty Deed but the title company prepares a standard Warranty Deed, the recorded document may not match the parties' intent. The [Arizona Legislature's official statutes](https://www.azleg.gov/arsDetail/?title=11) provide the authoritative text for all recording requirements.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Arizona carry supervisory responsibility under A.R.S. 32-2151.01. That statute requires brokers to maintain a system of oversight sufficient to ensure compliance with state law and ADRE rules. For recording fees and transfer documentation, this translates into concrete audit points.

Brokers should verify that every transaction file contains a copy of the completed Affidavit of Property Value (Form 82162) or documentation that an exemption applies. Missing affidavits in the file suggest the agent did not verify the form was included in the recording package—a gap that could surface during an ADRE audit.

**Brokers must also confirm that estimated closing statements prepared by agents accurately reflect recording fees.** The ADRE's enforcement division has cited brokerages where agents consistently provided inaccurate cost estimates, treating it as a pattern of negligent supervision. Under A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(19), a broker can face discipline for failing to exercise reasonable supervision over licensees.

Settlement statement reviews should cross-reference the AAR contract's Section 3 allocations with the title company's final figures. Discrepancies of even $30-$50 suggest either a contract amendment was not documented or the escrow officer overrode the contract terms without authorization.

Brokers handling multi-county operations—say, teams closing in both Maricopa and Pinal counties—should maintain a reference sheet of county-specific recording requirements and confirm their agents consult it before preparing client estimates. While base fees are now standardized, ancillary requirements like e-recording surcharges and courier fees vary.

## Liability Exposure When Agents Get It Wrong

The consequences of mishandling recording documentation in Arizona range from minor delays to formal ADRE complaints. A rejected recording package means the deed is not of public record, which means the buyer does not have constructive notice of ownership. If the transaction is purchase-money financed, the lender's deed of trust is also unrecorded, leaving the lender unsecured.

Under the AAR contract, failure to close escrow by the specified date triggers the [cure period and potential cancellation provisions](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines). If the delay is attributable to an agent's failure to ensure proper documentation—say, a missing affidavit—the agent's errors and omissions carrier may face a claim. Arizona courts have held that agents owe a duty of reasonable care in handling transaction documentation, and a recording rejection caused by negligence can support a breach-of-duty claim.

The ADRE can impose discipline ranging from a letter of concern to license revocation under A.R.S. 32-2153. For recording fee issues specifically, the most likely outcome is a citation for violating A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(3) (misrepresentation) or A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(22) (conduct that constitutes dishonest dealing). Fines for first-time violations typically range from $1,000 to $5,000, but repeat offenders face suspension.
