## Understanding Georgia FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

Every Georgia agent working with an international seller faces a compliance minefield that can torpedo closings and generate personal liability. The Georgia FIRPTA withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 combine federal obligations under IRC Section 1445 with Georgia's own nonresident withholding statute, creating a dual-layer system that trips up even experienced licensees. Getting this wrong does not just delay a deal—it can expose the buyer, the closing attorney, and the listing agent to financial penalties that far exceed any commission earned.

**FIRPTA—the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act—requires buyers to withhold 15% of the gross sales price when purchasing real property from a "foreign person" as defined under federal tax law.** Georgia layers its own 3% withholding on top of that under O.C.G.A. Section 48-7-128. The combined bite can reach 18% of the sales price held back at closing, which makes upfront planning and proper documentation non-negotiable for agents on both sides of the transaction.

## How Federal FIRPTA and Georgia State Withholding Interact

The federal and state withholding requirements operate independently. A seller might qualify for a federal exemption while still being subject to Georgia's nonresident withholding, or vice versa. Agents who conflate the two systems—or assume the closing attorney will "handle it"—create gaps that surface days before closing when the title commitment reveals unresolved tax obligations.

Under [IRC Section 1445](https://www.irs.gov/individuals/international-taxpayers/firpta-withholding), the buyer acts as the withholding agent. The buyer must remit 15% of the gross amount realized to the IRS using Form 8288 (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Certain Dispositions by Foreign Persons) and Form 8288-A (Statement of Withholding on Dispositions by Foreign Persons) within 20 days of the transfer. Failure to remit makes the buyer liable for the tax, plus interest and penalties under IRC Section 1461.

Georgia's separate requirement under O.C.G.A. Section 48-7-128 mandates that the buyer or withholding agent remit 3% of the sales price (or the seller's gain, if an affidavit is provided) to the Georgia Department of Revenue using Form G-2(RP). The seller can also file Form IT-AFF2 to certify Georgia residency and avoid state withholding entirely—but a foreign national cannot truthfully execute that form.

| Requirement | Federal FIRPTA | Georgia Nonresident Withholding |
|---|---|---|
| Statute | IRC Section 1445 | O.C.G.A. 48-7-128 |
| Standard Rate | 15% of gross sales price | 3% of sales price or gain |
| Key Form | IRS Form 8288 / 8288-A | Georgia Form G-2(RP) |
| Filing Deadline | 20 days after transfer | Due at closing (remitted to DOR) |
| Liable Party if Missed | Buyer | Buyer or withholding agent |

## Exemptions and Reduced Rates That Actually Apply

Not every foreign seller transaction requires the full 15% federal withholding. Three scenarios reduce or eliminate the obligation, and agents should know them cold rather than relying on last-minute attorney guidance.

First, if the property will be used by the buyer as a residence and the sales price does not exceed $300,000, the withholding rate drops to zero. Second, for buyer-residence transactions between $300,001 and $1,000,000, the rate is 10% instead of 15%. Third, the seller can apply for a withholding certificate from the IRS using Form 8288-B, which can reduce withholding to the seller's actual tax liability—but this application must be filed before or on the date of transfer, and processing takes 90 days on average.

On the Georgia side, the seller may provide a certified affidavit of gain to reduce the 3% withholding to 3% of the net gain rather than the full sales price. This requires Form NRW-Exemption or a comparable affidavit accepted by the Department of Revenue. Foreign sellers who have filed Georgia returns and have no outstanding tax liability may qualify for a reduced amount, but the documentation burden falls on the seller and their tax counsel.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with FIRPTA in Georgia

**The most frequent error is assuming FIRPTA only applies to non-U.S. citizens.** FIRPTA applies to "foreign persons," which includes U.S. residents who have abandoned their green cards, certain trusts with foreign beneficiaries, and foreign corporations. An agent who checks a passport and sees a green card is not necessarily looking at a domestic seller for FIRPTA purposes.

The second mistake is waiting until the week of closing to raise the withholding issue. By then, a seller expecting full proceeds discovers that 18% of the sales price will be held back, and the deal collapses. Agents should surface this obligation during the listing appointment or, at minimum, when the purchase agreement is executed. If you have ever seen [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down), FIRPTA surprises rank high among preventable failures.

Third, agents frequently confuse the roles of "buyer's agent," "seller's agent," and "withholding agent." Under FIRPTA, the term "transferee's agent" has a specific statutory meaning—it includes the real estate broker who represents the buyer. If that broker has actual knowledge that the seller is foreign and the withholding is not properly handled, the broker can be held secondarily liable under IRC Section 1445(d). This is not theoretical; the IRS has assessed penalties against brokerages.

Fourth, some agents rely solely on a Non-Foreign Affidavit (the so-called "FIRPTA affidavit" or IRS-recommended certification under Treasury Regulation 1.1445-2(b)) without verifying its accuracy. If the seller provides a false affidavit and the agent has reason to know it is false—perhaps because the MLS notes reference the owner living abroad—the affidavit does not shield the buyer or the agent from liability.

Fifth, agents fail to coordinate the federal and state timelines. The Georgia G-2(RP) is due at closing, while the federal Form 8288 is due within 20 days. Missing either deadline triggers separate penalty regimes. A [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) that includes both deadlines prevents this from falling through the cracks.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokerages have supervisory obligations under Georgia Real Estate Commission Rule 520-1-.07, which requires brokers to maintain adequate oversight of affiliated licensees' transactions. When a transaction involves a foreign seller, the compliance stakes multiply.

**Brokers should implement a mandatory intake question on every listing file: "Is the seller a U.S. person for federal tax purposes?"** This single question, documented in writing, creates a paper trail showing the brokerage addressed FIRPTA at the outset. If the answer is "no" or "unsure," the file should be flagged for withholding review before the property hits the market.

Brokers also need to verify that their closing attorneys are aware of dual withholding obligations. Georgia is an attorney-closing state, and the settlement agent typically handles remittance—but the legal obligation remains with the buyer. If a closing attorney misses the filing, the buyer's agent's brokerage could face a claim. Quarterly file audits should check for the presence of FIRPTA affidavits or withholding documentation in every transaction involving a seller with a foreign address, a foreign entity, or an out-of-state LLC.

| Broker Audit Point | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Listing intake form | FIRPTA status question answered | Blank or "unknown" response |
| Seller entity type | LLC, trust, or corporate veil | Foreign-organized entity |
| Closing file | Form 8288 confirmation or Non-Foreign Affidavit | Neither document present |
| Georgia withholding | G-2(RP) or IT-AFF2 in file | Missing from closing package |
| Timeline compliance | 20-day federal / closing-day state | Filed late or not at all |

## Liability Exposure When Compliance Fails

The penalty structure is not abstract. Under IRC Section 1461, the buyer who fails to withhold becomes liable for the full 15% plus interest from the transfer date and potential penalties of up to 25% of the underpayment. The IRS can assess this amount directly against the buyer without first pursuing the foreign seller.

For the agent, the exposure comes through IRC Section 1445(d), which makes the "transferee's agent" liable for the withholding amount if the agent had actual knowledge that the transferor was foreign and the withholding was not properly effectuated. The liability is capped at the agent's compensation in the transaction, but that is cold comfort when a $30,000 commission becomes a $30,000 penalty. The brokerage, as the entity that received the commission, may bear this burden.

Georgia's Department of Revenue can separately assess interest and penalties on the missed 3% state withholding. Under O.C.G.A. Section 48-2-44, late payment penalties accrue at 5% per month of the underpayment, up to 25%. The Georgia Real Estate Commission could also treat a pattern of FIRPTA non-compliance as a violation of the licensee's duty of competence, potentially triggering disciplinary proceedings under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-25.

## Building a FIRPTA-Ready Workflow for Your Georgia Practice

Start at the listing agreement stage. Add a FIRPTA disclosure addendum that asks the seller to certify their tax status. If the seller is foreign, immediately engage a tax attorney or CPA who specializes in international transactions. Do not wait for the buyer's side to raise it.

During contract negotiation, disclose the withholding requirement to all parties in writing. The buyer needs to understand they may be responsible for remitting 18% of the purchase price to two separate government agencies. Build withholding timelines into the contract's closing date calculus—if the seller is applying for a reduced withholding certificate via Form 8288-B, the 90-day processing time must be factored in or the closing will slip.

At closing, confirm that the settlement agent has prepared and will file Form 8288 and Form 8288-A within 20 days, and that Georgia Form G-2(RP) is included in the disbursement sheet. Document everything in the transaction file.
