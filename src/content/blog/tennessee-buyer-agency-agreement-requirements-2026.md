## Tennessee Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026: The Compliance Standard Has Changed

The era of informal buyer relationships in Tennessee real estate is over. Tennessee buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 demand that every licensee secure a written, signed buyer representation agreement before providing substantive brokerage services to a prospective purchaser. This is not a suggestion from your managing broker or a best-practice recommendation from your association — it is a regulatory mandate enforced by the Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) under Tennessee Code Annotated Section 62-13-405.

**Agents who treated the 2024 rule changes as optional are now facing real consequences.** TREC disciplinary actions for buyer agency violations increased markedly in late 2025, and the commission has signaled continued enforcement priority through its 2026 audit cycle. The stakes include license suspension, monetary penalties, and deal-killing disputes over compensation that could have been resolved with a properly executed form.

This post breaks down exactly what Tennessee law requires, which forms satisfy the mandate, where agents keep making preventable errors, and what brokers must do to keep their offices clean.

## What Tennessee Law Actually Requires

Tennessee Code Annotated Section 62-13-405 establishes that a licensee must enter into a written agreement with a buyer before providing real estate brokerage services. The statute was amended following the NAR settlement framework, and TREC Rule 1260-02-.12 further clarifies the timing, content, and disclosure obligations tied to buyer representation.

The agreement must be executed before — not during, not after — the agent engages in substantive services. TREC defines substantive services broadly: property tours, preparing offers, advising on pricing strategy, and negotiating on a buyer's behalf all qualify. A casual conversation at an open house does not trigger the requirement, but the moment an agent schedules a private showing or sends a CMA at the buyer's request, the line has been crossed.

The written agreement must include a definite duration with a specific termination date, a clear description of the compensation the buyer's agent will receive, and an explanation of how that compensation may be paid. Vague language about "customary commission" or "whatever the seller offers" no longer passes muster under TREC's interpretation.

## Required Forms and Acceptable Alternatives

Tennessee REALTORS (the state association) publishes the standard forms most agents use. The two primary buyer agency forms are RF401 (Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement) and RF402 (Non-Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement). Both were updated in late 2024 and revised again in early 2026 to reflect TREC's latest guidance on compensation disclosure.

| Form | Type | Key Feature | Best Used When |
|------|------|-------------|----------------|
| RF401 | Exclusive | Buyer works with one agent only during the term | Buyer is committed and ready to purchase |
| RF402 | Non-Exclusive | Buyer may work with multiple agents | Buyer wants flexibility or is early in the search |
| RF403 | Single-Property Showing Agreement | Covers one specific property tour only | Open house follow-up or one-time showing request |
| Custom Brokerage Form | Varies | Must meet all T.C.A. 62-13-405 requirements | Large brokerages with legal counsel drafting proprietary documents |

RF403 deserves special attention. This single-property showing agreement was introduced to address the practical reality of open houses and one-off showing requests where a buyer is not yet ready to commit to representation. It satisfies the TREC requirement for that specific interaction but does not establish ongoing agency.

**Brokerages using custom forms must ensure those documents contain every element TREC mandates.** Missing a required disclosure — particularly the compensation terms — renders the agreement deficient, which TREC treats the same as having no agreement at all. If your brokerage has proprietary forms, have them reviewed against TREC Rule 1260-02-.12 annually.

## Compensation Disclosure: The Section Most Agents Get Wrong

The compensation section of the buyer agency agreement is where most compliance failures originate. Tennessee law now requires that the agreement state a specific amount or rate the buyer's agent will be compensated, and that the buyer understands they are responsible for that amount if the seller does not offer sufficient compensation.

This is not a theoretical concern. In transactions where the listing broker offers less than what the buyer's agent agreement specifies — or offers nothing at all — the buyer is contractually obligated to cover the shortfall. Agents who gloss over this section during signing are creating future disputes and potential TREC complaints.

The agreement must also disclose whether the agent's compensation is negotiable. Per [TREC's published guidance](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regboards/trec.html), any suggestion that commissions are fixed or standardized across the industry violates Tennessee antitrust principles and can trigger disciplinary review. Every compensation field should be filled in with a specific number, and the buyer should initial or acknowledge that section separately.

Similar disclosure rigor applies in other states adopting post-settlement frameworks. Agents licensed in multiple jurisdictions can compare approaches by reviewing how [Washington structured its buyer agency requirements](/blog/washington-buyer-agency-agreement-requirements-2026) or how [West Virginia handles agency disclosure](/blog/west-virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026).

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Agents Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in TREC disciplinary files and brokerage compliance audits. Each one is preventable.

**First, agents present the agreement at the wrong time.** The most frequent violation is showing a property before the agreement is signed. An agent who meets a buyer at a listing, walks them through the home, and then asks them to sign the representation agreement in the car afterward has already violated the statute. The sequence matters.

Second, agents leave the compensation field blank or write "TBD." TREC has stated explicitly that an incomplete compensation section means the form does not satisfy the statutory requirement. Writing "to be determined" is functionally identical to not having an agreement.

Third, agents fail to provide a copy of the signed agreement to the buyer at the time of execution. Tennessee Code Annotated Section 62-13-312(b)(6) requires that clients receive copies of all documents they sign, immediately upon signing. Promising to email it later does not comply.

Fourth, agents use expired forms. The RF401 and RF402 went through revisions in early 2026 that changed disclosure language in the compensation section. Agents pulling old PDFs from their files or using outdated transaction management templates are submitting noncompliant agreements.

Fifth, agents conflate the buyer agency agreement with the purchase offer. These are separate documents serving separate legal functions. The agency agreement establishes the representation relationship and compensation terms. The purchase offer is a contract between buyer and seller. Combining them or treating the agency agreement as a formality to rush through at offer time misses the point entirely and violates the timing requirement.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in Tennessee carry direct supervisory responsibility under T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(1). If an affiliated agent violates the buyer agency agreement requirement, the broker faces potential discipline alongside the agent. This is not vicarious liability by implication — it is a statutory obligation to supervise.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|------------|---------------|-----------|
| Agreement on file before first showing | Compare showing date (MLS access log or lockbox record) against agreement execution date | Every transaction |
| Compensation field completed | Verify specific dollar amount or percentage is stated, no blanks or "TBD" | Every transaction |
| Current form version | Confirm RF401/RF402/RF403 revision date matches 2026 update | Quarterly |
| Buyer received copy | Confirm delivery timestamp in transaction file or email record | Every transaction |
| Termination date included | Verify agreement has a specific expiration, not open-ended | Every transaction |

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance are building a liability portfolio.** Systematic audits — whether manual or automated through transaction management software — are the only reliable safeguard. Spot-checking three or four files per month per agent catches patterns before they become TREC complaints.

Brokers should also maintain a policy manual section addressing buyer agency agreements, updated at least annually. When TREC investigates, the existence of a written policy and documented training reduces the broker's exposure significantly compared to a brokerage operating without formal compliance procedures.

## Penalties for Noncompliance

TREC's disciplinary authority under T.C.A. 62-13-313 includes license revocation, suspension, civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation, mandatory additional education, and public censure. For buyer agency agreement violations specifically, the commission has most commonly imposed fines and mandatory continuing education in 2025-2026 cases, reserving suspension for repeat offenders or cases involving consumer harm.

Beyond TREC discipline, a missing or deficient buyer agency agreement creates civil liability exposure. If a compensation dispute arises and the agent cannot produce a valid written agreement establishing their right to payment, they have no enforceable claim. Tennessee courts have consistently held that oral agreements for real estate brokerage services are unenforceable under the statute of frauds.

Deal cancellation is another practical risk. A listing agent or seller's attorney who discovers the buyer's agent lacks a proper representation agreement may raise the issue during negotiations or at closing. While this does not void the purchase contract between buyer and seller, it creates chaos around commission disbursement that can delay closings or trigger last-minute renegotiations.

## Preparing Your Practice for the Rest of 2026

The Tennessee buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 framework is not expected to change mid-year, but TREC has indicated it will issue additional interpretive guidance on edge cases — particularly around team structures where one team member signs the agreement but another handles showings. Agents operating within teams should confirm that their agreements authorize the team (not just the individual agent) to provide services.

Training is not a one-time event. Brokerages should conduct quarterly refreshers focused on form updates, common audit findings, and real disciplinary case studies from TREC's published actions. Agents who view the buyer agency agreement as a compliance checkbox rather than a relationship-defining document will continue to generate complaints.
