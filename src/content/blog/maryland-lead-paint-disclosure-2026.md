## Understanding Maryland Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

Maryland's housing stock is old. According to U.S. Census data, more than 60 percent of residential structures in the state were built before 1978 — the year the federal government banned lead-based paint in housing. That means Maryland lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 touch the majority of transactions you will handle this year, and the consequences for getting them wrong range from deal cancellation to five-figure fines.

The legal framework here is layered. Federal law under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, codified at 42 U.S.C. 4852d) sets the floor. Maryland's Environment Article, Title 6, Subtitle 8 adds state-specific obligations — particularly for landlords, but with implications that ripple into purchase transactions. Agents working in Baltimore City, Montgomery County, and Prince George's County should be aware of additional local enforcement mechanisms that can accelerate penalties.

This post breaks down every form, deadline, and liability exposure you need to know as a licensed Maryland agent or supervising broker.

## The Federal Baseline: What Title X Demands in Every Pre-1978 Sale

Before any buyer becomes contractually obligated, three things must happen. The seller must disclose known lead-based paint or hazards. The buyer must receive the EPA pamphlet titled *Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home*. And the buyer must be given a 10-day opportunity to conduct a lead inspection or risk assessment at their own expense.

**That 10-day inspection window is a default, not a mandate.** The parties can mutually agree to a different period — longer or shorter — or the buyer can waive the inspection entirely. What the buyer cannot waive is the right to receive the disclosure and the pamphlet. Those are non-negotiable under 40 CFR 745.107.

The form itself is straightforward: the EPA's *Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards*. In Maryland, this form is typically attached as an addendum to the sales contract and requires signatures from seller, buyer, and both agents confirming compliance. If you represent either side, your signature is on the line.

## Maryland-Specific Layers: MDE Forms and the Environment Article

Maryland does not simply defer to the feds. The Maryland Department of the Environment (MDE) administers its own lead paint program under Environment Article Section 6-801 through 6-852. While the bulk of state-level requirements target rental property owners — requiring lead paint registration (MDE Form 301), risk reduction standards, and tenant notification — these obligations bleed into sales transactions in two important ways.

First, if the property being sold is currently tenant-occupied or has been a rental within the prior 12 months, the seller may have outstanding MDE compliance obligations that affect marketability and title. Buyers' attorneys routinely check MDE's lead paint registry, and an unregistered rental property can delay or kill a deal.

Second, sellers who previously received an MDE inspection order or lead paint violation notice must disclose that history under both federal law (as a known hazard) and Maryland's seller disclosure requirements. The Maryland Association of Realtors (MAR) Residential Property Disclosure and Disclaimer Statement (Form 1035) includes a specific section addressing lead paint knowledge, which works in tandem with the federal EPA form.

| Document | Who Completes It | When It's Required |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| EPA Lead Paint Disclosure Form | Seller + agents | Every pre-1978 sale |
| MAR Form 1035 (Lead Section) | Seller | Every residential sale (disclosure or disclaimer) |
| MDE Form 301 (Registration) | Landlord/owner | Rental properties built pre-1978 |
| EPA Pamphlet delivery | Seller's agent | Before buyer is obligated under contract |
| Lead Inspection Waiver/Election | Buyer | Within 10-day window or as negotiated |

## What Agents Actually Get Wrong: Five Common Compliance Mistakes

Knowing the law exists is different from executing it cleanly in every transaction. Here are the errors I see most often when auditing Maryland files.

**First, agents confuse "no knowledge" with "no obligation."** A seller who checks the box stating they have no knowledge of lead paint has still completed a disclosure — that disclosure just says "unknown." Agents sometimes skip the form entirely when the seller says "I don't know anything about lead paint," which creates a compliance gap with federal law. The form must still be signed and delivered.

Second, agents fail to document the 10-day inspection period or its waiver. The buyer must affirmatively acknowledge in writing that they received the opportunity for an inspection. A missing signature on the inspection election/waiver line means your file is incomplete, and you have personal liability exposure under 40 CFR 745.115.

Third, listing agents in Maryland sometimes forget that condominiums and townhouses in pre-1978 buildings still trigger disclosure. The exemption under Title X applies to zero-bedroom units, housing for the elderly (with no children under six), and short-term leases of 100 days or less. A 1965 condo conversion in Federal Hill is not exempt.

Fourth, agents rely on the property disclosure form alone without attaching the separate EPA disclosure addendum. MAR Form 1035's lead paint section does not satisfy the federal requirement for a standalone signed disclosure with the specific EPA-mandated language. You need both documents in the file.

Fifth, dual agents or transaction coordinators handling both sides occasionally obtain only one set of signatures rather than ensuring all four parties (seller, buyer, listing agent, buyer's agent) have signed. Each party has a distinct signature line on the federal form for a reason — each carries independent liability.

## Penalties and Liability: What Happens When You Skip the Disclosure

The enforcement teeth here are real. Under 42 U.S.C. 4852d, each violation of the disclosure requirements can trigger civil penalties of up to $19,507 per occurrence (adjusted for inflation as of 2025 guidelines). Criminal penalties for knowing and willful violations can reach $19,507 per day of violation.

Beyond federal fines, Maryland's Real Estate Commission (under the Department of Labor) can take disciplinary action against your license for failure to comply with disclosure requirements. This falls under COMAR 09.11.02.01, which mandates that licensees act in conformity with all applicable laws. A pattern of missing lead paint disclosures could support a finding of incompetence or untrustworthiness.

| Consequence | Authority | Maximum Exposure |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Civil fine per violation | EPA / HUD | $19,507 |
| Criminal penalty (knowing violation) | DOJ | $19,507/day + imprisonment |
| License discipline | Maryland Real Estate Commission | Suspension or revocation |
| Buyer rescission of contract | Contract law | Full unwinding of deal |
| Private lawsuit (treble damages) | 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3) | Three times actual damages |

Buyers who discover undisclosed lead hazards after closing can sue the seller and both agents for treble damages under the federal statute. That means if a buyer spends $40,000 on remediation, you could face a $120,000 judgment — plus attorney's fees. This is not a theoretical risk in a state with as much pre-1978 housing as Maryland.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers carry vicarious liability for their agents' disclosure failures. Under Maryland Real Property Article Section 2-405 and COMAR 09.11.02, the broker is responsible for ensuring that all required documents are properly completed and retained. This is not optional oversight — it is a regulatory mandate.

**Brokers should implement a file checklist that specifically flags pre-1978 transactions.** At minimum, that checklist should confirm: the EPA disclosure form is signed by all four parties, the pamphlet delivery is acknowledged, the buyer's inspection election or waiver is documented, and any known hazards referenced in MDE records have been disclosed on the seller's form.

Transaction management systems can help here. If your brokerage is still relying on agents to self-audit, you are accepting risk that a single missed signature could expose the entire firm. Many brokerages now delegate this verification to [transaction coordinators who track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) and flag missing documents before closing.

Quarterly file audits should sample at least 10 percent of closed pre-1978 transactions. Look specifically for the five errors described above. When you find a gap in a closed file, document it, notify the agent in writing, and consult legal counsel about whether retroactive disclosure to the buyer is warranted.

## Comparing Maryland to Neighboring States

Agents licensed in multiple jurisdictions — common along the DC-Maryland-Virginia corridor — should note that Maryland's state-level lead paint regime is significantly more prescriptive than Virginia's or West Virginia's. Virginia relies almost entirely on the federal disclosure framework without a parallel state registration system. Maryland's MDE registry and risk reduction standards create an additional compliance layer that [Virginia does not impose](/blog/virginia-lead-paint-disclosure-2026).

For agents working Tennessee or Missouri transactions as well, those states similarly follow the federal baseline without Maryland-style state addenda. The takeaway: do not assume your compliance habits from another state transfer cleanly into Maryland practice.

## Timeline Management: When Each Step Must Happen

Timing errors are a subset of compliance errors, but they deserve their own attention because they are the most likely to result in contract rescission rather than just a fine. The buyer's 10-day inspection period begins when the buyer receives both the disclosure and the pamphlet — not when the contract is ratified. If you deliver the pamphlet two days after ratification, the clock starts two days late.

**Agents who routinely miss contractual deadlines should examine their workflow systems.** The lead paint inspection window is just one of many concurrent timelines running in a Maryland transaction. If you are managing five or more active deals, the probability of a timing error increases substantially. ## Keeping Your 2026 Files Audit-Ready

Every Maryland transaction file involving a pre-1978 property should contain five distinct items: the signed EPA disclosure form, the signed MAR Form 1035, proof of pamphlet delivery, the buyer's inspection election or waiver, and any MDE records or violation history the seller is aware of. If all five are present with complete signatures and correct dates, your file will survive any audit or litigation challenge.
