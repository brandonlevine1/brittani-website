## Tennessee Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026: The Rules Are Tighter Than You Think

Wire fraud losses in real estate topped $446 million nationally in 2023, and Tennessee has responded with increasingly specific expectations for licensees. Understanding the Tennessee wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 standards is no longer optional — it is a condition of compliant practice for every agent and broker in the state. The Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) has made clear through advisory opinions and enforcement actions that agents bear direct responsibility for warning clients about wire fraud risks before money moves.

Tennessee is not a state where you can point to the title company and say it was their job. TREC Rule 1260-02-.12, which governs the duties of licensees regarding transaction documentation, requires that all material advisories be maintained in the transaction file. That includes wire fraud disclosures. If it is not in your file, it did not happen — at least not in the eyes of the commission during an audit.

## The Legal Framework Agents Must Know

**Tennessee Code Annotated Section 62-13-403 establishes the duty of care licensees owe to all parties in a transaction, and TREC has interpreted this to include affirmative warnings about known transaction risks.** Wire fraud is now firmly in that category. The commission's 2024 advisory bulletin explicitly named business email compromise (BEC) schemes as a foreseeable risk that agents must address with clients.

TREC operates under the Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance, and its [disciplinary actions are published online](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regboards/trec.html) for public review. Agents who fail to document wire fraud warnings have appeared in enforcement proceedings with increasing frequency since 2024. The typical outcome is a consent order with a monetary penalty and mandatory continuing education hours.

Tennessee REALTORS (the state association) developed Form TF-01, the Wire Fraud Warning Advisory, specifically to give agents a standardized disclosure document. This form is not mandated by statute, but TREC has cited its use — or the use of substantially similar language — as the minimum standard of care in multiple enforcement contexts. Agents using proprietary brokerage forms should confirm their language meets or exceeds what TF-01 covers.

## When and How to Deliver the Advisory

Timing matters more than most agents realize. The wire fraud advisory must be delivered early enough that the client can act on the information before wiring funds. Best practice in Tennessee is to present Form TF-01 at the same time as the purchase and sale agreement or at the initial buyer consultation.

Delivering the form at closing is too late. By that point, a client may have already received fraudulent wiring instructions and sent funds to a compromised account. TREC's enforcement position treats a closing-day delivery as functionally equivalent to no delivery at all when a loss has occurred.

**Both buyers and sellers should sign the advisory, because wire fraud schemes target both sides of the transaction.** Seller proceeds are increasingly redirected through compromised email accounts belonging to listing agents or closing attorneys. The advisory must make clear that neither the brokerage nor the title company will ever change wiring instructions via email alone.

| Delivery Timing | TREC Compliance Status | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| At contract execution | Compliant | Low |
| At earnest money deposit | Likely compliant | Moderate |
| One week before closing | Questionable | High |
| At closing table | Non-compliant per TREC advisory | Very High |
| Never delivered | Violation | Maximum exposure |

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Agents Make

The first and most frequent error is treating the wire fraud advisory as a closing document rather than a contract-phase document. Agents bundle it with the closing package because that is when they think about wire transfers, but the entire purpose of the form is to warn clients before they encounter wiring instructions.

The second mistake is failing to obtain signatures from all parties. In a transaction with co-buyers or multiple sellers (such as an estate sale with several heirs), every party who might send or receive wired funds needs to sign. An unsigned form in the file is better than nothing, but a signed copy for each party is what TREC expects.

Third, agents frequently forward wiring instructions from closing attorneys or title companies to clients via unencrypted email. This single act creates the exact vulnerability that wire fraud schemes exploit. If a hacker has compromised the agent's email, that forwarded message becomes the vehicle for fraud. The correct procedure is to tell the client to contact the closing office directly at a phone number verified independently — not one from an email.

Fourth, some agents assume that because they work with a title company that has its own fraud prevention protocols, they have no independent duty. TREC does not recognize delegation of this responsibility. The agent's obligation under TCA Section 62-13-403 is personal to the licensee.

Fifth, agents in teams often assume the team leader or transaction coordinator handled the advisory. Unless the TC is a licensed assistant operating under proper supervision and the brokerage has documented the delegation, the [agent of record remains liable](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) for missing disclosures.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

TREC has authority under TCA Section 62-13-312 to impose civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation. A missing wire fraud advisory in a single transaction file counts as one violation. An agent with 15 closings and no advisories in any of them faces potential exposure of $15,000 in fines alone — before any private civil liability enters the picture.

**Beyond fines, agents face license suspension or revocation for patterns of non-compliance.** TREC treats repeated failures as evidence of incompetence or untrustworthiness under TCA Section 62-13-312(b)(1). A consent order requiring additional continuing education is the lightest outcome; revocation proceedings are initiated for agents whose omissions contributed to actual client losses.

Civil liability is the larger financial threat. If a buyer loses $300,000 to a wire fraud scheme and can demonstrate that their agent never warned them about the risk, that agent — and their brokerage's errors and omissions policy — becomes the primary recovery target. Tennessee courts have not yet established a bright-line rule on agent liability in these cases, but multiple pending lawsuits suggest the judiciary is moving toward holding agents to the standard TREC has articulated.

| TREC civil penalty | Missing form in file during audit | $1,000 per violation |
| Consent order | Pattern of missing advisories | Fine + 6-12 CE hours |
| License suspension | Repeated non-compliance after warning | 30-180 day suspension |
| License revocation | Client loss attributable to agent failure | Revocation proceedings |

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under TCA Section 62-13-312(b)(14) for failing to exercise reasonable supervision over affiliated licensees. A brokerage that has no wire fraud advisory policy, no audit procedure, and no training program is exposed to the same penalties its agents face — multiplied across every transaction in the office.

**The minimum brokerage standard in 2026 is a written office policy requiring Form TF-01 (or equivalent) in every transaction file, verified at two checkpoints: contract ratification and one week before closing.** Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance are not exercising reasonable supervision. A quarterly random audit of 10-15 percent of closed files is the baseline TREC expects.

Brokers should also maintain documentation that they provided annual training on wire fraud prevention to all affiliated licensees. TREC's 2025 guidance memorandum specifically recommended that brokerages conduct this training and retain attendance records. While not yet a hard regulatory mandate, this recommendation carries weight in enforcement proceedings where the commission evaluates whether supervision was reasonable.

Training should cover how to recognize phishing emails, why agents must never forward wire instructions, and how to direct clients to verify information through independent channels. Brokers who operate [teams with multiple agents](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) need to verify that each team member understands their individual duty — not just the team leader.

## Practical Steps for Every Transaction

Start by adding Form TF-01 to your standard document package alongside the agency disclosure and property condition disclaimer. Treat it as non-negotiable as the lead-based paint disclosure on pre-1978 homes. When you present the purchase agreement, present the wire fraud advisory at the same time and obtain signatures.

Next, include language in your initial client communication (whether a buyer consultation packet or a listing presentation) that addresses wire fraud verbally. A signed form is essential, but a verbal explanation reinforces the message. Tell clients explicitly: if you receive an email with wiring instructions that differ from what the closing attorney told you on the phone, call before you send anything.

Document your verbal warning in your CRM or transaction notes with a date and timestamp. If a dispute arises later, contemporaneous notes carry significant weight. Agents who track their [transaction milestones systematically](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) catch compliance gaps before they become liability events.

Finally, confirm with your closing attorney or title company that they have their own fraud prevention protocol in place, including callback verification procedures. You are not responsible for their internal security, but you are responsible for choosing competent settlement service providers — and knowing their procedures strengthens your position if something goes wrong.
