Remote closings are no longer an experiment—they are the default workflow for a majority of residential transactions in 2026, and any brokerage still treating them as optional is volunteering to lose relocating buyers to competitors who closed the deal from a laptop. Forty-seven states plus the District of Columbia now permit some form of Remote Online Notarization (RON), and the remaining three holdouts are under active legislative pressure. The question is no longer whether your state allows it, but which specific requirements your team must satisfy to avoid a rejected recording or a title claim.

## The Current State Count and What Changed in 2025

**Between January 2025 and March 2026, five states enacted or expanded RON statutes, bringing the total to forty-seven.** Massachusetts signed its permanent RON law in October 2025 after years of emergency pandemic-era extensions. Georgia's HB 413, effective January 1, 2026, removed the in-state-notary restriction that had blocked many out-of-state closings. Illinois broadened its existing law to include deed recordings without a wet-ink backup.

The three states without permanent RON authorization as of this writing are Connecticut, Louisiana, and Mississippi. Connecticut has a draft bill in committee; Louisiana permits Remote Ink-Signed Notarization (RISN) but still bars full RON for real property conveyances. Mississippi's 2024 pilot program expired without renewal. If your transaction touches any of those jurisdictions, plan for a traditional closing or a hybrid approach where the notary is remote but the signer appears physically.

The Mortgage Bankers Association's 2025 RON Adoption Report found that 38 percent of all purchase closings in RON-authorized states used full remote notarization last year, up from 22 percent in 2023. That trajectory means the operational question has shifted from "should we set this up?" to "how do we keep compliance tight at scale?"

## What "Allowed" Actually Means: Three Tiers of Authorization

Not all RON statutes are created equal. States fall into roughly three tiers, and lumping them together will get your recording rejected.

Tier one states—Virginia, Texas, Florida, Nevada, Arizona—permit RON with broad flexibility: any notary commissioned in the state can perform the act, the signer can be located anywhere in the world, and county recorders accept the electronic document without supplemental affidavits. Virginia was the first state to pass a permanent RON law back in 2012 ([Virginia Code Section 47.1-2](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title47.1/chapter1/section47.1-2/)), and it remains the gold standard for statutory clarity.

Tier two states authorize RON but impose location or technology restrictions. New York requires the signer to be physically in New York unless the notary uses an approved multi-factor identity verification platform from the Secretary of State's certified vendor list. Ohio mandates that the notary maintain a tamper-evident recording for ten years, stored with a qualified custodian.

Tier three states technically permit RON but have county-level recording offices that refuse electronic documents or demand wet-ink "confirmatory" deeds within a set number of days. Parts of Pennsylvania and certain rural counties in Michigan still operate this way. Before scheduling a remote closing in these areas, confirm recording acceptance with the specific county clerk—not just the state statute.

## Identity Verification: The Requirement Most Teams Underestimate

**Every RON statute requires multi-factor identity proofing, and the standards have tightened since 2024.** Knowledge-based authentication (KBA) alone no longer satisfies the threshold in twelve states that adopted the Revised Uniform Law on Notarial Acts (RULONA) 2024 amendments. Those states now require credential analysis—meaning a live scan of a government-issued ID verified against an issuing-authority database—plus at least one additional factor such as biometric comparison or device-bound authentication.

For brokers managing teams, this matters operationally. Your agents cannot simply tell a buyer to "hop on a Zoom call with the notary." The notary must use a platform certified in the state of their commission, the session must be recorded in its entirety, and the identity proofing must occur within that platform's auditable environment. If your preferred title company uses Notarize, Proof, or Pavaso, confirm which states those platforms are currently certified in—vendor certification lists change quarterly.

## Lender Overlays Still Create Friction

Even when a state allows RON and a county records electronic documents, the lender can refuse to fund a remote closing. This is the strongest counterargument against treating remote closings as universal: investor guidelines from Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac still contain overlays that restrict RON in certain loan scenarios.

Fannie Mae's Selling Guide (B8-7-01, updated November 2025) permits RON for conventional purchases and refinances but requires the lender to confirm that the notary's state authorizes electronic notarization and that the technology provider meets the American Land Title Association's standards. Some correspondent lenders interpret these requirements conservatively and refuse RON for jumbo loans, construction-to-perm conversions, or transactions involving powers of attorney. Freddie Mac's Single-Family Seller/Servicer Guide similarly permits eClosings but carves out exceptions for certain non-QM products.

The practical fix: build a lender-preference matrix for your market. Track which lenders in your pipeline accept full RON, which accept hybrid eClosings (electronic documents but in-person notarization), and which still demand wet ink across the board. Update it monthly. The fifteen minutes spent maintaining that matrix will save your TC hours of last-minute rescheduling.

## Cross-Border Transactions: Where It Gets Complicated

A seller in Texas closing on a property in New York must satisfy New York's RON rules, not Texas's. The governing law follows the property's location for recording purposes, but the notary's authority follows their state of commission. This dual-jurisdiction issue trips up teams handling relocation or investor clients.

Some states have adopted "multi-state notary" provisions that allow a notary commissioned in one state to notarize documents for property in another, provided both states authorize RON. Florida's 2023 amendment explicitly allows this. Others, like California, require the notary to hold a commission in California regardless of where the signer sits.

If your brokerage operates across state lines, designate one person on your operations team to own the compliance matrix for each state pair you commonly encounter. ## What Brokers Should Be Doing Right Now

**Stop treating remote closing capability as a tech feature and start treating it as a compliance function.** The brokerage that wins the next three years will not be the one with the flashiest signing platform—it will be the one whose agents can confidently tell a client, "Yes, we can close this remotely, and here's exactly how it works in your county."

Three moves to make this quarter. First, audit every active listing and pending transaction for RON eligibility at the county level, not just the state level. Second, confirm that your preferred title partners carry RON-certified notaries in every state where you do repeat business. Third, train your agents on the identity-verification workflow so they can prep buyers before the signing session—reducing failed sessions and rescheduled closings.
