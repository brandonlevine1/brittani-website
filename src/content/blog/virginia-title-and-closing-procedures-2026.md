## Understanding Virginia Title & Closing Procedures 2026

Virginia operates as an attorney-settlement state, which fundamentally shapes how every residential transaction reaches the finish line. If you are licensed in this Commonwealth, your working knowledge of Virginia title & closing procedures 2026 directly affects whether deals close on time, whether your clients are protected, and whether your license stays intact. The Virginia Real Estate Board (VREB), operating under the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), enforces compliance through audits, complaint investigations, and disciplinary actions outlined in Va. Code Title 54.1, Chapter 21.

Unlike states where title companies independently run the show, Virginia requires a licensed attorney to certify the title examination and supervise settlement under Va. Code Section 55.1-1000 through 55.1-1005. This means your role as an agent is not to perform title work but to coordinate timelines, verify deliverables, and ensure your client understands every document placed in front of them. Failing at any of these steps creates liability that extends beyond the transaction itself.

The regional Association of Realtors forms most agents rely on — including the [NVAR K-1331 residential contract](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov) and the REIN purchase agreement — contain specific contingency deadlines tied to title review. Missing those deadlines has contractual consequences that no amount of goodwill can fix.

## The Closing Timeline: From Ratification to Recording

**The moment a contract is ratified, the clock starts on a sequence of events that agents must track with precision.** In a standard Virginia residential transaction, the settlement agent (an attorney or a title company operating under attorney supervision) orders a title examination within 24 to 48 hours of receiving the ratified contract. The title search itself reviews the property's chain of title going back at least 60 years in most Virginia jurisdictions.

Once the title commitment is issued, buyers typically have a contractual window — often 14 days under the NVAR contract — to review and raise objections. If title defects surface (unpaid liens, boundary disputes, unreleased deeds of trust), the settlement attorney works to cure them before the scheduled closing date. Agents who do not actively follow up on the status of title curative work often find themselves scrambling 48 hours before settlement.

The actual closing day in Virginia involves the settlement attorney reading and explaining key documents, collecting funds (via certified check or verified wire), and recording the deed and deed of trust with the local circuit court clerk's office. Recording typically happens same-day or next business day, and until recording occurs, the transaction is not legally complete.

## Key Virginia Statutes and Forms Agents Must Know

Virginia law imposes several specific requirements that touch agents directly. The Virginia Residential Property Disclosure Act (Va. Code Section 55.1-700 through 55.1-714) governs what sellers must disclose, but it also affects title because undisclosed encumbrances can become post-closing disputes that name the listing agent.

| Document / Form | Purpose | Who Provides It |
|---|---|---|
| NVAR K-1331 (Regional Purchase Agreement) | Governs contingency deadlines including title review | Buyer's agent prepares |
| Virginia Residential Disclosure Statement (Va. Code 55.1-703) | Seller's property condition disclosure | Listing agent ensures delivery |
| HUD / TRID Closing Disclosure | Final settlement figures under CFPB rules | Settlement agent issues |
| Deed of Trust (Va. Code 55.1-300 et seq.) | Secures lender's interest in property | Settlement attorney prepares |
| Owner's Title Insurance Policy | Protects buyer against title defects | Settlement agent issues at closing |

Agents should also be aware of Va. Code Section 6.2-2011, which requires settlement agents to maintain escrow accounts and follow specific handling procedures for earnest money deposits. If an agent's brokerage holds earnest money rather than the settlement agent, the brokerage must comply with VREB's [escrow account regulations](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) under 18 VAC 135-20-180.

## Common Mistakes Virginia Agents Make at Closing

**Errors in the title and closing phase are the most expensive mistakes in the transaction because they occur at the point of maximum financial exposure.** Based on DPOR disciplinary actions and settlement attorney feedback, these are the most frequent failures.

First, agents routinely fail to confirm the title commitment has been delivered to the buyer within the contractual review period. Under most NVAR contracts, if the buyer does not receive and object to title issues within the stated window, the contingency expires automatically. An agent who assumes the settlement company "handled it" may find their buyer contractually bound to accept a defective title.

Second, agents submit incorrect legal descriptions or property addresses on contract documents. In Virginia's independent cities and counties — where a property at the same street address can exist in both a city and an adjacent county — this error triggers title search failures, delayed closings, and occasionally clouds on title that require corrective instruments. The cost of a corrective deed or affidavit typically falls on the party whose agent made the error.

Third, many agents neglect to verify that prior liens (particularly HOA liens, which are super-priority liens under Va. Code Section 55.1-1833) have been satisfied before closing. Virginia HOA liens can take priority over first-position deeds of trust under certain conditions, making them uniquely dangerous in this Commonwealth.

Fourth, agents fail to coordinate the timing of the buyer's final walkthrough relative to the settlement appointment. If a walkthrough reveals issues that require contract amendments, but the buyer has already signed closing documents, the remedy becomes exponentially more complex. Virginia courts have held that post-settlement claims based on conditions discoverable at walkthrough face an uphill battle.

Fifth, listing agents sometimes fail to obtain proper authorization for proceeds disbursement when the seller is a trust, estate, or entity. Virginia requires specific documentation (letters testamentary, corporate resolutions, or trust certificates under Va. Code Section 64.2-700 et seq.) before the settlement agent can disburse funds to a non-individual seller. Transactions have been [delayed or cancelled entirely](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) because listing agents did not flag entity ownership early enough.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Va. Code Section 54.1-2130 and VREB regulations. This means a broker whose agents repeatedly mishandle closing-related procedures can face disciplinary action even if the broker was not personally involved in the transaction.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Earnest money deposit timing | Funds deposited within 5 business days per VREB regs (18 VAC 135-20-180) | Every transaction |
| Title contingency tracking | Was commitment received and acknowledged before deadline? | Every transaction |
| Wire verification procedures | Did agent follow firm's wire fraud prevention protocol? | Every transaction |
| Contract form version | Is the agent using current-year regional forms? | Quarterly review |
| Entity seller documentation | Were trust/estate/LLC docs obtained before listing went active? | At listing intake |

**Brokers should conduct file audits at minimum quarterly, with random spot-checks monthly for high-volume offices.** The VREB has increased enforcement actions related to earnest money handling and transaction documentation in recent years. A broker who can demonstrate a documented audit trail — showing that agents were trained and monitored — has a significantly stronger defense if a complaint reaches DPOR.

Additionally, brokers must ensure that agents are not inadvertently practicing law. In Virginia, explaining the legal effect of title documents to a client crosses the line into unauthorized practice of law (UPL) under Virginia State Bar opinions. Agents can explain the process and timeline, but they cannot advise a client on whether to accept or reject a title exception. That guidance must come from the client's own attorney or the settlement attorney.

## Liability Exposure When Procedures Break Down

The consequences of non-compliance in Virginia range from contract cancellation to license suspension. Under Va. Code Section 54.1-2109, DPOR can impose fines up to $2,500 per violation, require additional education, suspend or revoke licenses, and mandate restitution to harmed parties. These are not theoretical outcomes — DPOR publishes [disciplinary case summaries](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) regularly.

Beyond regulatory action, agents face civil liability. Virginia courts have permitted negligence claims against agents who failed to ensure timely delivery of title-related documents, particularly when the failure resulted in a buyer losing a financing lock or a seller incurring carrying costs from a delayed closing. The duty of reasonable care under Va. Code Section 54.1-2131 is the standard against which agent conduct is measured.

> "A licensee shall not be liable for inaccurate information provided by a settlement agent unless the licensee knew or should have known of the inaccuracy." — Va. Code Section 54.1-2131.1

This statutory safe harbor protects agents who rely on settlement agent information in good faith. But "should have known" is an elastic standard. If an agent receives a title commitment showing an obvious defect — say, an unsatisfied judgment lien — and fails to flag it for the client, the safe harbor likely will not apply.
