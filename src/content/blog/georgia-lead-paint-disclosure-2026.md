## Georgia Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026: The Rules That Still Trip Up Experienced Agents

Every year, agents in Georgia lose deals—or worse, face federal enforcement actions—over a disclosure requirement that has existed since 1996. The Georgia lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain governed primarily by federal law, but the state-specific forms, brokerage obligations, and enforcement patterns create a compliance environment that demands precision. If you hold a Georgia real estate license and list or sell pre-1978 housing, this is not optional reading.

The Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulations at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart A, set the floor. Georgia does not have a separate state lead paint disclosure statute that overrides or adds to the federal mandate. Instead, the Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) expects licensees to comply fully with 42 U.S.C. 4852d and the joint EPA/HUD rule. Failure to do so constitutes a violation of Georgia license law under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(a)(33), which prohibits conduct that demonstrates incompetence or untrustworthiness.

## Who Must Disclose—and Who Is Exempt

**The obligation falls on sellers and landlords of any residential dwelling built before January 1, 1978.** This includes single-family homes, condos, duplexes, and apartments. The listing agent has an independent duty to ensure the disclosure occurs. Buyer's agents must also ensure their clients receive the required pamphlet and acknowledgment form.

Exemptions are narrow. Properties sold at foreclosure, zero-bedroom studios and efficiencies, housing certified lead-free by a certified inspector, and dwellings exclusively for the elderly or disabled (where children under six are not expected to reside) fall outside the requirement. Short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or fewer are also exempt.

Georgia's housing stock makes this particularly relevant. According to the U.S. Census Bureau's American Housing Survey, roughly 1.2 million housing units in Georgia were built before 1978. In metro Atlanta alone, neighborhoods like Inman Park, Grant Park, Virginia-Highland, and Druid Hills contain dense concentrations of pre-1978 homes that change hands frequently.

## The Specific Forms Georgia Agents Must Use

The Georgia Association of Realtors (GAR) publishes Form F803—the "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and Lead-Based Paint Hazards." This form satisfies the federal mandate and includes all required elements: seller's disclosure of known lead-based paint, records or reports provided to the buyer, buyer's acknowledgment of receipt of the EPA pamphlet ("Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home"), and the buyer's opportunity to conduct a lead inspection.

| Requirement | Form / Document | Responsible Party |
|---|---|---|
| Seller disclosure of known hazards | GAR F803 (Section A) | Seller and listing agent |
| EPA pamphlet delivery | "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" | Listing agent |
| Buyer acknowledgment | GAR F803 (Section C) | Buyer and buyer's agent |
| 10-day inspection period election | GAR F803 (Section D) | Buyer |
| Agent certification | GAR F803 (Section E) | All agents in transaction |

The form requires signatures from the seller, buyer, listing agent, and selling agent. Each party must retain a signed copy for three years after closing. If your brokerage uses a different form—such as one generated by your transaction management software—it must contain every element specified in [24 CFR 35.92](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-24/subtitle-A/part-35/subpart-A).

## The 10-Day Inspection Window: Where Deals Get Complicated

Buyers have the right to a 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense. This period is a default—parties can agree in writing to a different timeframe, or the buyer can waive the inspection entirely. The waiver must be documented on the disclosure form.

**Agents frequently mishandle timing when the buyer elects to inspect.** The 10-day clock starts when the buyer receives the disclosure and pamphlet, not when the purchase agreement is signed. If the disclosure is delivered three days after binding agreement, the inspection window extends accordingly—and this can collide with due diligence deadlines under the GAR Purchase and Sale Agreement (Form F20).

This timing conflict is one of the most common reasons [deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) at the compliance stage. Coordinate your lead paint disclosure delivery with your other contingency timelines from day one.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: Federal and State Exposure

The consequences of ignoring or botching lead paint disclosure in Georgia are not theoretical. The EPA and HUD enforce jointly, and civil penalties can reach $19,507 per violation as of 2024 (adjusted periodically for inflation under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act). Criminal penalties for knowing and willful violations can include fines up to $19,507 per violation and imprisonment.

| Violation Type | Potential Consequence | Enforcing Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Failure to disclose known hazards | Up to $19,507 per violation (civil) | EPA / HUD |
| Knowing/willful violation | Criminal fines + imprisonment | DOJ |
| Buyer's private action (treble damages) | 3x actual damages | Georgia Superior Court |
| License law violation | Reprimand, fine, suspension, revocation | GREC |
| Deal cancellation | Buyer may void contract | Per 24 CFR 35.90 |

Beyond federal penalties, buyers have a private right of action. Under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3), a buyer can recover treble damages if a seller or agent knowingly fails to comply. Georgia courts have jurisdiction over these claims, and they surface in post-closing litigation more often than agents realize.

The Georgia Real Estate Commission can also act independently. A complaint alleging failure to provide lead paint disclosures can trigger a GREC investigation under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25. Sanctions range from a letter of reprimand to license revocation, depending on severity and pattern.

## Common Mistakes Georgia Agents Make

Five errors account for the majority of lead paint disclosure failures in Georgia transactions. Each one is avoidable.

**First, agents assume the seller's property disclosure statement covers lead paint.** It does not. The GAR Seller's Property Disclosure Statement (Form F301) addresses general property conditions, but it is not a substitute for the separate lead-based paint disclosure required by federal law. Both forms must be completed independently.

Second, agents deliver the EPA pamphlet but fail to obtain a signed acknowledgment. Handing a buyer a booklet at showing time without documenting receipt on GAR F803 leaves the agent without proof of compliance. If a dispute arises years later, the burden shifts to the agent to prove delivery occurred.

Third, listing agents forget to disclose when the seller "doesn't know" about lead paint. The form still must be completed. The seller checks the box indicating no knowledge of lead-based paint, but the form itself—fully signed—must still be part of the transaction file. Skipping the form entirely because the seller claims ignorance is a violation.

Fourth, agents allow buyers to waive the 10-day inspection period verbally. A verbal waiver is worthless. The waiver must be documented in writing on the disclosure form. If you are [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) and managing overlapping deadlines, build the lead paint waiver into your workflow as a hard checkpoint.

Fifth, agents fail to retain records for the required three years. Georgia brokerages must maintain transaction files, but agents who leave a brokerage often lose access to their files. Keep your own copies. The federal three-year retention requirement runs from the completion date, not the listing date.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Georgia carry supervisory liability under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(a)(25) for the acts of their affiliated licensees. A single agent's failure to provide lead paint disclosures can expose the brokerage to federal fines, state discipline, and civil liability.

**Brokers should audit every closed transaction involving a pre-1978 property for the presence of a signed GAR F803.** This means your transaction coordinator or compliance officer must flag properties by year built at listing intake—not at closing. Catching a missing disclosure at the closing table is too late; catching it before the first showing is the standard.

Quarterly file audits should verify three things: the disclosure form is fully executed by all four parties, the EPA pamphlet delivery is acknowledged, and the buyer's election regarding the 10-day inspection period is documented. If your brokerage processes high volume, consider automating this check at the document upload stage. Missing a deadline on [something this mechanical](/blog/real-cost-of-missed-deadlines) is a systems failure, not a knowledge gap.

Brokers should also include lead paint disclosure compliance in onboarding training for new agents and in annual continuing education reminders. GREC does not currently mandate a standalone lead paint CE course, but the topic falls squarely within the "license law and regulations" category that every Georgia licensee must study during renewal cycles.
