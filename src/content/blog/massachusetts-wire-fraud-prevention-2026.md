## Understanding Massachusetts Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026

Wire fraud losses in real estate topped $446 million nationally in 2024, and Massachusetts has responded with regulatory teeth. The Massachusetts wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 framework places affirmative duties on licensed agents — not just closing attorneys — to warn clients, document disclosures, and follow verification protocols before any funds move. Ignoring these duties exposes agents to license discipline, civil suits, and brokerage-level sanctions.

The Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons, operating under the Division of Professional Licensure (DPL), has clarified that wire fraud advisories fall squarely within an agent's duty of care under M.G.L. Chapter 112, Sections 87PP through 87DDD. This is not optional guidance. It is an enforceable standard tied to your license.

Massachusetts is an attorney-closing state, which creates a unique dynamic. Agents sometimes assume the closing attorney handles everything related to funds, but the Board has made clear that the agent's advisory obligation exists independently and must occur earlier in the transaction timeline than the attorney's verification steps.

## The Regulatory Framework: Statutes and Rules That Apply

**M.G.L. Chapter 93A, the Consumer Protection Act, is the statute most frequently cited in wire fraud liability claims against Massachusetts agents.** When a buyer loses $300,000 to a spoofed email and the agent never provided a wire fraud warning, plaintiffs' attorneys reach for 93A because it allows treble damages and attorney fees. The argument is straightforward: failing to disclose a known, material risk constitutes an unfair or deceptive act in trade or commerce.

The Board's regulatory authority comes from 254 CMR 2.00 through 7.00, which govern the conduct of licensees. Section 7.00 addresses duties owed to consumers, including the obligation to provide timely, written disclosures about material risks in the transaction. The DPL's 2025 interpretive guidance explicitly named wire fraud advisories as falling within this disclosure requirement.

Additionally, the Massachusetts Association of Realtors (MAR) updated its standard forms package in early 2026 to include Form WFA-2026, the Wire Fraud Advisory. While MAR forms are not state-mandated, the Board has signaled that using an equivalent form satisfying the same content requirements is expected of all licensees. You can review the [Board's licensing standards](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/board-of-registration-of-real-estate-brokers-and-salespersons) directly for the most current interpretive guidance.

## Required Forms and Documentation

The practical compliance question every agent asks is: what form do I use, and when do I use it? Here is the framework.

MAR Form WFA-2026 is the industry-standard wire fraud advisory. It covers three elements: a warning that wire instructions may be intercepted or spoofed, a directive to verbally verify all wire instructions with the closing attorney's office by calling a number the client independently obtained, and a statement that the agent will never send wire instructions via email. The client signs and dates the form, and the agent retains a copy in the transaction file.

| Form | Purpose | Timing | Signatory |
|------|---------|--------|-----------|
| MAR Form WFA-2026 | Wire fraud advisory to buyer/seller | Within 48 hours of accepted offer | All parties + agent |
| Brokerage Wire Protocol Acknowledgment | Internal compliance record | At listing or buyer agency agreement | Agent + broker |
| Closing Attorney Verification Letter | Confirms funds transfer protocol | 5+ days before closing | Closing attorney |
| Email Communication Disclaimer | Footer warning on all transaction emails | Ongoing throughout transaction | Agent (automated) |

**Timing matters more than most agents realize.** The Board's position is that the advisory must be delivered early enough for the client to internalize the warning before they receive any wire instructions. Handing it to a buyer at the closing table — after wires have already been sent — does not satisfy the requirement. Best practice is delivery within 48 hours of mutual acceptance, alongside your other initial disclosures.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance break into three categories: regulatory, civil, and transactional.

On the regulatory side, the Board can issue formal reprimands, require additional continuing education, impose fines, or suspend or revoke licenses under 254 CMR 3.00. The Board has historically treated consumer protection failures as serious misconduct, particularly where actual financial harm results. A pattern of non-compliance across multiple transactions escalates from individual agent discipline to brokerage-level enforcement.

Civil liability is where the real financial pain hits. Under M.G.L. Chapter 93A, a defrauded buyer can bring a claim against the agent and brokerage for failure to warn. If the court finds the failure was willful or knowing, damages triple. A $400,000 wire fraud loss becomes a $1.2 million judgment plus attorney fees. Even without 93A, common law negligence claims require only showing that the agent breached a duty of care — and the Board's published guidance establishes what that duty includes.

Transactionally, closing attorneys have begun requiring proof of wire fraud advisories before scheduling closings. If your file is missing the signed WFA-2026, the attorney may delay or refuse to close. This creates cascading problems: rate lock expirations, seller breach claims, and reputation damage with referral sources. ## Common Mistakes Massachusetts Agents Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in Board complaints and civil filings. Each one is avoidable with basic process discipline.

**First, agents email wire instructions directly to clients.** This is the single most dangerous thing you can do. Even if you copy the instructions from a legitimate source, your email may be intercepted, modified, or used as a template for a later spoofed message. The rule is absolute: never transmit wire instructions via email, text, or any unencrypted digital channel.

Second, agents deliver the wire fraud advisory too late. Handing the form to a buyer three days before closing — after the buyer has already received wire instructions from the closing attorney — defeats the purpose. The client needs the warning before they encounter any wire instructions, not after. Deliver it with your agency disclosures or purchase agreement documents.

Third, agents fail to document delivery. A verbal warning at a showing or during a phone call does not create a defensible record. If you cannot produce a signed, dated form from your transaction file, the Board will treat it as if no advisory was given. Keep originals or verified digital copies with timestamps.

Fourth, agents assume the closing attorney handles it. Massachusetts closing attorneys do provide their own wire verification protocols, but this does not excuse the agent's independent obligation. The Board views these as separate duties serving different purposes at different transaction stages. Your advisory is about awareness; the attorney's protocol is about verification.

Fifth, agents use outdated forms. The 2024-era wire fraud advisories may not contain language meeting the Board's current interpretive standards. If your brokerage has not updated its advisory form since mid-2025, verify that the content aligns with MAR Form WFA-2026's three required elements.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under M.G.L. Chapter 112, Section 87XX, which means an agent's failure is the broker's failure. The Board has been explicit: claiming ignorance of agent non-compliance is not a defense when the broker failed to implement reasonable supervisory systems.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Documentation Required |
|-------------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Signed WFA-2026 in every active transaction file | Per-transaction | Signed form with date |
| Email footers containing wire fraud disclaimer | Quarterly spot-check | Screenshot or IT confirmation |
| Agent training on wire fraud protocols | Annual minimum | CE certificate or internal training log |
| Brokerage policy manual updated with wire fraud section | Annual review | Dated policy with agent acknowledgment signatures |
| Incident response plan for suspected wire fraud | Annual review | Written plan with attorney contact info |

**Brokers should conduct random file audits at least monthly, pulling five to ten active transaction files and confirming the WFA-2026 is present and properly dated.** Waiting for a complaint to trigger a review is a recipe for multi-agent exposure. Set calendar reminders, assign the task to your transaction coordinator, or automate the check.

Training is non-negotiable. The Board expects brokers to provide annual wire fraud prevention training beyond what agents receive in their standard continuing education hours. Document attendance and content. If you operate across multiple offices, ensure consistent implementation — a single office's lax practices can trigger brokerage-wide discipline.

## How Massachusetts Differs From Other States

Massachusetts occupies a somewhat unusual position because of its attorney-closing requirement. In states without mandatory closing attorneys, agents bear an even heavier burden for wire verification. But in Massachusetts, the layered system — agent advisory plus attorney verification — creates what the Board calls "dual gatekeeping."

This dual system does not reduce the agent's responsibility. It increases it, because the Board expects the agent to serve as the first warning and the attorney to serve as the verification checkpoint. If either layer fails, liability attaches to both. Agents cannot point to the attorney and say the verification was their job; the Board will ask why the advisory layer failed first.

The attorney-closing requirement also means Massachusetts agents must coordinate with closing attorneys on timing. If your client receives the attorney's wire instructions before you have delivered the WFA-2026, you have already failed chronologically. Build form delivery into your pre-closing workflow immediately after mutual acceptance — not in the week before closing. ## Building a Compliant Workflow for Every Transaction

A defensible wire fraud prevention workflow in Massachusetts has five steps, executed in sequence with documentation at each stage.

Deliver MAR Form WFA-2026 within 48 hours of mutual acceptance. Obtain dated signatures from all parties. Upload the signed form to your transaction management system immediately — not at closing, not next week.

Add a wire fraud disclaimer to every email signature and every email containing transaction documents. The disclaimer should state that your brokerage will never send wire instructions via email and that recipients should verbally verify any wire instructions they receive from any source.

Confirm with the closing attorney, at least ten days before closing, that their office will provide wire instructions only through verified channels. Document this confirmation in your file. If the attorney's office uses an encrypted portal, note the portal name and date access was provided to the client.

**Remind your client verbally, at least 48 hours before closing, to call the closing attorney's office to verify wire instructions before sending any funds.** Document that you made this call in your file notes with the date and time.

If your client reports receiving wire instructions that look suspicious, invoke your brokerage's incident response plan immediately. Contact the closing attorney, instruct the client not to send any funds, and report the incident to the FBI's IC3. Document every step.
