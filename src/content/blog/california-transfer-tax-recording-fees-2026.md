## Understanding California Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Getting the numbers wrong on transfer taxes or recording fees does not just irritate escrow officers—it can delay closings, expose agents to liability claims, and trigger audits from the California Department of Real Estate (DRE). The California transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 introduce no sweeping legislative overhauls, but incremental county and city adjustments mean agents who rely on last year's estimates are already behind. This article breaks down the specific statutes, forms, fee tables, compliance pitfalls, and broker-level responsibilities you need to master before your next closing.

California's documentary transfer tax is governed by Revenue and Taxation Code Sections 11901 through 11934. The base county rate—$1.10 per $1,000 of the sale price—has remained unchanged since voters passed it under the Documentary Transfer Tax Act. Cities operating under charter authority can impose additional transfer taxes, which is why selling a $2 million home in San Francisco costs dramatically more than selling the same-priced home in unincorporated Riverside County.

## State and County Base Rates: The Numbers That Matter

**The county documentary transfer tax applies uniformly at $1.10 per $1,000 of consideration across all 58 California counties.** This tax is collected at the time of recording and typically appears on line items of the grant deed or quitclaim deed submitted to the county recorder's office. Agents should reference the Preliminary Change of Ownership Report (PCOR, Form BOE-502-A) and ensure the declared consideration matches the amount used to calculate the tax.

Cities that are not charter cities generally cannot impose their own transfer tax. Charter cities, however, have broad taxing authority under Article XI, Section 5 of the California Constitution. The result is a patchwork of city-level taxes layered on top of the county rate.

| Jurisdiction | Rate per $1,000 | Threshold or Tiered? | Effective 2026 |
|---|---|---|---|
| All CA Counties (base) | $1.10 | Flat | Yes |
| City of Los Angeles | $4.50 | Flat | Yes |
| City of San Francisco | $5.00–$30.00 | Tiered (price-based) | Yes |
| City of Oakland | $10.00–$25.00 | Tiered (Measure U-1) | Yes |
| City of San Jose | $3.30 | Flat | Yes |
| City of Berkeley | $15.00–$25.00 | Tiered (Measure P) | Yes |

San Francisco's tiered structure under the San Francisco Business and Tax Regulations Code Article 12-C means that transactions exceeding $10 million face a rate of $30.00 per $1,000—a figure that sometimes shocks agents new to luxury listings in the city. Oakland's Measure U-1, upheld by voters and effective since April 2023, similarly imposes a $25.00 rate on sales above $5 million.

## Recording Fees: What the County Recorder Charges

Recording fees are separate from transfer taxes and cover the administrative cost of filing documents with the county recorder. Under California Government Code Section 27361, base recording fees are set at $14 for the first page and $3 for each additional page. An additional $75 fee per recorded document funds the SB 2 Building Homes and Jobs Act trust fund, per Government Code Section 27388.1, although this fee does not apply to the first single-family dwelling transfer per transaction.

**Agents frequently forget that the SB 2 fee applies per document, not per transaction.** If your deal requires recording a grant deed, a deed of trust, and a reconveyance, each one triggers the $75 surcharge (with limited exceptions). A five-page grant deed plus a twelve-page deed of trust costs far more than agents who quote "$14 to record" realize.

| Document | Pages | Base Fee | SB 2 Fee | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Grant Deed (2 pages) | 2 | $17 | $75 | $92 |
| Deed of Trust (10 pages) | 10 | $41 | $75 | $116 |
| Reconveyance (1 page) | 1 | $14 | $0* | $14 |
| Substitution of Trustee (2 pages) | 2 | $17 | $75 | $92 |

*Reconveyances recorded to release the seller's existing lien may qualify for exemption under Government Code Section 27388.1(a)(2).

Agents quoting closing costs should pull fee schedules directly from the specific county recorder's website rather than relying on generic title company estimates. Los Angeles County, for example, publishes its complete [recording fee schedule](https://registrar.lacounty.gov/) online and updates it when statutory changes take effect.

## Forms and Documents Agents Must Get Right

The California Association of Realtors (C.A.R.) publishes the Estimated Buyer's Net Sheet and Estimated Seller's Net Sheet that agents typically use to outline costs. Transfer tax calculations belong on the Estimated Seller's Net Sheet unless the purchase agreement allocates them differently. The relevant C.A.R. form is the Seller's Estimated Net Proceeds (ENP) form, and agents must ensure the tax line item reflects both county and city rates.

At recording, the grant deed itself must include a declaration of the documentary transfer tax amount or indicate that it is being computed on the full value of the property transferred minus liens. This declaration appears directly on the face of the deed—typically in the upper-left corner—pursuant to Revenue and Taxation Code Section 11932. Omitting it means the county recorder rejects the deed outright.

The Preliminary Change of Ownership Report (PCOR, BOE-502-A) is required by Revenue and Taxation Code Section 480.3 and must be filed with every deed. If the buyer fails to file the PCOR at recording, the county assessor adds a $20 penalty. Agents representing buyers should confirm the PCOR is completed and delivered to escrow well before the closing date. ## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Transfer Taxes and Recording Fees

**Mistake number one is quoting only the county rate when the property sits inside a charter city.** An agent listing a home in Oakland who estimates transfer tax at $1.10 per $1,000 instead of $11.10 (county plus city base) is underestimating costs by roughly 90 percent. On a $900,000 sale, that is the difference between a $990 estimate and a $9,990 actual bill. Sellers who discover this shortfall at closing rightfully blame the listing agent.

Mistake two involves misidentifying who pays. While California custom typically places the county transfer tax on the seller and splits the city tax (or assigns it regionally), the allocation depends on the purchase agreement. Agents who assume customs are contracts open themselves to disputes. The California Residential Purchase Agreement (C.A.R. RPA, paragraph 7) addresses cost allocation—read it line by line for every transaction.

Third, agents routinely underestimate recording fees by ignoring the SB 2 surcharge. This creates a gap between the estimated and actual closing statements. Fourth, some agents fail to account for exemptions—transfers between spouses under Revenue and Taxation Code Section 11927, transfers by court order, or transfers to revocable trusts under Section 11930—and either overcharge or undercharge. Miscalculating exemptions can lead to refund claims that delay the final settlement statement.

Fifth, agents occasionally submit deeds without the documentary transfer tax declaration, causing recorder rejection and pushing closing past the contractual deadline. ## What Happens When You Get It Wrong: Liability and Consequences

The DRE does not issue fines specifically for miscalculating transfer taxes, but it does discipline licensees for negligence and misrepresentation under Business and Professions Code Sections 10176 and 10177. An agent who provides a net sheet with materially incorrect transfer tax figures—and the seller relies on it—has arguably misrepresented a material fact.

> Revenue and Taxation Code Section 11933 provides that any tax erroneously or illegally collected may be refunded upon timely claim, but the burden falls on the taxpayer, not the agent who caused the error.

Civil liability is the more common risk. A seller who nets $15,000 less than the agent's estimate may file a negligence claim or demand mediation under the purchase agreement's dispute resolution clause. Errors and omissions insurance may cover the claim, but repeated mistakes lead to policy non-renewal, increased premiums, and broker intervention. In extreme cases—where an agent knowingly misrepresents costs—DRE administrative proceedings can result in license suspension or revocation.

Deal cancellation is also a realistic outcome. If the seller's estimated net proceeds were based on flawed tax calculations, and the seller cannot close at the corrected figures, the deal may collapse entirely. The listing agent loses the commission and may owe marketing costs or other damages depending on the brokerage agreement.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under Business and Professions Code Section 10164 and California Code of Regulations Section 2725. A broker who fails to review transaction files—including closing cost estimates—can be held responsible for agent errors. Auditing transfer tax and recording fee calculations should be part of every file review.

**Brokers should verify three things in every transaction file: that the correct city and county transfer tax rates were applied, that the allocation matches the signed purchase agreement, and that the documentary transfer tax declaration appears on the recorded deed.** Spot-checking net sheets against the actual closing disclosure catches discrepancies before they become claims.

## Staying Accurate Through 2026 and Beyond

California's transfer tax landscape is not static. Ballot measures at the city level can alter rates overnight—Oakland's Measure U-1 and San Francisco's Proposition I both passed within the last few election cycles and dramatically changed agent cost estimates. Agents should monitor local election results every November and update their closing cost templates accordingly.

The California transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 demand precision, not guesswork. Agents who build systems to verify rates, double-check form declarations, and confirm fee allocations before documents reach escrow will close faster, face fewer disputes, and build reputations that generate referrals. Those who treat this as someone else's problem will eventually become the cautionary tale in a continuing education seminar.
