## Indiana Teams & Supervision Rules 2026: The Regulatory Framework

The phrase "Indiana teams & supervision rules 2026" is not just a search term—it represents a tightening regulatory environment that demands attention from every licensee operating within a team structure. Indiana's real estate commission has grown increasingly focused on how teams advertise, delegate tasks, and maintain supervisory accountability. If you are running or joining a team in Indiana this year, ignorance of the rules is a liability you cannot afford.

Indiana's regulatory framework for teams is governed primarily by Indiana Code Title 25, Article 34.1 (the Real Estate Broker Licensing Act) and the administrative rules found in 876 IAC. The Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC) sits within the Indiana Professional Licensing Agency (PLA) and holds enforcement authority over team conduct, advertising standards, and broker supervision mandates. Unlike some states that have passed standalone team legislation, Indiana addresses team operations through existing supervision statutes and advertising regulations, meaning compliance is scattered across multiple code sections rather than consolidated in one place.

This structure creates confusion. Agents assume that if they are on a team, the team leader handles compliance. Brokers assume team leaders are supervising. The reality under Indiana law is that only the principal broker holds supervisory responsibility, regardless of how many layers a team adds to its internal hierarchy.

## How Indiana Defines a "Team" Under Current Regulations

**Indiana does not have a statutory definition of "team" in the same way states like Texas or Colorado do.** Instead, IREC treats teams as marketing arrangements operating under the umbrella of a licensed brokerage. The practical effect is that all team activities—advertising, client communication, transaction management—must conform to the same rules governing individual agents working under a broker.

Under IC 25-34.1-10-10, the principal broker is responsible for the "supervision of all licensed activities" performed by associate brokers and salespersons. This responsibility cannot be delegated to a team leader who is not a principal broker. A team leader who is an associate broker may assist with mentoring and oversight as a practical matter, but legal liability remains with the principal broker listed on [IREC Form 46241](https://www.in.gov/pla/professions/real-estate-commission/), the brokerage license application.

The distinction matters because team leaders often behave as de facto brokers—reviewing contracts, approving offers, training new members—without holding the license classification that authorizes those supervisory functions. When IREC investigates a complaint, they follow the chain of responsibility back to the principal broker, not the team leader.

## Advertising Rules That Catch Teams Off Guard

Indiana's advertising requirements under 876 IAC 6-1-3 require that all advertising by a licensee include the name of the brokerage under which they are licensed. For teams, this means the team name alone is never sufficient. Every advertisement—yard signs, social media profiles, business cards, email signatures, and website headers—must display the brokerage name in a clear and conspicuous manner.

**The "clear and conspicuous" standard is where most team violations occur.** A team name in 48-point font on a yard sign with the brokerage name in 8-point font at the bottom does not satisfy the rule. IREC has issued guidance that the brokerage name must be reasonably prominent relative to the team name. While no specific ratio is codified, enforcement actions have targeted cases where consumers could reasonably be confused about which licensed entity they were working with.

| Advertising Element | Required Content | Common Violation |
|---|---|---|
| Yard Signs | Brokerage name, team name, agent name | Brokerage name too small or absent |
| Social Media Profiles | Brokerage name in bio or display name | Team-branded pages with no brokerage reference |
| Business Cards | Brokerage name, license status | Only team name and personal contact info |
| Email Signatures | Brokerage name | Generic team signature block missing brokerage |
| Websites | Brokerage name on every page | Brokerage name only on "About" page |

## Unlicensed Assistants: The Bright Line That Gets Crossed

Every team eventually hires administrative help. Indiana law draws a firm line between tasks that require a license and those that do not. Under IC 25-34.1-3-2, any person who negotiates, offers to negotiate, lists, or offers to list real property for sale must hold a valid license. Unlicensed assistants may handle scheduling, data entry, marketing coordination, and transaction file organization—but the moment they discuss pricing, negotiate terms, or show property independently, the team has crossed into unlicensed practice of real estate.

The consequences are severe. Under IC 25-34.1-10-12, engaging in unlicensed practice is a Class A misdemeanor, punishable by up to one year in jail and fines up to $5,000. The principal broker also faces disciplinary action from IREC, which can include license suspension, probation, or revocation. Deals facilitated by unlicensed individuals may face validity challenges, creating title issues that surface months or years after closing.

**Many teams blur this line unintentionally.** A transaction coordinator who answers a client question about inspection deadlines. A marketing assistant who responds to a lead inquiry with property recommendations. An administrative staff member who schedules showings and then "happens to" open the door and discuss the property. Each of these scenarios potentially constitutes unlicensed activity. ## Common Mistakes Indiana Teams Make

Five recurring violations dominate IREC complaint files related to team operations. Any one of these can trigger investigation, and combinations frequently lead to formal disciplinary proceedings.

The first mistake is operating a team without a written team agreement filed with the brokerage. While Indiana statute does not mandate a specific "team agreement" form, IREC expects brokers to maintain documentation of how teams are structured, how compensation flows, and who performs what functions. Without this documentation, the broker cannot demonstrate adequate supervision during an audit.

The second is allowing team members to use personal email accounts or phone numbers in advertising without brokerage disclosure. IC 25-34.1 and 876 IAC 6-1-3 require brokerage identification in all advertising. A personal Gmail address in a listing advertisement with no brokerage reference is a violation waiting to happen.

Third, teams frequently fail to ensure that all members maintain current continuing education and active license status. When one team member lapses, transactions they touched during the lapsed period are potentially voidable. The principal broker bears responsibility for tracking this under their supervisory duties.

Fourth, teams create separate LLCs or business entities for receiving commission payments without proper disclosure. All compensation in Indiana must flow through the principal broker per IC 25-34.1-10-9.5. A team LLC that receives commission directly from a title company—bypassing the brokerage—violates this provision.

Fifth, team leaders conduct training sessions where they instruct new licensees on how to handle offers, counteroffers, and disclosures without documenting that this training falls under the principal broker's supervision plan. This creates an accountability gap that IREC views as a supervision failure.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

**Principal brokers in Indiana cannot treat team oversight as a set-it-and-forget-it function.** IREC's enforcement philosophy holds brokers accountable for creating and maintaining systems that prevent violations before they occur, not merely reacting to complaints after the fact.

The first audit priority is advertising compliance. Brokers should conduct quarterly reviews of every team member's social media profiles, websites, yard sign inventory, and printed materials. Any advertisement lacking the brokerage name in a clear and conspicuous position must be corrected immediately with documentation of the correction.

| Audit Category | Frequency | Key Documents |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising Compliance | Quarterly | Screenshots, sign photos, printed materials |
| License Status Verification | Monthly | PLA license lookup records |
| Transaction File Review | Per-transaction or monthly | Purchase agreements, disclosures (Form 46234, 48962) |
| Team Agreement Review | Annually | Written team structure, comp splits, duties |
| Unlicensed Activity Assessment | Quarterly | Job descriptions, task logs for assistants |

Second, brokers must verify that every team member's license is active and in good standing through the Indiana PLA verification system. A monthly automated check prevents the scenario where a lapsed licensee continues conducting business under the team's umbrella.

Third, transaction files must be reviewed for proper use of Indiana-specific forms. The Indiana Association of Realtors (IAR) Purchase Agreement (Form 46234), the Seller's Residential Real Estate Sales Disclosure (Form 46234-SD, required by IC 32-21-5), and the agency disclosure form (required under IC 25-34.1-10-9.5) must be present and properly executed in every file. Missing forms create both regulatory exposure and civil liability for the brokerage. Understanding [why checklists outperform memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) in transaction management is particularly relevant here—brokers managing multiple teams need systematic file review, not spot-checks based on intuition.

Fourth, brokers should maintain a supervision log documenting how they oversee team activities. This is not explicitly required by statute, but IREC investigators routinely ask for evidence of supervision during complaint proceedings. A broker who can produce dated records of file reviews, advertising audits, and training oversight is far better positioned than one who simply states they "trust their team leaders."

## Liability When Things Go Wrong

When an Indiana team violates supervision rules, liability flows upward to the principal broker—always. IREC's disciplinary process under 876 IAC 6-3 allows for sanctions including formal reprimand, probation, license suspension, and revocation. Monetary fines can reach $1,000 per violation under IC 25-1-11-12. For advertising violations specifically, IREC may require corrective advertising at the licensee's expense.

> IC 25-34.1-10-10: "A principal broker shall supervise the activities of all associate brokers and salespersons affiliated with the broker and shall be responsible for insuring that all real estate activities of the associate brokers and salespersons are conducted in accordance with this article."

Beyond regulatory penalties, civil liability exposes teams to lawsuits from consumers who relied on representations made by unlicensed team members or who were confused about which entity represented them. Indiana courts have held that brokerage supervision failures can support negligence claims, and errors and omissions insurance may deny coverage for acts performed by unlicensed or improperly supervised individuals.

Deal cancellation is also on the table. A buyer who discovers their "agent" was actually an unlicensed team assistant may have grounds to void the purchase agreement. Title insurance companies may refuse to insure a transaction where the selling agent's license was lapsed at the time of contract execution. These downstream consequences cost far more than the initial fine.
