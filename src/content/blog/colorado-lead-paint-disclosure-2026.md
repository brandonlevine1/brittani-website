## Understanding Colorado Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

Every residential transaction involving a home built before 1978 triggers a federal mandate that Colorado agents cannot sidestep. The Colorado lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain rooted in the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulations at 24 C.F.R. Part 35 and 40 C.F.R. Part 745. These federal rules preempt state law—meaning Colorado has no separate state-level lead paint disclosure statute, but enforcement still falls on agents and brokers operating under Colorado Real Estate Commission (CREC) oversight.

**Failure to comply does not just expose agents to federal penalties; it puts broker licenses at risk under CREC Rule E-35, which requires licensees to comply with all applicable federal and state laws as a condition of licensure.** The penalty structure is severe: HUD and the EPA can jointly impose fines up to $22,331 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2026), and private lawsuits can result in treble damages. Colorado courts have upheld buyer claims for rescission under C.R.S. § 38-35.7-101 et seq. when material disclosures—including lead paint—were omitted.

This is not a topic where "close enough" paperwork passes muster. The disclosure obligation attaches at the moment a purchase contract is executed, and it runs through closing.

## Which Properties Trigger the Requirement

The threshold is simple but often misapplied: any residential dwelling constructed before January 1, 1978. That includes single-family homes, condominiums, duplexes, and units within multi-family buildings. It does not include properties sold at foreclosure, zero-bedroom units (studios and lofts defined as zero-bedroom under HUD standards), housing designated exclusively for elderly persons, or housing designated for persons with disabilities unless a child under six resides or is expected to reside there.

Colorado's housing stock makes this rule particularly relevant. According to the U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey, approximately 29% of occupied housing units in the Denver metro area were built before 1978. In mountain communities like Leadville, Aspen's older core, and historic districts throughout Colorado Springs, that percentage climbs significantly.

Agents listing in neighborhoods like Denver's Park Hill, Wash Park, or Boulder's Mapleton Hill should treat lead paint disclosure as a default step in their listing workflow rather than an exception.

## The Exact Forms Colorado Agents Must Use

Colorado agents working under the Colorado Association of Realtors (CAR) form library will use specific documents to satisfy federal requirements. The primary form is the **Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (Seller's Property) — CAR Form LBP40-6-21** or its most current version. This form captures the seller's known information about lead-based paint, records about lead-based paint testing, and the buyer's acknowledgment of receiving the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home."

| Form/Document | Purpose | Who Signs |
|---|---|---|
| CAR Form LBP40 (Lead-Based Paint Disclosure) | Seller disclosure and buyer acknowledgment | Seller, buyer, and all agents |
| EPA Pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" | Required informational document for buyer | Buyer acknowledges receipt |
| Inspection contingency language (within CBS or separate addendum) | Documents buyer's 10-day inspection opportunity | Buyer (waiver or election) |
| Broker file retention record | Proves compliance for 3+ years per federal rules | Broker/office manager |

The federal regulation at 40 C.F.R. § 745.113 requires that sellers and agents retain a copy of the completed disclosure for no fewer than three years from the date of sale. CREC Rule E-20, however, requires brokers to maintain transaction files for four years—so the stricter Colorado standard controls for practical purposes.

Agents not using CAR forms (for example, those using proprietary brokerage forms or the DORA-approved Commission-promulgated contracts) must verify that their lead paint disclosure form contains every element required by 40 C.F.R. § 745.113(b)(1)-(4). Missing any one element—particularly the agent's certification that the agent informed the seller of their obligations—creates a compliance gap.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosure

Five errors surface repeatedly in CREC disciplinary actions and federal enforcement cases. Each one is preventable with proper systems.

**First, agents forget to sign the disclosure form themselves.** The federal rule explicitly requires that the agent representing the seller certify they informed the seller of their lead paint obligations. A form signed only by the seller and buyer is incomplete. Both the listing agent and buyer's agent must sign.

Second, agents deliver the EPA pamphlet but fail to document delivery. Handing a buyer the pamphlet at a showing or emailing a PDF without obtaining a dated signature of receipt leaves no audit trail. If the buyer later claims they never received it, the burden of proof falls on the agent.

Third, listing agents omit the disclosure entirely for estate sales or trust sales, incorrectly assuming that a personal representative or trustee who never lived in the property has no disclosure obligation. The regulation requires disclosure of known information—if the estate has records of lead testing or abatement, those must be shared regardless of whether the signer personally lived there.

Fourth, agents allow buyers to waive the 10-day inspection period verbally rather than in writing. The waiver must be documented in the contract or an addendum. A verbal agreement to skip the lead inspection has zero legal weight and leaves the transaction exposed. Similar deadline management issues plague agents across [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) when inspection timelines overlap.

Fifth, agents in lease-purchase or rent-to-own transactions forget that the disclosure requirement applies to leases as well as sales for pre-1978 housing. Colorado's rental market, particularly in older college-town housing stock in Boulder and Fort Collins, triggers this obligation frequently.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

CREC places supervisory responsibility squarely on employing brokers. Under Rule E-1 and E-20, a broker who fails to establish office policies ensuring legal compliance can face independent disciplinary action even when the individual agent made the error.

**Brokers should implement a file review checkpoint that verifies four items before any pre-1978 transaction reaches closing.** Those four items are: (1) the completed and fully-executed LBP disclosure form with all required signatures, (2) documented proof of EPA pamphlet delivery with the buyer's dated acknowledgment, (3) written evidence that the buyer either exercised or waived the 10-day inspection opportunity, and (4) confirmation that the listing agent signed the certification of seller notification.

| Audit Item | Where to Find It | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| All-party signatures on LBP form | CAR Form LBP40 in transaction file | Missing agent signature line |
| EPA pamphlet receipt | Buyer acknowledgment section of LBP40 | No date next to buyer signature |
| Inspection period election | Contract or addendum | Verbal waiver noted in MLS agent remarks only |
| Seller knowledge disclosure | Section A of LBP40 | "Unknown" checked without supporting explanation |
| Retention period compliance | Broker file management system | Files purged before 4-year CREC retention window |

Brokers running [transaction coordination workflows](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) should build lead paint verification into the listing-launch checklist rather than the closing checklist. By the time a transaction approaches closing, a missing disclosure creates far more disruption—including potential deal cancellation under the buyer's right to rescind if proper notice was never provided.

The Colorado Division of Real Estate has explicitly stated in past enforcement bulletins that "I didn't know my agent skipped it" is not a defense available to brokers. Supervisory liability attaches when the broker's systems fail to catch a compliance gap that reasonable oversight would have identified.

## Liability Exposure When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance operate on three levels: federal, state, and civil.

At the federal level, the [EPA's Lead Paint Program](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-laws-and-regulations) authorizes penalties up to $22,331 per violation. Both the seller and the listing agent can be held independently liable. The buyer's agent can also face penalties if they knew or should have known the disclosure was incomplete and failed to act.

At the state level, CREC treats lead paint disclosure failures as a violation of C.R.S. § 12-10-217(1)(a), which prohibits licensees from engaging in conduct that would violate applicable laws. Sanctions range from letters of admonition to license suspension or revocation. The Commission's disciplinary records show multiple cases in the past five years where agents received formal discipline specifically for disclosure failures in pre-1978 property sales.

In civil litigation, buyers who discover undisclosed lead hazards after closing can pursue treble damages under 42 U.S.C. § 4852d(b)(3). Colorado courts can also award attorney's fees to prevailing plaintiffs. A $15,000 remediation claim can balloon into a $45,000 judgment plus $20,000 in legal fees. Agents who understand [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) recognize that disclosure failures represent one of the highest-cost post-closing liability risks in the industry.

## Practical Workflow for Staying Compliant

Building compliance into your standard operating procedure eliminates the need to remember it transaction by transaction. The moment a listing agreement is signed for any pre-1978 property, three actions should fire immediately: pull the LBP disclosure form, provide the seller with the EPA pamphlet, and schedule the disclosure conversation before the property hits MLS.

**On the buyer side, the cooperating agent must confirm EPA pamphlet delivery and document the 10-day inspection election before the contract's inspection objection deadline.** In Colorado's standard Contract to Buy and Sell (CBS), the lead paint inspection period runs concurrently with—but is legally separate from—the general inspection period. Agents who conflate the two create confusion when a buyer wants to waive general inspection but retain their lead inspection right.
