# Maryland Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026: What Every Agent Must Get Right

Maryland property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 rules remain one of the most frequently mishandled compliance areas for residential agents statewide. A single missed form or incorrectly answered question can unwind a deal days before settlement—or worse, invite litigation months after closing. This guide breaks down the specific statutes, form numbers, penalties, and operational safeguards agents and brokers need to enforce right now.

## The Statutory Framework Governing Maryland Disclosures

Maryland's disclosure obligations stem primarily from Maryland Real Property Article, Title 10, Subtitle 7 (Sections 10-702 through 10-709). This statute mandates that sellers of improved residential real property containing one to four dwelling units deliver a written property condition disclosure statement to the buyer. The obligation attaches to the seller, but listing agents carry a duty to advise clients and ensure the form is part of the transaction file.

**Environmental disclosures operate under a separate but overlapping legal regime.** The Maryland Environment Article, particularly Title 6 (Lead Paint) and Title 7 (Hazardous Waste), imposes additional obligations. Federal requirements under 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d (the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992) also apply to all pre-1978 housing sales across the state.

The [Maryland Real Estate Commission (MREC)](https://www.dllr.state.md.us/license/mrec/), housed within the Department of Labor, enforces licensing violations connected to disclosure failures. Agents who ignore these requirements risk not just civil suits but disciplinary action against their licenses.

## Key Forms and When to Deploy Them

Maryland residential agents must be familiar with at least three core disclosure documents in every qualifying transaction.

The first is the **Maryland Residential Property Condition Disclosure Statement**, codified under Section 10-702. While Maryland does not assign a single state-issued form number (unlike some states), the Maryland Association of Realtors (MAR) publishes a standardized version that the vast majority of brokerages adopt. This form includes sections on structural systems, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, water and sewer, environmental hazards, and known neighborhood nuisances.

The second form addresses lead-based paint. For properties built before 1978, agents must deliver the federal EPA disclosure form (sometimes referenced as the "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form) along with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." Maryland's Environment Article Section 6-819 further requires owners of affected properties to register with the Maryland Department of the Environment (MDE) if the property is rental, though this registration obligation has disclosure implications even in sales contexts.

The third document involves the **Notice Regarding Mold**, which MAR includes as a separate advisory. While Maryland does not have a standalone mold statute requiring seller disclosure, known mold presence must be disclosed under the general property condition statement, and many brokerages layer this additional advisory for liability protection.

| Document | Legal Basis | Timing | Applies To |
|----------|------------|--------|-----------|
| Residential Property Condition Disclosure | MD Real Property Art. 10-702 | Before or at contract signing | All 1-4 unit improved residential sales (with exemptions) |
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure + EPA Pamphlet | 42 U.S.C. 4852d; MD Env. Art. 6-8 | Before contract ratification | Pre-1978 residential properties |
| Mold Advisory | Brokerage policy / MAR form | At or before contract | All residential sales (best practice) |
| Notice of Proximity to Military Installation | MD Real Property Art. 10-702(b)(2) | With disclosure statement | Properties near qualifying bases |

## Exemptions That Trip Up Even Experienced Agents

Not every sale triggers the full disclosure requirement under Section 10-702. The statute exempts sales by personal representatives of estates, sales pursuant to court order, transfers from government entities, sales of new construction not previously occupied, transfers between co-owners, and certain foreclosure sales. However, exemption from the property condition disclosure does **not** exempt the transaction from the federal lead paint disclosure—these are independent obligations with independent penalties.

Agents frequently conflate these exemptions. An estate sale may skip the Maryland property condition form, but if the house was built in 1955, the personal representative still must provide the lead paint disclosure and pamphlet. Failing to recognize this distinction is one of the most common compliance errors in the state, and it has cost agents and brokerages real money in mediation settlements.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**The consequences of disclosure failures in Maryland range from deal rescission to personal liability and license discipline.** Under Section 10-702, if a seller fails to deliver the property condition disclosure before contract execution, the buyer may rescind the contract within five days after receiving the late disclosure—or at any point before settlement if it is never delivered.

For lead paint violations, federal penalties under Section 1018 of Title X can include fines up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2026). The EPA and HUD enforce these penalties jointly, and Maryland agents are not shielded by the fact that the seller is the primary obligor. Both the seller's agent and the buyer's agent have affirmative duties to ensure compliance.

At the state level, the MREC can pursue disciplinary action under Business Occupations and Professions Article Section 17-322 for incompetence, dishonesty, or failure to deliver required documents. Sanctions include reprimand, probation, fine, suspension, or revocation of license. Civil courts may also award actual damages plus attorneys' fees to buyers who can prove material non-disclosure or fraudulent concealment, with the listing agent named as a co-defendant.

The relationship between [disclosure compliance and where deals typically break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) is well documented—missing forms account for a disproportionate share of settlement delays in Maryland.

## Common Mistakes Maryland Agents Make

Five recurring errors surface in MREC complaints and brokerage E&O claims related to property condition and environmental disclosures.

**First, agents allow sellers to answer "unknown" on items the agent personally knows to be deficient.** Maryland case law, including the landmark Golt v. Phillips decision, establishes that agents who have actual knowledge of material defects cannot hide behind the seller's "unknown" response. If you walked the property and saw the water stain on the basement wall, your duty to disclose is independent of what the seller writes on the form.

Second, agents deliver the disclosure after contract ratification rather than before or simultaneously. Section 10-702 specifies delivery timing—handing the form at the home inspection or after contract acceptance creates a rescission window the buyer can exploit. This timing error is especially common in multiple-offer situations where agents rush to get signatures.

Third, agents confuse the property condition disclosure with the "Disclaimer Statement" option. Maryland law does allow sellers to elect a disclaimer (a refusal to make representations about condition) under Section 10-702(b)(4), but choosing this path does not eliminate the duty to disclose known material defects. Agents who tell sellers "just disclaim everything" may be providing unlicensed legal advice and creating liability simultaneously.

Fourth, buyer's agents fail to confirm receipt and provide copies to their clients. The statute places delivery obligations on the seller and seller's agent, but buyer's agents who know the form was never delivered and proceed to settlement without raising the issue may face contributory negligence arguments.

Fifth, agents neglect the 10-day lead paint inspection election period. Federal law gives buyers 10 days (or a mutually agreed period) to conduct a lead paint inspection. Agents who draft contracts waiving this right without informed buyer consent create actionable claims.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Maryland's Business Occupations and Professions Article Section 17-532. A broker who fails to implement reasonable policies ensuring disclosure compliance may face direct discipline, regardless of whether they personally handled the transaction.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|-----------|--------------|----------|
| Disclosure timing | Date on form vs. contract ratification date | Disclosure dated after contract |
| Lead paint form | Signed by all parties, pamphlet receipt confirmed | Missing signatures or no pamphlet acknowledgment |
| Seller "unknown" answers | Cross-reference with listing photos and agent notes | Visible defects contradicted by "unknown" responses |
| Exemption claims | Verify statutory basis for any skipped disclosure | Estate sale missing lead paint form |
| File completeness | All forms present in transaction file before settlement | Forms uploaded post-closing |

Brokers should institute a pre-settlement file review no later than seven days before closing. This review should specifically verify that the property condition disclosure is signed and dated, the lead paint forms (if applicable) include all required signatures and the pamphlet acknowledgment, and any environmental advisories match brokerage policy.

Brokerages that [train new agents on timeline management](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) during onboarding see measurably fewer disclosure violations. Building disclosure checkpoints into the first 48 hours after listing activation—not after contract—prevents most timing errors.

## Maryland Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026: The Environmental Layer

Beyond lead paint, Maryland's environmental disclosure obligations cover several additional hazard categories. The property condition statement specifically asks about underground storage tanks (USTs), contaminated soil or groundwater, proximity to Superfund or Maryland Voluntary Cleanup Program (VCP) sites, and known asbestos-containing materials.

Properties in the Chesapeake Bay Critical Area carry additional regulatory considerations under the Critical Area Act (Natural Resources Article, Title 8, Subtitle 18). While this does not create a separate disclosure form, agents marketing waterfront or near-waterfront properties should advise sellers to disclose any known Critical Area restrictions, buffer zone violations, or pending MDE enforcement actions.

**Radon disclosure is another growing area of liability.** Maryland does not mandate a standalone radon disclosure form, but the property condition statement asks whether radon testing has been performed and whether mitigation systems are present. Agents who advise sellers to skip answering these questions—or who discourage buyers from testing—expose themselves to claims of negligent misrepresentation, particularly as awareness of radon risks in Western Maryland and parts of the Piedmont region increases.

For agents managing multiple transactions simultaneously, keeping environmental disclosures aligned with property-specific risk factors requires structured file management. If you are [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), automated compliance reminders tied to property age and location reduce the risk of a pre-1978 home slipping through without lead paint documentation.
