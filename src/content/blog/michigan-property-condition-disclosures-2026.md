## Why Michigan Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026 Demand Your Attention

Michigan's disclosure framework is not optional, and the consequences for getting it wrong go beyond embarrassment at closing. The Seller Disclosure Act (MCL 565.951–565.966) governs what sellers must reveal, when they must reveal it, and what agents must facilitate. Understanding Michigan property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 requirements is the difference between a clean file and a post-closing lawsuit that names you personally.

The Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) oversees agent licensing, and violations of disclosure obligations can trigger license complaints, civil liability, and deal cancellation. If you have not reviewed your disclosure workflow since the statute's last interpretive update, now is the time to do so.

## The Seller Disclosure Act: Statutory Foundation

Michigan's Seller Disclosure Act requires transferors of residential property (one to four units) to deliver a written disclosure statement to prospective buyers before a purchase agreement is signed—or, if delivered later, the buyer gains a statutory rescission window. The form must address the physical condition of the property, known material defects, and specific environmental hazards.

**The statute explicitly lists categories the seller must address, including structural systems, mechanical systems, water and sewer, environmental conditions such as underground storage tanks, and known contamination.** This is not a general "tell us what you know" invitation; it is a detailed, category-by-category obligation. Sellers who skip categories or mark "unknown" without a good-faith basis expose themselves—and potentially their agents—to fraud claims.

The Michigan Association of Realtors (MAR) publishes a Seller's Disclosure Statement form that tracks the statutory requirements. Most brokerages use this form or a substantially similar version approved by their legal counsel.

## Key Forms and When to Use Them

Michigan agents work with several disclosure-related documents depending on the transaction type. The following table clarifies which forms apply and their triggering conditions.

| Form / Document | Source | When Required | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seller's Disclosure Statement | MAR / Brokerage | All residential transfers (1-4 units) unless exempt | MCL 565.954 |
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | Federal (EPA) | Pre-1978 residential properties | 42 USC 4852d; 24 CFR 35.88 |
| Radon Disclosure | Included in Seller's Disclosure | All covered transactions | MCL 565.954(3) |
| Property Transfer Affidavit (L-4260) | Michigan Dept. of Treasury | Within 45 days of transfer | MCL 211.27a(10) |
| Environmental Contamination Notice | Site-specific | Properties with known contamination under Part 201 | MCL 324.20116 |

The Property Transfer Affidavit (Form L-4260) is not technically a condition disclosure, but agents frequently confuse it with one. It serves a tax uncapping function and must be filed with the local assessor. Failing to file does not void a sale, but it can create tax headaches for the buyer that trace back to your transaction.

## Environmental Disclosures: Beyond the Basics

Michigan's environmental disclosure requirements go further than most agents realize. Part 201 of the Natural Resources and Environmental Protection Act (MCL 324.20101 et seq.) imposes obligations on property owners who know or have reason to know of contamination. When a seller is aware of environmental conditions—underground storage tanks, contaminated groundwater, proximity to a known cleanup site—that information must appear in the disclosure statement.

**Agents who represent properties near Michigan's many former industrial sites, Superfund locations, or agricultural land with historical pesticide use should flag environmental questions early in the listing process.** The DEQ (now EGLE—Environment, Great Lakes, and Energy) maintains a public database of contaminated sites. A quick search of EGLE's Environmental Mapper before listing can reveal whether additional disclosures are warranted.

Radon is specifically called out in the Seller Disclosure Statement. Michigan does not mandate radon testing, but the seller must disclose known radon test results. If your seller had a test done and buried the report in a drawer, they cannot legally ignore it on the form.

For properties built before 1978, the [federal lead-based paint disclosure rules](/blog/texas-lead-paint-disclosure-2026) mirror what Michigan agents already know from other states—but the penalties remain steep. Failure to provide the EPA pamphlet, disclose known lead paint, or offer a 10-day testing period can result in treble damages under federal law.

## Timing, Delivery, and the Rescission Window

Timing is where deals unravel. The statute contemplates two scenarios for delivery of the Seller's Disclosure Statement.

If the disclosure is delivered before the buyer signs the purchase agreement, no rescission period applies—the buyer is deemed to have accepted the property's condition by signing. If the disclosure arrives after execution, the buyer gets 72 hours from personal delivery (or 120 hours from deposit in the mail) to rescind without penalty under MCL 565.960.

| Delivery Method | Rescission Period | Starts When |
|---|---|---|
| Personal delivery (before signing) | None | N/A |
| Personal delivery (after signing) | 72 hours | Receipt by buyer |
| Mail (after signing) | 120 hours | Deposit in mail |
| Not delivered at all | Buyer may void at any time | Until closing or delivery |

Agents should document the exact method and time of delivery. A disclosure handed to a buyer's agent at a showing is not the same as one formally transmitted through the MLS document system with a timestamp. If litigation arises, you want a clear record.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make

Five errors surface repeatedly in Michigan disclosure disputes, and each one is avoidable with basic process discipline.

**First, agents coach sellers on how to answer disclosure questions.** Telling a seller to mark "unknown" on a question they clearly know the answer to is not protecting your client—it is setting up a fraud claim. The agent's role is to explain what the form asks, not to dictate answers.

Second, agents fail to deliver the disclosure before the purchase agreement is signed and then forget to track the rescission window. When the buyer rescinds at hour 71 and the listing agent claims they never received notice, the deal collapses into a he-said-she-said dispute that could have been avoided entirely.

Third, listing agents treat the disclosure as a one-time event and fail to update it when new information surfaces. If the basement floods between listing and closing, the seller has an affirmative duty to amend the disclosure under MCL 565.955. The agent who knows about the flood and says nothing shares liability.

Fourth, agents skip the lead paint disclosure on older homes because "we always do it later" or assume the title company handles it. No one else is responsible for ensuring compliance before the buyer signs. The listing agent and the seller are both on the hook under [federal lead paint rules](/blog/virginia-lead-paint-disclosure-2026).

Fifth, agents confuse exempt transactions with transactions that still require good-faith conduct. A bank-owned foreclosure is exempt from the Seller Disclosure Act under MCL 565.957, but the selling agent still cannot actively conceal known defects. Exemption from the form is not exemption from common-law fraud.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Michigan's Occupational Code (MCL 339.2512e). If an agent in your office consistently fails to secure timely disclosures, the broker's license is at risk—not just the agent's.

**A quarterly audit of closed files should verify three things: the disclosure was signed before the purchase agreement (or the rescission timeline was properly tracked), the lead paint disclosure is present for pre-1978 homes, and any amendments are dated and initialed.** These are not suggestions; they are the minimum defensible standard if LARA opens an investigation.

Brokers should also maintain a written policy on disclosure coaching. Agents need explicit guidance that they may explain the form but may not suggest answers, minimize defects, or advise sellers to withhold information. A single email from an agent saying "just mark unknown on the foundation question" can become Exhibit A in a buyer's lawsuit.

Transaction management systems help here. When you are [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), it is easy for a disclosure deadline to slip through the cracks. Automated reminders tied to contract dates reduce the risk of a missed rescission window turning into a license complaint.

## Liability Exposure: What Actually Happens When You Fail

The consequences of non-compliance are not hypothetical. Under MCL 565.961, a buyer who suffers damages from a seller's failure to disclose a known defect can recover actual damages, court costs, and attorney fees. If the agent participated in the concealment—actively or through willful ignorance—they can be named as a co-defendant.

LARA can impose administrative penalties including fines, mandatory education, and license suspension or revocation. Michigan courts have also held that fraudulent concealment can void the entire purchase agreement, leaving the seller liable for the buyer's inspection costs, financing fees, and moving expenses.

Beyond formal penalties, the reputational cost is real. A single disclosure lawsuit generates a public record that follows you through every future listing presentation. Buyers' agents in your market will flag your name, and referral networks dry up fast.
