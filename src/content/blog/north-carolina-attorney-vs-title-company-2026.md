## Why the Question of North Carolina Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026 Still Confuses Agents

North Carolina is one of a handful of states where you cannot simply hand a transaction to a title company and walk away. The question of "North Carolina attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026" comes up every single year because agents who relocate from non-attorney states, or who close their first deal after licensure, assume the process works the same everywhere. It does not.

The state's requirement is rooted in statute and reinforced by decades of North Carolina State Bar Advisory Opinions. Misunderstanding it does not just create awkward moments at the closing table — it exposes agents to disciplinary complaints, deal cancellations, and malpractice liability. This post breaks down exactly what the law says, how it affects your day-to-day workflow, and where agents routinely get it wrong.

## The Legal Foundation: N.C.G.S. 84-2.1 and State Bar Opinion 2002 FEO 9

**North Carolina General Statute 84-2.1 defines the "practice of law" broadly enough to encompass virtually every act performed at a residential real estate closing.** Preparing deeds, drafting deeds of trust, examining title, certifying title opinions, and disbursing closing funds all fall within that definition. The statute does not carve out an exception for title companies or lay closers acting independently.

The North Carolina State Bar reinforced this position in [2002 Formal Ethics Opinion 9](https://www.ncbar.gov/for-lawyers/ethics/adopted-opinions/2002-formal-ethics-opinion-9/) and subsequent opinions. Those rulings make clear that a licensed North Carolina attorney must supervise real estate closings, even when a title insurance company is involved in the transaction. A title company can underwrite the policy and conduct the title search, but the closing act itself requires attorney involvement.

This is not a technicality that enforcement agencies ignore. The North Carolina State Bar has filed unauthorized-practice-of-law actions against out-of-state entities attempting to conduct closings without local attorney supervision.

## Attorney vs. Title Company: What Each Party Actually Does

Agents often conflate the roles because in states like Texas or California, a title company or escrow officer handles everything. North Carolina draws a sharp jurisdictional line.

| Function | Attorney (Required) | Title Company (Optional) |
|----------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Prepare deed and deed of trust | Yes | No |
| Certify title opinion | Yes | No |
| Conduct closing / preside over signing | Yes | No |
| Disburse funds from trust account | Yes | No |
| Issue title insurance policy | No (orders from underwriter) | Yes |
| Perform title search | May delegate but must supervise | Yes (under attorney direction) |
| Record documents at Register of Deeds | Yes | No |

The title company's role in North Carolina is essentially that of an underwriter and search vendor. It backstops the transaction with insurance but does not run the closing. Agents who tell a buyer "just pick a title company and they'll handle it" are giving advice that could result in a failed closing or worse.

## What the North Carolina Real Estate Commission Expects From Agents

The North Carolina Real Estate Commission (NCREC) governs agent conduct under 21 NCAC 58A. Rule 58A .0110 requires that licensees avoid engaging in, or facilitating, the unauthorized practice of law. The Commission has consistently held that agents who direct clients away from attorney involvement — or who attempt to prepare legal documents themselves — violate this rule.

**On the forms side, agents should understand how NCAR Standard Form 2-T (Offer to Purchase and Contract) handles this issue.** Paragraph 11 of Form 2-T specifically addresses the closing and assigns responsibility. The buyer selects the closing attorney, and the settlement is conducted under that attorney's supervision. Agents who use Form 2-T are already working within a framework that assumes attorney involvement.

Form 2-T is not the only relevant document. Agents should also be familiar with Form 2-G (Guidelines for Completing Offer to Purchase) and the Due Diligence Request and Agreement, all published by the North Carolina Association of Realtors and the NC Bar Association jointly. These forms presuppose — and in some cases explicitly require — attorney participation in the transaction lifecycle.

If an agent steers a client toward a closing that lacks attorney supervision, the NCREC can impose sanctions including license suspension, mandatory continuing education, and fines. The Commission's investigative staff actively reviews complaints from consumers and attorneys.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in North Carolina Closings

Mistake one: recommending an out-of-state title company that promises to "handle everything." Agents who have worked in Florida or Georgia sometimes default to familiar national title companies. Those companies can issue the insurance, but they cannot conduct the North Carolina closing without engaging a local attorney. When an agent sets this expectation with a buyer, the deal stalls two weeks before closing when everyone realizes no attorney has been retained.

Mistake two: preparing or modifying legal documents. Some agents draft addenda that cross into legal territory — modifying title provisions, creating custom deed language, or altering trust account disbursement terms. Under 21 NCAC 58A .0111, agents may fill in blanks on preprinted NCREC-approved forms, but they may not draft legal instruments. Crossing that line is both an unauthorized practice of law violation and a license rule violation.

**Mistake three: failing to confirm attorney engagement early in the transaction timeline.** The due diligence period in North Carolina (typically negotiated as 14-30 days) runs fast. If the buyer has not selected a closing attorney by the midpoint of due diligence, title work may not be completed before the due diligence deadline expires. Agents who wait until week three to ask "who's your attorney?" are creating unnecessary risk that a [blown closing becomes expensive for everyone](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Mistake four: confusing the buyer's attorney with the lender's attorney. In some transactions, the lender retains separate counsel for document review. That lender's attorney does not represent the buyer and does not satisfy the closing attorney requirement. Agents must ensure the buyer has independent counsel handling the settlement.

Mistake five: assuming the listing agent has no responsibility here. Listing agents who notice the buyer has not engaged a closing attorney have a professional obligation to flag the issue. Sitting silently while the transaction approaches a preventable failure is not a neutral act — it is a [missed deadline](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) the listing agent could have helped prevent.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers-in-charge carry supervisory liability under NCREC rules. If an agent under your brokerage facilitates an unlawful closing, the Commission can and does hold the BIC accountable. Here is what a broker should be actively reviewing.

First, audit every transaction file for evidence of closing attorney engagement. The attorney's name, contact information, and engagement confirmation should appear in the file no later than the midpoint of the due diligence period. If your agents are not documenting this, you have a compliance gap.

Second, verify that agents are using current NCAR/NC Bar approved forms. The joint forms committee updates forms periodically, and outdated versions can create liability. Form 2-T was revised in 2024, and agents still using the 2021 version may be missing updated language on closing procedures. A quick audit of your transaction management platform should reveal version discrepancies.

**Third, establish a written brokerage policy that prohibits agents from recommending or coordinating closings without attorney involvement.** This policy should be part of your office procedures manual and acknowledged in writing by every licensee. If a complaint arises, having that policy on file demonstrates supervisory diligence to the NCREC.

Fourth, review how your agents communicate with out-of-state buyers. Relocation buyers from non-attorney states are the most likely to push back on the requirement. Your agents need scripted language — approved by your broker's legal counsel — explaining the requirement without practicing law themselves. A [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) that includes attorney engagement as a required milestone prevents these issues from slipping through.

## Liability Exposure When the Requirement Is Ignored

The consequences are not hypothetical. An agent who facilitates a closing without proper attorney involvement faces multiple simultaneous risks.

| Risk Category | Potential Consequence | Enforcing Body |
|--------------|----------------------|----------------|
| License discipline | Suspension, revocation, or mandatory CE | NCREC |
| Unauthorized practice of law | Injunction, civil penalties | NC State Bar |
| Civil liability | Buyer/seller lawsuit for damages | Superior Court |
| Title defect | Policy voided, buyer uninsured | Title underwriter |
| Brokerage sanction | BIC reprimand, office audit | NCREC |

A voided title insurance policy is perhaps the most devastating practical outcome. If the closing was not conducted under proper attorney supervision, the title underwriter may deny coverage on a future claim. The buyer then has an uninsured title defect and a very motivated plaintiff's attorney looking at the agent who set up the closing.

## How the 2026 Regulatory Environment Reinforces These Requirements

Nothing in the 2025-2026 legislative session has softened North Carolina's attorney requirement. If anything, the trend is toward greater enforcement. The NC State Bar's unauthorized practice of law committee has increased its activity in recent years, particularly regarding out-of-state digital closing platforms that attempt to operate without local counsel.

The NCREC's 2025-2026 continuing education curriculum includes a module on proper closing procedures, signaling that the Commission views ongoing agent confusion as a compliance priority. Agents who completed their CE before July 2025 may not have received this updated material and should review the Commission's published guidance independently.

Remote online notarization (RON) has added a layer of complexity but has not changed the fundamental requirement. Even when a closing is conducted via RON under N.C.G.S. 10B-25, an attorney must still supervise the transaction. The notarization is a separate act from the legal supervision of the closing itself.
