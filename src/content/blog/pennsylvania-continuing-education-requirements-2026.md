## Pennsylvania Continuing Education Requirements 2026: The Rules Have Not Changed, but Your Deadline Is Approaching

The Pennsylvania continuing education requirements 2026 cycle catches more agents off guard than any disclosure deadline on their calendar. Every two years, the Pennsylvania State Real Estate Commission (SREC) expects licensed salespersons and brokers to complete 14 hours of approved continuing education before their renewal date. Miss that window, and you are not just facing an inconvenience — you are facing a lapsed license and potential disciplinary action under the Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. Section 455.

The current biennial renewal period for most Pennsylvania licensees runs through May 31, 2026. That means if you have not mapped out your CE coursework yet, the clock is already ticking. This post breaks down exactly what the state demands, what brokers must verify, and where agents routinely stumble.

## What Pennsylvania Actually Requires: Hours, Topics, and Approved Providers

Pennsylvania's CE structure is straightforward on paper but dense enough to trip up agents who wait until the last month. The SREC mandates 14 total hours, split into specific categories.

**The mandatory component is a 3.5-hour module covering updates to RELRA and Commission regulations.** This module cannot be substituted, waived, or replaced by elective hours. It must be taken from a provider approved by the SREC, and the content changes each renewal cycle to reflect legislative and regulatory updates.

The remaining 10.5 hours are elective, but "elective" does not mean "anything goes." Courses must be pre-approved by the Commission, and they must fall within real estate practice areas such as fair housing, ethics, agency law, property management, or transactional skills. The SREC publishes a roster of [approved education providers on its website](https://www.dos.pa.gov/ProfessionalLicensing/BoardsCommissions/RealEstateCommission/Pages/default.aspx).

| Requirement | Hours | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| RELRA Update Module | 3.5 | Mandatory, non-substitutable |
| Elective CE Courses | 10.5 | Must be SREC-approved topics |
| Total Required | 14.0 | Due before biennial renewal date |
| Renewal Deadline (current cycle) | May 31, 2026 | Online renewal via PALS system |

Agents renew through the Pennsylvania Licensing System (PALS), and course completion data is reported directly by approved providers. However, the system is not instantaneous — providers have up to 30 days to report completions, which is another reason to avoid last-minute cramming.

## What Happens When You Fail to Comply

The SREC does not issue gentle reminders after your license lapses. Under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.382, a licensee who fails to complete CE by the renewal deadline cannot engage in any licensed real estate activity. This is not a gray area. Listing a property, showing a home, negotiating an offer, or collecting a commission on a lapsed license constitutes unlicensed practice.

**Penalties escalate quickly.** The Commission can impose civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation under Section 604 of RELRA. If a transaction closes while your license is lapsed, the Commission may void your right to the commission, and you may face disciplinary proceedings that go on your permanent record with the Bureau of Professional and Occupational Affairs.

Beyond fines, there is deal-level fallout. A buyer or seller who discovers their agent was operating without a valid license has grounds to challenge the enforceability of the agreement of sale. Title companies routinely verify license status during the closing process. If your license shows as inactive in the SREC database, expect the deal to stall — or worse, unwind entirely. For more on how these failures cascade, read about the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Reinstatement after lapse requires completing all outstanding CE, paying the renewal fee, and paying an additional late fee. If the license has been lapsed for more than five years, you must retake the state licensing examination.

## Common Mistakes Pennsylvania Agents Make With CE

After speaking with dozens of brokers across the state, a pattern emerges. These are not edge cases — they are the five most frequent errors agents make heading into a renewal cycle.

**First, agents assume all real estate courses count toward CE.** They attend a lender's lunch-and-learn or a title company's seminar and believe those hours apply. Unless the course was delivered by a Commission-approved provider and carries an SREC-approved course number, it does not count. Period.

Second, agents complete coursework from an out-of-state provider that lacks Pennsylvania approval. Online platforms often sell courses advertised as "multi-state" but only carry approval in certain jurisdictions. Always confirm the provider appears in the SREC's approved directory before paying.

Third, agents wait until the final two weeks before the deadline and discover their preferred courses are full or that provider reporting delays push their completion past the cutoff. The 30-day reporting window means a course finished on May 28 might not appear in PALS until late June — well past the May 31 renewal deadline.

Fourth, licensees forget the RELRA module is a distinct requirement. Completing 14 hours of electives does not satisfy the mandate. The system will reject a renewal application that does not show the mandatory 3.5-hour module as complete.

Fifth, agents who hold dual licenses (for example, a salesperson license and a cemetery broker license) assume one CE submission covers both. Each license category has its own CE and renewal requirements under different chapters of 49 Pa. Code.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

The broker of record carries supervisory liability under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.242. That section explicitly states the broker must exercise reasonable supervision over all licensees operating under the brokerage. CE compliance is part of that mandate.

**If an agent in your office is practicing on a lapsed license, you share in the regulatory exposure.** The Commission has historically cited brokers who failed to verify that their agents held active licenses at the time of transaction activity. Form REC-1, the individual license renewal application, is ultimately the agent's responsibility — but the broker's obligation to confirm active status is well-established in Commission enforcement actions.

Brokers should build a verification cadence into their operations. Pull each agent's license status from the SREC's online verification tool at least quarterly, and set a hard internal deadline 60 days before the state renewal date. Agents who have not completed at least half their hours by that checkpoint need direct intervention.

| Broker Audit Action | Timing | Source/Tool |
|---|---|---|
| Verify agent license status | Quarterly | SREC online license verification |
| Confirm RELRA module completion | 90 days before renewal | Agent self-report + PALS |
| Internal CE completion deadline | 60 days before state deadline | Brokerage policy |
| Final license status pull | Day of renewal deadline | SREC verification tool |
| Document audit trail | Ongoing | Brokerage compliance file |

This is not optional compliance hygiene — it is risk management. A single lapsed agent closing a deal can expose the brokerage to commission disgorgement, E&O claims, and Commission sanctions against the broker personally. ## Pennsylvania Continuing Education Requirements 2026: Deadlines and the Renewal Process

The renewal process itself runs through PALS, Pennsylvania's online licensing portal. Agents receive renewal notices approximately 60 days before their expiration date, but the system does not prevent you from renewing earlier once your CE is complete.

The renewal fee for a salesperson license is currently $96 for the biennial period. Broker renewals carry the same fee. Late renewals add a penalty fee, and the exact amount depends on how long the license has been lapsed.

Here is the practical timeline every agent should follow for the 2026 cycle. Complete your RELRA module by February 2026. Finish your elective hours by April 1, 2026. Log into PALS by mid-April to verify that your provider has reported all completions. Submit your renewal application no later than May 15, 2026, to leave buffer for any system issues.

> Section 404.1 of RELRA states: "A licensee who does not complete the required continuing education by the end of the biennial renewal period shall not be eligible for renewal and shall not engage in any activity requiring a license until the licensee has met all requirements for renewal."

That statutory language leaves zero room for interpretation. There is no grace period and no provisional status.
