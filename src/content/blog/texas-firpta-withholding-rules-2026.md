## Texas FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026: The Agent's Full Liability Picture

Every year, foreign nationals sell billions of dollars in Texas real estate—and every year, agents get blindsided by withholding obligations they assumed were someone else's problem. Understanding the Texas firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 is not optional if you hold a license issued by the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC). The federal Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act (FIRPTA), codified at 26 U.S.C. Sections 1445–1446, imposes a mandatory tax withholding when a "foreign person" disposes of a U.S. real property interest.

Texas has no state income tax, which means there is no additional state-level withholding layer. That simplifies things compared to states like California or Virginia. But it also creates a false sense of security—agents in Texas sometimes assume FIRPTA doesn't apply here because they've never dealt with a state withholding form.

**The federal obligation exists independently of state tax law, and it follows the transaction regardless of where the property sits.** A foreign seller disposing of a $2 million single-family home in Austin triggers the same 15% withholding requirement as one selling a condo in Manhattan. Your job is to recognize the trigger before the title company scrambles at the eleventh hour.

## Who Qualifies as a "Foreign Person" and Why It Matters at Contract

Under 26 U.S.C. Section 1445(f)(3), a "foreign person" includes nonresident alien individuals, foreign corporations, foreign partnerships, foreign trusts, and foreign estates. Permanent residents holding a green card are generally treated as U.S. persons and are exempt from FIRPTA withholding—but only if their status was valid at the time of disposition.

The practical question for listing agents: when do you ask? The answer is at listing intake. TREC does not prescribe a specific disclosure form for foreign status, but the Texas Association of Realtors (TAR) promulgated forms include language in the TAR Listing Agreement (TAR 1101) requiring the seller to disclose material facts. An agent who knows or should have known a seller is foreign—and fails to flag the issue—creates downstream liability for the buyer and potentially the brokerage.

Buyers' agents carry exposure too. If the buyer fails to withhold and later cannot collect from the seller, the IRS assesses the tax against the buyer under Section 1445(a). That means a buyer's agent who assured their client "the title company handles it" may find themselves explaining how a $150,000 tax bill landed on the buyer's lap post-closing.

## The Withholding Rates and Exemptions: A Side-by-Side Breakdown

Not every sale to or from a foreign person triggers the full 15%. Congress built in tiered rates and specific exemptions. The table below maps out the most common scenarios Texas agents encounter.

| Scenario | Withholding Rate | Key Condition |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Standard sale, price above $1,000,000 | 15% of gross sales price | Seller is a foreign person; no exemption applies |
| Buyer will use as residence, price $300,001–$1,000,000 | 10% of gross sales price | Buyer signs affidavit of intended residential use |
| Buyer will use as residence, price $300,000 or less | 0% (exempt) | Buyer signs affidavit of intended residential use |
| Seller obtains IRS Withholding Certificate (Form 8288-B approved) | Amount stated on certificate | Must be received before closing or funds held in escrow |
| Seller provides valid affidavit of non-foreign status | 0% (no withholding) | Affidavit meets requirements of Treas. Reg. 1.1445-2(b) |

The residence-use exemption trips up agents more than any other provision. The buyer—not the seller—must intend to occupy the property. An investor buying a rental from a foreign seller gets no rate reduction, period.

## Forms You Must Know: IRS 8288, 8288-A, 8288-B, and the FIRPTA Affidavit

Texas title companies handle the mechanical withholding, but agents need to recognize these form numbers when they appear in a closing package—or when they're conspicuously absent.

**IRS Form 8288** (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Certain Dispositions by Foreign Persons) is the return filed by the buyer or the buyer's agent (often the title company acting in that capacity) to remit the withheld amount to the IRS. It must be filed within 20 days of closing. Form 8288-A accompanies it as a statement showing the amount withheld from the foreign seller.

IRS Form 8288-B is the seller's application for a withholding certificate requesting a reduced or zero withholding amount. Foreign sellers with substantial tax basis in the property often file this well before closing—sometimes 90 days in advance—because IRS processing can take that long. If the certificate hasn't arrived by closing, the full 15% must be withheld and held in escrow pending the IRS decision.

The non-foreign affidavit (sometimes called the FIRPTA affidavit or Certificate of Non-Foreign Status) is the document that exempts a transaction from withholding altogether. Treasury Regulation 1.1445-2(b) specifies it must include the seller's name, U.S. taxpayer identification number, and a statement under penalty of perjury that the seller is not a foreign person. In Texas, most title companies use their own proprietary version, but TAR Form 26-16 (Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition) does not substitute for this affidavit—they serve entirely different purposes.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**The liability chain under FIRPTA is unforgiving—it flows uphill to the buyer, and reputationally sideways to the agents.** Section 1445(a) makes the buyer (called the "transferee") liable for the tax if withholding doesn't happen. The buyer can then seek indemnification from the seller, but good luck collecting from someone who's already left the country with their proceeds.

Agents face indirect but serious consequences. TREC's Standards of Conduct under 22 TAC Section 531.2 require licensees to act with honesty, trustworthiness, and competence. An agent who knew a seller was foreign and failed to alert the title company—or worse, helped the seller avoid withholding—risks a TREC complaint for misrepresentation or incompetence. Sanctions range from a formal reprimand to license revocation.

Title companies that fail to withhold face IRS penalties under Section 6651 (failure to file) and Section 6656 (failure to deposit taxes). Those penalties compound quickly: 5% per month for failure to file Form 8288, up to 25% of the tax owed. The title company's errors-and-omissions carrier may decline coverage if the failure was due to willful disregard of known facts.

Deal cancellations are also common. When a FIRPTA issue surfaces at closing for the first time, buyers—especially those financing the purchase—often walk rather than absorb the withholding logistics. ## Five Common Mistakes Texas Agents Make With FIRPTA

The first mistake is assuming the title company will catch everything. Title companies rely on information you provide. If you never disclose that your seller holds a Canadian passport and has no SSN, the title company may process closing without withholding—and the IRS will come calling months later.

The second mistake is confusing residency with citizenship. A French citizen who has lived in Dallas for 15 years on a green card is likely a U.S. person for FIRPTA purposes. Conversely, a U.S. citizen who renounced citizenship three years ago and now lives in Dubai is a foreign person. Status at the moment of disposition controls, not where someone was born.

The third mistake is accepting a non-foreign affidavit from an entity without verifying its domestic status. A Texas LLC owned entirely by foreign nationals is treated as a foreign partnership for FIRPTA purposes. The LLC's Texas Secretary of State filing means nothing under federal tax law. Agents must ask the right questions about beneficial ownership.

**The fourth mistake is failing to build FIRPTA timing into the contract timeline.** A Form 8288-B application takes 90 days on average. If the contract has a 30-day close and the seller hasn't filed, the full 15% gets withheld or the closing gets pushed. Neither outcome makes anyone happy. Build this into your [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) so it's addressed the moment a foreign seller is identified.

The fifth mistake is conflating FIRPTA with FinCEN's Geographic Targeting Orders (GTOs). GTOs—which apply to all-cash purchases in certain Texas metro areas like Houston, San Antonio, and Dallas—are anti-money-laundering reporting requirements imposed on title companies. They target the buyer side. FIRPTA targets the seller side. Different laws, different forms, different obligations.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers designated under TREC as the managing entity for a brokerage bear supervisory responsibility under 22 TAC Section 535.2. That includes ensuring agents handle FIRPTA-triggering transactions competently. A broker who never trains agents on FIRPTA—and later faces a complaint—has a weak defense.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Action if Deficient |
|-------------------|-----------|---------------------|
| Agent training on FIRPTA identification | Annually, documented | Require completion before handling international listings |
| Foreign seller flag in transaction management system | Every new listing | Add mandatory field; reject submissions without answer |
| Confirmation that title company received FIRPTA affidavit or withholding instructions | Before closing | Halt file until confirmation received |
| Review of entity ownership for LLC/trust sellers | At contract execution | Request operating agreement or trust certificate |
| Post-closing verification that Form 8288 was filed timely | Within 30 days of closing | Follow up with title company; document in file |

Brokers should also maintain a written policy—separate from their general compliance manual—addressing international seller transactions. The [Texas Real Estate Commission's website](https://www.trec.texas.gov/) provides guidance on supervisory duties, but FIRPTA-specific policies must be built internally because TREC does not publish a FIRPTA checklist.

## 2026 Updates and Enforcement Trends

The IRS has increased FIRPTA enforcement resources in recent fiscal years, and 2026 continues that trend. The agency's Large Business and International Division now cross-references FinCEN data with property transfer records to identify unreported dispositions by foreign persons. Texas, as the second-largest state by transaction volume, is a priority jurisdiction.

No legislative changes to FIRPTA withholding rates took effect for 2026. The 15% rate established by the PATH Act of 2015 remains in force. However, proposed Treasury guidance on digital asset-funded real estate purchases—where a foreign seller receives cryptocurrency—may create new reporting complexities later this year. Agents should watch for final regulations.

One operational shift worth noting: several major Texas title underwriters now require a completed FIRPTA questionnaire before issuing a title commitment on any transaction where the seller's TIN cannot be verified through standard IRS matching. This front-loads the compliance burden but protects everyone in the chain.
