The regulatory environment for Massachusetts real estate agents shifted substantially on January 1, 2026. If you hold an active license in the Commonwealth, the Massachusetts new real estate laws 2026 affect how you take listings, represent buyers, handle disclosures, and structure compensation. Ignoring these changes is not an option—penalties now include personal liability, license suspension, and deal rescission.

This post breaks down the statutes, the forms, and the specific compliance steps that agents and brokers need to follow right now.

## The Legislative Framework Behind the 2026 Changes

Massachusetts Governor Healey signed H.B. 4312, the Real Estate Transaction Transparency Act, in September 2025. The law amends several sections of Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 112, Sections 87PP through 87DDD, which govern real estate brokerage. The Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons (BORREBS) issued implementing regulations effective January 1, 2026.

Separately, the Massachusetts Association of Realtors (MAR) updated its standard form library to align with H.B. 4312 and with post-NAR settlement practices. Forms 501, 35, 35A, 58, and 704 all received revisions. Agents still using 2024 or early-2025 versions of these forms are operating out of compliance.

The net effect is a system that demands earlier disclosures, written agreements before substantive activity, and explicit compensation transparency at every stage. Massachusetts joins a growing list of states—[including Washington](/blog/washington-new-real-estate-laws-2026)—that have codified buyer agency agreement mandates into statute rather than relying solely on MLS policy.

## Massachusetts New Real Estate Laws 2026: Buyer Representation Requirements

**The single biggest operational change for buyer's agents is the mandate under amended M.G.L. c. 112 Section 87AAA(3) requiring a signed buyer representation agreement before any showing, tour, or property-specific consultation.** The statute uses the phrase "prior to the provision of substantive real estate services," which BORREBS has interpreted to include sending listing details tailored to a specific buyer's criteria.

MAR Form 501 (Buyer Agency Agreement, revised January 2026) is the standard-form vehicle. It must include the agent's compensation amount or rate, a clear statement of services, the agreement's duration (capped at 180 days under the new law), and a cancellation clause. Any agreement exceeding 180 days is voidable at the buyer's option.

One critical nuance: open house attendance triggers the requirement. If a buyer approaches you at an open house and asks substantive questions about the property's value, condition, or negotiation strategy, you must present Form 501 before answering. The statute does not carve out casual conversation, so the practical standard is "when in doubt, get it signed."

## Seller Disclosure and Lead Paint Updates

The revised Seller's Statement of Property Condition (Form 35, 2026 edition) now includes twelve additional line items covering septic system age, prior insurance claims, environmental contamination awareness, and flood zone history. Previously, Massachusetts had one of the lighter seller disclosure regimes in the Northeast. That gap has closed.

**Agents must deliver the updated Form 35 within three calendar days of the executed purchase and sale agreement—not three business days.** Calendar days include weekends and holidays. Missing this window gives the buyer an automatic five-day extension to the inspection contingency under the new statute, which can derail closing timelines.

For properties built before 1978, the Property Transfer Lead Paint Notification (Form 35A, revised) now requires the seller to disclose not only known lead paint presence but also any prior deleading certificates, partial remediation records, or childhood lead poisoning reports associated with the property's address. The Massachusetts Department of Labor Standards maintains a searchable database that agents should reference during listing intake. Failure to deliver Form 35A before offer acceptance triggers penalties under [M.G.L. c. 111 Section 197A](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleXVI/Chapter111/Section197A), including fines up to $5,000 per violation.

## Compensation Transparency and Form 704

H.B. 4312 codifies compensation disclosure in a way that goes beyond the NAR settlement terms. Massachusetts now requires a standalone Compensation Disclosure Statement (Form 704) to be signed by all parties—buyer, seller, listing agent, and buyer's agent—no later than the execution of the purchase and sale agreement.

Form 704 must specify the exact dollar amount or percentage each agent will receive, the source of those funds, and whether any portion of compensation is contingent on specific terms. The form cannot use the phrase "as per MLS" or reference any external source—the number must be explicit on the page.

| Requirement | Before 2026 | After January 1, 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer agency agreement timing | Recommended, not required by statute | Required before any showing or substantive service |
| Compensation disclosure form | Not required | Form 704 mandatory at P&S execution |
| Seller disclosure delivery | "Reasonable time" standard | 3 calendar days after P&S |
| Lead paint disclosure scope | Known presence only | Presence + deleading records + poisoning reports |
| Buyer agency agreement max duration | No statutory cap | 180 days maximum |

## Common Mistakes Agents Are Already Making

Three months into these new laws, BORREBS has already issued guidance letters addressing recurring errors. Here are the five most frequent compliance failures being flagged.

**First, agents are using outdated forms.** The 2024 version of Form 35 is still circulating in some brokerages' document management systems. Any transaction submitted with a superseded form is non-compliant, and the buyer gains the right to void the P&S within ten days of discovering the deficiency.

Second, agents are backdating buyer agency agreements. If your first showing was January 15 but you present Form 501 on January 22, signing it with a January 15 date is fraud under M.G.L. c. 266 Section 67A. BORREBS has stated it will refer backdating cases to the Attorney General's office.

Third, teams are treating one buyer agreement as covering all team members. The statute requires the agreement to name the specific licensee providing services. If agent A signs the agreement but agent B conducts the showing, the agreement does not cover agent B's activity. Each licensee must be named or added via an amendment form.

Fourth, listing agents are failing to collect Form 58, the Beneficial Ownership Certification, for entity-owned properties. Any property sold by an LLC, trust, or corporation requires this form. The listing agent—not the closing attorney—bears initial collection responsibility under the new regulations.

Fifth, agents are not disclosing dual agency status on Form 704 when their brokerage represents both sides. Massachusetts still permits disclosed dual agency under M.G.L. c. 112 Section 87AAA(2), but Form 704's compensation section must clearly mark the dual arrangement and both clients must initial the dual agency acknowledgment box. Agents familiar with [dual agency rules in other states](/blog/washington-dual-agency-rules-2026) should note that Massachusetts imposes stricter documentation standards.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under M.G.L. c. 112 Section 87PP for their agents' compliance failures. The 2026 amendments expand this liability to include a "knew or should have known" standard—meaning passive oversight is no longer a defense.

**Every Massachusetts brokerage should conduct a form audit immediately, removing all pre-2026 versions of Forms 35, 35A, 501, 58, and 704 from digital templates, transaction management platforms, and physical file cabinets.** Replace them with the January 2026 editions available from the MAR form library.

Brokers must also implement a buyer agreement tracking system. BORREBS expects brokerages to maintain a log showing the date each buyer agreement was executed relative to the first substantive service provided. During audits, regulators will compare showing dates in MLS history against agreement execution dates. Discrepancies trigger investigations.

| Broker Obligation | Compliance Action | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Form version audit | Remove outdated forms, confirm 2026 editions | Quarterly |
| Buyer agreement tracking | Log execution dates vs. first showing dates | Per transaction |
| Compensation disclosure review | Verify Form 704 completeness before P&S | Per transaction |
| Dual agency documentation | Confirm both-party initials on Form 704 | Per dual agency transaction |
| Entity ownership certification | Verify Form 58 collected for LLC/trust sellers | Per applicable listing |
| Agent training documentation | Record attendance at compliance training | Semi-annually |

Brokers should also budget for semi-annual compliance training. BORREBS now requires that continuing education providers include H.B. 4312 content in their 2026-2027 CE cycles. But waiting for CE renewal is too late—agents need training now. The Massachusetts Division of Professional Licensure has published a [compliance guide](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/board-of-registration-of-real-estate-brokers-and-salespersons) on its website that brokers should distribute to all licensees.

## Penalties and Liability Exposure

The penalty structure under the amended statute is tiered. A first offense for failure to provide required disclosures carries a fine of $1,000 to $5,000. A second offense within 24 months doubles the fine range and triggers a mandatory 30-day license suspension. Three or more violations within 36 months result in revocation proceedings.

Beyond regulatory penalties, agents face civil liability. A buyer who does not receive the updated Form 35 within the three-day window can pursue rescission of the purchase and sale agreement and recover reasonable attorney's fees under M.G.L. c. 93A if the failure is deemed an unfair or deceptive act. Sellers who are harmed by a failed transaction due to their agent's disclosure failure can pursue malpractice claims against both the agent and the supervising broker.

Errors and omissions insurance carriers have already updated their Massachusetts policy endorsements. Several major carriers now exclude coverage for claims arising from "willful non-compliance with statutory disclosure mandates enacted after January 1, 2025." Agents who ignore the new laws may find themselves uninsured when a claim hits.
