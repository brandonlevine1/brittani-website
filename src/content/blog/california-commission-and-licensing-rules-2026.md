## Understanding California Commission & Licensing Rules 2026

The California commission & licensing rules 2026 represent one of the most heavily regulated frameworks in residential real estate. Between the NAR settlement fallout, updated California Association of Realtors forms, and evolving Department of Real Estate enforcement priorities, agents who operated on autopilot last year are sitting ducks for violations this year. This is not a topic you can afford to skim.

California's Department of Real Estate (DRE) governs approximately 425,000 licensees. The agency has ramped up audits in 2025 and 2026, focusing specifically on commission disclosure compliance and proper licensing documentation during transactions. Agents and brokers who fail to keep up face real financial consequences — not hypothetical ones.

## The Statutory Foundation: B&P Code Sections That Control Your Income

**California Business and Professions Code Section 10136 is the statute every agent should have bookmarked.** It states plainly that no person may bring or maintain any action for commission or compensation for performing acts requiring a real estate license without proving they held a valid license at the time the services were performed. No license, no paycheck — full stop.

Section 10137 adds another layer: a salesperson may only receive compensation from their employing broker. An agent cannot receive a commission directly from a client, another agent, or another brokerage. This rule catches newer agents off guard, particularly those who freelanced in other industries before entering real estate.

Section 10147.5 requires that any agreement authorizing or employing an agent to purchase or lease property must be in writing. With the 2024 NAR settlement now fully in effect throughout 2026, this section has become the enforcement backbone for buyer representation agreements in California. The [California DRE's official licensing page](https://www.dre.ca.gov/Licensees/) spells out the requirements in administrative detail.

## Key Forms Agents Must Use in 2026

C.A.R. has updated its form library to reflect both the NAR settlement terms and California-specific statutory requirements. The forms below are not optional — they are the documented proof that you complied with commission and licensing rules during a transaction.

| Form Number | Form Name | When Required |
|---|---|---|
| BRBC | Buyer Representation and Broker Compensation Agreement | Before showing property to a buyer |
| SPBC | Seller Payment to Buyer's Broker Confirmation | When seller agrees to pay buyer's agent |
| RLA | Residential Listing Agreement | At listing appointment |
| AD | Agency Disclosure | As soon as practicable, before substantive discussions |
| DC | Disclosure and Consent for Dual Agency | Before dual agency begins |

**The BRBC form became the single most consequential document for buyer agents starting August 2024.** It requires a specific compensation amount or rate, a clear description of services, and the buyer's acknowledgment that compensation is negotiable. Agents who skip this form and later attempt to collect compensation are exposed to forfeiture claims under both B&P Code 10147.5 and federal antitrust guidance from the NAR settlement.

The RLA (Residential Listing Agreement) must also now explicitly address whether and how the seller will compensate a buyer's broker. Field 5A of the current RLA revision provides the mechanism for this. If you are still using a pre-August 2024 RLA, you are using an outdated form that may not satisfy disclosure requirements.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Consequences range from inconvenient to career-ending. The DRE's disciplinary actions fall into three broad categories.

First, license suspension or revocation. Under B&P Code Section 10176, the DRE can suspend or revoke a license for fraud, misrepresentation, or conduct that constitutes dishonest dealing. Failure to disclose material facts about compensation — including who pays what to whom — qualifies as misrepresentation. In 2025, the DRE issued 147 formal accusations against licensees, a 22% increase from the prior year.

Second, commission forfeiture. If an agent cannot prove they had a valid, written agreement authorizing their compensation at the time of service, the commission is not merely reduced — it is eliminated. Courts will not enforce an oral commission agreement in California residential transactions. Period.

Third, civil liability. Buyers and sellers who discover they were not properly informed about commission structures can sue for damages. These lawsuits have multiplied since the NAR settlement made commission transparency a national conversation. Agents without proper documentation are defending themselves without evidence, which is another way of saying they are losing.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Commission and Licensing Compliance

Mistake one: relying on verbal buyer agreements. Many agents still operate under the assumption that a handshake or text message confirmation is sufficient to establish a buyer representation relationship. It is not. B&P Code 10147.5 requires a signed writing. A text thread does not satisfy this requirement, even if the buyer expresses clear intent. Every showing without a signed BRBC is a showing you may never be paid for.

**Mistake two: failing to track license renewal deadlines.** California real estate licenses expire every four years for salespersons. The DRE sends renewal notices, but agents who have moved, changed emails, or simply ignored the notice find themselves practicing on an expired license. Any transaction conducted during that gap — even a single day — renders the commission uncollectable. Agents managing [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) are especially vulnerable when renewal deadlines slip through the cracks.

Mistake three: collecting referral fees without proper documentation. Agents frequently refer clients to other agents and expect a referral fee in return. Under Section 10137, that fee must flow through the employing broker. An agent who receives a direct Venmo payment or check from another agent — even with the best intentions — has violated state law. The referring agent's broker must be involved in the payment chain.

Mistake four: using outdated listing agreements that omit buyer-broker compensation disclosures. The 2024 form revisions were not cosmetic. Agents using pre-settlement RLA versions expose themselves and their sellers to liability because the older forms do not address the new disclosure requirements regarding cooperative compensation.

Mistake five: dual agency without proper consent documentation. California permits dual agency, but only with informed written consent from both parties using form DC. Agents who drift into dual agency — representing both sides after a buyer walks into an open house — without securing this form have committed a violation that can void the transaction entirely.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under B&P Code Section 10159.2 for the acts of their agents. This is not passive liability — the DRE holds brokers to an active supervision standard. A broker who "didn't know" their agent was operating without a signed BRBC is still on the hook.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| License status | Verify every agent's DRE license is active and current | Quarterly |
| Buyer agreements | Confirm signed BRBC on file before first showing | Per transaction |
| Commission disbursement | Ensure all payments flow through brokerage | Per transaction |
| Continuing education | Track CE completion for upcoming renewals | Annually |
| Form versions | Confirm agents use current C.A.R. form revisions | Semi-annually |

**Brokers who skip these audits are gambling with their own license.** The DRE's 2026 enforcement bulletin specifically calls out "failure to supervise" as a top-three violation category. A single rogue agent using outdated forms or operating on a lapsed license can trigger an audit of the entire brokerage.

Supervision also means having systems in place. Brokers should maintain a transaction file checklist that flags missing documents before close of escrow. If you have been [training new agents](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) without a structured compliance onboarding process, you are building liability into your team from day one.

The DRE expects brokers to review files within five business days of close. Waiting until a complaint arrives to check whether forms were signed correctly is not supervision — it is negligence in the eyes of the regulator.

## How the NAR Settlement Changed California Commission Rules

The August 2024 NAR settlement eliminated offers of buyer-agent compensation from MLS systems nationwide. In California, this meant the end of the cooperative compensation field in CRMLS, CCAR, and other local MLS platforms. Sellers can still choose to pay a buyer's agent, but the offer cannot appear in the MLS listing data.

This shift forced every buyer-agent relationship into a written agreement before home tours begin. The practical effect: agents must have an uncomfortable compensation conversation earlier in the relationship than they are accustomed to. Agents who [miss this deadline](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) risk performing unpaid labor with no legal recourse.

California's implementation is stricter than some states because B&P Code 10147.5 already required written buyer agreements for purchases. The NAR settlement simply added enforcement teeth and eliminated the MLS workaround that allowed agents to avoid the conversation. The two rules now reinforce each other — state statute demands the writing, and federal settlement terms demand the specificity of compensation within it.

## Continuing Education and License Maintenance for 2026

California requires 45 hours of continuing education every four-year renewal cycle. Of those, 18 hours must cover specific mandatory topics including agency, ethics, trust fund handling, fair housing, and risk management. The remaining 27 hours are elective but must come from DRE-approved providers.

Agents whose licenses expire in 2026 should verify their CE status immediately through their DRE online account. Late renewals are permitted within two years of expiration but require additional fees and completion of all CE before reactivation. During the late period, the agent cannot practice, cannot collect pending commissions, and cannot represent themselves as licensed.

For brokers managing teams, CE tracking is not optional administrative work — it is a regulatory obligation. A brokerage that allows an agent with an expired license to close a transaction faces DRE sanctions at the broker level, including potential restrictions on the broker's own license.
