## Understanding Oregon Seller Disclosure Requirements 2026

Oregon's seller disclosure statute is one of the more prescriptive frameworks on the West Coast, and the penalties for noncompliance have only gotten steeper. If you are listing residential property in 2026, the **Oregon seller disclosure requirements 2026** demand your attention at the very first listing appointment—not when an angry buyer's attorney sends a demand letter sixty days post-closing. The statutory backbone lives in ORS 105.462 through 105.490, and the Oregon Real Estate Agency (OREA) treats disclosure failures as a licensee discipline matter under ORS 696.301.

Oregon does not let agents play the "I didn't know" card easily. The state expects you to take an active advisory role: confirm the form is complete, flag inconsistencies, and document delivery with timestamps. Agents who treat the disclosure as just another piece of paperwork frequently end up in the [same pattern of missed deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) that tanks deals entirely.

## The Statutory Framework: ORS 105.462–105.490

The Oregon Property Condition Disclosure Statement is governed by a tightly connected chain of statutes. ORS 105.462 defines the scope—residential real property of one to four dwelling units. ORS 105.464 spells out the categories of disclosure, including structure, systems, environmental hazards, land use, and neighborhood conditions. ORS 105.475 gives the buyer a five-business-day revocation window after receiving the completed form.

**Sellers must deliver the disclosure before or at the time the buyer signs a binding sale agreement.** If the seller delivers late, the buyer's revocation clock resets—meaning the deal is legally fragile until that window closes. Agents who wait until mutual acceptance to chase the disclosure often discover they have given the buyer a free exit ramp weeks into escrow.

The Oregon legislature also carved out a short list of exemptions under ORS 105.470. Foreclosure sales, court-ordered transfers, transfers between spouses or co-owners, and the first sale of a dwelling never occupied (when a home warranty is provided) are all exempt. Every other residential deal requires the form.

## The Form: Oregon Real Estate Agency's Official Disclosure

Oregon does not use a proprietary OREA-numbered form the way some states number their commission-issued documents. Instead, the Property Condition Disclosure Statement prescribed by ORS 105.464 is typically delivered via the [OREA-aligned statutory form](https://www.oregon.gov/rea/Pages/index.aspx) or through an Oregon Association of REALTORS form that mirrors the statutory requirements precisely. The most commonly used version in MLS transactions is the Oregon REALTORS form 2090—often referred to simply as the "Seller's Property Disclosure."

Form 2090 covers approximately ten categories. Each requires the seller to mark "yes," "no," "unknown," or "not applicable" and then explain affirmative answers in the space provided. Categories include title and ownership, water and sewer, structural systems, heating and cooling, electrical, environmental hazards, land use restrictions, and neighborhood nuisances.

## Delivery Timing and Buyer Revocation Rights

Timing is everything. ORS 105.475 requires the seller to deliver the disclosure to the buyer before or concurrently with the buyer's written offer acceptance. If the disclosure arrives after acceptance, the buyer receives a fresh five-business-day revocation right that begins when the buyer actually receives the completed form—not when it was emailed or uploaded to the transaction management platform.

**Agents listing property should treat disclosure delivery as a day-one task, not a post-offer scramble.** Obtaining the completed form during the listing appointment or within forty-eight hours of signing the listing agreement gives you a buffer that eliminates last-minute chaos. It also gives the listing agent time to review for obvious inconsistencies—a seller checking "no" on water intrusion while the home inspector's pre-listing report references staining in the crawlspace, for instance.

Buyer's agents must confirm receipt in writing. Best practice is a signed acknowledgment with a date stamp, kept in the transaction file. Without this, disputes over when the revocation clock started become costly arbitration battles.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

Consequences for disclosure noncompliance break down into three tiers: civil liability, license discipline, and deal cancellation.

On the civil side, ORS 105.485 allows the buyer to pursue damages for any loss caused by a seller's knowing misrepresentation or failure to disclose. Agents can be pulled into these lawsuits when the buyer alleges the licensee knew or should have known about the defect. Oregon courts have found agents liable in cases where red flags were visible during property tours and the agent failed to advise the seller to disclose.

On the license discipline side, OREA can pursue action under ORS 696.301(1), which lists conduct that constitutes grounds for discipline—including misrepresentation, failure to disclose material facts, and conduct that demonstrates incompetence. Sanctions range from a formal reprimand to license suspension or revocation. Monetary fines can reach $5,000 per violation under the agency's authority.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk. A buyer who never received a timely disclosure can revoke under ORS 105.475(2), unwinding the transaction and potentially triggering earnest money refund disputes. Agents who have already ordered inspections, appraisals, and title work eat those costs in client goodwill.

| Consequence | Triggering Event | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer revocation | Late or missing disclosure delivery | Deal dies; earnest money refunded |
| Civil lawsuit (ORS 105.485) | Knowing misrepresentation or omission | Damages, attorney fees, potential judgment |
| OREA license discipline | Agent concealment or failure to advise | Reprimand, fine up to $5,000, suspension |
| E&O insurance claim | Buyer alleges agent knew of defect | Premium increase, potential policy limits hit |

If your brokerage has dealt with an E&O claim, you know it echoes across every future renewal. Getting disclosure right is cheaper than getting it wrong.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Oregon Disclosures

Five errors show up repeatedly in OREA disciplinary cases and post-closing disputes.

**First, agents accept a partially completed form and move forward without flagging the gaps.** A seller who leaves entire sections blank has not met the statutory standard. Agents must advise the seller that unanswered sections create legal exposure—and document that advice in writing.

Second, agents confuse "unknown" with a blanket shield. Marking "unknown" is legitimate when the seller genuinely has no knowledge, but patterns of "unknown" across fifteen line items raise credibility problems in litigation. If your seller lived in the home for twenty years and marked "unknown" on whether the basement floods, expect a judge to be skeptical.

Third, listing agents fail to update the disclosure after new information surfaces. If a roof leak develops between listing and closing, the seller has a continuing duty to amend. Agents who know about the new condition and do not advise their client to update the form are exposed under ORS 696.301.

Fourth, buyer's agents do not confirm delivery in a verifiable way. Uploading a PDF to a portal without a read receipt or signed acknowledgment creates ambiguity about when the five-day clock started. This [kind of operational gap](/blog/real-cost-of-missed-deadlines) regularly costs buyers their revocation rights and costs agents their credibility in dispute resolution.

Fifth, agents assume the exemptions apply without verifying. Calling a sale "bank-owned" when the foreclosure was never formally completed, or claiming a new-construction exemption when the builder did not provide the required home warranty, exposes everyone involved.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carry supervisory liability under ORS 696.395. If an agent in your office repeatedly mishandles disclosures, OREA will look at whether the broker had systems in place to catch the problem.

**Every Oregon brokerage should have a disclosure audit checklist that runs at three milestones: listing signed, mutual acceptance, and pre-closing.** At listing signed, verify the disclosure form is in the file—completed, signed, and dated. At mutual acceptance, confirm the buyer received the form and signed an acknowledgment. At pre-closing, check whether any amendments were required based on inspection findings or seller-reported changes.

Brokers should also track how often agents use the "unknown" designation. A quarterly review of disclosure forms across the office reveals patterns—agents who coach sellers to mark everything unknown to avoid liability are creating the opposite outcome. Document review conversations in the agent's supervision file.

Training matters. Oregon does not mandate a standalone disclosure CE course, but smart brokerages build disclosure compliance into their onboarding for new licensees. Pair it with [your monthly compliance audit process](/blog/monthly-compliance-audit) and you catch problems before they become claims.

Finally, brokers should maintain a relationship with their E&O carrier that includes disclosure-related loss data. Knowing where claims originate—structural defects, environmental hazards, water intrusion—helps you target training to the highest-risk categories.

Build your listing workflow so the disclosure is non-negotiable before marketing begins.
