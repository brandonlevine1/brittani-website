## Understanding Illinois Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Getting a deal to the finish line in Illinois means accounting for a layered system of transfer taxes and recording fees that trips up even experienced agents. The Illinois transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 reflect a multi-tiered structure—state, county, and municipal—that requires precision at every level. Miss a line item on the settlement statement or file the wrong form, and you are staring at a rejected recording, a delayed closing, or worse.

Illinois stands out from most states because it allows home-rule municipalities to impose their own transfer taxes on top of the state and county levies. Chicago is the most aggressive example, but Evanston, Oak Park, and several other municipalities maintain their own schedules. Agents who work across county or municipal lines cannot rely on muscle memory from their last deal.

The governing statute is the Illinois Real Estate Transfer Tax Act, codified at [35 ILCS 200/31-1 through 31-50](https://www.ilga.gov/legislation/ilcs/ilcs4.asp?DocName=003502000HArt%2E+31&ActID=596). Every dollar on the HUD-1 or ALTA settlement statement that relates to transfer taxes traces back to this statute or to a home-rule ordinance authorized under Article VII, Section 6 of the Illinois Constitution.

## State, County, and Municipal Rate Breakdown

**The state transfer tax rate has remained at $0.50 per $500 of consideration since its inception, but the layers above and below it change the math dramatically.** Cook County adds $0.25 per $500, and the City of Chicago adds $3.75 per $500 for the seller and $7.50 per $500 for the buyer under the 2024 Bring Chicago Home ordinance's restructured rates still in effect for 2026.

| Taxing Body | Rate per $500 | Paid By | Statutory/Ordinance Authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| State of Illinois | $0.50 | Seller (presumed) | 35 ILCS 200/31-10 |
| Cook County | $0.25 | Seller (presumed) | Cook County Ordinance |
| City of Chicago (seller portion) | $3.75 | Seller | Chicago Municipal Code 3-33 |
| City of Chicago (buyer portion) | $7.50 | Buyer | Chicago Municipal Code 3-33 |
| DuPage County | $0.00 (no county tax) | N/A | N/A |

Outside Cook County, most collar counties do not impose an additional county transfer tax, but agents must verify whether the specific municipality does. Evanston's rate, for instance, sits at $5.00 per $1,000, while Schaumburg does not impose a local transfer tax at all.

For a $400,000 home sale in Chicago, the combined transfer tax burden exceeds $9,400—a number that shocks buyers who relocated from DuPage or Will County, where the total might be $400 at the state level alone. Agents who fail to disclose these costs early in the transaction risk [blown closings and angry clients](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

## Recording Fee Schedules and Document Requirements

Recording fees in Illinois are set by each county recorder's office under the Counties Code (55 ILCS 5/3-5018). These are not transfer taxes—they are administrative fees for placing documents into the public record. The confusion between the two is a recurring source of settlement statement errors.

| Document Type | Cook County Fee | DuPage County Fee | Will County Fee |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deed (first page) | $60.00 | $51.00 | $48.00 |
| Each additional page | $2.00 | $2.00 | $2.00 |
| Mortgage (first page) | $60.00 | $51.00 | $48.00 |
| Release/Satisfaction | $60.00 | $51.00 | $48.00 |
| PTAX-203 filing | Included with deed | Included with deed | Included with deed |

**Recording fees increased in several Illinois counties effective January 2025, and those schedules carry into 2026 without further adjustment in most jurisdictions.** Cook County added a $3.00 surcharge for its Rental Housing Support Program fund, which applies to most recorded instruments. Agents should pull the current fee schedule directly from the county recorder's website within 48 hours of scheduling a closing.

The critical form here is the PTAX-203, formally titled the Illinois Real Estate Transfer Declaration. It must accompany any deed presented for recording unless the transfer qualifies for an exemption under 35 ILCS 200/31-45. Without a completed PTAX-203—or PTAX-203-A for exempt transactions—the recorder will reject the deed outright.

## Form Numbers Every Illinois Agent Must Know

The Illinois Department of Revenue and county recorders require specific forms at distinct stages of the transaction. Filing the wrong version or an outdated edition causes rejection at the recorder's window.

The PTAX-203 requires the full legal description, the PIN (Property Index Number), the sale price, and the signatures of both the buyer and seller (or their attorneys). In Cook County, the form must also include the buyer's intended use of the property—a field that many agents overlook. The PTAX-203-A covers exempt transfers such as those between spouses, transfers into trusts, or court-ordered conveyances.

Chicago transactions demand an additional form: the Chicago Real Property Transfer Tax Declaration, which is filed with the Department of Finance. This is separate from the PTAX-203 and is specific to calculating the municipal transfer tax owed. Failure to file it triggers a lien on the property and penalties beginning at 10% of the unpaid tax.

Agents who [track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) across different municipalities often confuse which forms apply where. A transaction in Naperville requires only the PTAX-203. A transaction in Chicago requires the PTAX-203 plus the Chicago declaration. A transaction in Evanston requires the PTAX-203 plus Evanston's own municipal transfer tax stamp application.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Transfer Taxes and Recording

Five errors appear repeatedly in audit findings and rejected recordings across Illinois. Each one is preventable.

**First, agents miscalculate the tax by using the net sale price instead of the gross consideration.** The statute defines "consideration" broadly under 35 ILCS 200/31-5 to include assumed mortgages, liens paid by the buyer, and personal property excluded from the contract but included in the real transaction. A $350,000 sale where the buyer assumes a $20,000 special assessment lien means the taxable consideration is $370,000.

Second, agents forget to account for the "fraction thereof" rounding rule. The tax is imposed on each $500 of value or fraction thereof. A sale price of $350,001 gets taxed as if it were $350,500. This small rounding error appears on settlement statements constantly and results in underpayment.

Third, agents submit an outdated PTAX-203 revision. The Department of Revenue updates the form periodically, and recorders in some counties reject forms that are more than one revision behind. Always download the form fresh from the Illinois Department of Revenue website rather than reusing a saved PDF from a prior deal.

Fourth, agents in dual-tax jurisdictions forget to order municipal stamps in advance. Chicago and Evanston both require the stamp to be obtained before recording—not simultaneously. This sequencing error causes day-of-closing scrambles. Fifth, agents fail to note transfer tax credits for properties sold at a loss or in short-sale situations, leaving money on the table for their clients.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under the Illinois Real Estate License Act (225 ILCS 454/20-20) for their agents' transactional compliance. A pattern of transfer tax errors is not merely an administrative headache—it exposes the brokerage to regulatory action by the Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation (IDFPR).

**Brokers should audit settlement statements for transfer tax accuracy on at least a quarterly sample basis.** The audit should confirm that the correct rate was applied, that the PTAX-203 was completed with the correct PIN and legal description, and that any municipal declarations were filed. A single transposed digit in the PIN can result in the tax being applied to the wrong parcel, creating title clouds that surface months or years later.

Brokers must also verify that their agents are not contractually shifting transfer tax responsibility in ways that violate local ordinances. Chicago's municipal code, for example, prohibits sellers from contractually shifting the buyer's portion of the transfer tax back to the buyer in certain affordable-housing transactions. An agent who drafts or accepts such a provision exposes the brokerage to fines.

Training systems matter here. Firms that rely on institutional knowledge rather than written compliance protocols find themselves vulnerable when experienced agents leave or when new agents [miss critical deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) on their first solo deals. A documented procedure for transfer tax calculation and form selection—updated annually—is the minimum standard.

## Liability, Fines, and Deal Cancellations

When transfer taxes are underpaid, the county or municipality can assess the deficiency plus interest against the property. Under 35 ILCS 200/31-30, penalties for underpayment or failure to file can reach 25% of the unpaid tax, plus 1% per month interest. In Chicago, the penalty structure is even steeper—10% immediately upon discovery, plus 1.5% monthly interest.

The practical consequence for agents is more immediate than statutory penalties. A rejected recording means the deed does not transfer, the title company cannot issue its final policy, and the lender's security interest is not perfected. The deal is effectively in limbo until the deficiency is corrected. If correction requires re-signing by parties who have already moved to another state—or who are now disputing the transaction—the agent's failure becomes the proximate cause of a deal collapse.

Professional liability insurance (E&O) may cover defense costs but often excludes coverage for fines or penalties. Agents who repeatedly miscalculate transfer taxes may find their E&O premiums adjusted upward or their coverage non-renewed. IDFPR complaint investigations triggered by consumer harm from closing delays can result in license discipline ranging from a letter of reprimand to suspension.
