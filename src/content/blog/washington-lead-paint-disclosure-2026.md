## Understanding Washington Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

Federal law does not care how hot your market is or how many backup offers are stacked behind the primary buyer. If the home was built before 1978, Washington lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 apply to your transaction, period. The Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) created an obligation that sits squarely on listing agents, sellers, landlords, and their representatives.

Washington state does not layer its own separate lead paint disclosure statute on top of the federal requirement. Instead, compliance flows through 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d and the corresponding EPA/HUD regulations at 24 CFR Part 35 and 40 CFR Part 745. The Washington Department of Licensing (DOL) enforces agent compliance through its authority over license holders under RCW 18.85.

That means your exposure as an agent is two-pronged: federal enforcement through the EPA, and state license discipline through the DOL. Getting this wrong is not a paperwork inconvenience. It is a career-threatening mistake.

## Which Transactions Trigger the Requirement

Not every deal requires a lead paint disclosure. The rule applies exclusively to target housing, which the EPA defines as residential dwellings constructed before 1978. That includes single-family homes, condominiums, cooperatives, and multi-family buildings.

Several transaction types fall outside the disclosure requirement. These exemptions include zero-bedroom units (studios and dormitories), housing exclusively for the elderly or persons with disabilities where children under six are not expected to reside, short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or fewer, foreclosure sales, and properties that have been certified lead-free by a licensed inspector.

**Agents frequently stumble on the foreclosure exemption.** Bank-owned REO properties sold after foreclosure are exempt at the point of foreclosure sale, but if the lender later markets the property as a traditional resale, the disclosure obligation kicks back in. If you are listing a bank-owned pre-1978 property in Tacoma or Spokane, do not assume the exemption carries forward automatically.

## The Forms Washington Agents Must Use

The primary document is the EPA's "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form. This is a federal form, not a state-specific document, and it has not been revised since its original publication under 40 CFR 745.113.

In practice, most Washington agents working through the Northwest Multiple Listing Service (NWMLS) encounter this obligation through NWMLS Form 22J (Lead-Based Paint Disclosure). Form 22J incorporates the required federal disclosures and acknowledgments into a format familiar to agents operating in western Washington.

| Document | Purpose | Who Signs | Timing |
|----------|---------|-----------|--------|
| EPA Lead Paint Disclosure Form | Federal compliance | Seller, buyer, both agents | Before mutual acceptance |
| NWMLS Form 22J | MLS-integrated disclosure | Seller, buyer, both agents | Before mutual acceptance |
| EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" | Buyer education | Delivered to buyer | Before mutual acceptance |
| Lead inspection waiver (within Form 22J or EPA form) | Buyer's election | Buyer only | Within 10-day period |

The seller must disclose any known lead-based paint or hazards, provide available records and reports, and the buyer must receive the EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." The buyer then has a 10-day opportunity period to conduct a lead inspection at their own expense, unless they waive that right in writing.

## Penalties and Liability When Agents Fail to Comply

**The financial consequences of noncompliance are severe and have increased over time.** Under 40 CFR 745.118, civil penalties for each violation can reach $19,507 per occurrence (adjusted for inflation as of 2024, with further adjustments possible by 2026). Criminal penalties for knowing and willful violations can include fines and imprisonment.

Beyond federal enforcement, Washington buyers who were denied proper disclosure can pursue private civil action. Under the statute, courts may award treble damages (three times actual damages), attorney fees, and costs. A single missed disclosure on a $750,000 Seattle listing could easily generate six figures in legal exposure.

The Washington DOL can also act independently. Under RCW 18.85.361, the Director of Licensing may impose sanctions including license suspension or revocation for violations of federal or state laws related to the practice of real estate. A disciplinary finding stays on your license record permanently and surfaces in every future license renewal and brokerage application.

Buyers also retain the right to rescind the purchase agreement if the disclosure was not provided before mutual acceptance. This means a transaction can unravel at any point when the omission surfaces, including well after closing. The practical fallout extends to your brokerage, your E&O carrier, and your referral reputation. ## Common Mistakes Washington Agents Make

Years of transactions reveal predictable failure patterns. These five errors account for the majority of lead paint disclosure problems in Washington residential sales.

**First, agents assume the seller's property disclosure form covers lead paint.** Washington's Form 17 (Seller Disclosure Statement, required under RCW 64.06) includes a line about environmental hazards, but it does not satisfy the separate federal lead paint disclosure requirement. Form 17 and the lead paint disclosure are distinct obligations that must both be completed independently.

Second, listing agents deliver the disclosure at closing instead of before mutual acceptance. The regulation is explicit: the disclosure, pamphlet, and 10-day inspection opportunity must be provided before the buyer is obligated under a contract. Handing over lead paint forms at the signing table is a clear violation, even if the buyer signs them willingly.

Third, agents omit their own signatures from the disclosure form. The EPA form requires signatures from both the listing agent and the buyer's agent confirming that they informed their respective clients of their obligations. A form bearing only buyer and seller signatures is incomplete and noncompliant.

Fourth, agents skip the disclosure on exempt properties without documenting why. If you determine a property was built in 1978 or later and therefore exempt, note the basis for that determination in your transaction file. County assessor records showing the year built provide defensible documentation. Relying on a seller's verbal statement alone is risky.

Fifth, agents in competitive markets encourage buyers to waive the 10-day inspection period without making clear it is a voluntary election. While the waiver is legal, pressuring a buyer to waive it—or presenting waiver as a condition of offer acceptance—creates liability. The buyer's waiver must be freely given and clearly documented within the disclosure form.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers and managing brokers carry supervision responsibility under RCW 18.85.275. If an agent in your office fails to deliver a lead paint disclosure, the brokerage can face vicarious liability, E&O claims, and its own DOL disciplinary action.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Red Flag |
|-------------|---------------|----------|
| Pre-1978 determination | County assessor year-built data in file | No documentation of build year |
| Form completion | All four signatures present (seller, buyer, both agents) | Missing agent signature(s) |
| Delivery timing | Date on disclosure vs. mutual acceptance date | Disclosure dated same day or after mutual acceptance |
| Pamphlet delivery | Buyer acknowledgment of receipt | No acknowledgment line signed |
| 10-day period | Waiver signed or inspection contingency noted | Neither waiver nor inspection documentation |

Brokers should build a checklist into their transaction review process that flags every pre-1978 listing at intake. The time to catch a missing lead paint disclosure is at listing launch, not during file review after closing. Consider running a quarterly audit of closed files where the assessor year-built field shows a date before 1978, then spot-check for complete disclosures.

## The 10-Day Inspection Period in Practice

The buyer's 10-day opportunity to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment is not a contingency in the traditional sense. It is a federally mandated window that exists regardless of what the purchase agreement says about inspection timelines.

Buyers and sellers can mutually agree to a different time period (longer or shorter than 10 days), but they cannot eliminate the opportunity entirely unless the buyer affirmatively waives it. The waiver must appear on the disclosure form itself, not buried in an addendum or communicated verbally.

**In practice, most Washington buyers in 2026 either waive the lead-specific inspection or fold it into their general home inspection contingency.** This is acceptable as long as the paperwork reflects the buyer's informed choice. Agents who fail to present the option, however, are in violation regardless of whether the buyer would have elected an inspection.
