## Understanding Massachusetts Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

Massachusetts handles earnest money differently than most states, and the rules governing escrow accounts carry real teeth. If you are licensed in the Commonwealth, **Massachusetts earnest money & escrow laws 2026** should be at the top of your compliance checklist this year. The Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons, operating under the Division of Professional Licensure, enforces these regulations through audits, complaints, and disciplinary hearings that can end careers.

The governing authority sits in two places: M.G.L. c. 112, §§ 87PP–87DDD and the administrative regulations at 254 CMR 2.00. Together they dictate how deposits are received, held, disbursed, and accounted for. Agents who treat these rules as suggestions rather than mandates expose their brokers—and themselves—to license discipline, civil liability, and deal collapse.

## The Statutory Framework: M.G.L. c. 112 and 254 CMR 2.00

**Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 112, Section 87AAA specifically prohibits comingling of client funds with a broker's personal or business operating funds.** Violations constitute grounds for suspension or revocation under § 87AAA(a)(5). The statute does not distinguish between intentional comingling and negligent bookkeeping—both carry the same potential penalty.

The companion regulation, 254 CMR 2.00(12), fills in the operational details. It requires brokers to maintain a separate escrow or trust account in a Massachusetts-chartered bank, federally chartered bank, or credit union. The account must be clearly labeled to indicate it holds client funds held in trust.

Under 254 CMR 2.00(12)(a), deposits must hit the escrow account by the close of the next business day following receipt of the executed purchase and sale agreement—unless the offer or P&S specifies a different timeline in writing. Agents who sit on checks over a weekend and forget Monday morning create an instant regulatory violation.

## How Massachusetts Differs From Other States

Massachusetts is an attorney-closing state, which shifts certain escrow dynamics compared to states where title companies handle everything. The presence of attorneys at nearly every residential closing means that escrow can be held by the listing broker or the buyer's or seller's attorney, depending on what the contract says. This flexibility creates confusion when newer agents assume the attorney always holds the deposit.

| Feature | Massachusetts | Typical Non-Attorney State |
|---------|--------------|---------------------------|
| Who commonly holds deposit | Listing broker or attorney | Title/escrow company |
| Deposit deadline | Next business day after P&S execution | Varies (often 2-3 business days) |
| Governing body | Board of Registration (DPL) | State real estate commission |
| Comingling penalty | License suspension/revocation | Varies; often fines first |
| Interest on deposits | Allowed but not required; interest belongs to party specified in P&S | State-specific rules |

The table above highlights why agents moving into Massachusetts from other jurisdictions often stumble. If you have worked in states like Washington where escrow companies serve as neutral depositories, the Massachusetts model—where the broker's office literally holds buyer funds—demands a different level of internal discipline. ## Forms and Documents Agents Must Know

Massachusetts residential transactions typically use the Greater Boston Real Estate Board (GBREB) Standard Form Purchase and Sale Agreement or a similar local board form. The escrow provisions appear in Section 2 of the standard P&S, which identifies the deposit holder, the amount, and the conditions for release or forfeiture.

Before the P&S is signed, most deals begin with an Offer to Purchase—sometimes called the "OTP" or "hand money" agreement. The Massachusetts Association of Realtors (MAR) Form 501 Offer to Purchase is widely used statewide. It typically specifies a deposit of $1,000 or more held by the listing broker's office until the P&S is executed, at which point the full earnest money deposit (often 5% of the purchase price) comes due.

**Agents must verify that the escrow holder, deposit amount, and deposit deadline are consistent between the OTP and the P&S.** Discrepancies between the two documents are one of the most common sources of post-acceptance disputes. When the OTP names the listing broker as holder but the P&S names the seller's attorney, someone needs to coordinate the transfer—and document it.

The [Board of Registration's regulations](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/board-of-registration-of-real-estate-brokers-and-salespersons) require brokers to maintain transaction records, including escrow receipts and disbursement authorizations, for at least three years after closing or termination.

## Massachusetts Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026: Common Agent Mistakes

Mistakes in this area rarely stay small. A single escrow error can trigger a Board complaint, malpractice claim, or blown deal. Here are the five most frequent missteps Massachusetts agents make with earnest money.

**First, failing to deposit on time.** The next-business-day rule under 254 CMR 2.00(12) is strict. Agents who receive a deposit check on Friday afternoon but do not get it to the broker's escrow account until Tuesday are already in violation. The fix is simple: hand-deliver or scan-deposit the check the same day whenever possible, and never hold checks in a desk drawer.

Second, releasing deposits without mutual written authorization. Massachusetts law requires the consent of both parties—or a court order—before escrow funds can be disbursed after a dispute arises. An agent who releases a deposit to one party based on a verbal instruction or a single party's demand creates personal and brokerage liability. The GBREB P&S Section 2 contains specific language on forfeiture conditions, but agents still need both signatures on a release form when the deal dies outside those conditions.

Third, failing to disclose interest earned on escrow deposits. If the broker's escrow account is interest-bearing, the parties must agree in the P&S who receives the interest. Agents who ignore this create a quiet liability that surfaces at closing or, worse, in a post-closing complaint.

Fourth, accepting deposits made payable to the agent personally rather than the brokerage. This is a textbook comingling setup. Every check should be made payable to the brokerage's escrow account, not to an individual licensee.

Fifth, not confirming deposit receipt in writing. Buyers and their agents deserve a written acknowledgment that funds were received and deposited. Without it, "he said, she said" disputes about whether the deposit was ever delivered become nearly impossible to resolve. Agents dealing with [deadline management challenges](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) should build deposit confirmation into their task workflows rather than relying on memory.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers hold ultimate responsibility for escrow compliance under 254 CMR 2.00. The Board does not accept "my agent handled it" as a defense. Every brokerage in Massachusetts should have written escrow policies, and brokers should be auditing compliance at least quarterly.

| Audit Item | Frequency | What to Check |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| Bank reconciliation | Monthly | Account balance matches sum of all open escrow deposits |
| Deposit timing | Every transaction | Check was deposited within one business day of P&S execution |
| Disbursement authorization | Every transaction | Written mutual release or closing settlement statement on file |
| Account labeling | Annually | Bank records show "escrow" or "trust" designation |
| Record retention | Annually | Three years of transaction escrow records accessible |

**Brokers who discover a shortfall during reconciliation must report it immediately and fund the difference from operating accounts pending investigation.** Waiting to "figure out what happened" while the account remains short exposes the brokerage to additional Board sanctions and potential criminal referral under M.G.L. c. 266 if the shortfall appears intentional.

The Division of Professional Licensure can conduct random audits of escrow accounts. Brokers have no right to advance notice in complaint-driven investigations. Keeping records current and reconciled is not optional—it is the single best insurance against a surprise audit turning into a license action.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, Suspension, and Civil Liability

The Board of Registration has broad disciplinary authority. Under M.G.L. c. 112, § 87AAA, penalties for escrow violations include license suspension (typically 30-90 days for first offenses), license revocation for repeat or egregious violations, fines up to $1,000 per violation, and mandatory continuing education.

Civil exposure runs parallel to Board discipline. A buyer whose deposit is lost due to broker negligence or comingling has a direct cause of action. Massachusetts courts have awarded treble damages under consumer protection statutes (M.G.L. c. 93A) when escrow mishandling is deemed an unfair or deceptive practice. The threshold for a 93A claim is lower than many agents realize—a single instance of unreasonable delay in returning a deposit after a failed transaction can qualify.

Deal cancellation is another consequence. If a buyer learns that the deposit was never properly deposited or that the escrow account is out of compliance, they may have grounds to void the P&S and recover their deposit plus damages. Sellers lose their deal and potentially months of market time. Neither party forgets who caused the problem.

Errors and omissions insurance may cover some civil claims, but most E&O policies exclude intentional acts, comingling, and criminal conduct. Agents cannot assume their policy will save them if the violation was preventable through basic compliance.
