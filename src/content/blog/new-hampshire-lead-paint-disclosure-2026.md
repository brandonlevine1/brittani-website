New Hampshire's housing stock skews old. More than half of the state's residential units were built before 1978, which means the **New Hampshire lead paint disclosure requirements 2026** affect the majority of transactions you will touch this year. Failing to comply is not a minor paperwork oversight — it is a federal violation carrying five-figure fines and the power to unwind a signed contract weeks after closing. This post breaks down what you owe your clients, where agents keep stumbling, and what brokers should be auditing in every pre-1978 file.

## The Federal Framework That Controls New Hampshire Transactions

Lead paint disclosure in New Hampshire does not originate from a standalone state disclosure statute the way [seller disclosure obligations work in other states](/blog/vermont-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026). The controlling law is the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018), codified at 42 U.S.C. 4852d. The implementing regulations sit at [24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-24/subtitle-A/part-35/subpart-H), enforced jointly by the EPA and HUD.

New Hampshire supplements this framework through RSA 130-A, the state's Lead Paint Poisoning Prevention and Control Act, which gives the New Hampshire Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS) authority over lead hazard remediation orders and childhood blood-level reporting. For transactional purposes, though, your disclosure obligations flow from the federal rule — and every agent involved in the sale or lease of pre-1978 housing must comply regardless of brokerage size or transaction price.

**The New Hampshire Real Estate Commission (NHREC) expects licensees to understand these federal duties as a condition of competent practice.** NHREC disciplinary actions can and do reference failure to deliver required disclosures as a violation of RSA 331-A:26, which governs unprofessional conduct for licensees.

## Specific Forms and Documents You Must Deliver

There is no New Hampshire-specific lead paint disclosure form issued by the state. Agents rely on the EPA/HUD disclosure form titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards." Most MLS-integrated transaction platforms and the New Hampshire Association of Realtors provide this form bundled with their residential purchase agreements.

Here is exactly what must be delivered before a buyer is obligated under a purchase contract:

The seller (or lessor) must disclose any known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards in the dwelling, provide copies of any available records or reports regarding lead in the property, and supply the buyer with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" (EPA-747-K-12-001, revised 2021). The buyer must then receive a 10-day opportunity to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense — or waive that right in writing.

All parties — seller, buyer, and their respective agents — must sign and date the disclosure form acknowledging compliance. That signed form must be retained for three years from the date of sale, per 24 CFR 35.92.

| Requirement | Responsible Party | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Disclose known lead paint/hazards | Seller | Before contract ratification |
| Provide EPA pamphlet | Listing agent or seller | Before contract ratification |
| Deliver available reports/records | Seller | Before contract ratification |
| Offer 10-day inspection period | Seller (agent ensures inclusion) | Written into contract |
| Sign acknowledgment form | All parties and agents | At or before ratification |
| Retain signed disclosure | Each agent/broker | 3 years minimum |

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: Federal Teeth, Local Consequences

**Federal enforcement is not theoretical.** The EPA and HUD have assessed penalties against individual agents, sellers, and property management firms across New England. Under 24 CFR 35.88, each violation of the disclosure rule can trigger civil penalties adjusted annually for inflation — currently up to $19,507 per violation as of 2025 adjustment figures published in the Federal Register.

Beyond fines, a buyer who was not provided proper disclosure can file a private action under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3) and recover treble damages (three times the amount of actual damages), plus court costs and reasonable attorney fees. If a child is later found to have elevated blood lead levels traceable to an undisclosed hazard, the damages calculation expands dramatically.

At the state level, NHREC can pursue disciplinary action under RSA 331-A:28 for any licensee found to have neglected mandatory federal disclosure requirements. Sanctions range from a formal reprimand and mandatory continuing education to license suspension. The commission also has authority to impose civil penalties of up to $2,000 per violation under RSA 331-A:29.

A contract can be rescinded entirely if the buyer proves disclosure was not made before ratification. This is not a hypothetical risk — it has been litigated successfully in multiple jurisdictions, and New Hampshire courts recognize the federal private right of action.

## Common Mistakes New Hampshire Agents Make

After reviewing disciplinary files and transaction audits, five errors appear with alarming frequency in New Hampshire lead paint compliance.

**First, agents assume the seller "already handled it" because the property disclosure form mentions paint condition.** The New Hampshire Seller's Property Disclosure form (commonly the NHAR form) includes a general question about known hazards, but it does not satisfy the federal lead paint disclosure requirement. The EPA/HUD form is a separate, standalone obligation. Checking a box on the general disclosure is not a substitute.

Second, agents skip the disclosure entirely on properties that have been "renovated." A gut renovation does not eliminate the requirement. Unless the property was constructed in 1978 or later, the disclosure must be made. Even if every surface has been encapsulated or abated, the seller must still disclose the history of any known lead-based paint or hazards — and the agent must still deliver the pamphlet and offer the inspection period.

Third, listing agents provide the pamphlet at the wrong time. The pamphlet and disclosure must be delivered before the buyer is obligated under the contract. Handing it over at closing or stuffing it in the closing package after ratification violates the rule. If your MLS packet arrives after an offer has been accepted, you have already missed the deadline.

Fourth, agents allow the buyer to waive the 10-day inspection period without a clear written waiver on the disclosure form itself. A verbal statement or a text message does not satisfy the regulation. The waiver must appear on or be attached to the signed disclosure form, and it should reference the buyer's decision explicitly.

Fifth, the signed forms disappear. Agents change brokerages, files get purged, and three years later nobody can produce the acknowledgment. The retention requirement is non-negotiable — and it attaches to the agent individually, not just the brokerage. If you cannot prove compliance, you cannot defend yourself.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers in New Hampshire carry supervisory liability under RSA 331-A:25-b for the acts of their affiliated licensees. A pattern of missing lead paint disclosures in transaction files is not just an agent problem — it is a brokerage compliance failure that NHREC and federal regulators can trace upward.

| Audit Point | What to Look For | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure form present in file | Signed EPA/HUD form for every pre-1978 sale | Missing form or unsigned lines |
| Pamphlet delivery documented | Copy of pamphlet or agent attestation in file | No record of delivery |
| 10-day period or waiver | Written waiver or inspection contingency language | Neither present in contract |
| Date sequencing | Disclosure signed before or at ratification | Disclosure dated after contract execution |
| Retention compliance | Files retained minimum 3 years | Files purged at 12 or 24 months |

Brokers should build a checklist into their file review process that specifically flags properties by construction date. If the MLS listing shows a build year prior to 1978, the file should not pass review without the EPA/HUD disclosure form, evidence of pamphlet delivery, and either a signed inspection waiver or documentation that the 10-day window was honored.

Training is equally important. New agents entering the New Hampshire market often have minimal exposure to lead paint issues during pre-licensing education. Brokerages that fail to address this topic during onboarding are accepting unnecessary risk — especially given how many transactions in communities like Portsmouth, Concord, Keene, and Manchester involve pre-1978 housing. ## The Intersection With New Hampshire's RSA 130-A

While the transactional disclosure obligation is federal, RSA 130-A creates additional context that agents should understand. Under this statute, the NH DHHS maintains a registry of properties with known lead hazards and issues lead hazard reduction orders when children under six are found to have elevated blood lead levels connected to a specific dwelling.

If a property has been subject to a lead hazard reduction order, that fact almost certainly constitutes a "known lead-based paint hazard" that must be disclosed on the federal form. Sellers who conceal an active or resolved DHHS order are exposing themselves — and potentially their agents — to both federal disclosure liability and state enforcement under RSA 130-A:6-a.

Agents listing properties with a history of lead orders should request documentation of completed abatement or interim controls and include those records in the disclosure package. This is not optional generosity — it is what "available records and reports" means under 24 CFR 35.88(a)(2).

## Practical Workflow for Compliant Transactions

For agents who want to get this right every time, the workflow is straightforward. At listing intake, confirm the build year from tax records (not just the MLS data, which can be inaccurate). If the property was built before 1978, prepare the EPA/HUD disclosure form immediately and have the seller complete their portion — disclosing known hazards, attaching any reports, or affirming they have no knowledge of lead-based paint.

When an offer comes in, ensure the buyer receives the completed disclosure form, the EPA pamphlet, and any available records before signing the purchase agreement or simultaneously with contract execution. The contract itself should include language granting the 10-day inspection opportunity or a written waiver. Do not rely on boilerplate that merely references "inspections" generically — the lead inspection period is a distinct federal right.

After ratification, retain signed copies in both the listing and buyer-side files. If you are using a transaction coordinator or [managing multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously, build a system that tags pre-1978 properties for lead paint compliance verification at each milestone.
