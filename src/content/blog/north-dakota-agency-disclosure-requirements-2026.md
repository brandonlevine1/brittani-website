## Why North Dakota Agency Disclosure Requirements 2026 Demand Your Attention

The North Dakota agency disclosure requirements 2026 remain one of the most misunderstood compliance obligations in the state. Every year, the North Dakota Real Estate Commission opens disciplinary files on agents who either delivered their disclosure late or used outdated language. The stakes are not abstract—they include license suspension, deal rescission, and personal liability.

North Dakota Century Code (NDCC) Chapter 43-23 governs real estate licensee conduct, with Section 43-23-12.1 specifically mandating how and when agents must disclose their agency relationships. The statute requires written disclosure to all parties at the earliest substantive contact, which the Commission interprets as the moment a conversation moves beyond general market information. If you are still handing over paperwork at the purchase agreement stage, you are already late.

## The Statutory Framework: NDCC 43-23-12.1

**Section 43-23-12.1 of the North Dakota Century Code is the single statute that controls agency disclosure for every residential and commercial transaction in the state.** It establishes three permissible agency relationships: seller's agent, buyer's agent, and dual agent. The law further requires that licensees provide a written disclosure describing the nature of these relationships before any confidential information is exchanged.

The [North Dakota Real Estate Commission](https://www.realestatend.org/) enforces this statute through its complaint and disciplinary process. Violations fall under NDCC 43-23-11.1, which lists grounds for disciplinary action including "failure to make disclosures required by law." Penalties range from a formal reprimand to license revocation, depending on the severity and pattern of noncompliance.

North Dakota does not use a single state-mandated form number in the way some states do. Instead, the Commission requires that the disclosure form meet the content standards of NDCC 43-23-12.1. In practice, most agents rely on the standardized form published by NDREALTORS (the North Dakota Association of REALTORS), which is periodically updated to reflect statutory changes. The current version includes signature lines for acknowledgment by the consumer and a checkbox section identifying the type of agency being offered.

## Timing: When the Disclosure Must Be Delivered

Timing is where most violations occur. The statute says disclosure must happen at the "earliest substantive contact." The Commission's administrative rules clarify that substantive contact means any interaction where the licensee begins to act in a capacity that could create an implied agency—showing property, discussing pricing strategy, or soliciting confidential financial details.

| Scenario | Disclosure Required By |
|----------|----------------------|
| Buyer calls asking about a listing you hold | Before discussing the buyer's financial position or motivation |
| Seller contacts you about listing their home | Before you begin a CMA or discuss pricing strategy |
| Open house visitor asks you to write an offer | Before accepting any offer instructions |
| Dual agency arises mid-transaction | Before continuing to act for both parties |
| Referral from another agent in your brokerage | At your first substantive contact with the referred client |

Handing the form to a buyer at the offer stage does not satisfy the statute if you already showed them three properties and discussed their loan pre-approval. The Commission has issued guidance letters making this explicit.

## Dual Agency: Written Consent Is Non-Negotiable

**Dual agency is legal in North Dakota, but only with the informed written consent of both parties.** NDCC 43-23-12.1(3) requires that agents disclose the specific limitations dual agency places on their duties—primarily, the inability to advocate for one party's interests over the other's.

The NDREALTORS dual agency consent addendum is a separate document from the initial agency disclosure. Agents must obtain signatures from both the buyer and seller before performing any acts as a dual agent. Simply disclosing the possibility of dual agency at the start of a listing agreement is not enough. The written consent must come at the point dual agency actually arises.

This distinction trips up agents in small markets across North Dakota, where a listing agent frequently ends up working with an unrepresented buyer. If you find yourself in that position, you need both parties to sign the dual agency consent addendum before you draft the purchase agreement. Proceeding without it exposes you to disciplinary action and gives either party a potential exit from the contract.

For agents working in states with different dual agency rules, the contrast is worth studying. The approach in [South Dakota](/blog/south-dakota-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) shares some similarities, while [Montana's framework](/blog/montana-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) diverges in key areas around designated agency.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in North Dakota

Five specific errors show up repeatedly in Commission disciplinary actions and audit findings.

**First, agents disclose too late.** They wait until the purchase agreement is being drafted rather than at first substantive contact. The Commission has sustained complaints even when the consumer ultimately suffered no financial harm, because the statute imposes a bright-line timing rule.

Second, agents use outdated forms. The NDREALTORS disclosure form is revised when statutory language changes. Agents who print a stack of forms and use them for years without checking for updates risk using language that no longer matches the statute. The Commission does not grandfather old forms.

Third, agents conflate the initial agency disclosure with the dual agency consent. These are two separate obligations. The initial disclosure explains the types of agency available. The dual agency consent is a transaction-specific agreement that must be executed only when dual agency actually occurs. Combining them into a single signature event—or skipping the consent because "they already signed the disclosure"—is a violation.

Fourth, agents fail to get signatures from both parties in a dual agency situation. One signature is not enough. Both the buyer and seller must acknowledge and consent in writing.

Fifth, agents working as teams sometimes assume that the team leader's disclosure covers the entire team. It does not. Each individual licensee who provides services to a client must ensure that the disclosure is properly documented under their own interactions.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of noncompliance operate on three tracks: regulatory, civil, and transactional.

On the regulatory track, the North Dakota Real Estate Commission can impose fines, require continuing education, suspend a license for a defined period, or revoke a license entirely. NDCC 43-23-11.1 gives the Commission broad authority. A first offense with no consumer harm might result in a letter of reprimand and mandatory education. Repeated violations or violations that cause harm escalate quickly.

On the civil track, an aggrieved party can sue for damages. If a buyer can show that an undisclosed dual agency led them to overpay—or that the agent's undisclosed loyalty to the seller caused them to waive inspections they otherwise would not have waived—the damages claim can be substantial. North Dakota courts have recognized that agency disclosure failures can support claims of breach of fiduciary duty, fraud, and negligent misrepresentation.

On the transactional track, a failure to disclose can give the affected party grounds to rescind the purchase agreement before closing. Even after closing, undisclosed agency conflicts have been used as the basis for post-closing litigation. The risk does not disappear once the deed is recorded.

| Consequence | Regulatory | Civil | Transactional |
|-------------|-----------|-------|---------------|
| First offense, no harm | Reprimand or fine | Unlikely | Low risk |
| Repeated offense | Suspension or revocation | Moderate exposure | Moderate risk |
| Offense causing financial harm | Revocation likely | High exposure, damages | Rescission possible |
| Dual agency without consent | Suspension minimum | Breach of fiduciary claim | Contract voidable |

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under NDCC 43-23-11.1(16), which lists "failing to exercise reasonable supervision" as independent grounds for discipline. A broker cannot rely on agents self-certifying their own compliance.

**Every brokerage in North Dakota should maintain a system that confirms agency disclosure forms are signed and dated before any purchase agreement is executed.** This means building a file review checkpoint—either manual or automated—that flags transactions missing a signed disclosure.

Brokers should audit for five specific items. Is the form current and consistent with the latest NDREALTORS version? Is it signed by the consumer, not just the agent? Does the date on the disclosure precede the date on any purchase agreement or listing agreement? In dual agency situations, is there a separate dual agency consent signed by both parties? And does the file contain evidence that disclosure happened at first substantive contact rather than at the offer stage?

Quarterly file audits catch problems before the Commission does. Brokers who rely on annual reviews often discover noncompliance only after a complaint has already been filed.
