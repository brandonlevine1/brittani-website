## Understanding California Continuing Education Requirements 2026

The California Department of Real Estate (DRE) does not offer grace periods, amnesty windows, or gentle reminders when your license renewal deadline arrives. If you are a licensed salesperson or broker in California, the continuing education requirements 2026 cycle demands the same 45 hours of approved coursework that prior cycles did, but with updated topic mandates and enforcement priorities that reflect recent legislative changes. Missing these requirements does not just pause your career — it exposes you, your brokerage, and your clients to legal and financial risk.

California's CE framework is governed by the Real Estate Law, found in Division 4 of the Business and Professions Code (Sections 10170 through 10170.5). The DRE, formerly known as the Bureau of Real Estate, administers all licensing and renewal activity. Every four years, licensees must submit proof of completion using the appropriate renewal application — Form RE 209 for salespersons and Form RE 209B for brokers.

Understanding what is required and when saves agents from scrambling in the final weeks before a deadline. It also saves brokers from the compliance headaches that come with supervising agents whose paperwork is incomplete or whose licenses quietly lapse mid-transaction.

## Mandatory Course Breakdown for the 2026 Renewal Cycle

**California separates its CE requirements into mandatory topics and elective hours, and the split differs depending on whether you are completing a first renewal or a subsequent renewal.** For agents renewing their license for the first time after passing the exam, all 45 hours must come from DRE-mandated topics. Subsequent renewals require 18 hours of mandatory coursework and 27 hours of approved electives.

The mandatory courses for subsequent renewals include the following subject areas, each at a minimum of three hours: Ethics, Agency, Fair Housing, Trust Fund Handling, Risk Management, and Management and Supervision. First-time renewees must complete a longer prescribed list that also covers Real Estate Practice, Legal Aspects of Real Estate, Real Estate Finance, Real Estate Appraisal, and additional specified topics outlined in Business and Professions Code Section 10170.5.

All courses must be completed through a provider approved by the DRE. The Department maintains a searchable list of approved schools and courses on its website at [dre.ca.gov](https://www.dre.ca.gov/Licensees/ContinuingEducation.html). Online courses are permitted, but they must include timed progress tracking and a final examination to qualify.

| Renewal Type | Total Hours | Mandatory Hours | Elective Hours | Key Distinction |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| First Renewal (Salesperson) | 45 | 45 | 0 | All topics prescribed by statute |
| Subsequent Renewal (Salesperson) | 45 | 18 | 27 | Six mandatory topics at 3 hours each |
| Broker Renewal | 45 | 18 | 27 | Same mandatory topics plus supervision emphasis |
| Late Renewal (within 2 years of expiration) | 45 | Same as applicable type | Same | Must also pay late fee per Section 10202.1 |

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, Liability, and Deal Cancellation

**Failing to complete CE before your expiration date results in an immediate lapse of your license — not a suspension, not a warning.** Under Business and Professions Code Section 10153.4, a person operating under a lapsed license is performing unlicensed activity. The DRE can impose fines, issue a citation under Section 10081, or pursue disciplinary action ranging from a restricted license to outright revocation.

The financial exposure goes beyond DRE penalties. If an agent closes a deal while technically unlicensed, the transaction can be challenged. Buyers or sellers may seek to rescind the contract, and the agent's commission becomes unenforceable. Courts have held that contracts facilitated by unlicensed individuals may be voidable under Civil Code Section 1624 and related case law.

Errors and Omissions insurance may also deny coverage for claims arising from activity performed during a license lapse. This leaves agents personally exposed to lawsuits from clients who discover their representative was non-compliant at the time of the transaction. The downstream effect on the brokerage's reputation and regulatory standing compounds the problem, especially if [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) are affected simultaneously.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With California CE

The most frequent compliance failures are not dramatic — they are administrative oversights that accumulate into serious problems. Here are five specific errors the DRE encounters repeatedly.

**First, agents confuse course completion with DRE reporting.** Finishing a course through an approved provider does not automatically update your DRE record. While most approved schools electronically report completions, delays and errors occur. Agents must verify their own records through the DRE's eLicensing portal before submitting their renewal application on Form RE 209.

Second, agents take courses from non-approved providers and assume they count. National CE platforms that offer multi-state packages often include California-labeled courses that lack actual DRE approval numbers. Every qualifying course must carry a specific DRE approval code that matches the Department's database. Taking a three-hour ethics course from an unapproved source means you still owe three hours of ethics.

Third, agents submit their renewal application without all 45 hours completed, expecting to finish during a processing window. The DRE does not grant conditional renewals. Your application will be returned, and your license will expire if the deadline passes before resubmission.

Fourth, first-time renewees mistakenly complete the subsequent renewal curriculum, mixing mandatory and elective hours when they should be completing 45 hours of entirely mandatory topics. This error requires starting over with the correct courses.

Fifth, agents change their address or personal information without filing Form RE 214 (Change of Address) with the DRE. Renewal notices and compliance communications get lost, and the agent misses their deadline entirely because they never received the reminder correspondence. Staying ahead of [common deadline pitfalls](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) requires proactive record management, not reliance on mail.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Under Business and Professions Code Section 10159.2, the designated broker-officer of any real estate corporation or the responsible broker of a sole proprietorship bears supervisory liability for every agent operating under their license. This includes verifying that each agent's license is current, active, and properly renewed.

Brokers should implement a recurring audit process — quarterly at minimum — that checks each affiliated agent's license status through the DRE's public license lookup tool. Waiting until a transaction goes sideways to discover an agent's license lapsed three months ago is indefensible in a regulatory hearing. The DRE has historically viewed broker ignorance of agent compliance failures as evidence of inadequate supervision, not as a mitigating factor.

| Broker Responsibility | Frequency | Method | Risk if Missed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Verify agent license status | Quarterly | DRE eLicensing lookup | Vicarious liability for unlicensed activity |
| Confirm CE completion before renewal deadline | 90 days prior to each agent's expiration | Request certificates or check DRE records | Agent lapse during active transactions |
| Maintain supervision records | Ongoing | Internal compliance file per agent | Disciplinary action under Section 10159.2 |
| File RE 233 (Salesperson Change) if terminating non-compliant agent | Immediately upon discovery | Submit to DRE | Continued liability for agent's actions |

**Brokers who terminate a non-compliant agent must immediately file Form RE 233 (Notification of Salesperson Change) with the DRE.** Failure to disassociate promptly can extend the broker's liability for any transactions the agent attempts after termination. The form must be filed within a reasonable time frame; the DRE interprets delays unfavorably.

Documentation is protection. Brokers should maintain written records showing they informed agents of renewal deadlines, confirmed CE progress, and took corrective action when compliance gaps emerged. These records become evidence in any DRE audit or administrative proceeding.

## The Late Renewal Window and How It Works

California provides a two-year grace period after license expiration during which a licensee can apply for late renewal. This does not mean you can practice during those two years. It means you can restore your license without retaking the state examination, provided you complete all CE hours and pay an additional late fee as prescribed in Section 10202.1.

After two years, restoration is no longer an option. You must re-apply as a new applicant, satisfy current education requirements (including pre-license courses), and pass the DRE examination again. For agents who have been licensed for decades, this is an expensive and time-consuming reset that erases accumulated renewal history.

> Business and Professions Code Section 10170.5 states: "A real estate license which has expired may be renewed at any time within two years after its expiration upon filing of the proper form, payment of the fees required, and proof of completion of continuing education."

The late renewal fee is currently equal to the original renewal fee plus 50 percent. Combined with the cost of CE courses and lost income during the lapse period, the total expense of missing a deadline far exceeds the modest investment of completing coursework on schedule.
