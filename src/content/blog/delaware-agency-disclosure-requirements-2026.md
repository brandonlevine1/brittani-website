## Understanding Delaware Agency Disclosure Requirements 2026

Delaware agency disclosure requirements 2026 carry real teeth for agents who ignore them or treat them as afterthoughts. The Delaware Real Estate Commission (DREC) enforces these rules under Title 24, Chapter 29 of the Delaware Code, and violations can torpedo a deal faster than a failed inspection. Every licensee working in the First State needs to know exactly which forms to present, when to present them, and what specific language the state demands.

The statutory framework is rooted in [24 Del. C. Section 2913](https://delcode.delaware.gov/title24/c029/sc01/index.html), which mandates that agents disclose their agency relationship to all parties before performing brokerage services. This is not optional guidance from a trade association. It is black-letter law enforced by a commission that has shown increasing willingness to issue fines and suspensions for paperwork failures.

Unlike some neighboring states that allow verbal disclosure followed by written confirmation, Delaware requires the written disclosure up front. The form itself must be presented and acknowledged before any confidential information changes hands between agent and client.

## The Specific Forms Delaware Agents Must Use

**Delaware uses a standardized Agency Disclosure Form prescribed by DREC, and agents cannot substitute their own versions.** The current form is referenced in DREC Rule 8.0 and must include a clear explanation of the three agency relationships recognized in Delaware: seller's agent, buyer's agent, and dual agent.

The primary form agents use is the Delaware Agency Relationship Disclosure, which brokerages typically file as part of their transaction packet. In addition, when dual agency arises, agents must obtain signatures on a separate Dual Agency Consent Form that explicitly names both parties and outlines the limitations on the agent's duties.

| Form | Purpose | Timing | Who Signs |
|------|---------|--------|-----------|
| Agency Relationship Disclosure | Disclose agent's role to consumer | Before brokerage services begin | Consumer and licensee |
| Dual Agency Consent Form | Obtain informed consent for dual agency | Before dual agency commences | Both buyer and seller |
| Designated Agency Agreement | Assign specific agents within one brokerage | At point of dual agency situation | Broker, both designated agents, both clients |

The designated agency option under 24 Del. C. Section 2913(g) allows a broker to assign individual licensees within the same firm to represent buyer and seller separately. This requires its own written agreement and cannot simply be folded into the general disclosure form.

## Timing Rules That Trip Agents Up

The statutory language is precise: disclosure must occur "at the first scheduled meeting" with a prospective buyer or seller, or before the consumer discloses confidential motivation, financial qualification, or pricing strategy. Whichever comes first controls the deadline.

For buyer's agents, this typically means disclosure happens at the initial consultation or, at the latest, before the first property showing where substantive conversation occurs. For listing agents, it means disclosure at or before the listing presentation. The Commission has taken the position that a casual open house visit does not trigger the requirement unless the agent begins providing individualized brokerage advice.

**Agents who work open houses for other brokerages face a specific trap.** If a visitor begins discussing their purchasing plans, timeline, or financial situation, the hosting agent has triggered the disclosure obligation even though they are technically representing the seller. The smart practice is to have blank Agency Disclosure Forms available at every open house.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance in Delaware fall into three categories: regulatory, civil, and transactional. None of them are trivial.

On the regulatory side, DREC can impose fines up to $5,000 per violation under 24 Del. C. Section 2920. The Commission can also suspend or revoke a license, require additional education hours, or place a licensee on probation. Repeat offenders face escalating penalties, and DREC publishes disciplinary actions publicly.

Civil liability is the larger financial risk. A buyer or seller who discovers they were in an undisclosed dual agency situation can pursue damages for breach of fiduciary duty. Delaware courts have recognized that undisclosed dual agency is a per se breach, meaning the aggrieved party does not need to prove actual harm to the transaction — only that the disclosure was missing. This can result in commission disgorgement, rescission of the contract, or compensatory damages.

From a transactional standpoint, a missing agency disclosure gives either party grounds to challenge the enforceability of the purchase agreement. Title companies increasingly flag incomplete agency paperwork during their pre-closing audits, and a missing form at that stage can delay or kill a closing.

## Common Mistakes Delaware Agents Make

Five specific errors account for the majority of DREC complaints related to agency disclosure. Knowing them is the difference between a clean file and a commission hearing.

**First, agents present the disclosure form but fail to collect a signature before discussing pricing strategy.** The conversation happens naturally — a seller mentions what they paid for the house, or a buyer reveals their maximum budget — and the agent has already received confidential information without documenting the agency relationship. The form sitting unsigned in a folder does not satisfy the statute.

Second, teams within a brokerage fail to identify dual agency situations when one team member lists a property and another team member's buyer wants to write an offer. The brokerage is a single entity under Delaware law, and this triggers dual agency regardless of whether the two agents share an office or even know each other. The Dual Agency Consent Form must be executed before the offer is submitted.

Third, agents confuse "customer" service with "client" representation. Delaware distinguishes between providing ministerial acts to a customer (such as opening a door or handing over a flyer) and providing brokerage services to a client. The moment an agent offers opinions on value, negotiation strategy, or comparable sales, they have crossed into brokerage services and the disclosure clock starts.

Fourth, agents rely on their brokerage's onboarding paperwork and assume the buyer representation agreement covers the agency disclosure requirement. These are separate obligations under Delaware law. The agency disclosure explains the types of relationships available; the representation agreement formalizes the chosen relationship. Both must exist in the file. The [NAR settlement changes](/blog/nar-settlement-year-one) have made this distinction even more operationally important.

Fifth, agents working with FSBOs or unrepresented parties fail to disclose that they represent only the buyer. The seller in a FSBO transaction must receive written disclosure that the agent owes no fiduciary duty to them. Assuming the seller "knows" the agent represents the buyer is not a defense before DREC.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Delaware bear supervisory liability under 24 Del. C. Section 2912(a)(6) for the actions of their affiliated licensees. A broker who fails to implement systems for compliance can face their own disciplinary proceedings independent of the agent's violation.

**Every broker should audit transaction files for three specific items at minimum: a signed Agency Disclosure Form dated before or concurrent with the first substantive contact, a Dual Agency Consent Form in every file where the brokerage represents both sides, and documentation that unrepresented parties received disclosure of the agent's role.** These three checkpoints catch the vast majority of compliance gaps.

Brokers should also implement a policy requiring agents to submit disclosure forms within 24 hours of first client contact — not at the time of offer submission. Waiting until the offer stage to collect paperwork is a common brokerage-level failure. By then, weeks of confidential information may have flowed without documentation.

Quarterly file audits are the industry standard for well-run brokerages. Random sampling of 10-15 percent of active files per quarter will identify systemic training gaps before they become DREC complaints. Brokers who discover a missing disclosure mid-transaction should have a written protocol for remediation, including immediate client notification and a late-signed acknowledgment with a contemporaneous memo to the file explaining the circumstances.
