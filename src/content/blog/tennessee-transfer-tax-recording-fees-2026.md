## Tennessee Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026: The Numbers That Close Deals

Getting the transfer tax and recording fee wrong does not just embarrass you at the closing table—it can stall a deal, expose your brokerage to regulatory scrutiny, and cost your client hundreds of dollars they did not budget. Understanding Tennessee transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 is table-stakes knowledge for any agent closing residential transactions in this state. The rates themselves are straightforward, but the compliance details around affidavits, exemptions, and local surcharges trip up even experienced licensees.

Tennessee's transfer tax framework is governed primarily by Tennessee Code Annotated (T.C.A.) Section 67-4-409, which sets the state rate, and T.C.A. Section 67-4-410, which authorizes county legislative bodies to levy an additional local tax. The Tennessee Department of Revenue administers the state portion, while individual county registers of deeds handle collection and recording. Agents who treat this as a title company problem alone are ignoring their fiduciary duty to prepare clients for accurate closing costs from the moment they sign a listing or buyer representation agreement.

## State and Local Rate Breakdown for 2026

**The state transfer tax rate in Tennessee remains $0.37 per $100 of the property's sale price or consideration.** This has not changed in several years, though legislative proposals surface periodically. The tax applies to the total consideration, rounded up to the next $100 increment—meaning a $325,050 sale triggers tax on $325,100.

Local transfer taxes vary by county and are authorized up to specific caps set by statute. Most counties that impose a local tax charge between $0.01 and $0.15 per $100. Davidson County (Nashville), for instance, levies $0.037 per $100 in addition to the state rate. Knox County adds $0.0375. These small percentages add up quickly on six-figure transactions.

| Jurisdiction | Rate per $100 | Tax on $400,000 Sale | Collected By |
|---|---|---|---|
| State of Tennessee | $0.37 | $1,480 | County Register (remitted to state) |
| Davidson County | $0.037 | $148 | County Register |
| Knox County | $0.0375 | $150 | County Register |
| Shelby County | $0.01 | $40 | County Register |
| Hamilton County | $0.01 | $40 | County Register |

Recording fees are separate from the transfer tax. The base recording fee for deeds in Tennessee is set by T.C.A. Section 8-21-1001 and is currently $10 for the first page and $5 for each additional page. A deed of trust follows the same schedule. Many registers also charge a $2 automation fee per instrument under T.C.A. Section 8-21-1002, plus county-specific archive fees that typically run $5-$10 per document.

## Required Forms and Filing Procedures

Every deed submitted for recording in Tennessee must be accompanied by the Real Estate Transfer Tax Affidavit, sometimes called the Affidavit of Consideration or Value. The Tennessee Department of Revenue does not assign a single universal form number to this affidavit—counties often provide their own versions—but the content requirements are standardized under T.C.A. Section 67-4-409(b). The affidavit must state the actual consideration paid, identify the parties, describe the property, and declare whether any exemption applies.

If the transaction qualifies for an exemption under T.C.A. Section 67-4-409(b)(1) through (b)(22), the affidavit must cite the specific exemption subsection. Common exemptions include transfers between spouses, transfers to revocable trusts for the same beneficial owner, and transfers where the consideration is less than $100. Agents regularly encounter the $100-or-less exemption in quitclaim deeds among family members but incorrectly assume it applies to all quitclaims—it does not.

**The register of deeds will refuse to accept any deed for recording if the affidavit is missing or incomplete.** This refusal is not discretionary; T.C.A. Section 67-4-409(d) mandates it. An unrecorded deed leaves your client without constructive notice of ownership, which is a catastrophic title defect. For agents involved in [transactions where paperwork accuracy is paramount](/blog/why-agents-hate-paperwork), this is the exact scenario that erodes client trust.

## Who Pays What: Custom vs. Contract

Tennessee law does not assign the transfer tax obligation to a specific party. T.C.A. Section 67-4-409 imposes the tax on the "transfer" itself, leaving the allocation to contractual agreement. In practice, statewide custom places the full transfer tax burden on the seller, but this varies in competitive markets where buyers offer to absorb costs as deal sweeteners.

Recording fees for the deed are typically a buyer expense because the buyer holds the instrument after closing. Recording fees for the deed of trust (mortgage) are always a buyer cost since that instrument secures the buyer's loan. Release recording fees for the seller's existing mortgage payoff are a seller expense.

| Fee Type | Customary Payor | Contractual Override? |
|---|---|---|
| State Transfer Tax | Seller | Yes |
| Local Transfer Tax | Seller | Yes |
| Deed Recording Fee | Buyer | Yes |
| Deed of Trust Recording Fee | Buyer | Yes |
| Release of Lien Recording Fee | Seller | Rarely |
| Transfer Tax Affidavit Prep | Seller (via closing agent) | Yes |

Agents should document any deviation from custom explicitly in the Tennessee Association of Realtors (TAR) Purchase and Sale Agreement, specifically in the Additional Terms section. Ambiguity here creates disputes at the closing table that delay funding. If you are coordinating closings where [deadline management drives the outcome](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), fee allocation disputes can cascade into extension requests and appraisal expiration issues.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Transfer Tax and Recording Fees

**Mistake one: quoting the wrong county's local rate.** Agents who work across multiple Tennessee counties—especially those spanning the Nashville MSA's Davidson, Williamson, Rutherford, and Sumner counties—frequently default to one rate for all listings. Williamson County's local tax rate differs from Davidson County's, and misquoting costs at listing presentation undermines your credibility and net sheet accuracy.

Mistake two: assuming all family transfers are exempt. The exemption under T.C.A. Section 67-4-409(b)(4) covers transfers between spouses, but a transfer from parent to child does not automatically qualify unless it meets the specific criteria of another enumerated exemption. Agents who advise clients that a quitclaim deed to a family member avoids transfer tax are providing legal advice they are not licensed to give—and often incorrect advice at that.

Mistake three: omitting the affidavit when submitting documents for recording. Some agents who handle their own recording submissions (common in rural counties without a title company managing the process) forget that the affidavit is a separate document from the deed. The register rejects the package, and the agent must make a second trip or mailing.

Mistake four: failing to account for recording fee increases mid-year. Counties can adjust automation fees and archive surcharges without legislative session action in some cases. An agent who estimated closing costs in March using the prior year's schedule may deliver an inaccurate estimate at a June closing. Always verify current fees directly with the county register's office within 30 days of anticipated closing.

Mistake five: calculating transfer tax on the wrong number. The tax applies to consideration, not appraised value. If a property sells for $380,000 with a $5,000 seller credit, the consideration for transfer tax purposes is still $380,000—the credit does not reduce it. Agents who deduct credits from the taxable amount create underpayment situations that the register catches at submission.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) rules. Rule 1260-02-.01(3) requires brokers to supervise affiliated licensees' transaction activities, which includes ensuring that estimated closing cost disclosures reflect accurate transfer tax and recording fee amounts. A pattern of inaccurate net sheets from one agent can trigger a TREC audit of the entire brokerage.

**Brokers should implement quarterly audits of closed transaction files to confirm that transfer tax affidavits were properly executed and recorded.** If the closing agent handled this, the file should still contain a copy of the recorded affidavit or recording confirmation. Missing documentation suggests either sloppy file management or a compliance gap that TREC investigators will notice during a routine office inspection.

Training is not optional. Brokers who manage agents working in multiple counties should maintain an internal reference document listing current transfer tax rates and recording fee schedules for every county where the brokerage operates. This document should be updated at least annually and distributed with [the same rigor as checklist enforcement](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) for other compliance items. TREC can impose fines up to $1,000 per violation under T.C.A. Section 62-13-313 for brokers who fail to adequately supervise, and repeated offenses can result in license suspension.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

The most immediate consequence of unpaid transfer tax is recording refusal. Without recording, the chain of title breaks. Title insurance companies will not insure an unrecorded deed, and lenders will not fund a loan secured by an unrecorded deed of trust. The transaction effectively fails to close.

If transfer tax is underpaid—because the affidavit stated incorrect consideration—the Tennessee Department of Revenue can assess the deficiency plus interest and penalties under T.C.A. Section 67-1-801. The penalty for underpayment is 5% of the unpaid tax for each month the deficiency exists, capped at 25%. The register of deeds may also refer the matter to the county attorney for collection.

For the agent personally, providing materially inaccurate closing cost estimates can constitute a violation of TREC's standards of conduct. Rule 1260-02-.12(3)(b) prohibits making substantial misrepresentations concerning a transaction. If a buyer or seller relies on your estimate, budgets accordingly, and then discovers a $1,500 transfer tax they were never told about, the complaint to TREC writes itself. The [Tennessee Real Estate Commission](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regboards/trec.html) publishes disciplinary actions monthly, and transfer-tax-related complaints, while less dramatic than trust account violations, do appear.

## Practical Steps for Accurate Compliance in 2026

Build a county-specific fee sheet for every market you serve. Call the register's office in January each year and again in July to confirm that no mid-year surcharge changes took effect. This demonstrates professionalism and eliminates the surprise factor at closing.
