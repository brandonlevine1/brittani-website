## Why Florida HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Florida has more homeowner associations and condominium communities than any other state—roughly 50,000 associations governing over nine million residents. If you are a licensed agent working transactions in these communities, the Florida HOA & condo disclosure rules 2026 directly affect how and when you can close. Getting a single document wrong does not just annoy the title company; it hands the buyer a legal escape hatch and exposes you and your brokerage to liability.

The stakes increased after the Florida Legislature amended several sections of Chapters 718, 719, and 720 of the Florida Statutes in recent sessions, tightening timelines and adding new financial reporting obligations for associations. Agents who still rely on memory or outdated checklists are playing a dangerous game. This post breaks down the specific statutes, the forms you should be using, the consequences of noncompliance, and what your broker should be checking on every community-association file.

## The Statutes That Control Your Transaction

**Three chapters of the Florida Statutes govern community-association disclosures, and each carries its own delivery requirements.** Chapter 718 (the Florida Condominium Act) covers condominiums. Chapter 719 covers cooperative apartments. Chapter 720 (the Homeowners' Association Act) covers traditional HOA communities.

For condominium resales, Florida Statute 718.503 mandates that the seller provide the buyer with specific documents including the declaration of condominium, articles of incorporation, bylaws, rules, most recent year-end financial statement, and the frequently asked questions and answers sheet (FAQ sheet) required under Section 718.504. The buyer then has three days after receiving these documents to rescind the contract without penalty.

For HOA communities, Florida Statute 720.401 requires delivery of a disclosure summary—not the full governing documents—before or at the time the purchase agreement is executed. That summary must include specific language advising the buyer of their obligation to pay assessments, the association's right to impose fines, and the buyer's right to obtain and review the full governing documents. If the summary is not timely provided, the buyer may void the contract within three days of receiving it.

## Key Forms and Documents Agents Must Track

The [Florida Realtors](https://www.floridarealtors.org/) and Florida Bar jointly publish the standard contract forms used in most residential transactions. The relevant forms for community-association deals include the Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (FR/Bar), which contains Rider U (Homeowners' Association/Community Disclosure) and Rider O (Condominium). These riders trigger the disclosure obligations and set contractual deadlines that layer on top of the statutory requirements.

| Document | Governing Statute | Delivery Deadline | Buyer Rescission Period |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Condo resale documents package | 718.503(1) | Before or within specified contract period | 3 days after receipt |
| HOA disclosure summary | 720.401 | Before or at contract execution | 3 days after receipt if late |
| Condo estoppel certificate | 718.116(8) | 10 business days from request | N/A (financial clearance doc) |
| HOA estoppel certificate | 720.30851 | 10 business days from request | N/A (financial clearance doc) |
| Coop disclosure documents | 719.503 | Before or at contract execution | 3 days after receipt |

**The estoppel certificate is not a disclosure document in the traditional sense, but it is the single most deal-critical association document you will handle.** It confirms what the seller owes the association as of a specific date. Title companies will not close without one. Under Florida Statute 720.30851, an association may charge up to $250 for a standard estoppel and an additional $150 for expedited (three business day) delivery or when the account is delinquent.

Agents should also be aware of the DBPR Form RE-13 (Condominium Rider to Contract), which the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation references in licensing-exam materials and continuing-education courses. While most agents use the FR/Bar forms in practice, familiarity with the DBPR's expectations protects you during any regulatory complaint.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The consequences of missing a disclosure are not hypothetical. They fall into three categories: deal cancellation, civil liability, and disciplinary action.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk. If a condo buyer does not receive the documents required under Section 718.503, the buyer retains the right to void the contract for up to 15 days after execution—or three days after finally receiving the documents, whichever is later. That means a buyer who gets cold feet at day 12 can walk away with full deposit refund if you never delivered the association package.

Civil liability is the longer tail. Florida courts have held that a seller's agent who fails to deliver required disclosures may be liable for the buyer's damages, including costs incurred in reliance on the contract. Brokerage errors-and-omissions insurance may cover some claims, but carriers increasingly scrutinize whether the agent followed established procedures. If the brokerage had no written process, expect a coverage fight.

Disciplinary action comes from the Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) under Section 475.25 of the Florida Statutes. Failing to account for and deliver documents related to a transaction can be classified as a violation of professional standards. FREC can impose fines up to $5,000 per violation, suspend or revoke a license, and require additional education. The cost of tracking a few documents is trivial compared to defending a FREC complaint—something [we've explored in the context of blown closings](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) before.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With HOA and Condo Disclosures

**Five errors appear repeatedly in Florida transaction files, and each one is avoidable with a consistent workflow.** Here is what goes wrong:

First, agents confuse the HOA disclosure summary with the full governing documents. The statute only requires a summary at contract, but many agents either deliver nothing or dump hundreds of pages on the buyer without the statutory summary language—neither satisfies Section 720.401. The summary must contain specific statutory language verbatim; paraphrasing it does not count.

Second, agents order the estoppel certificate too late. Title companies typically need the estoppel at least one week before closing. If the association takes its full 10 business days to respond, and the agent did not request it until 7 calendar days before close, the math does not work. Smart agents request the estoppel as soon as the inspection period ends and the deal looks solid. Missed deadlines are a recurring theme [across every deal stage](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

Third, listing agents fail to verify whether the property is subject to more than one association. Many Florida communities have both a master association and a sub-association. Each one has its own disclosure requirements and its own estoppel. Missing the second association is embarrassing—and expensive if it delays closing.

Fourth, agents assume the buyer's attorney or title company will handle all association document delivery. The statutory obligation rests on the seller (and by extension the seller's agent who promised in the listing agreement to facilitate the transaction). Delegating without confirming completion creates a gap that surfaces at the worst possible moment.

Fifth, agents forget that the three-day rescission clock does not start until the buyer acknowledges receipt. If there is no signed acknowledgment in the file, the rescission window arguably never closes. That is a ticking liability that can detonate months after closing.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokerage compliance is not optional—Section 475.25(1)(e) holds brokers responsible for the transactions conducted under their license. If an agent in your office botches an HOA disclosure, FREC can pursue you alongside them.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|------------|--------------|-----------|
| HOA disclosure summary delivery | Signed acknowledgment in file before contract acceptance | Every HOA transaction |
| Condo document delivery | Delivery confirmation + 3-day rescission expiration documented | Every condo transaction |
| Estoppel request timing | Request sent within 5 days of effective date or inspection resolution | Every association transaction |
| Multiple-association identification | MLS data cross-referenced with title search for sub-associations | Every association transaction |
| Fee compliance | Estoppel fees verified against statutory caps ($250/$150) | Quarterly spot-check |

**Brokers should build a compliance checklist specific to community-association transactions and require its completion before any file is submitted for closing coordination.** The checklist does not need to be complex—it needs to be mandatory. Consider requiring agents to upload signed HOA/condo disclosure acknowledgments within 48 hours of contract execution.

Training matters too. If your office handles a high volume of association transactions, run a quarterly refresher that covers statutory updates and reviews actual near-miss examples from your files. The agents who struggle most are often newer licensees who have never read Chapters 718 or 720 outside of pre-licensing class. A good [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) bridges that knowledge gap between listing appointment and contract execution.

## The 2026 Regulatory Landscape for Florida Associations

Florida's legislative activity around condominiums accelerated after the 2021 Surfside building collapse, which triggered new structural-integrity reserve and milestone-inspection requirements under SB 4-D and its successor legislation. By 2026, many associations will have completed or be in the process of completing Structural Integrity Reserve Studies (SIRS), and the financial disclosures in resale packages will reflect dramatically higher reserve balances—or special assessments to fund them.

For agents, this means the documents you deliver now carry new financial weight. Buyers and their attorneys are scrutinizing the reserve schedules and recent assessment history far more carefully than they did five years ago. If your seller's association has not yet completed its SIRS or has underfunded reserves, expect buyer pushback during the three-day review period. You should proactively request the most recent reserve study and any pending special-assessment notices as part of your listing due diligence.

The Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR), Division of Condominiums, Timeshares, and Mobile Homes, oversees association compliance with these new requirements. Agents can verify an association's filing status through the DBPR's online portal. If an association is out of compliance with its filing obligations, that is material information a buyer would want before committing to a purchase.
