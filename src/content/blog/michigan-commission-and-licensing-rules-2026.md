## Understanding Michigan Commission & Licensing Rules 2026

The Michigan commission & licensing rules 2026 carry real teeth for agents who treat compliance as an afterthought. The Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) has been tightening enforcement, and the penalties for violations now extend well beyond a sternly worded letter. Every licensee operating under the Michigan Occupational Code (Public Act 299 of 1980) needs to understand exactly where the lines are drawn this year.

Michigan's real estate licensing framework sits primarily within MCL 339.2501 through MCL 339.2518. These statutes govern who can earn a commission, how brokerages must supervise agents, and what disclosures are mandatory before money changes hands. The administrative rules under R 339.22301 through R 339.22325 fill in the operational details that trip agents up during audits.

**LARA's Bureau of Professional Licensing is the enforcement body you answer to, not your local board.** Your local association handles MLS disputes and ethics complaints, but license-level discipline comes from the state. The distinction matters when you are deciding how seriously to take a procedural shortcut.

## Licensing Structure and Renewal Requirements

Michigan issues three tiers of real estate licenses: salesperson, associate broker, and broker. A salesperson license requires 40 hours of pre-licensure education and passage of the PSI-administered state exam. Associate brokers must hold a salesperson license for at least 3 years and complete an additional 90 hours of education before sitting for the broker exam.

Renewal cycles run every 3 years from the date of original issuance. Each renewal requires 18 hours of continuing education, broken into mandated topic areas. At minimum, 2 hours must cover legal updates and 2 hours must address fair housing — the remaining 14 hours can come from approved elective courses.

Failure to renew on time triggers a lapsed status. You cannot practice, earn commissions, or even advertise yourself as licensed during lapse. If 3 years pass without renewal, MCL 339.2505 treats the license as revoked, meaning you start from scratch.

| License Tier | Pre-License Hours | Exam Required | CE Per Cycle | Renewal Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Salesperson | 40 | Yes (PSI) | 18 hours | 3 years |
| Associate Broker | 130 total | Yes (PSI) | 18 hours | 3 years |
| Broker | 130 total | Yes (PSI) | 18 hours | 3 years |

## Commission Rules: Who Pays Whom and How

**Michigan law is explicit: a salesperson or associate broker can only receive compensation from their own broker.** MCL 339.2512(1) prohibits licensees from accepting a commission or any form of valuable consideration from anyone other than the broker under whom they are licensed. This rule survives even post-closing — you cannot collect a referral fee directly from another agent's client.

The commission itself is negotiable and always has been. Nothing in Michigan statute sets a minimum or maximum rate. However, the agreement must be in writing. LARA Administrative Rule 339.22307 requires that all compensation arrangements be documented, and the listing agreement or buyer representation agreement serves as the controlling document.

After the NAR settlement changes took effect in 2024, Michigan brokerages updated their buyer representation agreements to explicitly state compensation terms before showing properties. The Michigan Realtors form known as the Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement (Form MR-3300) now includes fields for commission source, amount, and conditions under which it may be modified. Agents who skip this form or leave blanks are exposed to both disciplinary action and civil liability.

## Michigan Commission & Licensing Rules 2026: Disclosure Obligations

Michigan's agency disclosure requirements are codified in MCL 339.2517 and enforced through a specific form. The Agency Disclosure form must be presented at the first substantive contact with a prospective buyer or seller. "Substantive contact" is defined as any interaction where confidential information could be shared or where the agent provides services beyond general information.

The Michigan Realtors Association publishes the standard Disclosure Regarding Real Estate Agency Relationships form (sometimes referred to internally as MR-5200 series). Agents must have the consumer sign this form, retain a copy, and provide one to the client. If a consumer refuses to sign, you note the refusal and the date directly on the form — but you still must present it.

> MCL 339.2517(3): "A licensee shall not provide services to a client or customer in a real estate transaction without first providing the written agency disclosure."

Failure here is not hypothetical. LARA enforcement actions from 2024 and 2025 show multiple agents receiving formal complaints specifically for missing or late agency disclosures. The penalty ranges from a formal reprimand to license suspension depending on whether the failure caused financial harm. ## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make

The following errors appear repeatedly in LARA disciplinary records and brokerage audit findings. None of them are ambiguous — they are clear rule violations that agents commit out of habit, haste, or ignorance.

**First: practicing on a lapsed license.** Agents forget their renewal date, continue showing homes and writing offers, then discover weeks later that their license lapsed. Every transaction conducted while lapsed is voidable, and LARA can pursue fines of up to $10,000 per violation under MCL 339.601(1). The broker shares liability if they failed to verify license status.

Second: commingling earnest money deposits. Administrative Rule 339.22315 requires that all deposit funds be placed into the broker's trust account within two business days of acceptance. Agents who hold checks "until the inspection" or deposit them into personal accounts violate this rule even if no harm ultimately occurs. The distinction between negligent commingling and intentional conversion determines whether you face suspension or revocation.

Third: advertising without broker identification. MCL 339.2512(5) requires that all advertising — including social media posts, yard signs, and digital ads — include the employing broker's name. Agents who run Facebook ads or Instagram stories under only their personal name violate this statute. LARA treats this as misleading advertising regardless of intent.

Fourth: failing to document dual agency consent. Michigan permits disclosed dual agency, but both parties must provide written consent using the form prescribed by the broker (typically MR-5200 series with the dual agency addendum). Verbal consent is legally meaningless. Agents who "get consent at closing" have already practiced undisclosed dual agency for the duration of the transaction.

Fifth: collecting referral fees without disclosure. Agents who receive compensation from ancillary service providers (home warranty companies, inspection firms, title companies) without written disclosure to all parties violate both RESPA at the federal level and MCL 339.2512 at the state level. The penalties stack.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under MCL 339.2512(3) for every agent operating under their license. This is not a theoretical burden — LARA routinely names brokers in disciplinary actions triggered by agent misconduct. The standard is whether the broker had adequate systems in place to detect and prevent the violation.

| Audit Area | Frequency | What to Check | Statute Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| License status verification | Monthly | Confirm active status via LARA online lookup | MCL 339.2505 |
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | Bank statement vs. transaction ledger | R 339.22315 |
| Agency disclosure compliance | Per transaction | Signed form in file before substantive contact | MCL 339.2517 |
| Advertising review | Quarterly | Broker name present on all agent marketing | MCL 339.2512(5) |
| Buyer representation agreements | Per transaction | Signed before showing, compensation terms complete | R 339.22307 |
| CE completion tracking | Annually | Hours logged, approved provider verification | MCL 339.2509 |

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance are building a house on sand.** The 2025 LARA enforcement bulletin specifically noted that "lack of supervisory systems" was cited as an aggravating factor in 60% of broker-level disciplinary actions from the prior year.

Transaction files should contain, at minimum: the signed agency disclosure, the listing or buyer representation agreement with commission terms, the purchase agreement, all addenda, proof of earnest money deposit within 2 business days, and closing documents. Missing any one of these during a LARA audit triggers a deficiency finding. ## Penalties and Liability for Non-Compliance

The penalty matrix in Michigan scales with severity and repetition. A first-time administrative violation — like a late CE renewal or missing disclosure — typically results in a formal complaint and possible fine of $500 to $2,500. Repeated violations, or violations causing consumer harm, escalate quickly.

License suspension removes your ability to practice for a defined period, typically 30 to 180 days. Revocation is permanent unless you petition for reinstatement after 5 years. In cases involving trust account violations or fraud, LARA refers the matter to the Michigan Attorney General's office for potential criminal prosecution under MCL 339.601 and MCL 339.604.

**Civil liability runs parallel to administrative penalties.** A buyer or seller harmed by an agent's non-compliance can sue for damages independently of any LARA action. Courts have awarded damages in Michigan based on undisclosed dual agency, failure to present offers, and misrepresentation of commission obligations. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond the immediate deal — it includes legal fees, E&O deductibles, and reputation damage that compounds over years.

Errors and omissions insurance covers some exposure, but policies typically exclude intentional acts, fraud, and violations of licensing law committed knowingly. An agent who practices on a lapsed license and causes harm may find their E&O carrier denying the claim entirely.
