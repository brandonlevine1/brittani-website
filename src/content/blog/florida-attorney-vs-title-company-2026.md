## Florida Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026

The question of Florida attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 trips up even experienced agents. Florida is one of a handful of states where the lines between what a title company can do and what constitutes the practice of law have been litigated repeatedly, yet no statute explicitly requires attorney attendance at a standard residential closing. Understanding the distinction is not optional — it directly affects your liability, your client's protection, and whether a deal actually records on time.

Florida's regulatory framework puts the burden on agents and brokers to ensure the closing entity is operating within its legal scope. Get it wrong, and you face more than an awkward conversation with your managing broker.

## The Statutory Framework Agents Must Know

**Florida Statute Section 626.8411 defines who qualifies as a title insurance agent and what they may lawfully do.** Under this statute, a licensed title agent may issue title insurance commitments, conduct closings, hold escrow funds, and prepare standard closing documents — but only those documents that are "incidental to" the title insurance transaction. The moment an act crosses into legal interpretation, it becomes the unauthorized practice of law under the Florida Supreme Court's jurisdiction.

The Florida Supreme Court, not the legislature, controls the definition of legal practice through The Florida Bar's Standing Committee on the Unlicensed Practice of Law. The landmark advisory opinion in The Florida Bar re: Advisory Opinion — Activities of Title Insurance Companies (Fla. 1983) carved out specific safe-harbor activities for title companies. That opinion, refined over decades, still governs the 2026 landscape.

Florida Bar Rule 10-2.1 codifies what constitutes unauthorized practice of law in real estate transactions. Drafting deeds, preparing new legal descriptions, advising on contract clauses, and interpreting title exceptions all require a Florida Bar member. A title company that wanders into those areas exposes itself — and the agent who referred the client there — to regulatory action.

## What a Title Company Can and Cannot Do at Closing

The practical line matters more than the theoretical one. Here is how the division breaks down in a typical residential deal:

| Activity | Title Company May Perform | Attorney Required |
|----------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Prepare HUD-1/Closing Disclosure | Yes | No |
| Issue title commitment and policy | Yes | No |
| Hold escrow/trust funds | Yes | No |
| Draft or modify a deed | No | Yes |
| Interpret survey exceptions | No | Yes |
| Advise on contract disputes | No | Yes |
| Witness signatures and notarize | Yes (if licensed notary) | No |
| Prepare standard mortgage documents from lender templates | Yes | No |

This table reflects current Florida Bar guidance and DFS regulatory bulletins as of early 2026. When agents assume the title company can handle "everything," they set their clients up for a problem that surfaces only after documents are recorded — or worse, rejected by the county clerk.

## When an Attorney Is Not Just Recommended but Necessary

Certain transaction types in Florida practically demand attorney involvement regardless of what the statute minimally requires. Short sales with deficiency judgment risk, transactions involving probate or trust-held property, and deals with unresolved title exceptions all qualify. The FAR/BAR "As-Is" Contract (Form ASIS-6) and the standard FAR/BAR Contract (Form CR-6) both contain Paragraph 18, which addresses title defects and cure periods — territory that routinely involves legal judgment.

**If your seller holds title through a land trust or an LLC with complex operating agreements, no title company can interpret those documents for the parties.** They can process the paperwork, but they cannot advise whether the signatory has authority to convey. That determination is legal work, period.

Agents who push closings through a title-company-only process on these complex files often discover the problem post-closing. Recorded documents with authority defects can cloud title for years. The liability chain runs from the closing agent back to the licensee who coordinated the transaction without flagging the issue.

## Common Mistakes Florida Agents Make

Five errors show up repeatedly in FREC disciplinary files and E&O claims related to closing procedures.

First, agents tell buyers "you don't need an attorney" as a blanket statement. This constitutes legal advice from an unlicensed person. Section 475.25(1)(j), Florida Statutes, prohibits licensees from practicing law, and offering opinions on whether legal counsel is necessary falls squarely into that prohibition. The correct response is always to recommend the buyer consult with an attorney if they have legal questions.

Second, agents select the closing agent on behalf of the party who is not paying for title insurance without proper authorization. Under RESPA Section 9 and Florida custom (where the seller typically pays for the owner's policy in most counties south of I-4, and the buyer pays in many counties north of it), the paying party generally has selection rights. Overriding that selection — even with good intentions — creates kickback exposure and RESPA violations.

Third, agents accept or relay revised closing documents without directing the parties to the closing agent or their attorney. When a licensee transmits a "corrected" deed or modified settlement statement, they risk crossing into document preparation. The [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) matters here — knowing exactly where your role ends protects your license.

Fourth, agents fail to verify that the title company's escrow account complies with Florida Statute Section 626.8473, which mandates specific trust account requirements. If an escrow account is mismanaged and funds disappear, the agent who recommended that company may face civil exposure even without direct fault.

Fifth, agents assume that because Florida is a "title state" and not an "attorney state," no attorney is ever needed. That binary framing ignores the dozens of scenarios where attorney involvement is the only lawful path to closing.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Section 475.25(1)(u), Florida Statutes. FREC has consistently interpreted this to mean that a broker must maintain policies ensuring agents do not inadvertently practice law or direct clients away from legal counsel when it is warranted.

**Every brokerage operating in Florida should maintain a written policy identifying which transaction types require attorney referral.** That policy should reference specific triggers: trust-held property, probate sales, boundary disputes, contract litigation, and HOA lien disputes at minimum. The policy should live in the brokerage's operations manual and be reviewed during onboarding.

Brokers should also audit closing agent selection practices quarterly. Review a random sample of transaction files to confirm that the paying party selected the closing agent, that no undisclosed affiliated business arrangements exist, and that the closing entity held a valid DFS license at the time of closing. The Florida Department of Financial Services maintains a [public licensee search tool](https://licenseesearch.fldfs.com/) that takes under a minute to verify.

Additionally, brokers need to confirm their agents are not filling in blanks on deeds or other legal instruments. The FAR/BAR forms are approved for licensee use, but any document outside that approved set — quitclaim deeds, affidavits of title, corrective instruments — requires attorney preparation. Tracking [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) often reveals that the breakdown started when someone at the brokerage touched a document they had no authority to prepare.

## How Closing Customs Vary Across Florida Counties

Florida's closing customs are not uniform. The state splits roughly along the I-4 corridor, with different traditions for who pays for title insurance and who selects the closing agent.

| Region | Who Typically Pays Owner's Title Policy | Who Typically Selects Closing Agent |
|--------|----------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| South Florida (Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach) | Seller | Seller (or seller's agent) |
| Central Florida (Orange, Osceola, Hillsborough) | Negotiable / often Seller | Varies by deal |
| North Florida (Duval, Alachua, Leon) | Buyer | Buyer (or buyer's agent) |
| Panhandle (Escambia, Okaloosa, Bay) | Buyer | Buyer (or buyer's agent) |

These customs are not codified in statute — they are market norms. The FAR/BAR Contract addresses this in Paragraph 9, allowing the parties to specify who pays and who selects. Agents who work across county lines without adjusting their assumptions create friction that can [blow a closing entirely](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

## Protecting Yourself and Your Clients in 2026

The 2026 regulatory environment has not relaxed enforcement. FREC continues to investigate complaints involving unauthorized practice of law by licensees, and the Florida Bar's UPL committee has increased referrals related to real estate transactions over the past three years. Your best protection is documentation: keep written records showing you recommended legal counsel when appropriate, that you did not select closing agents on behalf of parties without authorization, and that you limited your document preparation to approved FAR/BAR forms.
