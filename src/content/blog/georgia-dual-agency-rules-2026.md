## Understanding Georgia Dual Agency & Designated Agency Rules 2026

Georgia's statutory framework for brokerage relationships is more prescriptive than many agents realize. The Georgia dual agency & designated agency rules 2026 carry real consequences for agents who treat disclosure as an afterthought, and brokers who fail to police their files face shared liability. Every transaction that involves a single firm on both sides of the deal triggers specific statutory obligations under the Brokerage Relationships in Real Estate Transactions Act (BRRETA), codified at O.C.G.A. Title 10, Chapter 6A.

The Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) has made enforcement of agency disclosure a recurring audit priority. In recent disciplinary actions, the Commission has cited incomplete or late disclosures as grounds for sanctions ranging from reprimands to license suspension. This is not an area where "we usually handle it at the listing appointment" is a defensible answer.

Understanding the distinction between dual agency and designated agency—and knowing exactly when and how to document each—separates the agents who close cleanly from those who end up in front of a GREC hearing officer.

## BRRETA's Two-Path Framework: Dual vs. Designated

**Georgia law does not assume dual agency by default; it must be affirmatively created and consented to in writing.** Under O.C.G.A. 10-6A-12, a broker who wishes to act as a dual agent must obtain the informed written consent of both the buyer and the seller before providing brokerage services to both. The statute explicitly states that dual agency does not arise simply because a buyer walks into a listing agent's open house.

Designated agency, governed by O.C.G.A. 10-6A-12(d), offers an alternative that preserves client-level advocacy. The broker designates one licensee to represent the seller and a different licensee within the same firm to represent the buyer. Each designated agent owes full fiduciary duties—including confidentiality—to their assigned client.

The practical difference is significant. A dual agent cannot advise either party on price negotiation, repair demands, or concession strategy. A designated agent can. That distinction alone makes the form selection and timing of disclosure a material factor in whether a client receives the representation they expect.

## Key Forms and When to Present Them

Georgia agents working under the Georgia Association of REALTORS (GAR) form library must know three forms cold:

| Form Number | Form Name | When Presented | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| GAR F301 | Exclusive Seller Brokerage Engagement | At listing | Establishes seller-broker relationship |
| GAR F802 | Dual Agency Disclosure and Consent | Before dual agency begins | Written consent from both parties |
| GAR F803 | Designated Agency Agreement | Before brokerage services to second party | Assigns separate licensees |

The GAR F802 must be signed by both parties before the broker or agent renders advice or negotiation services in a dual capacity. Presenting it at closing—or worse, after ratification—does not satisfy BRRETA's timing requirement. The GREC has stated in published guidance that "before" means before the dual agency relationship begins, not before the closing occurs.

For designated agency, GAR F803 must identify the specific licensees assigned. A vague reference to "an agent in our office" is insufficient. The form requires names, and the broker must retain documentation of the assignment in the transaction file.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

**Failing to properly disclose and obtain consent for dual or designated agency in Georgia exposes agents to three distinct categories of risk.** These are not theoretical—each has been applied in GREC disciplinary proceedings within the past three years.

First, regulatory sanctions. GREC can impose fines of up to $5,000 per violation under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25. The Commission can also suspend or revoke a license, require additional education, or issue a public reprimand that appears on the licensee's record indefinitely.

Second, deal rescission. A party who was not given proper dual agency disclosure may have grounds to void the contract entirely. Georgia courts have recognized that a material breach of fiduciary duty—such as undisclosed dual agency—can support rescission even after closing, particularly where the non-consenting party suffered financial harm.

Third, civil liability. An agent who fails to disclose dual agency and then provides advice that harms one party may face a breach-of-fiduciary-duty claim. Damages in these cases can include the difference between the price paid and fair market value, lost equity, and in egregious cases, attorney fees. The broker is vicariously liable under O.C.G.A. 10-6A-14 for the acts of affiliated licensees.

## Common Mistakes Georgia Agents Make

The following five errors appear repeatedly in GREC complaints and broker audits. None of them require bad intent—most happen because agents are moving fast and rely on habit instead of process.

**Presenting the dual agency disclosure at contract ratification instead of before it.** The statute requires consent before the relationship begins. If you are already drafting an offer on behalf of a buyer for your own listing, dual agency has started. The disclosure must precede that activity, not accompany the ratified contract.

Confusing ministerial acts with brokerage services. Some agents believe that showing a buyer their own listing at an open house does not trigger agency obligations. BRRETA disagrees. Under O.C.G.A. 10-6A-3(3), providing real estate brokerage services includes showing property. If you answer substantive questions about the property or the seller's motivation, you have begun providing services to the buyer.

Failing to update agency status when a transaction evolves. A buyer's agent whose client decides to purchase a property listed by another agent in the same brokerage has just created a designated agency scenario—or a dual agency scenario if the broker does not designate. Many agents fail to pause and execute the correct form at this inflection point.

Using outdated forms. GAR updates its form library periodically, and GREC expects agents to use current versions. An expired F802 form may omit language that the Commission deems material. Brokers should verify form versions quarterly.

Not retaining signed originals. Georgia's record-retention requirement under GREC Rule 520-1-.08 mandates that brokers keep transaction documents for at least five years. A missing F802 or F803 in a complaint investigation is functionally the same as never having obtained consent.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers hold the ultimate responsibility for agency compliance in their firms. GREC Rule 520-1-.06 places supervisory obligations on the broker that cannot be delegated away to a team lead or transaction coordinator. Here is what a defensible audit program looks like.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| F802/F803 presence in dual-side files | Every dual-side transaction | Missing form or signatures dated after ratification |
| Designated agent assignment documentation | Every in-house both-sides deal | No named agents or same agent on both sides |
| Form version currency | Quarterly | Forms older than 12 months from GAR revision date |
| Timing verification (disclosure vs. ratification date) | Every dual-side transaction | Disclosure date same as or later than offer date |
| Five-year file retention compliance | Annual | Files purged before retention period expires |

Brokers should conduct random file audits at least quarterly—not just when a complaint arrives. A documented audit trail demonstrates good-faith compliance efforts to GREC and can mitigate sanctions if an individual agent makes an error.

Training is not optional. GREC expects brokers to provide ongoing education on agency law to their affiliated licensees. A one-time onboarding mention does not satisfy the supervisory duty. ## Designated Agency as the Preferred Risk-Reduction Path

Most Georgia brokerages with more than a handful of agents should default to designated agency rather than dual agency whenever possible. The risk profile is materially lower because each client retains a fiduciary advocate. Confidential pricing information, motivation details, and negotiation strategy remain protected within the designated agent relationship.

> "A designated agent owes the same duties to the client as if the broker had no other client in the transaction." — O.C.G.A. 10-6A-12(d)

That statutory language makes designated agency the closest analog to a fully independent representation model within a single firm. Brokers who train their agents to default to F803 rather than F802 reduce their exposure to allegations of conflicted advice.

The broker in a designated agency scenario becomes a neutral party—unable to disclose either client's confidential information to the other designated agent. This requires internal information barriers that some smaller offices find difficult to maintain. If your brokerage has three agents and all of them sit in the same bullpen, the argument that confidential information was truly walled off becomes harder to support in a dispute.
