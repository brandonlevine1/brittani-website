## Understanding Washington FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

Every year, transactions involving foreign sellers collapse in the final days because someone on the deal team failed to identify a FIRPTA obligation early enough. The Washington firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 carry the same federal framework agents have dealt with for years, but the consequences of getting it wrong remain severe—and surprisingly common in a state with heavy international investment in markets like Seattle, Bellevue, and Vancouver. If you hold a Washington license issued by the Department of Licensing under RCW 18.85, you have a professional duty to flag withholding issues before they detonate a closing.

FIRPTA—the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act, codified at 26 U.S.C. Sections 1445 through 1445(e)—requires buyers to withhold a percentage of the gross sales price when purchasing U.S. real property from a "foreign person." The term foreign person includes nonresident aliens, foreign corporations, foreign partnerships, foreign trusts, and foreign estates. Washington does not layer a separate state income withholding on top of the federal requirement, but the [state's real estate excise tax under RCW 82.45](https://apps.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=82.45) applies to every transfer and must still be collected at closing.

## The Federal Withholding Rate Structure

**The default FIRPTA withholding rate in 2026 remains 15% of the gross sales price.** That figure can shift based on the property value and the buyer's intended use. Agents who memorize only the 15% number miss opportunities to reduce their client's burden—or worse, under-withhold and expose the buyer to IRS liability.

| Sales Price | Buyer's Intended Use as Residence | Withholding Rate |
|---|---|---|
| $300,000 or less | Yes (affidavit required) | 0% |
| $300,001 – $1,000,000 | Yes (affidavit required) | 10% |
| Over $1,000,000 | Yes or No | 15% |
| Any amount | No (investment property) | 15% |

The buyer's intended-use affidavit is not a casual promise. It must be signed and retained in the transaction file, and the buyer must actually reside in the property for at least 50% of the days it is in use during each of the first two twelve-month periods after closing. Misrepresenting buyer intent to reduce withholding exposes both parties—and potentially the agents—to IRS scrutiny.

## Key Forms and Filing Deadlines

The IRS requires specific filings to document FIRPTA compliance. In Washington transactions, the escrow company typically handles remittance, but the legal obligation sits with the buyer as the "transferee" or withholding agent.

| Form | Purpose | Filed By | Deadline |
|---|---|---|---|
| IRS Form 8288 | Report and transmit withholding to IRS | Buyer (via escrow) | 20 days after closing |
| IRS Form 8288-A | Provide seller a statement of tax withheld | Buyer (via escrow) | Filed with Form 8288 |
| IRS Form 8288-B | Application for reduced withholding certificate | Seller | Before closing (ideally 90+ days) |
| Seller's Affidavit of Non-Foreign Status | Certify seller is not a foreign person | Seller | At or before closing |

**A seller who files Form 8288-B early enough can sometimes reduce the withholding to the actual expected tax liability.** This matters in transactions where the foreign seller's gain is minimal relative to the gross price. The IRS processing time on 8288-B applications often stretches to 90 days or longer, so listing agents need to raise this option at the listing appointment—not during the inspection period.

## Washington-Specific Compliance Layers

Washington lacks a state income tax, which means there is no separate state-level withholding mechanism analogous to what agents encounter in states like California (via FTB Form 593) or Oregon. However, agents still face two Washington-specific obligations that interact with a FIRPTA transaction.

First, the Real Estate Excise Tax Affidavit (REET form) must be filed with the county treasurer at closing. Foreign sellers owe REET at the same tiered rates as domestic sellers, and the excise tax must be paid before the deed records. Second, agents must comply with the Washington Seller Disclosure Statement requirements under RCW 64.06, which do not contain a FIRPTA carve-out. The [seller disclosure process in Washington](/blog/washington-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) runs on its own timeline and must not be conflated with FIRPTA documentation.

Agents working with foreign sellers should also confirm whether the entity holding title is a domestically organized LLC or a foreign entity. A single-member LLC organized in Washington but owned by a nonresident alien does not shield the transaction from FIRPTA withholding. The IRS looks through disregarded entities to the beneficial owner's tax status.

## What Happens If an Agent Fails to Comply

The liability chain in a botched FIRPTA transaction runs primarily to the buyer, but agents are not insulated. Under 26 U.S.C. Section 1445(d), if the transferee (buyer) fails to withhold the required amount, the IRS can assess the full withholding amount, plus interest from the date of transfer, against the buyer personally. The buyer's agent who facilitated closing without proper withholding has contributed to that client's potential six-figure tax liability.

**Agents with actual knowledge that a seller is a foreign person face a specific risk.** If you knew or had reason to know the seller was foreign, and the transaction closed without withholding or a valid exemption certificate, the IRS regulations provide that the agent may be held liable as a withholding agent under Treasury Regulation 1.1445-4. This is not theoretical—the IRS has pursued agents and escrow officers in enforcement actions.

Beyond federal exposure, the Washington Department of Licensing can investigate complaints tied to material negligence in transaction handling. A pattern of FIRPTA failures could support a license action under WAC 308-124C-137, which addresses unprofessional conduct and incompetence. The [agency disclosure framework in Washington](/blog/washington-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) already obligates agents to disclose material facts; a seller's foreign status, when known, fits squarely within that duty.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with FIRPTA in Washington

The first error is assuming the escrow company will catch everything. Escrow officers in Washington handle FIRPTA remittance frequently, but they rely on information provided by the parties. If no one identifies the seller as foreign, escrow has no trigger to withhold. The listing agent is typically the first person in the transaction who knows or should know the seller's residency status.

The second mistake is accepting a Non-Foreign Affidavit without verifying its contents. The affidavit requires a U.S. taxpayer identification number. If the seller provides an ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number beginning with 9), that alone signals the seller may be a nonresident alien. An ITIN is not proof of foreign status, but it demands further inquiry rather than passive acceptance.

Third, agents routinely confuse residency with citizenship. A Canadian citizen who holds a green card and has a U.S. tax home is generally not subject to FIRPTA withholding. Conversely, a U.S. citizen who has expatriated and formally abandoned their tax residency can trigger withholding. The test is tax status, not passport.

Fourth, listing agents fail to raise Form 8288-B at the listing stage. By the time a purchase agreement is signed, there are often fewer than 30 days to closing—nowhere near enough time for IRS processing. The seller loses the ability to reduce withholding and faces a cash flow problem that can kill the deal.

Fifth, agents neglect to document the FIRPTA determination in the transaction file. Whether the seller is foreign or domestic, the file should contain either the signed Non-Foreign Affidavit or the withholding instructions. This documentation protects the agent in any future audit or dispute. ## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Washington bear supervisory responsibility under RCW 18.85.186 and WAC 308-124C-125. That responsibility extends to ensuring agents under their license handle FIRPTA obligations correctly. A broker who ignores systematic FIRPTA failures across their office invites both regulatory and civil liability.

**Brokers should audit transaction files for the presence of either a signed Non-Foreign Affidavit or documented FIRPTA withholding instructions in every sale involving a seller whose residency is ambiguous.** This is not optional due diligence—it is a baseline compliance measure. The audit should verify that the affidavit contains a valid TIN, a current address, and is signed under penalty of perjury as required by Treasury Regulation 1.1445-2(b)(2).

Brokers should also establish a written office policy specifying at what point in the transaction the listing agent must inquire about the seller's tax residency status. Best practice is the listing agreement stage. If your brokerage uses transaction coordination software, build the FIRPTA inquiry into the listing checklist so it cannot be skipped. The [property condition disclosure process](/blog/washington-property-condition-disclosures-2026) already has a defined trigger point; FIRPTA deserves the same rigor.

Finally, brokers should require agents to escalate any FIRPTA-flagged transaction to the broker or a designated compliance officer before the transaction moves to mutual acceptance. This prevents last-minute scrambles and gives the seller time to pursue a withholding certificate if appropriate.

## Practical Workflow for Listing Agents

At the listing appointment, ask directly: "Are you a U.S. citizen or resident alien for tax purposes?" Document the answer. If the seller is foreign, discuss the 15% withholding immediately and introduce the option of filing Form 8288-B for a reduced certificate. Refer the seller to a tax professional who handles international returns—this is not within your scope of practice.

Once an offer is received, notify the buyer's agent and escrow officer of the FIRPTA withholding requirement in writing. Include the expected withholding amount in the estimated seller net sheet. On the buyer's side, confirm that the purchase and sale agreement accounts for the withholding and that the buyer understands their obligation as the transferee.

At closing, verify with escrow that Form 8288 and 8288-A will be filed within 20 days. Retain copies in your transaction file. If the seller obtained a withholding certificate, confirm that the original IRS determination letter is in escrow's possession before disbursement.

## Staying Current on Washington FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

The federal withholding rates and thresholds can change with new legislation, and the IRS periodically updates its processing guidance for Form 8288-B applications. Agents should monitor IRS.gov and their state association bulletins for updates that affect the Washington firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 framework. Building FIRPTA compliance into your standard operating procedures—rather than treating it as a one-off event—separates agents who protect their clients from those who expose them to avoidable tax liability.
