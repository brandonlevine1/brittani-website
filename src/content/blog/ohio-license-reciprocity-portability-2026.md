## Understanding Ohio Real Estate License Reciprocity & Portability 2026

Ohio sits at the crossroads of five state borders, making cross-border transactions an everyday reality for agents in Cincinnati, Toledo, Youngstown, and dozens of smaller markets. The rules governing **Ohio real estate license reciprocity & portability 2026** have not changed dramatically from prior years, but the consequences of getting them wrong have sharpened as the Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing (ODREPL) increases enforcement audits. If you hold a license outside Ohio or supervise agents who do, understanding the statutory framework is not optional—it is the baseline.

Ohio does not operate under a blanket reciprocity agreement the way some Sun Belt states do. Instead, the state uses a conditional recognition model codified primarily in Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Section 4735.07 and a cooperative licensure provision under ORC 4735.022. The distinction matters because agents frequently confuse the two pathways and end up practicing illegally on one side of the river.

## The Statutory Framework: ORC 4735.07 and ORC 4735.022

**ORC 4735.07 governs non-resident license applications and sets the terms under which an out-of-state licensee can obtain an Ohio license with reduced coursework.** To qualify, your home state must maintain education and examination standards that the Ohio Real Estate Commission deems "substantially equivalent." The Commission publishes an internal comparison matrix each biennium; as of the 2025-2026 cycle, states like Indiana, Pennsylvania, Kentucky, West Virginia, and Michigan meet the threshold for at least partial equivalency.

Even when equivalency is recognized, applicants must still pass the Ohio-specific portion of the state licensing exam (40 questions covering Ohio statutes, rules, and practices). There is no waiver for this section. You also must submit fingerprints for a BCI and FBI background check, provide a Certificate of Licensure from your home state, and file Form DRE-100 (Application for Real Estate Salesperson License) or Form DRE-101 (Application for Real Estate Broker License) with the Division.

ORC 4735.022 addresses a narrower scenario: cooperative transactions where a licensee from another state participates in a single deal alongside an Ohio-licensed broker without obtaining an Ohio license. This provision requires a written cooperative agreement between the out-of-state broker and the Ohio broker, limits the foreign licensee's activities, and mandates that the Ohio broker remain the supervising authority for the transaction. Violating the scope of 4735.022 converts the activity into unlicensed practice.

Agents from states not listed above—or from states with lower hour requirements—must complete Ohio's full 120-hour pre-license education before sitting for the exam. No shortcut exists for them beyond the standard new-applicant process.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The penalties for practicing without proper Ohio licensure are not theoretical. ORC 4735.02 makes unlicensed real estate activity a first-degree misdemeanor, punishable by up to six months in jail, fines of up to $1,000 per violation, and disgorgement of any commission earned. The Ohio Real Estate Commission can also issue cease-and-desist orders under ORC 4735.051.

**Beyond criminal exposure, unlicensed practice puts transactions at risk of rescission.** A buyer or seller who discovers that their agent lacked Ohio licensure can seek contract voidance under the theory of illegality. Courts have upheld this in multiple Ohio appellate decisions, meaning the deal collapses and the agent forfeits the commission entirely. Title insurance underwriters routinely flag these situations during their closing audits.

For brokers, the liability compounds. If a supervising broker knowingly or negligently allows an improperly licensed agent to practice, OAC 1301:5-1-03 authorizes suspension or revocation of the broker's own license. Errors and omissions insurers have also denied claims arising from unlicensed activity, leaving the brokerage exposed to direct civil liability from harmed parties. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond a single missed commission check.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Ohio Portability

Five errors surface repeatedly in ODREPL enforcement actions and audit findings. Each one is preventable with basic due diligence.

**First, agents assume that holding a license in a neighboring state automatically permits practice in Ohio.** It does not. Without either an active Ohio license or a properly executed cooperative agreement under ORC 4735.022, any brokerage activity on Ohio soil is unlicensed practice—regardless of where the property is listed or where the client resides.

Second, agents file Form DRE-100 but begin showing properties or writing offers before the license is actually issued. The application is not a temporary license. ODREPL has issued written guidance confirming that no brokerage activity is permitted until the applicant receives the physical or electronic license confirmation.

Third, agents operating under a cooperative agreement exceed its scope. ORC 4735.022 allows participation in a specific transaction, not ongoing business development, open houses, or independent client solicitation in Ohio. Agents who treat the cooperative provision as a general marketing license are practicing outside its boundaries.

Fourth, licensees let their home-state license lapse while holding an Ohio non-resident license. Under ORC 4735.07(C), the Ohio license becomes automatically inactive the moment the underlying foreign license expires or is suspended. Agents who continue transacting after that date are unlicensed.

Fifth, agents fail to disclose their non-resident status to clients. Ohio Administrative Code 1301:5-1-02 requires that licensees disclose material facts about their licensing status when it could affect the consumer's decision. An out-of-state agent operating under cooperative licensure who omits this disclosure opens themselves to disciplinary action and civil claims.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry the supervisory burden under Ohio law, and "I didn't know" has never been a viable defense before the Commission. A structured audit protocol should address three areas: onboarding verification, ongoing compliance, and transaction-level documentation.

**At onboarding, brokers must independently verify that every agent's Ohio license is active through the ODREPL eLicense portal.** Do not accept a photocopy of a license card—check the state database directly. For agents applying under ORC 4735.07, confirm that the application has been approved and the license number assigned before allowing any client-facing activity. Keep a dated screenshot of the verification in the agent's file.

For cooperative licensure situations, brokers must maintain the signed cooperative agreement in the transaction file. The agreement should specify the transaction address, the names of both brokers, the scope of permitted activity, and the duration. OAC 1301:5-1-03(B)(5) requires that brokers maintain such records for at least three years following the transaction's close. Missing documentation during a Division audit triggers an automatic deficiency finding.

Ongoing compliance means monitoring license renewal dates. Ohio salesperson licenses expire on the licensee's birthday in odd or even years depending on original issuance. Broker licenses follow a similar biennial cycle. Building a [weekly pipeline review system](/blog/weekly-pipeline-review-system) that flags approaching expirations prevents agents from unknowingly transacting on a lapsed license.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Frequency | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| License status | Active/inactive/suspended | Monthly | ODREPL eLicense portal |
| CE completion | 30 hrs per renewal period | 90 days before expiration | Agent self-report + transcript |
| Cooperative agreements | Signed, scoped, current | Per transaction | Transaction file |
| Non-resident disclosure | Documented in agency disclosure | Per transaction | Client file |

Brokers who manage agents across state lines should also review the [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) to ensure that cooperative transactions are properly documented at every milestone, not just at listing or closing.

## The Role of the Ohio Real Estate Commission in 2026

The [Ohio Real Estate Commission](https://com.ohio.gov/divisions-and-programs/real-estate-and-professional-licensing/real-estate-commission) is a seven-member body appointed by the Governor that establishes policy for the Division. In 2026, the Commission has signaled increased attention to non-resident compliance following a 2024 audit cycle that revealed a spike in cooperative licensure violations along the Ohio-Kentucky border.

Commission meetings are public, and proposed rule changes affecting reciprocity or portability are published in the Register of Ohio for a 30-day comment period before adoption. Agents and brokers operating in border markets should monitor these publications—rule changes can alter equivalency determinations overnight, stranding agents mid-application.

> "No person shall engage in the business of a real estate broker or real estate salesperson, or advertise or assume to act as such, without first being licensed as provided in this chapter." — ORC 4735.02

That statute has not been amended since its last revision, and its enforcement continues to carry real teeth.
