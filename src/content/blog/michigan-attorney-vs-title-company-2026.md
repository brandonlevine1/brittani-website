## Michigan Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Rules Agents Need to Know

The question of Michigan attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 catches agents off guard more often than it should. Michigan is not an attorney-state for residential closings, but that single fact creates a false sense of simplicity. The reality involves overlapping regulatory frameworks, unauthorized-practice-of-law risks, and broker-level compliance responsibilities that can sink a deal or trigger disciplinary action.

Every year, agents in Michigan misidentify who holds authority at the closing table, and that confusion bleeds into scheduling, document preparation, and client expectations. This post breaks down the statutory framework, the specific forms involved, and the liability traps you need to avoid.

## What Michigan Law Actually Says About Closing Authority

Michigan does not have a statute requiring attorney presence at residential closings. Unlike states such as New York, Georgia, or South Carolina, Michigan allows title insurance companies and their licensed agents to conduct settlements without attorney supervision. The governing authority is the Michigan Department of Insurance and Financial Services (DIFS), which regulates title insurance agents under the Michigan Insurance Code, MCL 500.7101 et seq.

**Title companies operating in Michigan must hold a title insurance agent license issued by DIFS.** They handle the settlement statement, disburse funds, record documents, and issue title insurance policies. An attorney may participate if either party retains one, but their presence is optional, not compulsory.

The Michigan State Bar's position, articulated through various ethics opinions, holds that drafting deeds, mortgages, and other instruments that affect legal rights constitutes the practice of law under MCL 450.681. However, the Michigan Legislature carved out a functional exception for title companies handling standardized closing documents in the course of issuing title insurance.

## Title Company vs. Attorney: A Side-by-Side Comparison for Michigan Closings

| Function | Title Company | Attorney |
|----------|--------------|----------|
| Prepare HUD-1/Closing Disclosure | Yes (standard practice) | Yes (if retained) |
| Conduct settlement/closing | Yes (licensed under DIFS) | Yes |
| Record deed and mortgage | Yes | Yes |
| Issue title insurance | Yes | No (must go through insurer) |
| Provide legal advice to parties | No — prohibited | Yes (to their client only) |
| Draft non-standard contract addenda | No — unauthorized practice risk | Yes |
| Required at closing by statute | No | No |

This table should be posted in your office. The critical distinction: title companies facilitate transactions but cannot advise parties on legal rights, obligations, or contract interpretation. When a buyer at closing asks "what does this clause mean?" the title company representative cannot answer that question without crossing into unauthorized practice territory.

## Forms Michigan Agents Must Know

The Michigan Association of Realtors (MAR) produces the standardized forms that agents use in most residential transactions. Several of these forms directly touch the attorney-versus-title-company question.

MAR Form 1040 (Purchase Agreement) includes provisions for selecting the title company and specifying closing arrangements. The commitment for title insurance, referenced in the agreement, triggers the title company's role. Agents must ensure the purchase agreement identifies the closing agent accurately — listing a title company that has not been engaged, or assuming an attorney will handle closing without confirming, creates [problems that blow up at closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

MAR Form 1050 (Seller's Disclosure Statement) is a property-condition document, not a closing-authority document, but agents sometimes confuse its role. The disclosure obligations under MCL 565.951-565.966 (the Seller Disclosure Act) must be completed before closing regardless of who conducts settlement.

MAR Form 1020 (Listing Agreement) does not dictate closing authority, but brokers should note whether the seller has a preference for attorney representation. Failing to communicate this preference downstream to the buyer's agent creates scheduling disasters.

## What Happens When Agents Cross the Line

**The liability exposure for agents who stray into legal territory at closing is not hypothetical — it results in real disciplinary action.** The Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) oversees real estate licensees through the Occupational Code, MCL 339.2512. An agent who interprets contract language, explains legal implications of title defects, or advises a party on whether to sign a document is practicing law without a license.

Consequences include formal complaints filed with LARA, potential license suspension or revocation under MCL 339.2512(1), civil liability for damages arising from incorrect advice, and errors-and-omissions insurance claims that may not cover unauthorized practice. Title companies can also face enforcement from DIFS if their closing agents exceed the scope of their license.

Beyond individual liability, the brokerage faces vicarious exposure. Michigan follows respondeat superior principles, meaning the broker can be held responsible for an agent's conduct during a transaction they supervised. This is why [broker-level auditing](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) is not optional — it is a risk-management necessity.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make at Closing

Three to five errors recur with alarming frequency across Michigan brokerages. Each one is avoidable with basic procedural discipline.

**First, agents tell buyers they do not need an attorney because "Michigan doesn't require one."** While technically accurate, this statement can be construed as legal advice. The correct approach is to inform clients that attorney representation is optional and that the agent cannot advise on whether legal counsel is necessary. MAR's own guidance recommends agents suggest parties consult an attorney when questions of legal interpretation arise.

Second, agents schedule closings without confirming the title company's availability or that the commitment for title insurance has been issued. Under MCL 500.7103, the title insurer must deliver a commitment before closing. Agents who push a closing date without verifying this document create last-minute scrambles and potential deal cancellations.

Third, agents attempt to resolve title defects themselves rather than directing the issue to the title company or an attorney. A boundary dispute, an unrecorded easement, or a lien that did not appear on the initial search requires legal analysis. Agents who "fix" these problems by drafting quit-claim deeds or informal agreements expose themselves and their clients to massive liability.

Fourth, agents fail to explain the difference between the title company's role and an attorney's role to first-time buyers. This results in clients expecting legal guidance from the title closer, creating confusion and sometimes anger at the closing table. A brief conversation during the initial consultation — not at closing — prevents this entirely.

Fifth, agents assume that because a title company is handling closing, they have no document-delivery obligations. LARA still requires agents to ensure all required disclosures, addenda, and contractual documents reach the title company in time. Missing the [deadlines that derail transactions](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) is the agent's failure, not the title company's.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Michigan carry supervisory obligations under MCL 339.2512 and the administrative rules promulgated by LARA. These obligations do not disappear because a title company is conducting the closing.

Brokers must verify that every transaction file includes confirmation of the closing agent's identity and license status with DIFS. A simple DIFS license lookup takes thirty seconds and eliminates the risk of closing with an unlicensed entity. Brokers should also audit for any instance where an agent provided written or verbal legal guidance — email threads, text messages, and transaction notes all constitute discoverable evidence.

## When an Attorney Should Be Involved (Even Though It Is Not Required)

Michigan's lack of an attorney mandate does not mean attorneys are irrelevant to residential transactions. Several scenarios demand legal involvement regardless of statutory minimums.

Commercial-residential hybrids, transactions involving estates or trusts, deals with IRS liens, and closings where one party is under a power of attorney all present issues that exceed a title company's scope. Agents who recognize these triggers and recommend (without requiring) attorney involvement demonstrate competence without crossing into unauthorized practice.

> "The fact that Michigan law does not require an attorney at closing does not relieve agents of the obligation to recognize when legal complexity exceeds their competence." — Michigan State Bar Real Property Law Section, Practice Advisory 2024-03

The distinction matters: recommending that a client consult an attorney is proper agent conduct. Telling a client what the law means is not. The line is bright, and agents who stay on the correct side of it protect both their clients and their license.
