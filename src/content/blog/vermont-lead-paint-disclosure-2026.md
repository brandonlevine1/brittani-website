## Understanding Vermont Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

If you represent sellers or buyers in Vermont on properties built before 1978, you are bound by both federal and state disclosure obligations that carry real financial teeth. The Vermont lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 have not changed in their core mandate, but enforcement patterns and agent liability exposure have sharpened noticeably over the past two years. Missing a single step in this process can unwind a deal or land you in front of the Vermont Real Estate Commission.

Vermont's housing stock is among the oldest in the nation. According to U.S. Census data, roughly 70 percent of Vermont's residential structures were built before 1978 — the year the federal government banned lead-based paint in housing. That means the majority of your listings trigger these disclosure obligations, not a small subset.

The governing federal law is the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992, codified at [42 U.S.C. 4852d](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/4852d), and implemented through EPA and HUD regulations at 24 CFR Part 35 and 40 CFR Part 745. Vermont enforces these requirements through its own Department of Health lead program and the Vermont Real Estate Commission under 26 V.S.A. Chapter 41.

## The Exact Forms and Documents You Must Deliver

**There is no Vermont-specific lead paint disclosure form with a unique state form number.** The state relies on the federal EPA disclosure form titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards," which is the same two-page document used nationally. The Vermont Association of Realtors (VAR) integrates this form into its standard transaction packet, and many brokerages include it as a mandatory attachment to the purchase and sale agreement.

You must also provide the buyer with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" before the buyer becomes obligated under the contract. This is not optional supporting material — it is a legal requirement with the same enforcement weight as the disclosure form itself.

The seller completes the disclosure portion, indicating whether they have knowledge of lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards in the property, and must attach any available reports or records. The buyer then acknowledges receipt in writing. Both the listing agent and the buyer's agent sign confirming their compliance role.

## The 10-Day Inspection Period: Non-Negotiable Unless Waived in Writing

Federal regulations grant buyers a 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense before becoming obligated under the contract. This period can be shortened, lengthened, or waived — but only through written mutual agreement between the parties.

A common misconception is that Vermont's standard inspection contingency period substitutes for this lead-specific window. It does not. The lead inspection opportunity is a separate legal right under 40 CFR 745.110(a), independent of any general inspection clause in the purchase agreement. If your contract shortens the general inspection to seven days but says nothing about the lead inspection period, the buyer still retains the full 10 days for lead testing.

Agents who fold the lead inspection window into the general contingency without explicit written acknowledgment from the buyer are creating liability for themselves and their brokerage. Keep the documentation distinct and clearly labeled.

## Penalties and Liability When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance operate on multiple levels, and agents often underestimate how personally exposed they are in this process.

| Consequence | Source | Potential Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Civil penalty per violation | EPA enforcement under 40 CFR 745.118 | Up to $19,507 per violation (2026 adjusted) |
| Treble damages | Private civil action under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3) | Three times the amount of damages in a lawsuit |
| Contract rescission | Buyer's right under federal and state law | Deal cancelled, earnest money returned |
| License discipline | Vermont Real Estate Commission under 26 V.S.A. 2296 | Reprimand, suspension, or revocation |
| Brokerage vicarious liability | Vermont agency law and broker supervision duties | Broker jointly liable for agent's failure |

**The EPA does not need to prove that lead paint actually caused harm.** The penalty attaches to the failure to disclose, not to the presence of actual contamination. An agent who skips the form on a property that turns out to have zero lead paint is still in violation.

Vermont courts have also recognized that buyers may rescind a purchase agreement if the disclosure was materially deficient, even after closing. This makes post-closing claims a real risk for listing agents who treated the form as a box-checking afterthought. ## Common Mistakes Vermont Agents Make With Lead Disclosures

Five errors come up repeatedly in Vermont transactions, and each one creates avoidable risk.

**First, agents let sellers check "unknown" without any follow-up conversation.** The "unknown" box on the disclosure form is legally permissible, but it does not relieve the seller from disclosing information they actually possess. If a seller renovated a pre-1978 home and encountered paint chips or had informal testing done, checking "unknown" is a misrepresentation — and the listing agent who watched them do it shares that liability.

Second, agents deliver the pamphlet after the contract is executed. The timing requirement is explicit: the buyer must receive the pamphlet before becoming obligated. Handing it over at closing or as part of a post-execution document package violates the regulation. Vermont's tight market sometimes pressures agents to rush contracts, but this timing requirement cannot be compressed.

Third, agents fail to retain signed copies for three years. The federal regulation at 40 CFR 745.113 requires that the signed disclosure and acknowledgment of receipt be retained for no less than three years from the date of the transaction. Many agents rely on their MLS file or brokerage transaction management system without confirming that the lead disclosure specifically is archived with proper signatures.

Fourth, agents treat exempt properties as non-exempt. Zero-bedroom dwellings, housing for the elderly (where children are not expected to reside), and housing that has been certified lead-free by an accredited inspector are exempt from the disclosure requirement. But a property listed as a "studio" or "loft" in a pre-1978 building is not automatically zero-bedroom for purposes of this rule. The exemption analysis requires actual verification, not MLS category assumptions.

Fifth, dual agents forget that they owe the disclosure obligation from both sides simultaneously. In a Vermont dual agency or designated agency situation, the firm still must ensure the buyer receives all required materials. The listing agent cannot assume the buyer's designated agent will handle it, especially when the transaction is moving quickly. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under 26 V.S.A. 2296 for their agents' compliance with disclosure obligations. The Vermont Real Estate Commission has made clear through enforcement actions that a broker's ignorance of an agent's failure is not a defense.

| Broker Audit Point | What to Verify | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure form presence | Signed EPA form in every pre-1978 transaction file | Every transaction before closing |
| Pamphlet delivery timing | Written acknowledgment dated before contract execution | Every transaction before closing |
| 10-day period documentation | Written waiver or evidence of opportunity provided | Every transaction before closing |
| Three-year retention | Signed originals or compliant electronic copies accessible | Annual file audit |
| Seller interview documentation | Notes or email confirming agent discussed known hazards with seller | Spot-check quarterly |

**Brokers who rely solely on their agents' self-reporting of compliance are exposing the firm to joint liability.** A structured audit system — whether manual or automated — should flag any pre-1978 transaction file that lacks the required lead documentation before the deal reaches closing.

The Vermont Real Estate Commission's disciplinary process allows for escalating consequences. A first offense involving negligent failure to disclose may result in a reprimand and mandatory continuing education. Repeated or willful violations can trigger license suspension. Brokers who demonstrate affirmative supervisory systems receive more favorable treatment in disciplinary proceedings than those who cannot produce evidence of oversight protocols.

## Vermont-Specific Considerations Beyond the Federal Baseline

Vermont adds a layer through its Department of Health lead program and the Essential Maintenance Practices (EMP) regulations under 18 V.S.A. Chapter 38. While EMP obligations primarily fall on landlords of rental properties, agents listing rental properties or multi-unit buildings must understand that an owner's failure to register and comply with EMP can create title and marketability issues that surface during due diligence.

The Vermont Department of Health maintains a registry of properties that have been tested for lead and properties where lead orders have been issued. Listing agents representing sellers of pre-1978 properties should verify whether the property appears in this registry, because any existing reports or orders constitute "records and reports available to the seller" under the federal disclosure rule. A seller who fails to attach a known lead order to the disclosure form — and an agent who fails to ask — has violated the disclosure standard.

Vermont's [seller disclosure requirements](/blog/vermont-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) under the Property Transfer Tax return and related forms also intersect with lead obligations. The property condition disclosure that Vermont sellers complete at listing should prompt a conversation about lead, but it does not substitute for the separate EPA-mandated lead disclosure form.
