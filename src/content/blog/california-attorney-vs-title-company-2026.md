## California Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026

The question of **California attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026** trips up even experienced agents, partly because California's escrow system works differently from the attorney-state model used across much of the East Coast and Midwest. California is not an attorney-closing state. Closings here run through licensed escrow holders — typically title companies or independent escrow companies regulated by the Department of Financial Protection and Innovation (DFPI) — and no statute mandates that a lawyer sit at the table.

That does not mean attorneys are irrelevant. It means the compliance burden falls on agents and brokers to understand exactly which entities are authorized to conduct escrow, what forms document the closing, and where personal liability begins when something goes sideways.

## The Legal Framework: Who Is Authorized to Close

California Financial Code Sections 17000–17702 govern escrow activities. Under Section 17006, an "escrow agent" is any person engaged in the business of receiving escrows for deposit or delivery. Only entities holding a valid DFPI escrow license — or those exempt under Section 17006(a) — may legally serve as the escrow holder.

**Title companies operating under a California Department of Insurance license are exempt from the separate DFPI escrow license requirement**, per Financial Code Section 17006(a)(4). This is why most residential closings in California are handled by title companies: they issue title insurance and conduct escrow under one roof without needing two licenses.

Attorneys are also exempt under Section 17006(a)(3), provided they are active members of the California State Bar and the escrow activity is incidental to their legal practice. A real estate broker holding a Bureau of Real Estate (CalBRE) license may conduct escrow under Business and Professions Code Section 10131(d), but only in transactions where the broker represents the buyer, the seller, or both.

| Entity | Licensing Authority | Statutory Basis | Scope of Escrow Authority |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|--------------------------|
| Independent escrow company | DFPI | Fin. Code § 17200 | General escrow for any transaction |
| Title company | Dept. of Insurance | Fin. Code § 17006(a)(4) | Escrow paired with title insurance |
| Attorney | State Bar | Fin. Code § 17006(a)(3) | Incidental to legal representation |
| Real estate broker | CalBRE (DRE) | B&P Code § 10131(d) | Only in transactions where broker is agent |

## What "Closing" Actually Looks Like in California

Unlike states where all parties gather around a conference table for a simultaneous signing, California uses an escrow closing model. Buyers and sellers typically sign documents at different times and locations. The escrow officer coordinates recording of the deed with the county recorder, disburses funds, and issues closing statements.

The California Association of Realtors (C.A.R.) form RPA (Residential Purchase Agreement, revised 2024) at Paragraph 6 addresses escrow selection and the timeline for opening escrow. The Escrow Holder Acknowledgment (form EHA) confirms the escrow company's receipt of the agreement. Agents must ensure both forms are properly executed, because an incomplete EHA can stall the entire timeline.

No statute requires a closing ceremony. Recording happens when the escrow officer determines all conditions have been satisfied — funds deposited, loan documents signed, title cleared, and contingencies removed via C.A.R. form CR (Contingency Removal).

## Common Mistakes Agents Make at California Closings

**First, agents recommend unlicensed escrow services.** If you refer a client to a friend who "handles closings" but lacks a DFPI license, CalBRE license, DOI-regulated title operation, or State Bar membership, you have facilitated an unlicensed escrow transaction. Under Financial Code Section 17414, penalties include fines up to $10,000 per violation and potential criminal prosecution. Your own license is also exposed to DRE disciplinary action under B&P Code Section 10176(i) for conduct that is "dishonest or fraudulent."

Second, agents assume they can hold earnest money in a personal account while waiting for escrow to open. CalBRE regulations under Commissioner's Regulation 2832 require that trust funds be deposited into a trust account or delivered to escrow within three business days. Mishandling trust funds is the single fastest route to license revocation in California.

Third, agents fail to disclose affiliated business arrangements. If your brokerage has a financial relationship with the title or escrow company you recommend, the Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA) Section 8 requires a written Affiliated Business Arrangement disclosure (AfBA). CFPB enforcement actions have resulted in six-figure penalties for brokerages that skip this form.

Fourth, agents confuse the title company's role with legal advice. When a buyer asks the escrow officer to explain a lien or easement, the escrow officer cannot provide legal interpretation. Agents who tell clients "the title company will handle it" without recommending independent legal counsel may face liability for negligent misrepresentation if the issue later causes financial harm.

Fifth, agents miss the distinction between preliminary title reports and title insurance commitments. The C.A.R. form PTR (Preliminary Title Report Review) gives buyers a window to review exceptions. If the agent [misses that deadline](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), the buyer may lose the right to object to recorded encumbrances — and the agent owns the blame.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

**Brokers carry supervisory liability under B&P Code Section 10177(h)** for failing to exercise reasonable supervision over their agents' activities. That means if an agent on your team directs trust funds to an unlicensed entity, the broker is co-liable.

Brokers should maintain a vetted list of approved escrow and title vendors, confirming each company's active license status through the DFPI's online license search and the CDI (California Department of Insurance) company lookup. Annual audits are not optional — they are a best practice that the DRE specifically evaluates during brokerage audits.

Every file should contain a completed EHA form and evidence that the AfBA disclosure was provided (if applicable) before the buyer selected the escrow or title company. Brokers who rely on agents to self-police this paperwork are [inviting exactly the kind of deal breakdown](/blog/where-deals-break-down) that triggers complaints to the DRE.

| Audit Item | Relevant Authority | Consequence of Non-Compliance |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| Trust fund handling within 3 days | Commissioner's Reg. 2832 | License suspension or revocation |
| AfBA disclosure for affiliated title companies | RESPA Section 8 | CFPB fine up to $100,000+ |
| Escrow holder license verification | Fin. Code § 17414 | Broker co-liability, DRE discipline |
| PTR review deadline tracked | C.A.R. form PTR | Loss of buyer objection rights, agent liability |
| Proper file documentation (EHA, CR, RPA) | DRE audit standards | Citation, fine, or license restriction |

Quarterly file reviews that confirm these five items exist in every closed transaction folder will insulate brokers from most regulatory exposure.

## When an Attorney Should Be Involved

California law does not require attorney presence, but competent agents know when to recommend one. Transactions involving estate sales, probate properties (governed by Probate Code Sections 10300–10316), 1031 exchanges, commercial-to-residential conversions, or properties with unresolved liens benefit enormously from legal counsel.

The [California Department of Real Estate's Reference Book](https://www.dre.ca.gov/Publications/ReferenceBook.html) explicitly states that agents must not practice law, and that drafting complex legal documents or interpreting contractual language beyond standard C.A.R. forms crosses that line. When you sense a transaction requires interpretation of easement law, boundary disputes, or trust instrument language, the referral to an attorney is not a courtesy — it is a compliance requirement under B&P Code Section 10177(g), which prohibits agents from performing acts requiring a law license.

Agents who [hand off complex files](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) to attorneys or transaction coordinators at the right moment protect both the client and their own license.

## The 2026 Regulatory Landscape for Escrow in California

The DFPI has increased examination frequency for licensed escrow companies following several high-profile escrow theft cases in 2024–2025. New examination protocols under Financial Code Section 17406 now include enhanced cybersecurity reviews for wire fraud prevention. Agents should confirm that their preferred escrow companies have implemented the DFPI's updated security standards before directing clients to wire six- or seven-figure sums.

CalBRE's 2025–2026 strategic plan also emphasizes trust fund audits as a top enforcement priority. Agents holding broker licenses who conduct escrow under the B&P Code 10131(d) exemption should expect heightened scrutiny of their trust account records and segregation practices.

**None of these changes require an attorney at closing.** The escrow-centric model remains firmly intact. But the compliance bar is rising, and the agents who treat escrow selection as an afterthought are the ones most likely to appear on a DRE disciplinary action list.
