## Virginia FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026: The Agent's Liability Landscape

A single missed checkbox on a foreign seller's transaction can shift tens of thousands of dollars in tax liability onto your buyer — or onto you. The **Virginia firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026** remain one of the most misunderstood compliance obligations in residential real estate, and the consequences of getting them wrong go far beyond an awkward phone call to your broker. With Northern Virginia's internationally mobile population and growing foreign investment in suburban markets like Loudoun and Prince William counties, agents encounter foreign sellers more frequently than many realize.

FIRPTA — the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act — requires that when a foreign person sells U.S. real property, the buyer must withhold a percentage of the gross sales price and remit it to the IRS. Virginia layers its own state withholding requirement on top. Together, these rules create a dual-withholding obligation that agents, settlement attorneys, and buyers must coordinate precisely at closing.

## How Federal FIRPTA Withholding Works in Practice

The IRS mandates withholding under 26 U.S.C. Section 1445. The buyer is technically the "withholding agent," though in Virginia transactions the settlement attorney or title company almost always handles mechanical compliance. The default federal withholding rate is 15% of the gross amount realized (the full sales price, not just the gain).

There are two reduced-rate exceptions that agents encounter regularly. If the property will be used by the buyer as a residence and the amount realized is between $300,001 and $1,000,000, the rate drops to 10%. If the amount realized is $300,000 or less and the buyer will use it as a residence, no withholding is required.

The seller can also apply for a withholding certificate using IRS Form 8288-B, which requests a reduced withholding amount based on the seller's actual expected tax liability. This application must be filed before or at closing, but the IRS can take 90 days or longer to process it — a timeline problem agents need to plan around.

## Virginia's Additional State Withholding Under Va. Code Section 58.1-324

**Virginia does not simply defer to FIRPTA — it imposes its own separate withholding requirement.** Under Virginia Code Section 58.1-324, any nonresident seller (foreign or domestic) of Virginia real estate is subject to state income tax withholding at closing. The rate is the lesser of 2.5% of the sales price or the seller's gain on the transaction.

The Virginia Department of Taxation administers this requirement. The withholding is collected by the settlement agent and remitted using Form 765 (Virginia Nonresident Real Property Withholding Return). The seller may apply for an exemption or reduced withholding by filing Form 765-C (Nonresident Withholding Exemption Certificate) with the Department of Taxation before closing.

This means a foreign seller in Virginia faces both the 15% federal FIRPTA withholding and the 2.5% Virginia state withholding — a combined 17.5% of the gross sales price held back at the settlement table. On a $750,000 sale, that is $131,250 withheld. Agents who fail to explain this to their foreign seller clients early in the listing process create enormous friction at closing.

## Key Forms and Filing Deadlines: A Side-by-Side Comparison

| Requirement | Federal (FIRPTA) | Virginia State |
|---|---|---|
| Governing Law | 26 U.S.C. § 1445 | Va. Code § 58.1-324 |
| Default Rate | 15% of gross sales price | 2.5% of sales price or gain |
| Primary Form | IRS Form 8288 + 8288-A | Virginia Form 765 |
| Reduction Request | IRS Form 8288-B | Virginia Form 765-C |
| Filing Deadline | 20 days after closing | 30 days after closing |
| Responsible Party | Buyer (as withholding agent) | Settlement agent |
| Exemption Available | Yes, via withholding certificate | Yes, via exemption certificate |

**The 20-day federal filing deadline is aggressive.** If the settlement attorney misses it, the IRS assesses interest from day one. The Virginia 30-day window provides slightly more breathing room, but the Virginia Department of Taxation does assess late-filing penalties under Va. Code Section 58.1-394.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The liability chain under FIRPTA is clear and unforgiving. If withholding is required and not performed, the buyer is personally liable for the tax that should have been withheld, plus interest and penalties. The IRS can place a lien on the property itself — meaning your buyer's new home could have a federal tax lien attached to it because the withholding obligation was not met at closing.

For agents specifically, liability exposure arises when you have actual knowledge that a seller is a foreign person and fail to inform the settlement agent or ensure withholding procedures are followed. The IRS has pursued transferee's agents in cases where documentation shows the agent knew the seller's foreign status. The Virginia Real Estate Board, operating under the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), can also pursue disciplinary action for violations of Va. Code Section 54.1-2131 (duties of licensees) if an agent's negligence in handling FIRPTA compliance constitutes a failure of competent service.

Beyond regulatory consequences, deal cancellations happen. Settlement attorneys will refuse to close if FIRPTA documentation is incomplete or contradictory. Buyers' agents who discover mid-closing that the seller is foreign and no withholding was planned will pull their clients from the table. Every day of delay costs money — and [understanding Virginia closing procedures](/blog/virginia-title-and-closing-procedures-2026) is essential to preventing these last-minute blowups.

## Common Mistakes Virginia Agents Make with FIRPTA

**The first and most frequent error is assuming the settlement attorney will handle everything without any input from the listing agent.** While settlement attorneys do manage the mechanical withholding, they rely on accurate information from the parties. If the listing agent never identifies the seller's foreign status — or worse, never asks — the attorney may proceed without withholding, exposing everyone to liability.

The second mistake is confusing "nonresident alien" with "nonresident of Virginia." These are distinct legal categories. A U.S. citizen living in Maryland selling Virginia property is a nonresident of Virginia (triggering state withholding under Va. Code Section 58.1-324) but is not a foreign person under FIRPTA. A Canadian citizen living in Fairfax County is a foreign person for FIRPTA purposes but may be a Virginia resident for state withholding purposes. Agents routinely conflate these categories.

Third, agents fail to build FIRPTA timelines into the contract. If a foreign seller wants to apply for a reduced withholding certificate (Form 8288-B), the IRS processing time can exceed 90 days. A standard 30-day closing simply does not accommodate this. Smart listing agents build extended settlement periods or [contingency structures](/blog/virginia-buyer-contingency-deadlines-2026) that account for the withholding certificate timeline.

Fourth, agents neglect to obtain the seller's Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN or ITIN) early in the process. Without it, Form 8288 cannot be properly completed. Many foreign sellers do not have an ITIN and must apply for one using IRS Form W-7, which adds weeks to the timeline.

Fifth, agents assume that because the seller has a U.S. mailing address or bank account, they are not a "foreign person" under Section 1445. Foreign person status is determined by citizenship and residency for tax purposes — not by where someone receives mail.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers operating under the Virginia Real Estate Board's supervision have affirmative obligations to ensure their agents are practicing competently. When it comes to FIRPTA compliance, this means building systems that flag foreign-seller transactions before they reach the closing table.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Risk if Missed |
|---|---|---|
| Listing intake forms | Does the form ask seller citizenship/residency status? | Transaction proceeds without withholding |
| File documentation | Is a FIRPTA affidavit or certification present? | No proof agent performed due diligence |
| Timeline review | Was settlement date adjusted for Form 8288-B processing? | Closing delayed or collapses |
| Settlement agent communication | Written confirmation that settlement agent is aware of foreign status | Liability shifts to buyer/agent |
| State withholding (Form 765) | Confirmation that Virginia withholding is addressed separately from FIRPTA | State penalties assessed |

Brokers should require that every listing file include either a Non-Foreign Affidavit (sometimes called a FIRPTA Affidavit or Certification of Non-Foreign Status) signed by the seller, or documentation that FIRPTA withholding procedures have been initiated. The [Virginia Real Estate Board](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov/Boards/Real-Estate) does not mandate a specific FIRPTA checklist, but DPOR's standards of practice require competent handling of all material aspects of a transaction.

**Implementing a transaction review system that catches foreign-seller status at listing intake — not at closing — is the single most effective compliance measure a brokerage can adopt.** This is especially relevant for brokerages operating in Northern Virginia, where international diplomatic, military, and corporate populations are concentrated. ## The Seller's Non-Foreign Affidavit: Your First Line of Defense

The simplest way to avoid FIRPTA withholding is to confirm that the seller is not a foreign person. This is done through a Non-Foreign Affidavit, also called a Certification of Non-Foreign Status, executed under penalties of perjury. The affidavit must include the seller's name, U.S. taxpayer identification number, and home address, along with a statement that the seller is not a foreign person as defined in Section 1445.

If the seller provides this affidavit and the buyer has no actual knowledge that it is false, the buyer is relieved of withholding obligations. This is the mechanism used in the vast majority of Virginia residential transactions. The problem arises when listing agents never request it, or when the seller refuses to sign it — which itself is a red flag that FIRPTA withholding may apply.

Settlement attorneys in Virginia routinely include Non-Foreign Affidavits in their closing packages. But waiting until the closing table to discover the seller cannot sign one creates avoidable chaos. Listing agents should request this affidavit during the listing period — ideally at the time of executing the listing agreement — so that any FIRPTA issues surface with enough time to address them.
