The Indiana General Assembly spent the 2025 legislative session overhauling key sections of the Indiana Real Estate License Act, and the results hit hard starting July 1, 2026. If you hold an active license in this state, the phrase **Indiana new real estate laws 2026** should already be circled on your calendar. These are not minor tweaks to paperwork flow—they restructure how buyer agency is formed, how disclosures are delivered, and how commission transparency is documented at the transaction level.

The Indiana Professional Licensing Agency (IPLA) published its final guidance in April 2026, confirming enforcement timelines that leave zero grace period for agents who claim ignorance. Below is a full breakdown of what changed, what it means for your daily practice, and what your broker needs to be auditing right now.

## Buyer Representation Agreements Now Required Before Touring

**House Enrolled Act 1247, codified at IC 25-34.1-10-4.5, mandates a written buyer representation agreement before an agent conducts any property showing.** This is the single largest shift in Indiana buyer-side practice in over a decade. Previously, agents could operate under informal arrangements until an offer was imminent.

The statute specifies that the agreement must include a definite termination date, the compensation structure or method for determining compensation, and a clear statement that commission rates are negotiable. IPLA has confirmed that the Indiana Association of Realtors Form 50—the Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement—satisfies these requirements, though brokerages can use custom forms if they contain the statutory elements.

Agents who show property without a signed agreement face disciplinary action under IC 25-34.1-6-11. The IPLA board can impose license suspension, fines up to $10,000, or both. Buyers also gain standing to void any resulting purchase agreement, which means your seller clients could lose deals through no fault of their own.

## Revised Seller Disclosure Requirements Under State Form 46234

Indiana's Residential Real Estate Sales Disclosure Form—State Form 46234—received its first substantive revision since 2019. The updated version, effective July 1, 2026, adds three new disclosure categories: known flooding history beyond FEMA-mapped zones, prior insurance claims related to mold or environmental hazards, and awareness of any pending or threatened governmental actions affecting the property.

Sellers must now complete the updated form, and agents are responsible for confirming the correct version is used. Using the pre-2026 State Form 46234 after July 1 does not satisfy the statutory obligation under IC 32-21-5-7. This is not a technicality—it creates a substantive disclosure gap that exposes both the seller and the listing agent to post-closing liability.

Delivery timing also changed. The disclosure must now reach the buyer no later than three business days after acceptance of the purchase agreement, shortened from the previous informal standard many agents treated as flexible. If the buyer does not receive it within that window, they gain an automatic five-business-day rescission right starting from actual receipt.

## Commission Transparency and Written Documentation

Senate Enrolled Act 389 added IC 25-34.1-10-6.1, which requires agents on both sides of a transaction to provide written disclosure of their compensation arrangement to their own client before any offer is presented. This mirrors the intent behind the [NAR settlement changes](https://www.nar.realtor/the-facts/nar-settlement-faqs) that took effect nationally in August 2024, but Indiana codified it into state statute with specific enforcement teeth.

The disclosure must state the exact amount or percentage the agent expects to receive, the source of that compensation, and whether the compensation is contingent on any conditions. Indiana Association of Realtors Form 55—the Compensation Disclosure to Client—was released in May 2026 to standardize this requirement.

| Requirement | Before July 2026 | After July 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| Written buyer agreement before showing | Not required | Required (IC 25-34.1-10-4.5) |
| Commission disclosure to client | Recommended practice | Mandatory, written, signed (IC 25-34.1-10-6.1) |
| Seller disclosure delivery deadline | No statutory deadline after acceptance | 3 business days after acceptance |
| Buyer rescission right if disclosure late | None codified | 5 business days from actual receipt |
| Penalty ceiling per violation | $1,000 | $10,000 |

## Indiana New Real Estate Laws 2026: Common Mistakes Agents Are Already Making

Since IPLA released its guidance, early enforcement actions and broker audits have surfaced recurring errors. These are not edge cases—they represent patterns that will catch even experienced agents off guard.

**First, agents are using verbal buyer agreements and texted confirmations as substitutes for the written requirement.** IC 25-34.1-10-4.5 explicitly states "written agreement," and IPLA's FAQ clarifies that electronic signatures are acceptable but the document itself must contain all required terms. A text message saying "yes, represent me" does not qualify, even with a reply confirmation.

Second, listing agents are distributing the old State Form 46234 because their transaction management software has not been updated. The form number did not change—only the content did. This means agents cannot rely on the form number alone as a compliance check. You must verify the revision date printed in the lower-left corner reads "Rev. 2026."

Third, agents are disclosing compensation in the MLS remarks field and assuming that satisfies the written disclosure requirement to their client. It does not. IC 25-34.1-10-6.1 requires a separate document signed by the client, not a public-facing MLS entry. The MLS field and the client disclosure serve entirely different legal purposes.

Fourth, dual agency consent forms are being signed after material negotiations have already occurred. Under IC 25-34.1-10-5 as amended, dual agency consent must be obtained before any negotiation activity—not merely before closing. Agents who present a counteroffer before obtaining dual agency consent have already violated the statute.

Fifth, agents are failing to include a definite termination date in buyer agreements. IPLA has flagged "until a property is purchased" or "ongoing until cancelled" language as non-compliant. The statute requires a specific calendar date. If you need to track these deadlines across multiple active buyers, the operational burden is real—which is exactly why [automated checklist systems beat memory alone](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory).

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce Immediately

Managing brokers carry heightened liability under Indiana's supervisory framework. IC 25-34.1-6-12 holds brokers responsible for ensuring their affiliated agents comply with all licensing law provisions. The 2026 amendments did not soften this obligation—they expanded it.

**Brokers should be auditing every active transaction file for four documents that must now exist before certain milestones.** The buyer representation agreement must predate any showing activity. The compensation disclosure (Form 55) must predate any offer submission. The seller disclosure (State Form 46234, Rev. 2026) must be delivered within three business days of acceptance. And dual agency consent, where applicable, must predate any negotiation.

| Audit Checkpoint | Document Required | Must Predate |
|---|---|---|
| First showing | Buyer Representation Agreement (IAR Form 50 or equivalent) | Any property tour |
| Offer submission | Compensation Disclosure (IAR Form 55) | Presentation of offer |
| Acceptance + 3 days | Seller Disclosure (State Form 46234, Rev. 2026) | Buyer's inspection period |
| Dual agency scenario | Limited Agency Consent (IAR Form 42) | Any negotiation activity |

Brokers who discover non-compliance after closing face a difficult choice: self-report to IPLA or risk that a consumer complaint surfaces the violation later under worse circumstances. IPLA has historically shown more leniency toward brokerages that self-report and implement corrective action than those caught through consumer complaints or audits.

Transaction file review should happen at minimum weekly. If your brokerage handles volume that makes manual review impractical, this is precisely [where deals tend to break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) without systematic oversight tools in place.

## Liability Exposure for Non-Compliance

The penalty structure under the amended IC 25-34.1-6-11 escalated significantly. Prior to 2026, maximum fines per violation sat at $1,000. The new ceiling of $10,000 per violation—combined with the ability to stack violations within a single transaction—means a single botched deal could generate $30,000 or more in fines before license suspension is even discussed.

Beyond regulatory fines, civil liability remains a separate exposure channel. Buyers who do not receive timely disclosure can rescind purchase agreements, leaving sellers without a deal and potentially triggering claims against the listing agent's errors and omissions policy. Agents who fail to obtain proper buyer representation agreements may find their compensation claims unenforceable if a dispute arises.

IPLA's disciplinary process also became faster. The 2026 amendments authorized the agency to issue emergency suspensions for "pattern violations"—defined as two or more violations of the same statutory section within a 12-month period. This means an agent who mishandles buyer agreements on two transactions could face suspension before a full hearing occurs.

## How These Changes Interact With Federal Requirements

Indiana's new laws do not replace federal obligations—they layer on top of them. RESPA, TILA, and fair housing requirements remain fully enforceable. The practical effect is that Indiana agents now operate under a tighter combined framework where [wire fraud prevention protocols](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) must coexist with state-specific disclosure timelines.

One area of overlap worth noting: the federal FIRPTA withholding rules apply regardless of Indiana's state-level changes. Agents representing foreign sellers still must ensure proper withholding or exemption documentation exists independently of the state disclosure timeline. The two systems run on different clocks, and missing either one creates separate liability.
