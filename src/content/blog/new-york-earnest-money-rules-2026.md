# New York Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026: What Every Agent and Broker Must Get Right

Mishandling a deposit check in New York can cost you more than a commission—it can cost you your license. Understanding New York earnest money & escrow laws 2026 is not optional for agents operating in one of the country's most attorney-driven real estate markets. The rules here differ sharply from the rest of the nation, and updates from the New York Department of State (DOS) mean that sloppy habits from prior years will not slide anymore.

This post breaks down the statutes that govern earnest money, the forms you must use, the penalties for noncompliance, the mistakes that trip up even experienced agents, and the audit steps brokers should enforce today.

## How New York Structures Earnest Money Differently

**New York is an "attorney state," and that single fact reshapes the entire deposit process.** Unlike most states where a brokerage or title company routinely holds the earnest money, New York's residential transactions overwhelmingly route the deposit to the seller's attorney's Interest on Lawyer Account (IOLA) or escrow account. This convention stems from the Judiciary Law Section 497 requirement that attorneys maintain client funds in segregated accounts and from the customary contract of sale language used across the five boroughs, Long Island, and the Hudson Valley.

Brokerages can still accept and hold deposits under New York Real Property Law (RPL) Article 12-A, specifically Sections 441 and 441-c. When they do, they must comply with 19 NYCRR 175.1, the regulation that mandates a separate trust or escrow account at a New York-authorized banking institution. The account must be clearly labeled as an escrow or trust account and cannot be used for operating expenses under any circumstances.

The practical result is a split system. Upstate transactions and some suburban deals still see brokerages holding deposits, while downstate deals almost always funnel deposits to an attorney. Agents must know which path their transaction follows because the compliance obligations differ depending on who holds the funds.

## Governing Statutes and Regulatory Bodies

| Authority | Scope | Key Provision |
|-----------|-------|---------------|
| NY Real Property Law §441-c | Broker license discipline | Commingling or misappropriating funds is grounds for revocation |
| 19 NYCRR 175.1 | Trust account rules | Requires separate escrow account, proper labeling, and records |
| Judiciary Law §497 | Attorney escrow | Governs IOLA accounts for attorney-held deposits |
| NY DOS Division of Licensing Services | Enforcement | Investigates complaints, audits brokerages, issues fines |
| NY RPL §441(1) | Agent conduct | Prohibits fraud, dishonest dealing, and untrustworthiness |

The [New York Department of State](https://dos.ny.gov/licensing) is the primary enforcement body for brokerage escrow violations. Attorneys holding deposits fall under the jurisdiction of the Appellate Division's disciplinary committees, but agents who facilitate a deposit handoff to an attorney still bear responsibility for timely delivery and accurate documentation.

RPL Section 175.3 also requires that brokers maintain complete records of every deposit received, the date it was deposited into escrow, and the eventual disbursement. These records must be available for DOS inspection for at least three years after the transaction closes or terminates.

## Forms and Contract Language Agents Must Know

New York does not use a statewide MLS purchase agreement the way many other states do. Instead, the standard residential contract of sale—commonly prepared by the seller's attorney—contains the deposit terms. The most widely used template is the combined Bar Association/Real Estate Board form often referred to as the "Blumberg Form 125" or the similar "NYBR Contract of Sale" form. In cooperative transactions, agents encounter the REBNY co-op contract template.

Within these contracts, the deposit clause specifies the amount, the holder (almost always the seller's attorney by name), and conditions for release. Riders attached to the contract frequently add a mortgage contingency deposit-return clause and an inspection contingency clause.

**Agents should pay close attention to the rider language governing when deposits become non-refundable.** Failing to explain these terms to a buyer—or worse, misrepresenting them—triggers RPL 441(1) liability and potential DOS complaints. If your brokerage uses supplemental forms such as an Agency Disclosure Form (DOS-1736), make sure the escrow holder's identity and role are consistent across all documents the buyer signs.

For transactions where the brokerage holds the deposit rather than an attorney, many firms require the buyer to sign an escrow agreement addendum that spells out the conditions under which funds may be released. This internal form is not state-mandated, but it is a best practice that protects the brokerage from interpleader disputes.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The penalties for mishandling earnest money in New York are severe and layered. At the licensing level, RPL 441-c gives the Secretary of State authority to revoke or suspend a broker's or salesperson's license for "demonstrating untrustworthiness or incompetent dealing." Commingling escrow funds with personal or operating accounts is the fastest path to revocation.

At the civil level, a wronged party can sue the agent and brokerage for conversion, breach of fiduciary duty, or fraud. New York courts have awarded both compensatory and punitive damages in deposit mishandling cases. The brokerage's errors and omissions insurance may or may not cover intentional acts—most policies exclude them.

At the criminal level, misappropriating escrow funds over $1,000 can constitute grand larceny under New York Penal Law 155.30, a class E felony carrying up to four years in prison. Even a single instance of "borrowing" from an escrow account to cover a short-term cash flow problem qualifies as criminal conduct if the funds belong to a client.

Beyond individual agents, the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends to lost referrals, damaged brokerage reputation, and potential class-action exposure if multiple clients are affected by systemic escrow mismanagement.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With New York Escrow

**The following errors come up repeatedly in DOS disciplinary proceedings and civil litigation.** Knowing them now means you can avoid the file that ends your career.

First, agents accept a deposit check and hold it in their desk drawer instead of delivering it immediately to the designated escrow holder. New York law does not specify a hard statutory deadline for agents to turn over funds once a fully executed contract exists, but the standard of reasonableness—interpreted by courts as one to two business days—applies. Anything beyond that invites scrutiny.

Second, agents tell buyers their deposit is "fully refundable" without qualifying that statement against the contract's specific contingency deadlines. Once a mortgage contingency expires or an inspection objection period passes, the deposit may become non-refundable by contract. Misrepresenting refundability creates a DOS complaint and civil exposure.

Third, agents fail to confirm the escrow holder's identity and wire instructions independently. [Wire fraud schemes](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) that intercept deposit instructions remain a top threat in New York, especially in high-value Manhattan and Brooklyn transactions. An agent who forwards unverified wire instructions may share liability for the loss.

Fourth, agents at small brokerages commingle their escrow account with their general operating account. This is the single most common violation found in DOS audits. Even temporary commingling—depositing a client check into the wrong account and correcting it days later—counts as a violation.

Fifth, agents neglect to document the chain of custody for cashier's checks and personal checks. If a dispute arises months later and the agent cannot produce a receipt, deposit slip copy, or email confirmation showing when the check was delivered, the agent's credibility collapses.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

| Audit Item | Frequency | Documentation Required |
|------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Escrow account reconciliation | Monthly, minimum | Bank statements matched to individual deposit ledger |
| Deposit delivery confirmation | Per transaction | Signed receipt or attorney acknowledgment email |
| Trust account labeling | Annually (and at account opening) | Bank confirmation letter showing "escrow" or "trust" designation |
| Agent training on RPL 441-c | Annually | Signed acknowledgment of escrow policy |
| Record retention check | Quarterly | Verify three-year retention of closed and terminated files |

Brokers bear personal liability for escrow violations committed by their agents under RPL 441-c and DOS enforcement precedent. A managing broker who fails to supervise escrow procedures cannot claim ignorance as a defense. The DOS has revoked broker licenses even when the actual mishandling was performed by an associate licensee.

**Every brokerage operating in New York should maintain a written escrow policy that agents sign upon affiliation.** That policy should spell out maximum delivery timeframes, prohibited actions (such as endorsing a check or accepting cash), and the exact process for documenting each deposit from receipt to release. Brokers who track multiple active deals simultaneously need a system that flags overdue deposits before they become violations—not after a client calls the DOS.

Quarterly internal audits of the escrow ledger against bank statements will catch discrepancies before they metastasize. ## Dispute Resolution: What Happens When Both Sides Claim the Deposit

New York does not have a statutory escrow dispute resolution mechanism that mirrors states with real estate commission-administered arbitration panels. Instead, when buyer and seller both claim the deposit and the escrow holder (whether attorney or brokerage) cannot determine rightful ownership, the holder must commence an interpleader action in court under CPLR 1006.

For brokerages, this means hiring counsel, filing papers, and depositing the funds with the court. The brokerage then steps out of the dispute, but the legal fees come out of its own pocket. Many brokerages include an indemnification clause in their escrow agreement addendum that allows them to deduct reasonable attorney fees from the escrowed amount before interpleading the remainder—but such clauses must be clearly disclosed to both parties at contract execution to be enforceable.

Agents should never release disputed funds to either party without written consent from both sides or a court order. Doing so, even under pressure from a demanding client, exposes the agent and brokerage to liability to the other party and potential DOS discipline.
