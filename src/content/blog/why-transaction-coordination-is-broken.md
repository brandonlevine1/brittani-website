## The Model Was Already Dying Before AI Showed Up

Transaction coordination as practiced by most brokerages in 2026 is a duct-tape solution pretending to be infrastructure. The role was invented to absorb administrative overflow from agents who could not or would not manage their own files, and it has never evolved beyond that reactive posture. What replaces it is not a better human in the seat — it is the elimination of the seat entirely.

The data tells a blunt story. NAR's 2025 Member Profile reported that 37% of agents involved in a failed transaction cited "missed deadlines or incomplete documentation" as a contributing factor. That number has held steady within two points since 2021, despite widespread adoption of TC services. The conclusion is unavoidable: adding a human coordinator to a broken process does not fix the process.

## Why the Traditional TC Model Cannot Scale

**A transaction coordinator managing 25-40 active files operates in perpetual triage mode.** They check email, scan for new documents, update a spreadsheet or CRM, and move on. This is batch processing applied to a workflow that demands event-driven responses. When a lender uploads a conditional approval at 3 PM on a Friday and the TC does not check until Monday morning, you have lost two days on a timeline that may only have five.

The economics compound the problem. Outsourced TCs charge $350-$550 per file at the national average, according to TC industry surveys published by the Real Estate Transaction Standard (RETS) working group in early 2025. An in-house coordinator demands $55,000-$75,000 in total compensation. Either way, the brokerage is paying for human attention distributed across dozens of files — which means no single file gets continuous attention.

This is not a criticism of the people doing TC work. Most are sharp, detail-oriented professionals operating inside a model that structurally prevents them from catching problems in real time. The constraint is biological: humans cannot monitor 35 concurrent timelines simultaneously without something slipping through.

## Where Deals Actually Break Down

The [common failure points in transactions](/blog/where-deals-break-down) follow a predictable pattern that reveals the TC model's blind spots. Consider the typical contract-to-close timeline:

| Phase | Critical Window | Traditional TC Response | Failure Risk |
|-------|----------------|------------------------|--------------|
| Inspection objection | 48-72 hours | Daily file check | High — missed by 12-24 hours |
| Appraisal contingency | 5-10 days | Calendar reminder | Medium — no monitoring of lender upload |
| Title commitment review | 3-5 days | Manual follow-up call | Medium — relies on title company responsiveness |
| Loan conditions due | Variable | Waiting for lender email | High — lender portals update before emails send |
| Closing disclosure review | 3 business days (TRID) | PDF comparison if time allows | High — errors caught at signing table |

Every one of these phases has a narrow window where delay creates cascading problems. The TC model addresses them sequentially. An autonomous system addresses them concurrently, because monitoring 35 timelines simultaneously is trivial for software and impossible for a person.

## The Post-NAR Settlement Reality

The commission restructuring triggered by the Burnett v. National Association of Realtors settlement has pressured agent margins across the board. When buyer-side compensation is no longer guaranteed through MLS, agents operating on thinner splits cannot afford a $450-per-file TC fee on every deal. The [industry's standard definition of what a TC does](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) was built for an era of predictable 2.5-3% co-broke commissions. That era ended.

**Brokers running teams of 8-15 agents now face a stark arithmetic problem.** A full-time TC handles roughly 300 files per year at capacity. At $65,000 loaded cost, that is $217 per file — affordable if margins hold. But when average commission per side drops from $8,400 to $6,100 (as Redfin reported in Q4 2025 for their markets), that $217 represents a materially larger percentage of gross revenue per transaction. Something has to give.

What gives, increasingly, is the TC headcount. T3 Sixty's 2025 brokerage benchmarking study found that 28% of mid-size brokerages (50-200 agents) reduced TC staff between 2024 and 2025, while 41% reported investing in "automated compliance or transaction tools." The shift is already happening. The question is whether it happens intentionally or by attrition.

## What Actually Replaces It

The replacement is not a chatbot that answers agent questions about their files. It is an autonomous layer that sits between the parties — agents, lenders, title companies, clients — and enforces timeline compliance without waiting to be asked. Three capabilities define the difference from a human TC:

Continuous monitoring means the system watches document portals, email threads, and calendar deadlines around the clock. When a lender uploads a clear-to-close at 11 PM, the system notifies the listing agent and title company immediately rather than waiting for a morning check-in.

Proactive escalation means the system does not just track a deadline — it calculates the risk of missing it based on current velocity. If an appraisal has not been scheduled by day 7 of a 14-day contingency period, the system flags the agent and the lender simultaneously, not on day 12 when it is already too late.

State-specific compliance means the system enforces disclosure requirements that vary dramatically by jurisdiction. An agent working [Virginia disclosure rules](/blog/virginia-property-condition-disclosures-2026) needs different document sets and timelines than one working under [Texas property condition standards](/blog/texas-property-condition-disclosures-2026). A human TC can learn these differences but cannot hold 50-state nuance in active memory across 35 concurrent files.

## The Strongest Counterargument

The most credible objection to removing human TCs is relational. Transactions involve emotional parties making the largest financial decision of their lives. A human coordinator provides a calming presence, answers panicked phone calls, and exercises judgment about when to push and when to give space. Software cannot read the room.

This is a real limitation, and I will not dismiss it. But it conflates two separate functions: administrative coordination and client relationship management. The TC role has always been an awkward hybrid of both. By removing the administrative burden from human hands entirely, you free the agent — or a dedicated client experience role — to focus exclusively on the relational work that actually requires emotional intelligence. The argument for keeping human TCs is actually an argument for better role definition, not for preserving the status quo.

**Stop hiring for a role that technology has made structurally obsolete at its core function.** If you have a strong TC on staff, redefine their position around exception handling, client communication, and quality assurance of the autonomous output.
