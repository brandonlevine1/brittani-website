## Understanding Tennessee FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

Every Tennessee agent who has ever listed a property for an international client knows the sinking feeling: closing is two weeks out, the title company asks for a FIRPTA affidavit, and nobody flagged the seller's citizenship status at listing intake. The Tennessee firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 remain governed primarily by Internal Revenue Code Section 1445, but the practical enforcement—who does what, which forms get filed, and who faces consequences—plays out differently in Tennessee than in states with their own layered withholding statutes. Getting this wrong does not just delay a closing; it creates personal liability for buyers, agents, and closing attorneys alike.

Tennessee's real estate closings are typically handled by attorneys rather than escrow officers, which shifts certain procedural burdens compared to escrow-state transactions. The Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) expects licensees to understand material facts that affect a transaction's ability to close, and a seller's foreign status is squarely within that expectation. This article breaks down the federal framework as it applies in Tennessee, identifies the specific forms and deadlines agents must track, and spells out the consequences of non-compliance.

## How FIRPTA Applies to Tennessee Real Estate Transactions

The Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act requires that when a "foreign person" sells U.S. real property, the buyer must withhold 15% of the gross sales price and remit it to the IRS. A foreign person includes non-resident alien individuals, foreign corporations, foreign partnerships, foreign trusts, and foreign estates. The statute does not care whether the seller has lived in Tennessee for twenty years—if they lack U.S. tax residency or citizenship, FIRPTA applies.

**Tennessee does not have a separate state-level withholding requirement for foreign sellers.** The Hall Income Tax, which once taxed certain investment income, was fully repealed effective January 1, 2021, under Tennessee Code Annotated Section 67-2-102. This means agents only need to manage federal FIRPTA compliance, not a parallel state form—a meaningful simplification compared to states like California or Maryland that stack state withholding on top of federal requirements.

The withholding rate sits at 15% of the amount realized (gross sales price) for properties sold above $1,000,000. For properties between $300,001 and $1,000,000 where the buyer intends to use the property as a residence, the rate drops to 10%. Properties at or below $300,000 where the buyer will reside are exempt from withholding entirely. These thresholds have not changed for 2026.

## Key Forms and Filing Deadlines Tennessee Agents Must Know

The primary IRS forms involved in a Tennessee FIRPTA transaction are Form 8288 (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Certain Dispositions by Foreign Persons) and Form 8288-A (Statement of Withholding on Dispositions by Foreign Persons of U.S. Real Property Interests). The buyer—or more practically, the closing attorney acting as withholding agent—must file these within 20 days of the closing date and remit the withheld funds to the IRS simultaneously.

If the foreign seller wants to reduce the withholding amount because their actual tax liability will be lower than 15%, they file IRS Form 8288-B (Application for Withholding Certificate for Dispositions by Foreign Persons of U.S. Real Property Interests) before closing. The IRS typically takes 90 days to process these applications, which means agents must identify foreign sellers early enough to allow this timeline.

| Form | Purpose | Filed By | Deadline |
|------|---------|----------|----------|
| 8288 | Remit withholding to IRS | Buyer/Withholding Agent | 20 days after closing |
| 8288-A | Provide seller credit statement | Buyer/Withholding Agent | Filed with 8288 |
| 8288-B | Request reduced withholding | Seller (foreign person) | Before closing (90-day processing) |
| W-7 | Apply for ITIN if seller lacks one | Seller | Before or with 8288-B |
| Affidavit of Non-Foreign Status | Certify seller is NOT foreign | Seller | At closing |

The Affidavit of Non-Foreign Status (sometimes called a FIRPTA affidavit or certification) is governed by Treasury Regulation Section 1.1445-2(b). In Tennessee closings, the closing attorney typically prepares this document, but the listing agent should confirm its presence in the closing package well before the settlement date.

## Liability Exposure When Agents Fail to Comply

**The statutory liability under FIRPTA falls on the buyer as the "transferee," but the chain of responsibility extends further in practice.** If the buyer fails to withhold, the IRS can assess the full 15% withholding amount against them, plus interest and penalties under IRC Section 6651. The buyer then has a cause of action against anyone—including the listing agent—who provided false information about the seller's status.

Tennessee closing attorneys who serve as withholding agents face direct liability under IRC Section 1461 for failure to collect and remit. TREC Rule 1260-02-.12 addresses licensee competence and requires agents to disclose material facts that affect a transaction. An agent who knows or should have known that a seller is a foreign person and fails to raise the issue could face disciplinary action, including fines up to $1,000 per violation and potential license suspension under Tennessee Code Annotated Section 62-13-312.

Beyond regulatory penalties, agents face civil liability. If a deal falls apart because FIRPTA was discovered at the last minute and the buyer cannot fund both the purchase price and the withholding obligation, the resulting damages—lost deposits, extended carrying costs, failed 1031 exchanges—can generate lawsuits naming the listing agent and their brokerage. Errors and omissions insurance may or may not cover FIRPTA-related claims depending on the policy's exclusions for tax advice.

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Agents Make With FIRPTA

The first and most frequent error is failing to ask about citizenship status at listing intake. Tennessee's standard listing agreement through the Tennessee Association of Realtors does not include a mandatory FIRPTA disclosure checkbox, so agents must affirmatively raise the question. A seller's Tennessee driver's license, local address, or fluent English does not establish U.S. person status for FIRPTA purposes—only citizenship or permanent resident status (green card) does.

The second mistake is conflating "resident alien" with "non-resident alien." A seller holding a valid green card (Form I-551) is treated as a U.S. person under FIRPTA regardless of where they actually live. Agents who see a foreign passport and assume FIRPTA applies without checking immigration status create unnecessary panic and delays. Conversely, agents who assume someone living in Memphis for a decade is automatically exempt—without confirming their immigration documentation—miss genuine FIRPTA obligations.

**Third, agents routinely underestimate the timeline for a withholding certificate application.** Filing Form 8288-B after the listing goes active but before an offer arrives gives the seller a realistic shot at reduced withholding. Waiting until the contract is signed and then discovering that 15% of the gross sales price will be held leaves everyone scrambling. The 90-day IRS processing window is not negotiable, and there is no expedited track for residential transactions.

Fourth, some agents incorrectly tell buyers that FIRPTA withholding comes out of the seller's proceeds like a normal closing cost. While the funds do originate from what would otherwise be seller proceeds, the legal obligation to withhold rests on the buyer. If the closing attorney distributes full proceeds to the seller without withholding, the buyer—not the seller—owes the IRS. This distinction matters enormously when structuring [closing cost responsibilities](/blog/washington-who-pays-closing-costs-2026) and explaining net sheets.

Fifth, agents sometimes accept a verbal assurance of U.S. citizenship without obtaining the written Affidavit of Non-Foreign Status. Treasury Regulations require that the certification be in writing, signed under penalties of perjury, and include the seller's name, U.S. taxpayer identification number, and address. A handshake confirmation has zero legal protective value.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Tennessee brokers have supervisory obligations under TREC Rule 1260-02-.01 that extend to FIRPTA compliance in their agents' transactions. A brokerage that repeatedly fails to withhold or flag foreign seller status risks being named in IRS enforcement actions and TREC complaints. Broker-level oversight should include at minimum three operational controls.

| Broker Audit Point | What to Check | When to Check |
|---|---|---|
| Listing intake form | Does it include a FIRPTA/citizenship question? | At listing agreement review |
| Transaction file pre-closing | Is a FIRPTA affidavit or withholding documentation present? | 10+ days before closing |
| Closing disclosure review | Does the settlement statement reflect proper withholding amount? | At final CD review |
| Post-closing file audit | Are Forms 8288/8288-A copies in the file? | Within 30 days of closing |
| Agent training records | Has the agent completed FIRPTA training? | Annual CE review |

Brokers should build FIRPTA into their standard [transaction coordination checklists](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) rather than relying on closing attorneys to catch everything. While Tennessee's attorney-close model means the lawyer bears significant responsibility, TREC holds the supervising broker accountable for the licensee's conduct throughout the transaction.

A broker-mandated policy requiring listing agents to document the seller's FIRPTA status within five days of listing execution creates a paper trail that protects the firm. If the seller refuses to answer or provides ambiguous information, the broker should require the agent to notify the closing attorney immediately and confirm in writing that FIRPTA may apply.

## Exemptions and Reduced Withholding Scenarios

Not every transaction involving a foreign seller triggers the full 15% withholding. The primary residential use exemption eliminates withholding entirely when the property sells for $300,000 or less and the buyer signs an affidavit confirming they will use it as a residence for at least 50% of the days the property is in use during the first two twelve-month periods after closing. For Nashville's current median home price—well above $400,000—this exemption applies less frequently than agents might hope.

The withholding certificate process under Form 8288-B allows sellers to demonstrate that their actual tax liability will be less than 15% of the gross price. Common scenarios include sellers who purchased the property recently with minimal gain, sellers with large deductible expenses against the sale, or sellers whose maximum tax rate on the gain is lower than the withholding percentage. The IRS will issue a certificate authorizing reduced or zero withholding if the application is supported.

Installment sales create additional complexity. When a foreign seller carries a note as part of a Tennessee transaction, the buyer must withhold 15% from each principal payment—not just at closing. Agents structuring seller-financed deals with foreign sellers need to coordinate closely with the buyer's tax counsel and the closing attorney to build [proper wire and payment procedures](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) into the installment agreement.

## Practical Workflow for Tennessee Listing Agents

The most effective approach begins before the listing agreement is signed. During the pre-listing consultation, ask directly: "Are you a U.S. citizen or permanent resident?" Document the answer in your CRM and on your intake form. If the answer is no—or if the seller hesitates—flag the file immediately and notify your closing attorney.

For confirmed foreign sellers, recommend they consult a tax professional and file Form 8288-B within the first two weeks of listing. This gives the IRS processing timeline the best chance of completing before closing. Meanwhile, prepare buyer agents to explain the withholding obligation to their clients at offer stage—buyers need to understand that the 15% comes from seller proceeds at closing, but the legal obligation is theirs.

**At contract negotiation, include specific language addressing the FIRPTA withholding timeline.** The Tennessee Association of Realtors Purchase and Sale Agreement allows additional provisions where parties can specify deadlines for withholding certificate applications and establish whether closing will be delayed pending IRS response. Failing to address this in the contract creates a dispute about who bears holding costs if the certificate is delayed.
