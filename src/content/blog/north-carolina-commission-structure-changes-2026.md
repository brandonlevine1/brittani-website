## The New Commission Reality for North Carolina Agents

The North Carolina commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 have reshaped how every licensed agent in this state earns, documents, and collects compensation. If you closed deals before August 2024 without a signed buyer representation agreement, that workflow is now a compliance violation waiting to happen. The settlement's practice changes, combined with existing North Carolina Real Estate Commission (NCREC) rules, created a layered regulatory environment that demands precision from both buyer agents and listing agents.

North Carolina was never a state where handshake deals on compensation flew under the radar easily. The NCREC has long required written agency agreements under N.C. General Statute 93A-6(a)(4) and the administrative rules codified in 21 NCAC 58A. But the NAR settlement added a federal overlay that eliminated cooperative compensation fields from MLS systems and mandated written buyer agreements before touring begins. The combination means agents now face enforcement pressure from two directions simultaneously.

## What the NAR Settlement Actually Changed in Practice

**The single biggest operational shift is that buyer agent compensation can no longer appear as a blanket offer in MLS listings.** Before August 17, 2024, a listing agent in Charlotte or Raleigh could enter "2.5% to buyer agent" in the MLS compensation field, and every buyer agent in the market knew what they would earn. That field no longer exists on any NAR-affiliated MLS, including Canopy MLS and Triangle MLS.

Sellers can still agree to pay a buyer's agent. That has not been outlawed. But the mechanism changed entirely. Compensation from the seller must now be negotiated as part of the purchase offer or through a separate concession agreement, documented outside the MLS. This means buyer agents must know their fee arrangement before writing an offer, not after.

The second major change is the mandatory written buyer representation agreement before any touring activity. NCAR Standard Form 220-T (Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement) is the most commonly used form for this purpose. It must include a specific dollar amount or percentage that the buyer agrees to pay their agent, and it cannot contain open-ended language like "whatever the seller offers."

## North Carolina Forms You Must Be Using

North Carolina agents work within the NCAR/NCREALTORS standard form library, and several forms received updates or increased importance after the settlement. Here is how the most relevant forms map to the new commission structure:

| Form Number | Form Name | When Required | Key Commission Element |
|---|---|---|---|
| 220-T | Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement | Before touring any property | Must state specific compensation amount or percentage |
| 101 | Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement | At listing appointment | No longer includes blanket co-op compensation offer |
| 2-T | Offer to Purchase and Contract | With every purchase offer | Buyer agent fee request may appear as seller concession |
| 104 | Compensation Agreement and Authority to Negotiate | When seller agrees to pay buyer agent | Documents off-MLS compensation arrangement |
| 201 | Dual Agency/Designated Agency Addendum | When firm represents both parties | Must clarify compensation split within firm |

The 220-T form underwent specific revisions in late 2024 to comply with the settlement's requirement that buyer compensation be stated as a specific, non-open-ended figure. Agents who are still using pre-revision versions of this form are exposing themselves to audit findings.

If you are managing multiple active transactions with different compensation arrangements simultaneously, tracking which agreements govern which deal becomes a serious administrative burden. ## Penalties for Non-Compliance in North Carolina

The NCREC does not treat commission documentation failures as minor paperwork oversights. Under 21 NCAC 58A .0116, agents must maintain records of all agency agreements and compensation arrangements for a minimum of three years. Failure to produce these records during an audit constitutes a violation of license law.

**Specific consequences include license suspension or revocation under N.C.G.S. 93A-6(b), civil fines up to $5,000 per violation, and mandatory continuing education requirements.** The Commission can also issue letters of reprimand that appear on your public license record permanently. For brokers-in-charge, the stakes multiply because they carry vicarious liability for their agents' documentation failures under 21 NCAC 58A .0110.

Beyond regulatory penalties, a deal can fall apart entirely if compensation was never properly documented. If a buyer refuses to pay their agent's fee at closing because no signed 220-T exists, the agent has no enforceable claim. North Carolina courts have consistently held that oral compensation agreements in real estate are unenforceable under the Statute of Frauds, N.C.G.S. 22-2. The agent simply loses the commission with no legal recourse.

## Common Mistakes North Carolina Agents Are Making Right Now

After eighteen months of living under the new rules, patterns have emerged. These are the five most frequent errors showing up in NCREC complaint files and brokerage audits across the state.

**First, agents are touring properties before executing the 220-T.** Some agents treat the buyer agreement as something to handle "when the client gets serious." The settlement requires it before any touring activity. A single showing without a signed agreement means the agent performed compensable services with no enforceable right to payment.

Second, agents are using vague compensation language. Writing "up to 3%" or "to be determined by seller concession" in the 220-T violates the settlement's specificity requirement. The form must state a definite amount. If you want to cap your fee at what the seller offers, you still need a specific maximum number in the agreement.

Third, listing agents are communicating cooperative compensation offers through back channels. Some agents have taken to advertising buyer agent compensation on social media, personal websites, or in agent-to-agent emails. The [NAR settlement FAQ](https://www.nar.realtor/the-facts/nar-settlement-faqs) explicitly prohibits sellers from making compensation offers through the MLS, but regulators are watching off-MLS channels for practices that functionally replicate the old system.

Fourth, agents are failing to explain the 220-T to buyers in plain language. When a buyer does not understand that they might owe their agent money out of pocket if the seller refuses a concession, the resulting complaint lands on the agent's license record. Informed consent requires actual explanation, not just a signature on a form.

Fifth, agents are not updating or terminating buyer agreements when relationships change. If a buyer fires their agent verbally but no written termination exists, the agent may later claim a commission on a property the buyer purchased with a different agent. This creates disputes that the NCREC must adjudicate, and both agents risk findings of misconduct.

## What Brokers-in-Charge Must Audit and Enforce

The broker-in-charge (BIC) in North Carolina carries direct supervisory liability under 21 NCAC 58A .0110. The NCREC expects BICs to maintain active oversight of their agents' compliance with commission documentation rules. Passive management is not a defense.

| Audit Item | Frequency | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|---|
| 220-T execution dates | Every new buyer client | Date signed vs. date of first showing | Showing occurred before agreement signed |
| Compensation specificity | Every 220-T on file | Dollar amount or fixed percentage stated | "TBD" or range language present |
| Form version | Quarterly | Current NCAR revision date on all active forms | Pre-August 2024 form versions still in use |
| Seller concession documentation | Every closed transaction | Form 104 or 2-T addendum on file | No written record of who pays buyer agent |
| Three-year retention | Annual | All closed and terminated agreements archived | Files purged before 36-month minimum |

BICs should also be conducting spot-check reviews of their agents' communications. If an agent is promising buyers "you won't pay anything out of pocket" without documenting how that works in writing, the brokerage faces liability when reality does not match the promise. The NCREC has indicated in recent rule interpretation letters that misleading statements about compensation violate N.C.G.S. 93A-6(a)(1), which prohibits "unworthiness or incompetency."

For brokerages managing high transaction volume, missing even one improperly documented deal creates exposure. ## How Compensation Gets Handled at Closing Now

The closing process in North Carolina runs through attorneys, not title companies acting independently. This means the closing attorney must see documentation supporting every disbursement on the settlement statement. If a buyer agent fee appears on the HUD or Closing Disclosure, the attorney will require either a signed 220-T showing the buyer agreed to pay, a seller concession documented in the purchase contract (Form 2-T), or a separate Form 104 compensation agreement signed by the seller.

Without one of those documents, the closing attorney will refuse to disburse the buyer agent's fee. This is not theoretical. Attorneys in the Triangle and Triad markets have reported increased frequency of commission holdups since mid-2025 because agents arrive at closing without clean paper trails. The agent does not get paid that day. Sometimes the agent never gets paid at all.

**Agents who build documentation checkpoints into their transaction workflow avoid this entirely.** The compensation arrangement should be confirmed in writing at three stages: when the buyer agreement is signed, when the offer is submitted, and when closing instructions are prepared. If the answer to "who pays the buyer agent and how much" changes between any of those stages, a new written agreement must memorialize the change.
