## Why Tennessee Teams & Supervision Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

The Tennessee Real Estate Commission has sharpened its enforcement posture around team structures, advertising compliance, and broker oversight obligations. If you operate within a team—or manage one—understanding Tennessee teams & supervision rules 2026 is no longer optional housekeeping; it is a license-preservation exercise. The regulatory framework sits primarily within Tennessee Code Annotated Title 62, Chapter 13, and the TREC Rules found in Chapter 1260 of the Tennessee Administrative Code.

TREC opened 247 complaint investigations in the 2024-2025 fiscal year, and advertising violations—many tied to team branding—ranked among the top three categories. The commission has signaled through its public meeting minutes that 2026 enforcement will target teams that obscure their brokerage affiliation or allow unlicensed persons to perform licensable activities. Agents who assume their broker "handles all that" are playing a dangerous game with their own licenses.

## The Statutory Framework Governing Teams

**Tennessee does not have a standalone "team" statute; instead, it regulates teams through existing licensure, supervision, and advertising provisions.** The controlling authority is T.C.A. 62-13-301 through 62-13-318, supplemented by TREC Rules 1260-02-.12 (advertising), 1260-02-.01 (definitions), and 1260-02-.09 (office requirements). Together, these sections create a matrix of obligations that apply whether you call your group a "team," a "group," or just use a catchy brand name on your yard signs.

Under T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(14), a licensee may be disciplined for "failing to exercise adequate supervision over the activities of any licensed or unlicensed person." That language applies to both the principal broker and to any team leader who holds an affiliate broker or broker license and directs the work of others. TREC has interpreted this broadly: if you assign tasks, review offers, or approve marketing for other licensees, you carry supervisory responsibility regardless of your formal title.

The [Tennessee Real Estate Commission's official rules page](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/trec.html) is the authoritative source for rule text updates, and agents should bookmark it for any mid-year amendments.

## Advertising Rules That Trip Up Teams

TREC Rule 1260-02-.12 governs all advertising, which the commission defines broadly to include signs, social media, business cards, websites, and even email signatures. Every advertisement must include the firm name as registered with TREC. A team name alone is never sufficient.

| Requirement | Compliant Example | Non-Compliant Example |
|---|---|---|
| Firm name prominence | "The Harper Team, XYZ Realty" | "The Harper Team" (no firm name) |
| Team leader identification | "Led by Jane Harper, Broker" | No individual license status shown |
| Phone number attribution | Firm or individual number disclosed | Generic Google Voice with no licensee tied |
| Social media profiles | Bio contains firm name | Only team branding visible |
| Yard signs | Firm name equal or greater prominence | Team logo dominates; firm name in 6pt font |

The "equal or greater prominence" standard trips up more teams than any other provision. TREC measures prominence by a combination of font size, color contrast, and placement. Burying the brokerage name at the bottom in gray text on a gray background fails this test, even if the name is technically present.

**Penalties for advertising violations range from a letter of warning to a $1,000 civil penalty per occurrence, and repeat offenders face license suspension.** Because each sign, each social post, and each business card constitutes a separate advertisement, a single branding mistake replicated across 200 postcards could theoretically generate 200 separate violations.

## Supervision Duties: What Brokers Owe Their Teams

The principal broker's supervisory obligation under T.C.A. 62-13-312 cannot be delegated away entirely. A broker may designate a supervisory affiliate broker or branch manager, but the principal broker remains the responsible party in any TREC disciplinary proceeding. This is a personal liability that follows the broker regardless of any internal agreement or team structure.

Supervision means more than signing off on closed files. TREC expects brokers to maintain written policies covering transaction management, trust account handling, advertising approval, and the assignment of duties to unlicensed assistants. These policies should be memorialized in a firm's office policy manual—a document TREC auditors routinely request during investigations.

For teams specifically, brokers must verify that every person performing licensable activities holds an active Tennessee license affiliated with the firm. The relevant forms here are the [TREC Application for Transfer](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/trec.html) (used when a licensee moves firms) and the Firm License Application, both filed through the Tennessee Online Regulatory Authority (ORA) portal. If a team recruits a new member from another brokerage, that agent's license must be transferred and active before they conduct any business under the team's banner—not after.

## Unlicensed Assistants: The Bright Line

Tennessee draws a clear boundary around what unlicensed team members can and cannot do. TREC's published guidance specifies that unlicensed assistants may perform purely clerical and administrative tasks—scheduling appointments, assembling documents for a licensee's review, placing signs, and answering phones with scripted factual information. They may not show property, hold open houses, discuss terms, explain contracts, or negotiate on behalf of any party.

**The moment an unlicensed assistant offers an opinion on price, explains a contingency, or walks a buyer through a property while the licensee is absent, both the assistant and the supervising broker face disciplinary action.** Under T.C.A. 62-13-301, engaging in real estate brokerage without a license is a Class A misdemeanor, carrying fines up to $2,500 and up to 11 months and 29 days in jail.

Teams that grow quickly often blur these lines unintentionally. A transaction coordinator hired as an unlicensed assistant starts "helping" a buyer understand the inspection report. A showing assistant begins answering questions about HOA dues. These incremental boundary crossings can accumulate into a serious enforcement action. Understanding [what a transaction coordinator is](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) versus what requires licensure is a baseline competency for every team leader.

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Teams Make

**First, teams frequently fail to update advertising materials when a member leaves the group.** A departed agent's name lingering on a website or a Zillow team page creates the false impression that the person still operates under that broker's supervision. TREC treats this as misleading advertising.

Second, team leaders sometimes execute buyer agency agreements or listing contracts in the team's name rather than the firm's name. Tennessee requires that all contracts be entered into in the name of the licensed firm. The Tennessee Association of Realtors forms—such as the TAR Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement (Form RF 101) and the Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement (Form RF 131)—have blanks designated for the firm name, not the team name.

Third, teams operating across multiple offices under the same principal broker sometimes fail to maintain separate office licenses. TREC Rule 1260-02-.09 requires each physical office location to hold its own firm license, and a team that operates from a satellite location without proper licensure exposes everyone involved.

Fourth, internal commission splits documented only in side agreements between team members sometimes omit the broker's awareness. Every compensation arrangement must flow through the principal broker per T.C.A. 62-13-304. An affiliate broker paying another affiliate broker directly—even within the same team—violates Tennessee law.

Fifth, teams neglect to include their license numbers in digital advertising. While not always required on every medium, TREC's guidance strongly recommends license number inclusion, and its absence raises red flags during audits.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

| Audit Area | Frequency | What to Check |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising compliance | Monthly | All active ads, social profiles, signs, and printed materials for firm name prominence |
| License status | Quarterly | ORA portal verification that every team member's license is active and affiliated |
| Contract execution | Per transaction | Contracts bear firm name, correct form numbers, and proper signatures |
| Unlicensed activity | Ongoing | Task logs showing assistants perform only permissible clerical duties |
| Compensation flow | Per closing | All commission disbursements route through the firm, not peer-to-peer |
| Policy manual acknowledgment | Annually | Written confirmation each team member received and read the office policy |

Brokers who skip these audits expose themselves to vicarious liability. Under T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(14), inadequate supervision is an independent ground for discipline—meaning the broker can lose their license even if the underlying violation was committed entirely by an affiliate broker or team member. The commission does not accept "I didn't know" as a defense when the broker's own supervisory duties required them to know.
