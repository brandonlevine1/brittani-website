## Why Ohio Commission & Licensing Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing (ODREPL) has tightened enforcement in recent years, and the Ohio commission & licensing rules 2026 cycle brings changes that affect how agents earn, document, and receive compensation. Agents who treated commission agreements as afterthoughts are now facing disciplinary actions that range from formal reprimands to license revocation. The regulatory framework is built on Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Chapter 4735 and Ohio Administrative Code (OAC) Chapter 1301:5-1, and both contain provisions that trip up even experienced licensees.

**Understanding the statutory backbone is non-negotiable if you want to keep your license active and your commission checks flowing.** The Ohio Real Estate Commission (OREC), which advises the ODREPL, has publicly stated that consumer complaints related to undisclosed compensation arrangements rose 18 percent between 2023 and 2025. That trend means audits are more likely, complaints are investigated faster, and the margin for error has shrunk.

This post breaks down the specific statutes, forms, compliance failures, and broker-level responsibilities that matter right now in Ohio.

## The Statutory Framework Governing Commissions in Ohio

ORC Section 4735.02 establishes that no person may engage in real estate brokerage activities without holding a valid license issued by the Ohio Division. Section 4735.21 goes further: it prohibits any licensee from paying or receiving compensation except through their employing broker. This is not a suggestion — it is a hard legal boundary.

The companion statute, ORC Section 4735.62, governs agency disclosure and written agreements. Since August 2024, Ohio requires that any agency relationship — and the compensation tied to it — be documented in writing before services are rendered. The Ohio Association of Realtors (OAR) publishes standardized forms that satisfy these requirements, including the Exclusive Right to Sell Agreement (OAR Form 101) and the Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement (OAR Form 201).

Compensation splits between cooperating brokerages are typically documented through MLS participation agreements and confirmed via the closing statement. However, the post-NAR settlement environment means agents should no longer assume buyer-side compensation will appear in MLS fields. Written buyer agreements under OAR Form 201 must now specify the compensation amount or method the buyer's agent will receive, regardless of any MLS offer.

## Form Numbers and Documents Ohio Agents Must Know

Ohio's compliance architecture relies on specific forms that serve as both contractual instruments and regulatory shields. Missing even one creates exposure.

| Form | Purpose | When Required |
|------|---------|---------------|
| OAR Form 101 | Exclusive Right to Sell (Listing Agreement) | Before marketing a property |
| OAR Form 201 | Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement | Before showing properties or negotiating |
| OAR Form 300 | Agency Disclosure Statement | At first substantive contact |
| OAR Form 800 | Residential Purchase Agreement | At offer submission |
| ODREPL CE Transcript | Proof of continuing education | At license renewal (every 3 years) |

**OAR Form 300 — the Consumer Guide to Agency Relationships — must be presented at the earliest meaningful contact with a prospective client.** ORC Section 4735.57 defines "substantive contact" as any interaction beyond casual conversation where real estate needs are discussed. Failing to present Form 300 at this stage is one of the most frequently cited violations in Ohio disciplinary cases.

Agents should also be familiar with the Dual Agency Consent form embedded within OAR Form 300. Ohio permits dual agency only with written, informed consent from both parties. Without it, any commission earned in a dual agency scenario is vulnerable to disgorgement — meaning you may be forced to return it entirely.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: What Agents Actually Face

The consequences of violating Ohio's commission and licensing rules go beyond a slap on the wrist. ORC Section 4735.18 lists grounds for disciplinary action, and the ODREPL has broad authority to impose sanctions.

A first offense for practicing without a valid license can result in a fine of up to $2,500 per transaction under ORC 4735.99. Repeat violations escalate to criminal misdemeanor charges. An agent whose license lapses — even by a single day — technically cannot receive compensation for any transaction that closes during that gap, and the employing broker may also face disciplinary review.

For commission-related violations, the most common penalty is a formal reprimand paired with mandatory continuing education. However, cases involving undisclosed dual agency or kickback arrangements under ORC 4735.18(A)(6) can lead to license suspension of one to three years. The ODREPL also cooperates with the Ohio Attorney General's office on consumer fraud referrals, which can add civil liability on top of administrative sanctions.

Deal cancellation is another real risk. If a buyer discovers that their agent failed to execute a proper representation agreement, they may have grounds to rescind the purchase contract entirely under Ohio common law principles of agency. The brokerage then loses the commission and potentially faces a civil suit from the seller's side for interference with the transaction. These cascading failures are [exactly where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) in practice.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make With Commission and Licensing

Five errors appear repeatedly in ODREPL disciplinary records and should be on every agent's radar.

**First: presenting the agency disclosure form too late.** Agents frequently wait until the offer stage to hand over OAR Form 300, but ORC 4735.57 requires it at first substantive contact. An open house conversation about a buyer's price range qualifies as substantive contact. Waiting until later creates a violation that the buyer can raise retroactively.

Second: failing to execute a buyer representation agreement before negotiating offers. Post-settlement rules make this non-optional, yet agents still submit offers without a signed OAR Form 201 in their file. The ODREPL has flagged this as a priority enforcement area for 2026.

Third: accepting referral fees or compensation directly from another agent rather than through the employing broker. ORC 4735.21 is unambiguous — all compensation flows through brokerages. Even a $500 referral check written agent-to-agent violates this statute, and both the paying and receiving agent can face discipline.

Fourth: letting a license expire during an active transaction. Ohio's three-year renewal cycle catches agents off guard, especially those who close transactions infrequently. The 30-hour CE requirement, including the mandatory 3-hour core law course, must be completed before the renewal deadline — not during a grace period, because Ohio does not offer one for salespersons.

Fifth: failing to document commission modifications in writing. When a seller agrees to reduce the listing commission to close a deal, that modification must be memorialized in a signed amendment to the listing agreement. Verbal agreements to adjust commission are unenforceable under Ohio's Statute of Frauds (ORC 1335.05) and can spark post-closing disputes that the brokerage will lose.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear direct supervisory liability under ORC 4735.18(A)(22) for the actions of their affiliated agents. The ODREPL does not accept "I didn't know" as a defense when an agent under your license violates commission or disclosure rules.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Statutory Basis |
|------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Agency disclosure (Form 300) in every file | Per transaction | ORC 4735.57 |
| Signed buyer/seller representation agreement | Per transaction | ORC 4735.62 |
| Agent license status and CE compliance | Quarterly | ORC 4735.14 |
| Commission disbursement records | Monthly | ORC 4735.21, OAC 1301:5-1-02 |
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | ORC 4735.18(A)(23) |

**Brokers should run a quarterly license status check on every affiliated agent through the ODREPL's online verification portal.** An agent whose license has lapsed — even unknowingly — exposes the brokerage to liability for every transaction that agent touches during the lapse period. The [training new agents on timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) process should include calendar reminders for renewal deadlines from day one.

Commission disbursement documentation is another audit priority. Every payment from the brokerage to an agent must correspond to a closed transaction with a fully executed commission agreement on file. Random audits by the ODREPL will request these records, and brokerages that cannot produce them within 30 days of a request face escalating fines under OAC 1301:5-1-02.

Trust account compliance remains a perennial issue. Ohio requires that earnest money deposits be placed in the brokerage trust account within two business days of acceptance under ORC 4735.18(A)(23). Commingling trust funds with operating funds — even temporarily — is grounds for immediate license suspension. Brokers should reconcile trust accounts monthly and maintain a paper trail that connects each deposit to a specific transaction file.

## How the Post-Settlement Landscape Changes Ohio Practice

The ripple effects of the 2024 NAR settlement have fully reached Ohio's market in 2026. Buyer agent compensation is no longer guaranteed through MLS cooperative compensation fields, which means the written buyer representation agreement (OAR Form 201) is now the primary document establishing an agent's right to payment.

Ohio-specific practice requires that Form 201 specify either a flat fee, a percentage, or a formula for calculating the buyer agent's compensation. The form must also disclose whether the agent will seek compensation from the seller, the buyer, or both. Ambiguity here creates collection problems — and the ODREPL has signaled that complaints arising from unclear compensation terms in buyer agreements will be treated as violations of ORC 4735.18(A)(9), which covers misleading representations.

Listing agents face a parallel obligation. If a seller agrees to offer compensation to a buyer's broker, that offer must be documented in the listing agreement or a separate written authorization. Verbal offers of cooperation made at showings or through informal channels are not enforceable and create audit risk for both sides. This is the kind of [paperwork friction that agents resist](/blog/why-agents-hate-paperwork) but cannot afford to ignore.

## Continuing Education and Renewal Requirements for 2026

Ohio salesperson licenses renew on a three-year cycle, with the next major cohort renewing by December 31, 2026. The [Ohio Division of Real Estate](https://com.ohio.gov/divisions-and-programs/real-estate-and-professional-licensing) requires 30 hours of approved continuing education, broken into 27 hours of elective coursework and 3 hours of mandatory core law.

The core law course for 2026 covers recent statutory changes, including amendments to disclosure requirements and post-settlement compensation documentation. Agents who complete CE through unapproved providers — or who fail to verify that their provider holds ODREPL approval — risk having their hours rejected at renewal, which can trigger a license lapse.

Broker licenses require 60 hours of CE per three-year cycle, with at least 9 hours in real estate law and 9 in broker management. Brokers whose licenses lapse automatically suspend the licenses of every agent affiliated under them — a cascading failure that can halt dozens of transactions simultaneously.
