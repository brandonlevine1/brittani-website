## Why Georgia Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Wire fraud losses in real estate transactions exceeded $446 million nationally in 2024, according to FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center data. Georgia ranked among the top ten states for reported incidents, and the Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) has responded by tightening its expectations around agent and broker accountability. Understanding the Georgia wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 is no longer optional — it is a license-preservation issue.

The problem is straightforward: criminals intercept email communications between agents, closing attorneys, and buyers, then send fraudulent wire instructions that redirect earnest money or closing funds to overseas accounts. Once the money moves, recovery rates hover below 20 percent. Georgia's attorney-state closing structure adds a layer of protection, but it also creates confusion about who owes what duty of warning.

GREC has made clear through multiple disciplinary actions and its 2025 advisory bulletin that agents cannot delegate wire fraud prevention entirely to the closing attorney. The commission treats failure to warn clients as a potential violation of O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(a)(22), which covers conduct that demonstrates incompetence or untrustworthiness. That single statute has become the enforcement hook for wire fraud negligence cases.

## The Legal Framework: Georgia Statutes and Regulatory Authority

**Georgia does not have a standalone wire fraud disclosure statute like some states, but the regulatory framework is built from existing license law and GREC enforcement authority.** O.C.G.A. 43-40-25 grants GREC broad power to discipline licensees for acts that harm clients, including failure to disclose material information about transaction risks.

GREC's 2025 Advisory Bulletin on Wire Fraud (published March 2025) explicitly states that agents must "take reasonable steps to warn all parties about the risk of wire fraud and business email compromise." The bulletin references the Georgia Association of Realtors (GAR) [Wire Fraud Warning form](https://www.garealtor.com) as an acceptable method of compliance, though it does not mandate that specific form. GREC treats the advisory bulletin as interpretive guidance — meaning it informs how the commission will apply existing statutes in disciplinary hearings.

The closing attorney's obligations arise separately under the Georgia Rules of Professional Conduct and the State Bar's guidelines for real estate closings. Agents cannot rely on the attorney's duties to satisfy their own obligations. GREC has stated this principle repeatedly: the agent's duty to warn is independent and cannot be delegated.

## Required Forms and Documentation

The Georgia Association of Realtors publishes Form F803 (Wire Fraud and Electronic Communication Warning), which has become the de facto standard disclosure in the state. This form warns clients about the mechanics of business email compromise, instructs them never to rely on emailed wire instructions without independent verification, and provides a signature block confirming receipt of the warning.

Form F803 should be delivered at the earliest practical point in the transaction — ideally at the time of contract execution or during the initial buyer consultation. Waiting until the week before closing defeats the purpose, since fraud attempts often target earnest money deposits that occur early in the timeline.

| Document | When to Deliver | Who Signs | Purpose |
|----------|----------------|-----------|---------|
| GAR Form F803 | At contract execution | All parties | Warns about wire fraud and email compromise |
| Closing attorney wire instructions | 24-48 hours before closing | Buyer (acknowledgment) | Provides verified account details |
| Broker's internal wire fraud checklist | Throughout transaction | Agent (internal) | Documents compliance steps taken |
| Verbal verification log | Before any wire transfer | Agent notation | Records callback confirmation |

Beyond the GAR form, many brokerages require agents to maintain a verbal verification log documenting that they reminded clients to call the closing attorney's office using a known phone number before wiring any funds. This internal documentation becomes critical evidence if a client later claims they were never warned.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**The consequences of non-compliance range from GREC disciplinary sanctions to civil liability that can exceed the transaction value itself.** Understanding the full spectrum of risk should motivate every agent to take wire fraud prevention seriously.

GREC can impose sanctions including formal reprimand, probation, suspension, fine up to $5,000 per violation, or license revocation under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25. The commission has issued citations in cases where agents failed to provide any wire fraud warning and a client subsequently lost funds. Even when no actual fraud occurs, failure to document delivery of warnings can result in findings of negligence during a routine audit or complaint investigation.

Civil liability presents an even larger financial exposure. Georgia courts recognize that real estate agents owe fiduciary duties to their clients, and failure to warn about a known, foreseeable risk can support claims for negligence or breach of fiduciary duty. If a buyer loses $300,000 to wire fraud and the agent never mentioned the risk, plaintiff's counsel will argue the agent's silence was a proximate cause of the loss.

Errors-and-omissions insurance carriers have added wire fraud exclusions to many policies unless agents can demonstrate documented compliance with prevention protocols. If your E&O carrier denies coverage because you cannot produce a signed Form F803, you face personal exposure for the full amount of the client's loss. The deal itself may also unravel — [blown closings carry costs](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) that extend far beyond a single commission check.

## Five Common Mistakes Georgia Agents Make

The first mistake is timing. Agents who deliver the wire fraud warning at the closing table have already missed the window of maximum risk. Earnest money wires happen within days of contract execution, and that initial transfer is the most common fraud target. The form must be presented at or before the binding agreement date.

The second mistake is assuming the closing attorney handles everything. Georgia's attorney-close requirement gives some agents false confidence that wire fraud prevention is "the lawyer's job." GREC disagrees. The agent's obligation to warn is independent, and the attorney's verification of wire instructions does not absolve the agent of failing to educate their client about the threat.

The third mistake is delivering the form without explanation. Handing a client a stack of documents that includes Form F803 buried on page twelve accomplishes nothing. Agents who fail to verbally explain what wire fraud looks like and how callback verification works are providing a signature, not a warning. If a GREC investigator asks your client whether you discussed wire fraud risks, the client's answer matters more than the signed form.

**The fourth mistake is using personal email accounts without encryption or security protocols for transaction communications.** Agents who send closing details, attorney contact information, or financial figures through unencrypted Gmail or Yahoo accounts create the exact vulnerability that criminals exploit. Agents who struggle to track communications and deadlines across multiple deals should consider [systems designed to manage active transactions](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without creating security gaps.

The fifth mistake is failing to update procedures when switching brokerages. Each brokerage may have different internal compliance requirements beyond the GAR form, and agents who carry over old habits without reviewing their new broker's wire fraud protocols risk gaps in documentation.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(a)(6) for the acts of their affiliated licensees. A single agent's wire fraud compliance failure can expose the entire brokerage to GREC investigation. Broker-level oversight is not optional.

| Broker Responsibility | Frequency | Documentation Required |
|----------------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Verify Form F803 delivery in every file | Per transaction | Signed form in transaction file |
| Audit verbal verification logs | Monthly random sample | Agent notation with date and time |
| Update wire fraud policies annually | Annual (minimum) | Written policy with agent acknowledgment |
| Train agents on current fraud schemes | Quarterly | Training attendance records |
| Review email security practices | Semi-annual | Technology audit report |

Brokers should implement a file review checkpoint that flags any transaction where Form F803 is missing before the earnest money deadline. Automated transaction management systems can trigger this alert, but the broker remains responsible for enforcement. The [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) concept applies here — every file that moves between agents, TCs, and closing attorneys needs verified wire fraud documentation at each stage.

GREC audits of brokerage files have increasingly included wire fraud documentation as a standard review item. Brokers who cannot produce signed warnings in at least 90 percent of closed files face potential findings of inadequate supervision. The commission has indicated through public comments at its quarterly meetings that wire fraud compliance will remain an enforcement priority through 2026 and beyond.

## Protecting Yourself Beyond the Minimum Requirements

**Meeting the minimum disclosure requirement is table stakes — agents who genuinely protect their clients go further.** Consider implementing a "two-call protocol" where you personally call clients 24 hours before any scheduled wire transfer to remind them of the verification process. Document each call with a brief note including the date, time, and what you discussed.

Some brokerages now require agents to include wire fraud warning language in their email signatures on all transaction-related communications. While not a GREC requirement, this practice creates a documented pattern of consistent warnings that strengthens your defense if a fraud event occurs. The language should instruct recipients never to trust wire instructions received via email without verbal confirmation.

Agents should also coordinate with their closing attorneys to establish a clear communication protocol for how and when wire instructions will be delivered. Many Georgia closing attorneys now refuse to email wire instructions entirely, instead requiring buyers to retrieve them in person or through a secure portal. Understanding your closing attorney's specific process allows you to prepare clients accurately.
