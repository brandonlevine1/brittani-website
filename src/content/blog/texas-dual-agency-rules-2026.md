## Why Texas Calls It Intermediary — Not Dual Agency

Texas does not recognize dual agency the way most states define it. The state's framework under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.559 instead establishes an "intermediary" relationship, which carries its own distinct obligations and restrictions. Understanding the Texas dual agency & designated agency rules 2026 requirements means abandoning out-of-state terminology and learning the specific statutory scheme that the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) enforces.

The distinction matters beyond semantics. A dual agent in many states still owes fiduciary duties to both parties simultaneously, creating an inherent conflict. Texas solved that tension by stripping the intermediary broker of the duty to give opinions or advice — unless the broker appoints licensees to each party, restoring individual advocacy within the same brokerage.

Agents who moved to Texas from states with traditional dual agency laws often stumble here. The forms are different, the disclosure timing is different, and the penalties for noncompliance carry real teeth.

## The Statutory Framework: Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1101

**Texas Occupations Code Sections 1101.558 through 1101.561 govern the intermediary relationship and appointed licensee process.** Section 1101.558 defines when a broker may act as an intermediary. Section 1101.559 outlines what the intermediary broker must not do — specifically, disclosing confidential information of one party to the other without written authorization.

Section 1101.560 permits the broker to appoint licensees associated with the brokerage to communicate with and advise each party. These "appointed licensees" function similarly to what other states call designated agents. The appointment must be made in writing, and each appointed licensee then owes duties of loyalty and advice exclusively to their assigned client.

Section 1101.561 addresses the broker's duty to treat both parties honestly and fairly, to comply with the Texas Real Estate License Act, and to not disclose that the seller will accept less or the buyer will pay more than the listed or offered price without written instruction. This framework is distinct from generic dual agency disclosures used elsewhere in the country.

## Required Forms and Disclosure Timing

The [TREC Information About Brokerage Services (IABS)](https://www.trec.texas.gov/forms/information-about-brokerage-services) form is the cornerstone disclosure document. TREC Rule 531.20 requires agents to provide this form to a party at the first substantive dialogue — meaning the first time a prospective buyer or seller begins discussing specific property needs, financial qualifications, or motivation.

| Form / Document | When Required | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| IABS (TREC OP-K) | First substantive dialogue | Discloses possible intermediary relationship |
| Listing Agreement (TAR 1101) | Before listing services begin | Contains intermediary consent paragraph |
| Buyer/Tenant Rep Agreement (TAR 1501) | Before representation begins | Contains intermediary consent paragraph |
| Written Appointment of Licensees | When intermediary status activates | Names specific appointed licensees for each party |

The intermediary consent itself is not a standalone form in most Texas transactions. Instead, it is embedded within paragraph 10 of the TAR Residential Listing Agreement and paragraph 11 of the Buyer/Tenant Representation Agreement. Both parties must affirmatively consent in writing before the broker can act as intermediary. Without that pre-existing consent language signed by both sides, the intermediary status cannot lawfully begin.

## What Happens When You Skip a Step

**TREC disciplinary actions for intermediary violations are not theoretical — they appear regularly in commission meeting minutes.** Under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.653, TREC can impose sanctions including reprimand, mandatory education, fines up to $5,000 per violation, probation, license suspension, or license revocation.

Civil liability adds another layer. A party who was not properly informed of the intermediary arrangement can seek rescission of the contract, meaning the deal unwinds entirely. Courts have also awarded compensatory damages where an intermediary broker shared confidential pricing information, and in egregious cases, the harmed party may claim treble damages under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA) if the agent's conduct constitutes an unconscionable action.

Beyond regulatory and civil exposure, E&O insurance carriers scrutinize intermediary compliance during claims. A missing IABS form or unsigned consent paragraph can be grounds for the insurer to deny coverage, leaving the agent and broker personally exposed. Agents who struggle with [keeping track of multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) are particularly vulnerable to these documentation gaps.

## Common Mistakes Texas Agents Make With Intermediary Rules

**Mistake one: delivering the IABS at the wrong time.** Many agents hand the form to buyers at a showing or open house but fail to provide it at first substantive dialogue when it occurs earlier — such as during a phone call where the buyer reveals their budget, timeline, or property criteria. TREC auditors look at the timeline, not just the signature.

Mistake two: assuming intermediary status activates automatically. The consent language in the listing and buyer rep agreements is permissive — it says the broker "may" act as intermediary. But the broker must still make the affirmative decision and, if appointing licensees, must document that appointment in writing before negotiations begin. A verbal heads-up to agents in the hallway does not satisfy the statute.

Mistake three: an appointed licensee sharing information with the other appointed licensee or the broker in a way that compromises confidentiality. Section 1101.559 prohibits disclosure of confidential information. The appointed licensee for the buyer cannot tell the listing-side appointee that their client will go higher. Training lapses cause this violation frequently — especially in smaller brokerages where agents share office space. Having a solid [transaction handoff process](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) between parties helps prevent inadvertent information leaks.

Mistake four: sole agents attempting intermediary status. If a single agent within the brokerage has a relationship with both buyer and seller, the broker can still serve as intermediary — but cannot appoint that same agent to both parties. The broker must either appoint two different licensees or operate as an unadvised intermediary where neither party receives opinions or advice from the broker or any associated agent.

Mistake five: failing to document the intermediary relationship when it arises mid-transaction. A buyer walks into an open house hosted by the listing agent, falls in love with the property, and wants to write an offer immediately. If that buyer has no existing buyer rep agreement, the agent scrambles. The correct procedure requires the broker to secure a buyer rep agreement with intermediary consent, confirm the listing agreement also contains consent, then either appoint licensees or proceed as a pure intermediary — all before the offer is submitted.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry the statutory responsibility for intermediary compliance. TREC holds the broker accountable under Section 1101.803 for failing to adequately supervise licensees. An agent's mistake becomes the broker's liability if the broker lacked systems to prevent it.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| IABS delivery documentation | Signed and dated by recipient, timestamped | Every new client file |
| Intermediary consent in agreements | Paragraph present and initialed in listing and buyer rep | Before offer submission |
| Written appointment letters | Named licensees, date, signatures | Each intermediary transaction |
| Confidentiality protocols | Office policy distributed, agent acknowledgment on file | Annually and at hiring |
| Transaction file review | Complete sequence of disclosures in chronological order | Within 5 days of closing |

Brokers should implement a quarterly file audit pulling random samples from both closed and pending transactions. TREC's standards of practice require records to be maintained for four years after the closing or termination date. Gaps discovered during TREC audits — which can be triggered by a single consumer complaint — expose the entire brokerage to sanctions.

Policy manuals should include explicit written procedures for what happens when an intermediary situation arises unexpectedly. The broker needs to designate who has authority to make appointments, how the communication firewall between appointed licensees is maintained, and what documentation the file must contain before the transaction proceeds past the offer stage. ## Intermediary vs. Appointed Licensee: When to Use Each

The pure intermediary — where the broker does not appoint licensees — is appropriate when the brokerage has only one agent involved or when the broker determines neither party needs individualized advice. In practice, this is rare for residential transactions because buyers and sellers almost always benefit from having an advocate.

Appointed licensee status is the more common and recommended approach. The broker names one associate to advise the buyer and another to advise the seller. Each appointed licensee owes that client the full range of duties under Section 1101.557, including loyalty, obedience, disclosure, confidentiality, reasonable care, and accounting. The broker, meanwhile, remains neutral and cannot favor either side.

The appointment restores something close to the normal agency relationship for each client. That makes it the safer path for consumer protection and for risk management. TREC guidance materials emphasize that failure to appoint when two qualified licensees are available could expose the broker to claims of inadequate representation.
