## Georgia New Real Estate Laws 2026: The Statutory Changes Taking Effect This Summer

The Georgia Real Estate Commission and the state legislature have been busy. Multiple bills signed during the 2026 session directly alter how licensed agents handle disclosures, binding agreements, and brokerage supervision. If you hold a Georgia license and have not reviewed the **Georgia new real estate laws 2026** updates, you are already behind—and the August 1 effective date is less than four weeks away.

This is not a cosmetic form revision cycle. The changes affect liability exposure, rescission windows, and supervisory obligations that brokers must enforce firm-wide. Agents who rely on muscle memory from last year's transaction flow will find themselves exposed to disciplinary complaints filed with the [Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC)](https://grec.state.ga.us/).

## Revised Seller Disclosure Requirements Under O.C.G.A. 44-1-16

**Georgia's seller disclosure statute received its most significant amendment since 2000.** House Bill 487, signed by the governor in March 2026, expands the scope of what sellers must disclose and changes the form itself. The updated Seller's Property Disclosure Statement now includes mandatory fields for prior insurance claims, known flooding history regardless of FEMA zone designation, and the presence of any pending code violations.

Previously, sellers could answer "unknown" on flooding questions if the property sat outside a mapped floodplain. That loophole is closed. Sellers must now disclose any actual knowledge of water intrusion, standing water, or drainage issues regardless of federal mapping. Agents who pre-fill these forms or coach sellers to select "unknown" without genuine basis risk personal liability under the amended statute.

The GAR Form F301 (Seller's Property Disclosure Statement) has been updated to reflect these changes. Any binding agreement dated on or after August 1, 2026, must include the revised F301. Old versions will not satisfy the statutory requirement, and a buyer who receives an outdated form retains rescission rights as if no disclosure was provided at all.

## Community Association Disclosure: The New Seven-Day Window

O.C.G.A. 44-3-222 previously gave buyers a three-day rescission window after receiving community association disclosures for condominium and HOA-governed properties. The 2026 amendment extends that window to seven calendar days. This change alone will force agents to restructure their [transaction timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) for any property subject to a community association.

The practical impact is significant. In a standard 30-day close, losing four additional calendar days to the rescission period compresses inspection, appraisal, and financing contingency timelines. Listing agents must now ensure the association disclosure package is delivered as early as possible—ideally at or before the binding agreement—to avoid pushing closings past contractual deadlines.

**Failure to deliver the community association disclosure within the timeframe specified in the binding agreement does not merely delay closing.** Under the revised statute, the buyer's rescission right does not begin running until actual delivery is documented. An agent who forgets to order the disclosure package from the HOA management company could inadvertently give the buyer an open-ended exit from the contract.

| Disclosure Element | Previous Requirement | 2026 Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer rescission window | 3 calendar days | 7 calendar days |
| Flooding disclosure trigger | FEMA zone only | Any actual knowledge |
| Wire fraud advisory | Recommended | Mandatory (F801) |
| Seller insurance claims | Not required | Required on F301 |
| Code violation disclosure | Not required | Required on F301 |

## Wire Fraud Advisory Now Mandatory: GAR Form F801

Georgia agents have been encouraged to provide wire fraud advisories for years, but it was never a regulatory mandate. That changes on August 1. Under the new GREC Rule 520-1-.08(7), all licensees must deliver the Wire Fraud Advisory (GAR Form F801) to buyers and sellers at the time of the binding agreement.

Both the listing agent and the buyer's agent share responsibility for delivery. The form must be signed by all parties, and a copy must be retained in the transaction file. GREC auditors will treat a missing F801 the same as a missing agency disclosure—grounds for disciplinary action ranging from a citation and fine to license suspension for repeat violations.

This is not a suggestion from your brokerage's risk management committee. It is now a rule with teeth. If you have been relying on your closing attorney's office to mention wire fraud at the closing table, you are [missing a deadline](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) that now exists weeks earlier in the transaction.

## Georgia New Real Estate Laws 2026: Common Mistakes Agents Will Make

Based on past regulatory transitions in Georgia, certain errors will appear repeatedly in GREC complaint files over the next twelve months. Here are the specific mistakes agents are most likely to make.

**First, using outdated GAR forms after August 1.** Every transaction management platform and forms library will need to be updated. Agents who store blank PDFs on their desktops or use locally saved templates will inevitably pull the wrong version. The old F301, F201 (Purchase and Sale Agreement), and F501 (Buyer Brokerage Agreement) will still open, still print, and still look official—but they will not satisfy the 2026 statutory requirements.

Second, miscounting the seven-day rescission window. Calendar days include weekends and holidays, but the count does not begin until the buyer acknowledges receipt of the community association disclosure in writing. Verbal delivery does not start the clock. Agents who hand a buyer the disclosure packet at a showing without obtaining a signed acknowledgment will have no proof the window ever began running.

Third, treating the wire fraud advisory as a closing document rather than a binding-agreement-stage document. The F801 must be executed when the binding agreement is signed, not at the closing table. Agents who batch their compliance paperwork for closing day will be out of compliance from day one of the contract.

Fourth, failing to update the brokerage's trust account procedures. Senate Bill 214 amended O.C.G.A. 43-40-20 to require that earnest money deposits be delivered to the escrow holder within three business days of the binding agreement date—reduced from the previous five-business-day standard. Agents who wait until Friday to drop off a Monday deposit will violate the new trust account rule.

Fifth, omitting the new dual agency consent language. The revised GAR Form F201 includes updated dual agency consent provisions that must be initialed separately from the general brokerage relationship acknowledgment. A missing set of initials on this section creates a voidable contract if either party later objects.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce Starting August 1

Brokers carry supervisory liability under O.C.G.A. 43-40-18 for the acts of their affiliated licensees. The 2026 changes create several new audit points that qualifying brokers and branch managers must build into their compliance oversight.

| Audit Point | Form/Statute | Frequency | Consequence of Failure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Correct form version in use | F201, F301, F501, F801 | Every transaction | GREC citation, contract voidability |
| Wire fraud advisory delivery timing | F801, Rule 520-1-.08(7) | Every transaction | Fine up to $1,000 per occurrence |
| Earnest money deposit timeline | O.C.G.A. 43-40-20 | Every transaction | Trust account violation, suspension |
| Dual agency initials present | F201 Section 11(b) | Every dual agency deal | Voidable contract, civil liability |
| Community association disclosure receipt | O.C.G.A. 44-3-222 | HOA/condo transactions | Indefinite rescission window |

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-audit are accepting personal regulatory risk.** GREC's disciplinary process does not excuse a broker who claims ignorance of an affiliated agent's non-compliance. The Commission's enforcement division has increased its audit staff by four investigators in 2026, specifically to address the transition period following these legislative changes.

A practical approach for mid-size and large brokerages is to implement a transaction review checkpoint at the binding agreement stage—not just at closing. This means someone in the office confirms that the correct F201 version is used, the F801 is attached, earnest money instructions are timestamped, and the community association disclosure has been ordered if applicable. ## Liability Exposure: What Happens When Agents Miss These Requirements

The consequences of non-compliance fall into three categories: regulatory, contractual, and civil.

On the regulatory side, GREC operates under a tiered enforcement model. A first offense for a missing wire fraud advisory or outdated form will typically result in a citation and a fine between $500 and $1,000. Repeated violations within a 24-month period escalate to formal disciplinary hearings, which can result in license suspension or revocation. All disciplinary actions appear on the licensee's public record and are searchable through the GREC verification portal.

Contractually, the use of a non-compliant disclosure form or a missing mandatory advisory creates rescission rights for the aggrieved party. A buyer who discovers mid-transaction that the seller's disclosure was provided on the old F301 could argue that no valid disclosure was ever delivered. Under O.C.G.A. 44-1-16(d), the buyer may then rescind and recover earnest money regardless of other contingency expirations.

Civil exposure is the most expensive category. An agent who fails to deliver the updated flooding disclosure, and whose buyer later discovers undisclosed water intrusion, faces a negligence claim that the statutory amendment was specifically designed to prevent. Georgia courts have historically been willing to find per se negligence when an agent violates a specific statutory duty. The [true cost of these compliance failures](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond the immediate transaction.

Strip your forms library down to zero and rebuild it. Download every updated GAR form directly from the Georgia Association of REALTORS forms portal. Do not rely on your MLS auto-population tool until your MLS provider confirms they have loaded the 2026 versions. Delete old PDFs from your desktop, your Google Drive, and your phone.

Build in a step to confirm that community association disclosures are ordered within 24 hours of a binding agreement on any HOA or condo property.

**Talk to your closing attorneys now.** Georgia is an attorney-closing state, and many law firms maintain their own compliance checklists that interact with agent obligations. Confirm that your preferred attorneys are aware of the revised rescission timeline and the new mandatory disclosure fields so their paralegals do not reject files for missing information at the eleventh hour.
