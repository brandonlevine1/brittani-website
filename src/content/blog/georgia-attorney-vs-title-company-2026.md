## Georgia Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Rule Agents Cannot Afford to Misunderstand

Georgia remains one of a handful of states where the question of "Georgia attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026" has a definitive, non-negotiable answer. A licensed Georgia attorney must supervise every residential real estate closing. This is not a best practice or a suggestion from your broker — it is a legal requirement rooted in decades of State Bar opinions and statutory interpretation.

The confusion persists because agents who work across state lines, or who trained in title-company states, assume the same model applies in Georgia. It does not. Getting this wrong exposes you, your brokerage, and your clients to serious liability.

## Why Georgia Is an Attorney-Close State

The foundation of Georgia's closing requirement rests on the State Bar of Georgia's Formal Advisory Opinions, particularly **Formal Advisory Opinion 86-5** and its subsequent reaffirmations. These opinions hold that the preparation of closing documents, the examination of title, and the conduct of a real estate settlement constitute the practice of law under **O.C.G.A. Section 15-19-50** through **15-19-54** (the Georgia statutes defining unauthorized practice of law).

**A title company operating independently — without a supervising Georgia attorney — is engaging in the unauthorized practice of law.** The Georgia Supreme Court has reinforced this position multiple times, most notably in *Formal Advisory Opinion No. 2003-2*, which clarified that even title insurance companies cannot unilaterally conduct closings without attorney involvement.

This does not mean title companies are banned from Georgia transactions. It means they must work under the direction and supervision of a Georgia-licensed attorney who takes responsibility for the legal sufficiency of the closing.

## What "Attorney Supervision" Actually Means in Practice

Agents often ask whether the attorney must physically sit at the closing table for the entire appointment. The answer has nuance but leans heavily toward presence. The supervising attorney must review all closing documents, certify title, and be available to answer legal questions from the parties at closing.

| Requirement | Attorney Role | Title Company Role |
|---|---|---|
| Title examination | Must perform or directly supervise | May assist with search but cannot certify |
| Document preparation | Must prepare or approve all legal documents | May prepare preliminary figures under attorney direction |
| Closing conduct | Must supervise; physical or virtual presence expected | May coordinate logistics, collect documents |
| Disbursement of funds | Attorney's trust account or under attorney direction | Cannot independently disburse without attorney oversight |
| Title insurance issuance | Attorney acts as agent for title insurer | Underwrites the policy |

The Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) does not mandate agent behavior at closing in the same way, but GREC Rule 520-1-.08 requires agents to act within lawful boundaries. Facilitating or encouraging a closing without proper attorney involvement could trigger a GREC disciplinary complaint against your license.

## The GAR Forms That Reference Attorney Involvement

If you use Georgia Association of Realtors forms — and nearly every agent in the state does — you will notice that attorney involvement is woven into the transaction structure. **GAR Form F20 (Purchase and Sale Agreement)** includes provisions for the closing attorney's designation, typically in the "Closing" section of the contract.

GAR Form F57 (Amendment to Address Concerns with Property) and GAR Form F503 (Exhibit of Stipulations) also reference the closing attorney's role in resolving title defects or holding earnest money. When agents fill in these forms without understanding who the closing attorney is — or worse, leave the field blank assuming a title company will handle it — they create gaps that can [derail deals at the worst possible moment](/blog/where-deals-break-down).

The earnest money holder designation in the GAR contract is another critical touchpoint. In Georgia, the closing attorney's escrow account is the most common holder for earnest money, not a title company's general account. Mishandling this distinction can result in trust account violations under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-20.

## Common Mistakes Georgia Agents Make at Closing

**First, agents from other states assume a title company can run closing independently.** Relocating agents or teams that work multistate deals sometimes schedule closings with a title company coordinator and no attorney. When this happens, the closing must be rescheduled, and the agent's reputation with the client takes a hit.

Second, agents confuse the listing-side attorney with the closing attorney. In Georgia, the buyer typically selects and pays for the closing attorney. The seller may have separate legal counsel, but the closing attorney represents the transaction process, not a specific party. Agents who tell their seller clients that "our attorney will handle closing" create confusion about fiduciary roles that can lead to bar complaints.

Third, agents fail to confirm the closing attorney's availability before setting a closing date. Unlike title companies that maintain large staffs of closers, many Georgia attorneys handle closings personally. Setting a date without confirming attorney availability — then blaming the attorney for delays — is a pattern that [costs agents deals and commissions](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Fourth, some agents attempt to explain legal documents to buyers or sellers at the closing table. Under Georgia law, this constitutes the unauthorized practice of law by the agent. Only the supervising attorney may explain the legal effect of documents being signed.

Fifth, agents occasionally direct clients to skip the attorney review period or pressure them not to hire independent counsel. This creates liability exposure under GREC's Code of Ethics and can result in license discipline.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Georgia carry heightened responsibility here because O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-18 places supervisory duties squarely on the broker's shoulders. If an agent in your office facilitates a closing without proper attorney involvement, your brokerage faces disciplinary action from GREC — not just the individual agent.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Risk if Missed |
|---|---|---|
| Closing attorney identified in contract | GAR F20 closing section completed | Deal delay, UPL complaint |
| Earnest money deposited properly | Attorney trust account confirmation | Trust account violation |
| Agent not providing legal advice | Review of client communications | GREC discipline, civil liability |
| Closing date confirmed with attorney | Calendar verification before commitment | Blown closing, client complaint |
| Out-of-state referral compliance | Verify receiving agent understands GA rules | Vicarious liability for brokerage |

**Brokers should implement a pre-closing checklist that verifies attorney engagement no later than five business days before the scheduled closing date.** This is not optional paranoia — it is risk management that protects your entire office. A systematized approach to [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) prevents these items from slipping through the cracks.

GREC conducts audits and investigates complaints. A pattern of closings in your brokerage without documented attorney supervision could trigger a formal investigation under GREC Rule 520-1-.09, which governs broker supervisory responsibilities.

## Liability When Things Go Wrong

If a closing proceeds without proper attorney supervision and a title defect surfaces later, the liability chain becomes extremely unfavorable for agents. The title insurer may deny the claim because the closing was not conducted in accordance with Georgia law. The buyer sues everyone — the seller, the agents, and the brokerage.

> "The practice of law in Georgia includes the preparation of legal instruments of all kinds whereby legal rights are secured." — Georgia Supreme Court, interpreting O.C.G.A. Section 15-19-50

Without a supervising attorney who examined title and certified the closing, the agent's errors and omissions insurance may also deny coverage, arguing the agent operated outside the scope of licensed activity. The financial exposure for a single improperly closed transaction can exceed the commission earned on dozens of clean deals.

GREC can impose fines, suspend licenses, or revoke them entirely under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-25. The State Bar can pursue unauthorized practice of law charges against non-attorneys who conducted closing functions. These are not theoretical risks — they are documented enforcement actions.

## How Agents Should Structure the Closing Process in 2026

The practical workflow for Georgia agents should follow a clear sequence. First, at contract execution, confirm the closing attorney is identified in the GAR F20. Second, within three business days of binding agreement, verify that the attorney has received the contract and initiated title examination. Third, coordinate with the attorney's office — not a standalone title company — on closing logistics.

Agents who build this process into their standard [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) eliminate the most common compliance gaps. The key is treating attorney coordination as a non-delegable step, not something that "someone else" handles.
