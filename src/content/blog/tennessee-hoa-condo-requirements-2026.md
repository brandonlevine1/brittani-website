## Why Tennessee HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026 Demand Agent Attention Now

Getting HOA and condo disclosures wrong in Tennessee does not just stall a closing—it can unwind a fully executed contract and expose your brokerage to disciplinary proceedings. The Tennessee hoa & condo disclosure rules 2026 framework rests on two major statutes, a handful of association-specific resale forms, and deadlines that leave no room for guessing. Agents who treat these disclosures as clerical afterthoughts are the same agents who show up in TREC complaint files.

Tennessee's residential condominium market has grown significantly across Nashville, Chattanooga, and Knoxville. With that growth comes a rising number of HOA-governed communities with varying reserve health, special assessment histories, and governing-document quirks. Buyers rely on agents to surface that information—and the state expects you to deliver it on time, every time.

## The Statutory Framework: Two Acts You Must Know

**Tennessee's HOA and condo disclosure obligations flow from two primary statutes, not one.** The Tennessee Condominium Act of 1982 (T.C.A. Title 66, Chapter 27) governs condominium properties created after July 1, 1982. The Tennessee Horizontal Property Act (T.C.A. Title 66, Chapter 27, Part 1) covers older condominium regimes formed before that date.

For planned unit developments and non-condo HOA communities, the Tennessee Homeowners Association Act (T.C.A. Section 66-27-401 et seq.) establishes disclosure expectations around governing documents, fees, and financial statements. The Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) enforces agent conduct standards through Rule 1260-02-.12, which mandates that licensees disclose material facts—including HOA obligations—that could affect a buyer's decision.

Each statute imposes slightly different content requirements on the resale disclosure package, but all share a common thread: the buyer must receive specified information before—or within a defined window after—contract execution, and failure triggers a statutory cancellation right.

## What the Resale Certificate Must Contain

The resale certificate (sometimes called a "disclosure certificate" or "Section 503 statement" in condo transactions) is the centerpiece document. Under T.C.A. Section 66-27-503, a condominium resale certificate must include the following items at minimum: the monthly common expense assessment for the unit, any pending special assessments, the association's current operating budget, a statement of reserves, any capital expenditure plans for the next fiscal year, and a disclosure of any pending litigation involving the association.

For HOA communities governed by the Homeowners Association Act, the disclosure package must similarly include assessment amounts, the most recent financial statement, and a copy of the governing documents (CC&Rs, bylaws, rules and regulations). The association typically prepares this package at the seller's request, but the listing agent bears the responsibility of ensuring the buyer actually receives it.

**Agents cannot rely on buyers "finding it online" or assume the management company will deliver directly.** The obligation runs to the party—and by extension, the party's agent—who must prove delivery occurred.

## Tennessee REALTORS Forms and Their Role

Most Tennessee agents work with standard forms published by Tennessee REALTORS (formerly Tennessee Association of REALTORS). The relevant forms for HOA and condo transactions include:

| Form Number | Form Name | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| RF401 | Purchase and Sale Agreement | Contains HOA/condo disclosure contingency language and allocation of certificate costs |
| RF411 | Condominium Disclosure Addendum | Addresses condo-specific disclosures, five-day review period, and buyer cancellation rights |
| RF413 | HOA Disclosure Addendum | Covers non-condo HOA communities, assessment disclosure, and governing document delivery |
| RF421 | Property Condition Disclosure | General seller disclosure—does not replace HOA/condo-specific requirements |

Form RF411 specifically incorporates the T.C.A. Section 66-27-503 five-day cancellation right. When a buyer signs this addendum and then receives the resale certificate, the clock begins. If the buyer does not cancel within five calendar days of receipt, the contingency is deemed satisfied. Agents who forget to attach RF411 to a condominium transaction create an ambiguous cancellation timeline that benefits no one and invites post-closing litigation.

Form RF413 serves an analogous function for HOA properties outside the Condominium Act. It requires sellers to disclose whether the community is governed by a mandatory association, the amount of current assessments, any known pending special assessments, and transfer fees. Failing to include this addendum when listing a property within a mandatory HOA is one of the most common compliance failures TREC sees in file audits.

## The Five-Day Cancellation Right and How Agents Mishandle It

Under T.C.A. Section 66-27-503, a condominium buyer has five calendar days after receiving the resale certificate to rescind the purchase contract without penalty. This is not a "review period" that agents can shorten by contract or waive verbally. It is a statutory right that survives any conflicting contract language.

**The five-day window begins on the date of actual delivery, not the date the certificate was ordered or the date it was mailed.** Agents must document receipt with a signed acknowledgment or verifiable electronic delivery confirmation. A management company's assertion that "we sent it" does not satisfy the standard if the buyer disputes receipt.

If a closing is scheduled within the five-day window—common in fast-moving Nashville markets—and the buyer has not yet received the certificate, the agent faces a binary choice: delay closing or risk a post-closing rescission claim. Neither outcome is acceptable. The correct practice is ordering the resale certificate the moment the property goes under contract, not during the final week before closing.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Tennessee HOA Transactions

Three to five errors account for the majority of TREC complaints and post-closing disputes in HOA and condo deals. Understanding these patterns can prevent your next transaction from becoming a cautionary tale.

**First, agents fail to order the resale certificate early enough.** Management companies in the Nashville metro often require seven to fourteen business days to compile a certificate. Agents who wait until week three of a thirty-day contract leave themselves no buffer, and the buyer's five-day review period compresses into days that barely exist.

Second, listing agents assume the seller's property condition disclosure (Form RF421) covers HOA obligations. It does not. The RF421 asks whether the property is subject to an HOA and whether there are known assessments, but it does not satisfy the statutory resale-certificate requirement under T.C.A. Section 66-27-503 or the HOA Disclosure Addendum requirement under RF413. These are separate obligations with separate consequences for omission.

Third, agents neglect to attach the correct addendum to the purchase agreement. A condo transaction without RF411 or an HOA transaction without RF413 creates a gap in the contractual framework that can be exploited by either party later. Some agents claim the RF401 purchase agreement's general HOA language is "sufficient." TREC's position, expressed in multiple disciplinary opinions, disagrees.

Fourth, buyer's agents fail to independently verify the resale certificate contents against the governing documents. If the certificate states monthly dues of $350 but the association approved an increase to $425 effective next quarter, and the buyer's agent never flagged the discrepancy, that agent's fiduciary duty has arguably been breached. Cross-referencing the certificate against the most recent board meeting minutes is a ten-minute step that prevents five-figure disputes.

Fifth, agents treat transfer fees as immaterial. Many Tennessee HOA management companies charge transfer fees ranging from $150 to $500, and some also charge "capital contribution" fees at resale. These must be disclosed and allocated in the contract. Agents who discover these fees at the closing table create last-minute friction that either party can use as grounds to walk.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Broker-level oversight of HOA and condo compliance is not optional—TREC Rule 1260-02-.01 places supervisory responsibility squarely on the principal broker. A quarterly file audit should verify at minimum the following items in every HOA or condo transaction file.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Addendum attached | RF411 (condo) or RF413 (HOA) present in executed contract | Missing addendum or unsigned copy |
| Resale certificate in file | Full certificate from management company or association board | Certificate absent or incomplete |
| Delivery confirmation | Signed receipt or electronic delivery log with timestamp | No proof of buyer receipt date |
| Five-day window respected | Closing date falls after the cancellation period expired | Closing scheduled within five days of certificate delivery |
| Transfer fee disclosure | Fee amount noted in contract or closing statement | Fee appears at closing with no prior written disclosure |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance are the same brokers who face office-wide audits after a single complaint. Building a checklist into your transaction management workflow—whether manual or automated—is the difference between catching errors at week one and discovering them in a TREC hearing room. ## Liability and Consequences for Non-Compliance

The consequences of violating Tennessee's HOA and condo disclosure requirements fall into three categories: contractual, regulatory, and civil.

Contractually, the buyer may cancel the purchase agreement and recover earnest money if the resale certificate is not delivered or is materially incomplete. Under T.C.A. Section 66-27-503, this cancellation right is self-executing—the buyer does not need court approval. The deal simply dies, and the agent's commission disappears with it.

Regulatorily, TREC can impose disciplinary action under T.C.A. Section 62-13-312, which includes civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation, license suspension, or revocation. The Commission has publicly issued consent orders against agents who failed to ensure proper HOA disclosure. These orders are searchable on the [Tennessee Real Estate Commission's website](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regboards/trec.html) and remain on the agent's permanent record.

Civilly, a buyer who closes without receiving proper disclosures and later discovers material defects in the association's finances—underfunded reserves, pending litigation, or undisclosed special assessments—can pursue damages against the seller, the listing agent, and potentially the buyer's agent for breach of fiduciary duty. These claims survive closing and are not barred by merger clauses in the deed. The cost of defending such a claim, even without a judgment, routinely exceeds $15,000 in attorney fees alone.
