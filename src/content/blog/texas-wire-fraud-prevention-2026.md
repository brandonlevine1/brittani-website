## Why Texas Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Wire fraud losses in Texas real estate transactions exceeded $150 million in 2024 according to FBI IC3 data, and the Texas Real Estate Commission has responded with increasingly aggressive enforcement expectations. Understanding Texas wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 is no longer optional knowledge—it is a baseline compliance obligation that directly affects your license. The consequences for ignoring these requirements range from deal collapse to personal civil liability.

**Texas House Bill 3436, which took effect September 1, 2024, codified specific obligations for title companies and created an enforcement framework that directly implicates real estate agents who fail to warn their clients.** The bill amended Chapter 2501 of the Texas Insurance Code, placing wire verification duties on title agents while simultaneously raising the standard of care expected from licensed brokers and salespersons who introduce clients to the closing process. TREC has since issued guidance reminding licensees that competency under Section 1101.652(b)(1) of the Texas Occupations Code includes adequate client warnings about wire fraud risk.

This is not an abstract regulatory concern. A single intercepted wire can kill a deal, trigger an insurance claim, and expose you to a TREC complaint—all in the same week.

## The Legal Framework: Statutes and Rules That Apply to Agents

The primary statutory authority comes from three sources that Texas agents must understand in combination. Chapter 2501 of the Texas Insurance Code (as amended by HB 3436) governs title companies directly, but its requirements create downstream obligations for agents who refer clients to those title companies. Section 1101.652 of the Texas Occupations Code gives TREC authority to discipline agents for incompetence, which TREC interprets to include failure to educate clients about known transaction risks. The Texas Administrative Code, Title 22, Part 23, Chapter 535, sets the rules for advertising, disclosure, and professional conduct that apply to every active licensee.

The Texas Association of Realtors has responded by creating TAR Form 1939, the Wire Fraud Warning, which became a standard inclusion in most MLS transaction packages starting in late 2024. This form is not promulgated by TREC, meaning it is not legally mandatory in the same way that TREC Form 20-16 (Information About Brokerage Services) is. However, failing to use it—or something substantially similar—leaves you exposed when a buyer claims they were never warned.

> "A licensee acts incompetently when the licensee fails to adequately inform a party to a transaction of reasonably foreseeable risks that fall within the licensee's duty of care." — TREC Staff Interpretation Memo, March 2025

Agents who rely on the title company alone to deliver wire fraud warnings are making a bet that TREC will never investigate their side of the transaction. That bet has become significantly worse since 2025 enforcement data showed a 40 percent increase in wire-fraud-related complaints filed against agents.

## Forms and Documentation: What You Actually Need to Use

There is a persistent misconception that a single verbal warning covers your obligation. It does not. Texas agents should be using a combination of written disclosures and documented communication practices to create an auditable compliance trail.

| Form/Document | Source | When to Deliver | Required or Recommended |
|---|---|---|---|
| TAR Form 1939 (Wire Fraud Warning) | Texas Association of Realtors | At contract execution | Strongly recommended |
| TREC Form 20-16 (IABS) | Texas Real Estate Commission | First substantive communication | Required by law |
| Brokerage-specific wire fraud addendum | Individual brokerage | At contract execution | Varies by brokerage policy |
| Title company wire verification letter | Title company under HB 3436 | Before wire transfer | Required of title company |
| Written email warning with verified phone number | Agent best practice | Before earnest money and before closing funds | Recommended |

**The IABS form (TREC 20-16) does not contain wire fraud language itself, but delivering it establishes the brokerage relationship context under which your duty of care is measured.** Your brokerage may have its own addendum—ask your compliance officer for the current version. If your brokerage does not have one, TAR Form 1939 is the industry default, and using it creates a documented paper trail that protects you in a complaint proceeding.

Most [wire instruction verification processes](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) involve a callback protocol where the buyer calls the title company at a number obtained independently from any email chain. Your job is to make sure your client knows this protocol exists before they receive wire instructions from anyone.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Wire Fraud Compliance

Agents lose on wire fraud compliance not because they are careless people, but because they treat it as a checkbox rather than a process. Here are the five most frequent errors that lead to TREC complaints and civil exposure.

**First, agents deliver the wire fraud warning too late.** Handing a buyer the TAR 1939 at the closing table does nothing to prevent a wire interception that happened three days earlier when earnest money was sent. The warning must go out at or before contract execution—ideally alongside the IABS and the executed contract itself.

Second, agents forward wire instructions from the title company via email without any disclaimer or verification language. The moment you become the conduit for wire instructions, you have inserted yourself into the chain of liability. If those instructions are intercepted and altered after leaving your outbox, a plaintiff's attorney will name you in the lawsuit.

Third, agents assume the title company's HB 3436 obligations relieve them of any duty. They do not. The statute places a floor on title company conduct—it does not create a ceiling for agent responsibility under the Occupations Code. TREC evaluates agent competency independently of what the title company did or failed to do.

Fourth, agents fail to document that the warning was delivered. An unsigned form sitting in your transaction management system is weaker evidence than a countersigned acknowledgment with a timestamp. If your system tracks [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously, ensure that each file has its own wire fraud documentation rather than relying on a blanket disclosure.

Fifth, agents use outdated template language that references procedures their title company no longer follows. If your form says "call us to verify" but your title company switched to a secure portal system, the inconsistency creates confusion that a fraudster can exploit.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability under Section 1101.803 of the Texas Occupations Code for the acts of their sponsored agents. This means that a single agent's failure to deliver wire fraud warnings can create brokerage-level exposure. Brokers who treat wire fraud compliance as optional are accepting unquantified risk across every transaction in their office.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Documentation Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Verify TAR 1939 or equivalent in every transaction file | Per transaction | Signed and dated by client |
| Confirm agents are not forwarding raw wire instructions | Quarterly spot-check | Email audit or TMS log review |
| Update brokerage wire fraud addendum language | Annually or when title company processes change | Version-controlled document |
| Train agents on callback verification protocol | At onboarding and annual CE | Signed training acknowledgment |
| Review E&O policy for wire fraud exclusions | At renewal | Written confirmation from carrier |

**Brokers should require a signed wire fraud acknowledgment in every transaction file before the file can be marked as compliant for closing.** This is not about paranoia—it is about creating the documentation that your E&O carrier will demand if a claim is filed. Carriers increasingly include wire fraud questionnaires in their applications, and a pattern of missing disclosures can result in higher premiums or outright coverage denial.

The brokerage's policy manual should state unequivocally that agents are prohibited from transmitting wire instructions via email without the client first confirming through an independent callback. Many brokerages now require that agents include a footer disclaimer on every email that reads: "Never trust wiring instructions sent via email. Always verify by calling your title company at a number you obtained independently."

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When You Fail to Comply

The consequences stack in ways that agents rarely appreciate until they are facing a complaint. TREC can impose administrative penalties under Section 1101.702 of the Texas Occupations Code, including fines up to $5,000 per violation, suspension, or revocation. Civil liability for negligence can exceed the amount of the lost wire transfer if a court finds that the agent's failure to warn was a proximate cause of the loss.

E&O insurance is not a guaranteed backstop. Most policies include exclusions for fraud, and while an agent's failure to warn is not itself fraud, the carrier may argue that the loss arose from a fraudulent act (the wire interception) and therefore falls outside coverage. You cannot afford to assume your policy covers this scenario without reading the exclusions yourself.

Deal cancellation is the immediate practical consequence. When a buyer's funds are stolen mid-transaction, the deal rarely survives. The seller moves on to a backup offer. The buyer is left fighting with their bank and the FBI while you—the agent—are left explaining to your broker why the file fell apart. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) should motivate every agent to treat wire fraud prevention as deal preservation, not just regulatory compliance.

## Building a Repeatable Prevention Protocol

The agents who never face wire fraud complaints are not lucky—they have a system. That system starts at contract execution and runs through the final closing confirmation. Here is what it looks like in practice.

At contract execution, deliver TAR Form 1939 (or your brokerage equivalent) alongside the executed contract. Have the buyer sign and date the acknowledgment. Store it in your transaction management system immediately. In the same conversation, verbally explain that wire instructions should never be trusted from email alone and that the buyer must call the title company at a number from the title company's website—not from any email.

Before earnest money is due, send a written reminder (email and text) restating that the buyer should verify all wire instructions by independent callback. Do not include the title company's phone number in that message—instruct the buyer to look it up themselves. This eliminates you as a potential point of interception.

Before closing funds are due, repeat the process. Confirm that the buyer has received wire instructions directly from the title company through a secure channel and that they have verified those instructions by phone. Document that you sent this reminder.
