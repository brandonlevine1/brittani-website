## Understanding Tennessee Title & Closing Procedures 2026

Tennessee stands apart from most states in one fundamental way: it is an attorney-close state. If you are a licensed agent operating under the Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC), your working knowledge of Tennessee title & closing procedures 2026 directly affects whether your deals close on time, whether your clients are protected, and whether your license stays intact. The rules governing who does what at the closing table are not suggestions—they are codified in Tennessee statute and enforced by both TREC and the Board of Professional Responsibility.

This post breaks down the specific statutes, forms, and procedural requirements you need to follow. It also identifies where agents most commonly fail and what brokers should be auditing right now.

## The Attorney-Close Requirement and Why It Matters

**Tennessee Code Annotated Section 23-3-101 defines the practice of law to include the preparation of deeds, mortgages, deeds of trust, and other instruments that transfer or encumber real property.** Only a licensed Tennessee attorney can prepare these documents, examine title, issue title opinions, and conduct the closing itself. Agents who overstep this boundary—even inadvertently—risk a TREC complaint and potential prosecution for unauthorized practice of law (UPL).

The practical effect is that you, as the agent, are not running the closing. You are facilitating the transaction up to the point where the attorney and title company take the procedural lead. Your role shifts to coordination, communication, and ensuring your clients understand what is happening.

TREC Rule 1260-02-.12 reinforces this by prohibiting licensees from performing any act that constitutes the practice of law. The penalty structure includes license suspension, revocation, and civil fines up to $1,000 per violation under T.C.A. Section 62-13-312.

## Key Forms and Documents Agents Handle

Tennessee agents work primarily with forms published by the Tennessee Association of Realtors (TAR) and approved by legal counsel. The most relevant forms for the title and closing phase include:

| Form Number | Name | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| RF 401 | Purchase and Sale Agreement | Establishes closing date, title requirements, and allocation of costs |
| RF 403 | Amendment to Purchase and Sale Agreement | Modifies closing timeline or title cure provisions |
| RF 201 | Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement | Defines commission split instructions sent to closing attorney |
| RF 141 | Disclosure and Consent for Dual Agency | Required if same firm represents both sides at closing |
| RF 404 | Notice to Terminate | Used if title defects cannot be cured within the agreed period |

The RF 401 specifically addresses title in Paragraph 9, which gives sellers a defined period to cure title defects after the buyer's title examination reveals issues. Agents must understand that this cure period is a hard contractual deadline—not an informal suggestion. Missing it triggers the buyer's right to terminate under RF 404.

## The Title Examination and Commitment Process

Once a purchase agreement is executed, the buyer's closing attorney or a title company orders a title search. In Tennessee, this search typically covers a minimum of 30 years of recorded history in the county Register of Deeds office, though many attorneys search back further when the chain is unclear.

The title commitment is issued by a title insurance underwriter and contains three critical schedules. Schedule A identifies the property and proposed insured parties. Schedule B-I lists requirements that must be satisfied before the policy will be issued—think outstanding mortgages, judgments, or probate matters. Schedule B-II lists permanent exceptions that the policy will not cover, such as easements, restrictive covenants, or mineral rights reservations.

**Agents must review Schedule B-II with their clients because these exceptions survive closing and permanently affect the property.** A buyer who does not understand that a utility easement runs through their backyard—because their agent never discussed the commitment—will become a complaint waiting to happen.

Tennessee title insurance rates are filed with the Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance. Unlike states with regulated rate schedules, Tennessee allows some variation between underwriters, so costs can differ depending on which title company the closing attorney uses.

## Who Pays What: Closing Cost Allocation

The default allocation of closing costs in Tennessee follows market custom but is always subject to negotiation in the RF 401. Here is how costs are typically assigned in most Tennessee markets:

| Cost Item | Typically Paid By | Authority/Source |
|---|---|---|
| Owner's title insurance policy | Seller | Market custom, RF 401 Paragraph 10 |
| Lender's title insurance policy | Buyer | Lender requirement |
| Closing attorney fee | Split or Buyer | Negotiated, varies by county |
| Recording fees (deed) | Buyer | T.C.A. Section 8-21-1001 |
| Transfer tax (state) | Seller | T.C.A. Section 67-4-409 |
| Transfer tax (local/county) | Seller | County resolution, varies |
| Title search fee | Buyer | Market custom |

Tennessee's state transfer tax (sometimes called the "recording tax") is $0.37 per $100 of consideration under T.C.A. Section 67-4-409. Most counties impose an additional local transfer tax. In Davidson County (Nashville), for example, the combined rate creates a meaningful line item on the seller's side of the settlement statement. Agents who fail to estimate this correctly during listing presentations erode their credibility fast.

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Agents Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in TREC disciplinary actions and title company complaints. Each one is preventable.

**First, agents give legal advice about title defects.** When a title commitment reveals a lien or encumbrance, some agents attempt to advise their clients on whether the defect is serious or curable. This crosses into UPL territory under T.C.A. Section 23-3-101. Your job is to refer the client to the closing attorney for interpretation—period.

Second, agents miss the title cure deadline in the RF 401. Paragraph 9 sets a specific number of days for the seller to cure objections raised by the buyer's title examination. If you are not calendaring this date and proactively checking with the closing attorney on cure status, you risk losing the deal or exposing your client to waiver arguments.

Third, agents fail to verify that the closing attorney has received the commission disbursement instructions from the listing brokerage. TREC Rule 1260-02-.09 requires that all commissions flow through the broker. If the closing attorney does not have proper written authorization, your check does not get cut at the table.

Fourth, agents send or relay wiring instructions via unencrypted email. Wire fraud losses in Tennessee have increased year over year, and TREC has indicated that failure to follow reasonable cybersecurity practices could constitute negligence under the licensee's duty of care.

Fifth, agents forget to confirm that the property survey matches the legal description on the commitment. Survey discrepancies—especially in rural Tennessee counties—cause last-minute title exceptions that delay closings by weeks.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under T.C.A. Section 62-13-312(b)(1) for the acts of their affiliated licensees. This is not a theoretical risk. TREC regularly assesses fines against brokers who fail to maintain oversight of closing procedures.

**Every Tennessee broker should be auditing three areas quarterly.** First, confirm that agents are not providing title opinions or legal interpretations in writing—review email correspondence samples. Second, verify that all commission disbursement forms (TAR RF 201 instructions and any brokerage-specific forms) are on file and transmitted to closing attorneys before the closing date. Third, audit your agents' compliance with wire fraud protocols.

TREC Rule 1260-02-.01(3) holds principal brokers responsible for maintaining policies and procedures that ensure compliance. If an agent's UPL violation or missed deadline results in a consumer complaint, the broker's response of "I didn't know" does not insulate them from discipline.

Brokers should also confirm that agents are using the current TAR form editions. Tennessee Association of Realtors updates its forms periodically, and using an outdated RF 401 can create enforceability issues—especially around title and closing provisions that have been revised to reflect new statutory requirements.
