## The New Reality of Michigan Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026

The Michigan commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 have redefined how agents earn, disclose, and negotiate compensation on every residential deal. If you are still operating under pre-settlement assumptions — blanket cooperative compensation offers, vague MLS fields, or handshake understandings about who pays what — you are exposed. The Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA), the state body overseeing real estate licensees under the Occupational Code (MCL 339.2501 et seq.), expects full compliance with both the national settlement terms and Michigan-specific regulatory guidance.

This is not merely a paperwork update. The settlement fundamentally restructured the economics of buyer representation, and Michigan's regulatory framework has layered additional expectations on top of the national requirements. Agents who treat this as a minor form change risk license complaints, deal collapses, and personal liability.

## What the NAR Settlement Actually Changed for Michigan Agents

The 2024 NAR settlement eliminated the practice of sellers making blanket offers of buyer-agent compensation through MLS systems. For Michigan agents operating on MLS platforms administered by Realcomp II Ltd., MiRealSource, or the various regional boards, the cooperative compensation field no longer exists as it once did. Compensation must now be negotiated directly between the buyer and their agent, memorialized in a written agreement before any property tours begin.

**Michigan's implementation differs from some states because LARA already required agency disclosure at first substantive contact under MCL 339.2512d.** The settlement layered a compensation disclosure requirement on top of this existing obligation. Agents must now execute both an agency disclosure (using the standard Michigan Realtors Disclosure Regarding Real Estate Agency Relationships form) and a written buyer representation agreement specifying exact compensation terms before touring property.

The written buyer representation agreement must state compensation as either a flat fee or a percentage — not an open-ended amount, not a range, and not "whatever the seller offers." Michigan Realtors Form 1050 (Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement) has been revised to include specific compensation fields that satisfy the settlement requirements.

## Forms and Documentation Michigan Agents Must Use

Getting the paperwork right is no longer optional polish — it is a compliance gate. The primary forms involved in a compliant Michigan buyer-side transaction now include several documents that must be executed in a specific sequence.

| Form / Document | When It Must Be Signed | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Agency Disclosure (MI Realtors standard form) | First substantive contact | Discloses agency relationship type |
| Form 1050 — Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement | Before touring any property | Specifies exact compensation terms |
| Form 1060 — Non-Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement | Before touring (if non-exclusive) | Alternative compensation agreement |
| Purchase Agreement (Form 2000 series) | At offer | Must reflect how buyer-agent compensation is funded |
| Seller Contribution Addendum (if applicable) | At offer | Documents seller's agreement to contribute toward buyer-agent fees |

The sequence matters. If a buyer agent shows property before Form 1050 or 1060 is signed, they have violated the settlement terms. If a complaint reaches LARA, the agent faces potential discipline under MCL 339.2512(1), which governs prohibited conduct including "failing to account for or remit any moneys coming into his or her possession that belong to others" and misrepresentation.

On the listing side, the seller's broker must document in the listing agreement (Michigan Realtors Form 1010) whether the seller is willing to contribute to buyer-agent compensation. This cannot be broadcast on the MLS but can be communicated through off-MLS channels, direct negotiation at offer, or concession fields within the purchase agreement itself. ## Penalties and Liability for Non-Compliance

LARA enforces Michigan's real estate licensing laws through its Bureau of Professional Licensing. Violations of commission disclosure and buyer representation requirements can result in formal complaints, investigations, and disciplinary action ranging from a letter of reprimand to license revocation.

Under MCL 339.2512, prohibited conduct includes misrepresentation, failure to disclose material facts, and acting without proper authorization. An agent who shows homes without a signed buyer agreement and later collects compensation could face allegations under multiple subsections of this statute. Civil exposure compounds the regulatory risk: buyers who later discover they could have negotiated lower compensation, or who feel misled about cost, have grounds for breach of fiduciary duty claims.

**Deal cancellation is the most immediate practical risk.** If a seller's attorney or the title company flags an improperly documented compensation arrangement during closing review, the deal can stall or collapse entirely. Title companies in Michigan have increasingly adopted pre-closing compliance checklists that flag missing buyer representation agreements, and some refuse to process commission disbursements without proper documentation on file. The financial impact of a blown closing extends far beyond the single transaction — our breakdown of the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) quantifies just how damaging one failed deal can be.

Fines from LARA itself can reach $10,000 per violation under MCL 339.604, and repeated offenses trigger automatic hearings before an administrative law judge. Brokers are jointly liable for their agents' failures under MCL 339.2512e, which makes this a brokerage-wide compliance issue rather than an individual agent's problem.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Are Making Right Now

After more than a year of implementation, patterns of non-compliance have emerged across Michigan brokerages. These are not edge cases — they are recurring errors that LARA investigators and transaction coordinators encounter weekly.

**First, agents are using verbal compensation agreements instead of written ones.** A phone conversation or text message confirming "I charge 2.5%" does not satisfy the written agreement requirement. Form 1050 or 1060 must be fully executed with wet or compliant electronic signatures before any property tour occurs. No exceptions exist for "quick showings" or open houses where the agent is not the listing agent.

Second, many buyer agents are still inserting compensation ranges rather than specific amounts. A term like "2-3% depending on the property" fails the specificity requirement. The settlement demands a definitive number — either a fixed dollar amount or a single percentage — that the buyer can evaluate before committing.

Third, listing agents are still advertising cooperative compensation through social media posts, agent-to-agent emails, and brokerage websites. The prohibition on MLS-based blanket offers extends to any broad, public communication that functions as the same kind of offer. Private negotiation at the offer stage remains permissible, but broadcasting "seller will pay buyer's agent 2.5%" to a wide audience invites scrutiny.

Fourth, agents are failing to update compensation terms when switching from one property to another within the same buyer relationship. If Form 1050 specifies that the buyer will pay 2.5% and the seller of a specific property offers to contribute 2% toward that amount, the net obligation changes. The buyer must acknowledge this in writing before proceeding. Many agents skip this step, assuming the original agreement covers all scenarios.

Fifth, dual agency situations are creating undocumented compensation splits. Michigan permits disclosed dual agency under MCL 339.2517, but the compensation terms for both sides must still be individually disclosed and agreed to in writing. Agents acting as dual agents cannot simply collect "both sides" without separate written authorization from each party specifying the exact amount.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry statutory responsibility for their agents' compliance under Michigan law. MCL 339.2512e makes clear that a broker who "fails to exercise reasonable supervision" over licensees operating under their license is subject to the same disciplinary actions as the offending agent.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Signed Form 1050/1060 in file before first showing | Every buyer transaction | Missing form or post-dated signature |
| Compensation specificity (no ranges) | Every buyer transaction | Vague or conditional language |
| MLS listing compliance (no coop comp fields populated) | Weekly listing audit | Legacy language in remarks or compensation fields |
| Dual agency disclosure with separate compensation terms | Every dual agency file | Single form covering "both sides" without specifics |
| Seller contribution documentation at offer | Every transaction with seller concessions | Verbal agreements, missing addenda |

**Brokers should implement a file review checkpoint before any showing activity begins, not just at contract execution.** The traditional compliance review at the time of an accepted offer is too late under the new rules. If the buyer representation agreement is missing when the first showing occurs, the violation has already happened and cannot be retroactively cured.

Monthly transaction audits should flag files where the compensation documentation timeline does not align with showing records from lockbox access or scheduling platforms. Brokerages using showing management software can cross-reference access logs against the execution date of Form 1050 to identify gaps. Recent advisory opinions have clarified gray areas around team compensation splits and referral fee documentation under the new framework.

## How Compensation Negotiation Actually Works Now

The practical mechanics of getting paid have shifted from passive to active. Buyer agents must now have a compensation conversation with every prospective client before any property search begins. This conversation must end with a signed agreement specifying what the buyer owes and under what conditions.

At the offer stage, the buyer agent can request that the seller contribute toward their compensation as a term of the purchase agreement. The seller is free to accept, counter, or reject this request. If the seller agrees to pay some or all of the buyer-agent compensation, this must be documented in the purchase agreement or a separate addendum — not assumed from MLS data or prior conversations between agents.

Buyers who cannot afford to pay agent compensation out of pocket may negotiate seller concessions, request lender-paid compensation arrangements (where permitted by the loan program), or agree to a lower flat fee for reduced services. The agent's fiduciary obligation requires explaining all available options rather than defaulting to the structure that maximizes their own income.
