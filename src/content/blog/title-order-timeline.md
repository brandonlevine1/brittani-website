## The Hidden Cost of Getting Title Timing Wrong

Every agent managing multiple deals has lived this nightmare: it is three days before closing, the title commitment finally arrives, and there is a $14,000 mechanics lien nobody knew about. The closing gets pushed. The buyer's rate lock expires. The seller threatens to walk. You spend eight hours on the phone playing firefighter instead of generating new business.

The question of when to order title and what to do while you wait is not a minor administrative detail — it is the single operational decision most likely to either protect your closing timeline or destroy it. According to the American Land Title Association, roughly 25% of all title searches reveal issues that require resolution before closing. When agents delay the order or go passive during the search period, those issues surface too late to fix without schedule damage.

The dollar cost is real. A delayed closing typically costs buyers $30-50 per day in rate-lock extension fees, plus potential per-diem charges on their current housing. Sellers face mortgage overlap costs averaging $75-150 daily. And for the agent? One blown closing can mean 4-6 hours of rework, a damaged reputation, and occasionally an E&O claim. Brokers managing teams see this multiply across 10-15 agents, each carrying 5-15 active files.

## The 24-Hour Rule: When to Place the Order

**The moment a contract is fully executed — all signatures, all initials, earnest money instructions sent — you have a 24-hour window to order title before you start losing ground.** Not 48 hours. Not "when I get around to it on Monday." Twenty-four hours.

Here is why the clock matters. In most markets, a standard title search takes 5-10 business days. Complex properties — those with multiple owners, estates, trusts, or rural parcels — can take 10-15 business days. If your contract calls for a 30-day close and you wait until day 5 to order title, you have already consumed one-sixth of your timeline doing nothing.

The math gets worse when issues surface. The average lien resolution takes 7-14 additional business days. A boundary dispute requiring a survey can add 10-20 days. If you ordered title on day 1 and an issue appeared on day 8, you still have 15+ business days to resolve it before a 30-day close. Order on day 5, and that same issue leaves you with fewer than 10 days — which is often not enough.

| Scenario | Title Ordered | Commitment Received | Days Remaining to Resolve Issues (30-day close) |
|----------|--------------|--------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| Best practice | Day 1 | Day 7 | 23 days |
| Common delay | Day 5 | Day 12 | 18 days |
| Worst case | Day 10 | Day 17 | 13 days |
| Complex property (Day 1 order) | Day 1 | Day 12 | 18 days |
| Complex property (Day 5 order) | Day 5 | Day 17 | 13 days |

That table should make one thing clear: every day you delay the order is a day subtracted directly from your problem-solving runway.

## What Information You Need Before Calling the Title Company

Calling the title company without the right data creates back-and-forth that adds 1-2 days to the process before they even begin the search. Have these items ready before you pick up the phone or submit the order form.

You need the full legal description of the property (from the MLS tax record or the deed, not just the street address), the fully executed contract, the buyer and seller legal names exactly as they appear on the contract, the lender's name and loan officer contact if it is a financed purchase, and the target closing date. If the property is held in a trust or LLC, you will also need the entity documentation — and flagging this upfront saves the title examiner from discovering it mid-search and having to restart.

**Missing even one of these items — particularly the legal description or entity docs — is the most common reason title orders sit in a queue untouched for 48 hours.** The title company will not start work until they have what they need. Agents who [track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) know that this prep work needs to happen systematically, not ad hoc.

## Days 1-3 After Ordering: Active Waiting, Not Passive Hoping

The phrase "wait for title" is dangerously misleading. It implies you should sit idle. You should not. The 5-10 days between ordering title and receiving the commitment are some of the most productive days in the transaction — if you use them correctly.

During days 1-3, confirm with the title company that they received the order and have begun the search. A quick email or portal check on day 2 catches intake errors before they cost you a full week. Simultaneously, begin collecting the seller's disclosure documents. Many agents treat disclosures as a separate track, but [seller disclosure requirements](/blog/tennessee-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) often reveal the same issues that title will surface — unpermitted additions, boundary disputes, HOA violations.

Use this window to order the home inspection, schedule the appraisal, and verify that the lender has everything needed to issue conditional approval. These tasks run in parallel with the title search. An agent who sequences them serially — title first, then inspection, then appraisal — adds 10-15 unnecessary days to the transaction timeline.

## Days 4-7: The Follow-Up Protocol

**Day 5 is your hard follow-up date.** If you have not received any communication from the title company — not even a "we're still searching" update — pick up the phone. Email is insufficient here. A two-minute call on day 5 catches 80% of the problems that would otherwise blindside you on day 10.

What are you asking on that call? Three things. First: have you begun the search, and are there any preliminary red flags? Second: is the property in a county with recording backlogs that might extend the timeline? Third: do you need anything else from me or my client to complete the work?

County recording backlogs are a hidden variable that agents routinely underestimate. In high-volume markets, counties can fall 2-4 weeks behind on recording documents. This means the title examiner may not see a recently recorded lien or satisfaction. Your day-5 call surfaces this risk early enough to address it — either by obtaining a copy of the unrecorded document directly from the relevant party or by adjusting the closing timeline before everyone has booked movers.

## Reading the Title Commitment: What to Look for in 15 Minutes

When the commitment arrives, most agents forward it to the closing attorney or lender and move on. This is a mistake that creates liability exposure. You do not need to be a title attorney to catch the three categories of problems that most commonly derail closings.

First, check Schedule B-II (requirements). These are conditions that must be met before the policy will be issued. Common items include paying off existing liens, obtaining releases, or providing legal documentation for entities. Each requirement is a task with a timeline — and if you do not assign responsibility and set a deadline immediately, these items drift until they become emergencies.

Second, check Schedule B-I (exceptions). These are items the title company will not insure against. Standard exceptions — like rights of parties in possession or unrecorded easements — are normal. But specific exceptions referencing actual disputes, encroachments, or unresolved claims need immediate attention. The [National Association of Realtors' Code of Ethics](https://www.nar.realtor/about-nar/governing-documents/code-of-ethics/2025-code-of-ethics-standards-of-practice) requires agents to disclose material facts; an exception you ignored on the commitment could become exactly that kind of material fact.

Third, verify the legal description and vesting match your contract. Mismatched names — a seller who signed as "James Smith" but the deed says "James R. Smith" — create corrective document requirements that add 3-5 days if caught late.

| Commitment Section | What to Check | Common Problem | Time to Resolve |
|-------------------|---------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Schedule A | Legal description, vesting | Name mismatch, wrong parcel | 3-5 days |
| Schedule B-II (Requirements) | Liens, payoffs, entity docs | Unpaid HOA dues, open mortgage | 7-14 days |
| Schedule B-I (Exceptions) | Specific vs. standard exceptions | Boundary disputes, encroachments | 14-30 days |
| Effective date | Gap between commitment and closing | New liens recorded after commitment | 1-3 days (gap search) |

## Assigning Resolution Tasks: Who Does What

Once you have identified issues on the commitment, the next step is assignment — not hope. Every requirement and problematic exception needs an owner, a deadline, and a follow-up date.

Seller-side issues — existing liens, judgments against the seller, open permits — belong to the listing agent to chase. Buyer-side issues — entity documentation for a buyer LLC, power of attorney requirements — belong to the buyer's agent. Title curative work — obtaining prior lien releases from defunct companies, correcting old recording errors — belongs to the title company, but you need to confirm they are working on it.

Agents who manage this assignment process with sticky notes and memory are the same agents [who miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines). A shared task list — whether in your CRM, a project management tool, or even a shared spreadsheet — creates accountability that passive email chains never will.

## The Gap Period: Closing Day Is Not the Finish Line

Between the title commitment date and the actual closing, new issues can appear. A contractor files a mechanics lien. The seller takes out a home equity line. A judgment gets recorded. This is called the "gap" period, and most title companies perform a gap search 24-48 hours before closing to catch new recordings.

**You should not rely solely on the title company to flag gap issues in time.** If you know the seller is doing any work on the property, has pending litigation, or is in financial distress, communicate that information to the title company proactively. A heads-up on day 15 prevents a closing-day surprise on day 30.

For brokers managing agent teams, the gap period is where [training new agents on timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) pays the highest dividend. Newer agents often assume that once the commitment comes back clean, the title work is done. Teaching them that the commitment is a snapshot — not a guarantee — reduces the team-wide rate of closing-day fires by a measurable margin.

## Building This Into a Repeatable System

The agents who close 15+ deals simultaneously without title emergencies are not smarter or luckier — they have a system. That system has four components: a trigger (contract execution), an immediate action (order title within 24 hours), a follow-up cadence (day 2 confirmation, day 5 call, commitment review within 24 hours of receipt), and an assignment protocol (every issue gets an owner and a deadline).

This system works whether you are a solo agent running 8 files or a broker overseeing 12 agents with 60 active transactions. The difference is scale — a solo agent can manage the cadence manually, but a team needs automation or delegation to maintain consistency across files.
