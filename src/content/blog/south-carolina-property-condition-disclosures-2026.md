South Carolina property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 remain one of the most litigation-prone areas of residential practice in the state. Every year, the South Carolina Real Estate Commission investigates complaints tied to incomplete or missing disclosure forms—and agents bear a share of that liability even when the seller is the one who filled in the blanks. If you hold a South Carolina license and list or sell residential property, the statutes that govern what must be disclosed, when, and how have not loosened.

This post breaks down the specific forms, statutes, and enforcement risks you face, plus the mistakes that keep showing up in disciplinary actions.

## The Statutory Framework: S.C. Code Title 27, Chapter 50

South Carolina's Residential Property Condition Disclosure Act lives in S.C. Code Sections 27-50-10 through 27-50-110. The law applies to the transfer of residential real property consisting of one to four dwelling units. It requires sellers to deliver a written disclosure statement to prospective buyers before or at the time the parties enter into a purchase agreement.

**The Act is not optional, and "as-is" clauses do not override it.** Section 27-50-40 mandates that the seller complete the disclosure form regardless of how the contract characterizes the sale condition. An as-is clause shifts risk for repairs, but it does not eliminate the buyer's right to know what the seller already knows.

Exemptions do exist under Section 27-50-20. Transfers by foreclosure, court order, fiduciary estate administration, and transfers between co-owners or spouses are carved out. New construction sold directly by the builder is also exempt—though builders still face implied warranty obligations under the state's construction defects statutes.

## Forms Agents Must Use in 2026

The primary document is the **SC Residential Property Condition Disclosure Statement**, which the South Carolina Association of Realtors distributes as form SCR 310. This multi-page form covers structural components, mechanical systems, plumbing, electrical, water and sewer, environmental hazards, and property boundaries. Sellers mark each item as either a known defect, no defect, or unknown.

For pre-1978 homes, agents must also provide the federal Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (commonly referenced as form SCR 311 or the EPA/HUD standard form). This obligation flows from 42 U.S.C. 4852d and its implementing regulation at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H. The seller and listing agent both sign this form, certifying that the buyer received the EPA pamphlet *Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home* and had a ten-day opportunity to conduct a lead inspection.

| Form | Purpose | Statute/Regulation | Who Signs |
|------|---------|-------------------|-----------|
| SCR 310 | Property condition disclosure | S.C. Code 27-50-40 | Seller |
| SCR 311 / EPA form | Lead-based paint disclosure | 42 U.S.C. 4852d; 24 CFR 35 | Seller + Listing Agent + Buyer |
| SCR 390 | Seller's property condition amendment | S.C. Code 27-50-60 | Seller |
| SCR 312 | Disclosure of stigmatized property (optional) | Common practice, not mandated | Seller |

Section 27-50-60 requires the seller to amend the disclosure if conditions change between acceptance and closing. Form SCR 390 serves this purpose—and too many agents forget it exists until a pipe bursts the week before closing.

## Environmental Disclosures Beyond Lead Paint

Lead paint gets most of the attention, but South Carolina's property condition form also asks sellers about environmental contaminants including underground storage tanks, asbestos, radon, flood zone status, and proximity to hazardous waste sites. If the property sits in a FEMA-designated Special Flood Hazard Area, the seller must disclose that fact on the form, and the buyer's lender will require flood insurance regardless.

**Radon disclosure in South Carolina is handled within the SCR 310 form rather than through a standalone statute.** Unlike some states that have adopted a separate radon notification act, South Carolina folds this into the general property condition disclosure. Agents should note, however, that the EPA considers radon a significant environmental health risk in parts of the Upstate—particularly Greenville, Spartanburg, and Anderson counties—and a buyer's agent who knows the property is in a high-radon zone should recommend testing even if the seller checks "unknown."

For properties near former military installations, Superfund sites, or industrial corridors, agents can reference the [South Carolina Department of Health and Environmental Control (DHEC)](https://scdhec.gov/) contaminated sites registry. This is not a disclosure the agent must affirmatively pull, but it becomes relevant under the duty to disclose material facts known to the licensee.

## Consequences of Noncompliance: What Agents Actually Risk

Failing to deliver the disclosure form creates a rescission right for the buyer under Section 27-50-80. If the buyer never receives the statement, the buyer may cancel the contract at any time before closing and recover earnest money. That alone can kill a deal and trigger commission disputes.

Beyond rescission, Section 27-50-70 establishes that a seller who knowingly makes a false statement on the form is liable to the buyer for actual damages. Agents are not automatically liable for a seller's misrepresentation—but the statute clarifies that a licensee who has actual knowledge of a defect and fails to disclose it shares liability. The South Carolina Real Estate Commission can also pursue disciplinary action under S.C. Code Section 40-57-145 for conduct demonstrating incompetence or untrustworthiness.

| Violation | Consequence | Authority |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Failure to deliver SCR 310 | Buyer rescission right; earnest money refund | S.C. Code 27-50-80 |
| False statements by seller | Actual damages to buyer | S.C. Code 27-50-70 |
| Agent knew of defect, stayed silent | Shared liability + Commission discipline | S.C. Code 27-50-70; 40-57-145 |
| Missing lead-paint disclosure | Up to $19,507 per violation (federal penalty, adjusted for inflation) | 42 U.S.C. 4852d; 24 CFR 35.88 |
| Pattern of noncompliance | License suspension or revocation | S.C. Code 40-57-145 |

Federal lead-paint penalties deserve emphasis. HUD and the EPA have increased civil penalties to over $19,000 per violation. The listing agent is independently liable—meaning you cannot shift blame to the seller for a missing lead disclosure if you were the one managing the listing file.

## Common Mistakes South Carolina Agents Make

**First, agents routinely accept a partially completed SCR 310 without pushing the seller to address every line item.** Blank fields are not the same as "unknown." A blank signals that the form was not completed as required, and it opens the door to a buyer argument that the disclosure was never properly delivered.

Second, listing agents fail to deliver an amended disclosure (SCR 390) when conditions change after contract execution. The seller reports a new water intrusion issue verbally to the agent, the agent relays it to the buyer's agent by text, and nobody updates the form. Verbal communication does not satisfy Section 27-50-60.

Third, agents conflate the disclosure obligation with the inspection contingency. These are separate mechanisms. The seller's disclosure is a pre-contract or at-contract obligation. The buyer's inspection is a due diligence tool. One does not substitute for the other, and advising a seller to "just let the inspector find it" is a recipe for liability.

Fourth, buyer's agents neglect to confirm that the lead-paint form was signed by all parties before submitting the executed contract to the transaction coordinator. This is especially common in fast-moving markets where agents rush to ratification. If you are [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), a simple checklist catch at the contract assembly stage prevents this from becoming a compliance emergency.

Fifth, agents assume short sales and bank-owned properties are exempt. They are not universally exempt under the South Carolina statute. Foreclosure transfers are exempt, but a short sale—where the seller is still the owner—requires the disclosure. Many agents miss this distinction and expose themselves to Commission scrutiny.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers-in-charge carry supervisory liability under S.C. Code Section 40-57-137. If an agent in your office repeatedly fails to secure completed disclosures, the Commission can cite you for inadequate supervision. This is not theoretical—disciplinary orders published by the SC Real Estate Commission regularly name brokers alongside agents.

**Brokers should audit every listing file for the presence of a signed and fully completed SCR 310 before the property goes active on the MLS.** A pre-MLS compliance check takes less than two minutes and prevents downstream chaos. Build it into your [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) so that the responsibility does not fall through the cracks during agent-to-TC handoffs.

For lead-paint compliance, brokers should maintain a policy that no pre-1978 listing goes live without the signed EPA disclosure and a copy of the pamphlet delivery confirmation in the file. Quarterly file audits—pulling ten random closed transactions and verifying disclosure completeness—give you a defensible record of supervisory diligence.

Brokers also need to train agents on the difference between seller disclosures and agent disclosures. Under South Carolina common law and Commission rules, agents have an independent duty to disclose material facts they know, even if the seller omits them from the form. If your agent personally witnessed flooding in the crawl space during a showing, that knowledge must be disclosed to the buyer whether or not the seller mentioned it on the SCR 310.

## Handling Disputes and Buyer Rescission Requests

When a buyer claims the disclosure was deficient, the timeline matters. Under Section 27-50-80, the buyer's rescission right exists until closing if no disclosure was ever provided. If the disclosure was provided but contained a misrepresentation, the buyer's remedy shifts from rescission to a damages claim under Section 27-50-70.

Agents should document every delivery. A signed acknowledgment page, a timestamped email delivery, or a transaction management system log showing when the buyer accessed the document all serve as evidence. If you are handling paperwork for [new agents learning timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines), drill into them that unsigned disclosures are as good as undelivered ones in a dispute.

The three-day rescission clock referenced in Section 27-50-80 applies specifically when the seller notifies the buyer that no disclosure will be provided (for exempt transactions or seller refusal). In that scenario, the buyer has three business days from receipt of that notice to terminate. Agents frequently confuse this limited three-day window with a general rescission period—it is not.

## Staying Compliant Without Drowning in Paper

Disclosure compliance is a process problem more than a knowledge problem. Most South Carolina agents know they need the SCR 310 and the lead-paint form. The failures happen when the forms are not tracked, not reviewed, not amended, or not stored properly.
