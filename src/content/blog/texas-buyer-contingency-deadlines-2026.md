## Why Texas Buyer Contingency Deadlines 2026 Demand Precision

Missing a single contingency deadline in a Texas residential transaction can cost your buyer thousands in earnest money, expose your brokerage to a TREC complaint, and collapse a deal that took weeks to negotiate. Texas buyer contingency deadlines 2026 are governed by the same statutory framework agents have relied on—Texas Property Code Chapter 5, Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1101, and the promulgated forms issued by the Texas Real Estate Commission—but the margin for error shrinks every year as median prices climb and sellers grow less forgiving. Understanding exactly how these deadlines function, how they are calculated, and what triggers default is not optional knowledge; it is baseline competency for anyone holding an active Texas license.

The stakes are not hypothetical. TREC disciplinary actions in recent years show that deadline mismanagement ranks among the top complaints filed against licensees, often alongside allegations of negligence or misrepresentation under Section 1101.652 of the Texas Occupations Code.

## The Anatomy of Key Deadlines in TREC Form 20-17

The One to Four Family Residential Contract (Resale), designated TREC Form 20-17, is the backbone of most residential deals in Texas. Its contingency structure revolves around specific blanks that agents fill in with calendar-day counts or hard dates. The deadlines that most frequently cause problems fall into five categories.

**The option period (Paragraph 23) is the most misunderstood deadline in Texas residential practice.** The buyer pays a separate option fee—delivered to the seller within three calendar days of the effective date—in exchange for an unrestricted right to terminate during a negotiated window. If the buyer does not deliver written notice of termination before 5:00 p.m. local time on the final day of the option period, that right expires permanently.

The financing contingency (Paragraph 4) requires the buyer to apply for financing and deliver a written financing approval or waiver within the number of days specified in the contract. The appraisal contingency in the Third Party Financing Addendum (TREC Form 40-9) grants additional protections but carries its own notice requirements. Title objections under Paragraph 6D must be raised within a specified window after the buyer receives the commitment, and the property condition notice under Paragraph 7B(2) is often confused with the option period but operates on its own timeline when buyers forgo the option.

| Contingency | Form/Paragraph | Default Day Count | Notice Method Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Option Period | 20-17, Para 23 | Negotiated (typically 5-10 days) | Written, before 5:00 p.m. on last day |
| Financing Approval | 20-17, Para 4 | Negotiated (typically 21-30 days) | Written notice per Para 21 |
| Appraisal | 40-9, Para 4 | Tied to financing deadline | Written objection per addendum |
| Title Objections | 20-17, Para 6D | Negotiated (after commitment receipt) | Written, per Para 21 |
| Property Condition | 20-17, Para 7B(2) | Negotiated (if no option used) | Written notice per Para 21 |

## How Day Counts Actually Work Under TREC Rules

Paragraph 5A of the promulgated contract specifies that the effective date is the date the last party signs or initials, and day counting begins the following day. All deadlines run on calendar days unless the contract states otherwise. If the final day for performance falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or state or federal legal holiday, the deadline extends to the end of the next business day.

This seems simple until agents encounter scenarios where the effective date is disputed—such as when counteroffers cross in electronic transmission—or when a holiday falls at the end of an option period. The Texas Association of Realtors Legal Hotline fields hundreds of these calls annually. Getting day counts wrong by even one day can mean your buyer's earnest money is forfeited or, conversely, that you allowed a termination right to lapse without your client's informed consent.

**Agents must also distinguish between "days after the effective date" and "days after receipt."** Title objection deadlines, for example, start running when the buyer actually receives the title commitment—not on the effective date. Confusing these triggers is one of the most common errors TREC investigators encounter during complaint reviews.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The consequences of blowing a deadline in Texas fall into three buckets: transactional, regulatory, and civil.

On the transactional level, the buyer may lose earnest money deposited with the title company. Under Paragraph 23 of the contract, if the buyer fails to terminate within the option period window, the seller can hold the buyer to the contract or refuse to release earnest money through the standard release process outlined in TREC Form 40-11 (Release of Earnest Money). This often results in a standoff that can only be resolved by mediation, arbitration, or litigation under the contract's dispute resolution provisions in Paragraph 16.

From a regulatory standpoint, TREC may pursue disciplinary action against a licensee under Section 1101.652(b)(1) for negligence or incompetence. Sanctions range from mandatory continuing education to suspension or revocation of the license, with administrative penalties up to $5,000 per violation under [Section 1101.702 of the Texas Occupations Code](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/OC/htm/OC.1101.htm). A pattern of deadline failures can escalate a complaint from a warning letter to a formal hearing before the State Office of Administrative Hearings.

Civil liability is the third risk. Buyers who lose earnest money due to an agent's negligence can sue for breach of fiduciary duty. Brokers can be vicariously liable under the respondeat superior doctrine if they failed to supervise. Errors and omissions insurance may cover the claim, but deductibles, premium increases, and reputational damage remain.

## Five Common Mistakes Agents Make With Contingency Deadlines

**First, agents routinely confuse the option fee delivery deadline with the option period termination deadline.** The option fee must be delivered to the seller or seller's agent within three calendar days after the effective date (Paragraph 23). The termination notice deadline is the last day of the option period itself. These are two separate obligations, and failing on either one can void the buyer's option rights entirely.

Second, agents treat verbal communications as sufficient notice. Paragraph 21 of TREC Form 20-17 specifies that notices must be in writing and delivered to the party or the party's agent at the address stated in the contract. A phone call telling the listing agent "my buyer wants to terminate" does not satisfy this requirement, even if both sides agree it happened.

Third, agents fail to account for the delivery method's impact on timing. Hand delivery is effective upon receipt. Email or fax is effective upon transmission if sent to the address in the contract. But mailing a notice—while permitted—can create ambiguity about when the clock stops. In a tight option period, choosing the wrong delivery method is a gamble that experienced agents should never take.

Fourth, agents neglect to calendar the financing contingency expiration separately from the option period. Many buyers assume that if they terminate during the option period, the financing deadline is irrelevant. That is true—but if the buyer does not terminate during the option period and later cannot obtain financing, the financing contingency deadline becomes the only exit ramp. Agents who fail to track it independently leave their buyers exposed to earnest money forfeiture if the financing falls through after the deadline passes without proper notice.

Fifth, agents mishandle the Third Party Financing Addendum (TREC Form 40-9) by not understanding when the appraisal contingency terminates independently of the financing deadline. If the property appraises below the sales price, the buyer must object in writing within the timeframe specified in the addendum. Silence equals acceptance, and the buyer loses the right to terminate on appraisal grounds.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under TREC Rules Section 535.2 and Section 1101.651(d) of the Occupations Code. A designated broker who allows agents to repeatedly miss contingency deadlines without intervention is not merely tolerating poor performance—they are creating regulatory exposure for the entire brokerage.

| Audit Item | Frequency | What to Check |
|---|---|---|
| Option fee delivery confirmation | Every transaction | Proof of delivery within 3 days of effective date |
| Contingency deadline calendar entries | At contract execution | All blanks filled, days calculated correctly, calendar reminders set |
| Written termination notices | At each contingency event | Proper form used, delivery method documented, timestamp confirmed |
| Financing addendum alignment | At contract execution | Financing and appraisal deadlines consistent with lender timelines |
| Earnest money deposit timing | Within 2 days of effective date | Receipt from title company confirming deposit per Paragraph 5 |

Brokers should implement a file review protocol that catches deadline errors before they become disputes. This means reviewing contracts within 24 hours of execution—not at closing. If your brokerage handles volume, a [transaction coordinator can manage this workflow](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator), but the broker still owns the oversight obligation.

Quarterly audits of terminated transactions are equally important. When a deal falls apart, brokers should examine whether contingency notices were timely and properly delivered. Patterns of late notices or missing documentation signal training deficiencies that need immediate correction before they trigger a TREC investigation.

## Protecting Your Pipeline and Your License

**Deadline tracking is not a "nice to have" administrative function—it is a risk management discipline that separates competent agents from those who end up in front of TREC.** Every transaction file should include a deadline summary sheet created at contract execution, listing each contingency, its calendar deadline, and the responsible party. Agents who rely on memory alone are the ones who [miss deadlines most often](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

For brokerages managing multiple active deals simultaneously, the complexity multiplies. Ten contracts in various stages mean ten different sets of option periods, financing deadlines, and title objection windows—all running concurrently.
