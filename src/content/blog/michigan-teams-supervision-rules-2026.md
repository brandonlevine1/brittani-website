## Michigan Teams & Supervision Rules 2026: The Regulatory Framework

The phrase "Michigan teams & supervision rules 2026" should be on every team leader's radar this year. The Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) has continued tightening enforcement around how real estate teams operate, advertise, and report to their supervising brokers. If you run a team—or belong to one—the rules that govern your daily operations sit in the Michigan Occupational Code (MCL 339.2501 et seq.) and Michigan Administrative Rules 338.2501 through 338.2537.

Michigan does not issue a separate "team license." Every team functions as an extension of a brokerage, and every team member's license hangs under the broker's authority. That distinction matters because it places extraordinary supervisory responsibility on the broker, not the team leader. Violations do not stay siloed within the team—they travel upward.

This post breaks down the specific statutes, required forms, and enforcement actions that apply to Michigan teams in 2026, along with the most common compliance failures LARA investigators flag during audits.

## How Michigan Defines Teams Under Current Law

Michigan's Occupational Code does not use the word "team" as a formal license category. Instead, LARA treats teams as marketing arrangements operating under a licensed broker's supervision. The legal relationship is governed by MCL 339.2501(d), which defines a "real estate salesperson" as someone who performs licensed activities under the supervision of a licensed broker.

**Administrative Rule 338.2535 is the critical regulation for team operations.** It requires that all advertising—including team names, yard signs, and digital marketing—prominently display the employing broker's legal name or registered trade name. A team name alone, without broker identification, constitutes a violation.

The Michigan Real Estate Commission has issued guidance letters clarifying that team names cannot contain words suggesting the team is an independent brokerage. Terms like "Realty," "Associates," "Real Estate Company," or "Brokerage" in a team name without proper registration will trigger enforcement. The Commission's 2024 guidance memo (reissued January 2026) specifically lists prohibited terms and requires brokers to pre-approve all team branding.

## Supervision Obligations Under MCL 339.2512

MCL 339.2512(1) places a direct mandate on brokers: they must "exercise supervision and control" over licensed salespersons and associate brokers. This is not optional language. Michigan courts have interpreted "supervision and control" to include reviewing contracts, monitoring trust account deposits, and ensuring disclosure compliance on every transaction a team handles.

For teams specifically, the broker must maintain written policies addressing transaction oversight, advertising review, and complaint resolution. LARA's audit checklist—used during brokerage inspections—asks for documentation proving these policies exist and are enforced. A broker who delegates supervision entirely to a team leader without maintaining personal oversight risks discipline under MCL 339.2512.

| Responsibility | Broker's Duty | Team Leader's Role |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising approval | Must pre-approve all team marketing | Submits materials for review |
| Trust account deposits | Must verify timely deposit | Delivers earnest money per brokerage policy |
| Contract review | Must review or have system for review | Ensures contracts reach broker promptly |
| Disclosure compliance | Must audit disclosure delivery | Delivers disclosures to clients, documents delivery |
| License status monitoring | Must verify active status of all team members | Reports any CE or status changes |

The distinction between broker and team leader duties matters enormously when LARA investigates complaints. A team leader who acts as a de facto broker—holding funds, signing on behalf of the brokerage, or making supervisory decisions without broker authorization—can face unlicensed brokerage charges under MCL 339.2512a.

## Required Forms and Documentation for Michigan Teams

Michigan teams interact with several specific forms during transactions. The Michigan Association of Realtors (MAR) publishes standardized forms, and LARA requires certain disclosures by statute.

**The Agency Disclosure form (MAR Form RC-100) must be provided at the first substantive contact with a buyer or seller.** Under MCL 339.2517, failure to provide this disclosure can void the agent's commission claim and expose the brokerage to civil liability. For teams, every member who interacts with a client must ensure disclosure has occurred—not just the team leader.

The Seller's Disclosure Statement (MCL 565.957) requires sellers to complete a property condition disclosure, but the team's responsibility is ensuring the form reaches the buyer before an offer is accepted. Teams that handle multiple listings simultaneously [often miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) on this requirement, particularly when listing agents and buyer agents are on the same team (creating dual agency scenarios governed by MCL 339.2517(3)).

Additional forms relevant to team compliance include the Buyer Agency Agreement, the Compensation Disclosure form (updated for 2025-2026 practice changes), and the Change of Agency Disclosure when team members on both sides of a transaction create in-house dual agency. Brokers must have systems to [track these across multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) when teams run high transaction volumes.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Teams Make

Five compliance failures appear repeatedly in LARA disciplinary actions and audit findings involving teams.

**First, team advertising that omits or minimizes the broker's name.** Rule 338.2535 requires the broker's name to be "clearly and conspicuously" displayed. Teams that place their brand in 72-point font with the brokerage name in 8-point footnote text violate this standard. Social media profiles, email signatures, and YouTube channels are not exempt—LARA has issued violations for Instagram bios that omit brokerage identification entirely.

Second, unlicensed assistants performing licensed activities. Michigan teams frequently hire transaction coordinators or administrative staff who cross the line into negotiating terms, showing property, or discussing contract provisions with clients. Under MCL 339.2501, anyone performing these acts must hold an active license. The [role of a transaction coordinator](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) has specific legal boundaries that teams must respect.

Third, failing to disclose in-house dual agency when buyer and seller agents are on the same team. MCL 339.2517(3) requires written consent from both parties before dual agency can proceed. Teams that assume the brokerage's general dual agency policy covers intra-team situations without specific client consent face voided commission claims and disciplinary complaints.

Fourth, team leaders holding earnest money checks instead of immediately delivering them to the broker for trust account deposit. Michigan Administrative Rule 338.2531 requires deposit within one business day of acceptance. Team leaders who hold checks "until Monday" or "until the office opens" violate this rule, and the broker faces trust account violations.

Fifth, team members operating under expired or inactive licenses. When a team has five or ten agents, CE compliance can slip. LARA's license database is public, and opposing agents or title companies increasingly verify license status before closing. A single transaction handled by an unlicensed team member can unravel an entire deal.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers who supervise teams carry the heaviest regulatory burden in Michigan. LARA's 2026 inspection protocol asks brokers to produce documentation in several categories.

| Audit Area | What LARA Inspectors Request | Frequency Recommended |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising files | Copies of all team ads, social posts, signs with broker name verification | Quarterly review |
| Transaction files | Signed agency disclosures, timely deposit records, contract copies | Every transaction |
| License verification | Proof that all team members hold active licenses | Monthly check |
| Policy manual | Written supervision policies specific to teams | Annual update |
| Complaint log | Records of consumer complaints and resolution | Ongoing |

**Brokers who rely on team leaders to self-police are gambling with their own license.** MCL 339.2512 does not allow delegation of supervisory responsibility—it can only be shared with appointed associate brokers who have specific, documented authority. Even then, the broker remains ultimately liable.

A practical approach: require team leaders to submit weekly transaction summaries, audit 10-20% of files monthly, and conduct annual advertising sweeps across all platforms. Brokers should maintain a dated log of these reviews. When LARA shows up, the question is not "did you supervise?" but "can you prove you supervised?"

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

Consequences for violating Michigan's team and supervision rules range from administrative fines to license revocation. MCL 339.2512 violations carry fines up to $10,000 per instance. Trust account violations under Rule 338.2531 can result in immediate license suspension pending hearing.

For agents, a single advertising violation might result in a warning letter or mandatory continuing education. Repeated violations, especially those involving consumer harm, escalate to formal complaints before the Michigan Board of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons. The Board publishes disciplinary actions publicly—meaning your name, violation, and penalty appear in a searchable database accessible to clients and competing agents.

Civil liability adds another layer. A buyer or seller who was not properly informed of agency relationships under MCL 339.2517 can pursue damages in civil court. Michigan courts have held that failure to disclose dual agency can constitute fraud, entitling the injured party to rescission of the transaction plus actual damages. For a team that handles 50+ transactions per year, one systemic disclosure failure could cascade into multiple lawsuits.

The [true cost of compliance failures](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends beyond fines. Lost referrals, damaged reputation within your MLS community, and increased E&O premiums compound the financial damage of a single LARA action.
