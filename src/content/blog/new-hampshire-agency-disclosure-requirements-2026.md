## Understanding New Hampshire Agency Disclosure Requirements 2026

New Hampshire agency disclosure requirements 2026 remain one of the most strictly enforced compliance obligations in the state. The New Hampshire Real Estate Commission (NHREC) treats disclosure failures as serious violations, and the consequences extend well beyond a slap on the wrist. Every licensee who represents buyers, sellers, or both must deliver the correct forms at the correct time or face disciplinary proceedings that can derail an entire career.

RSA 331-A, New Hampshire's real estate practice act, establishes the framework. The administrative rules under Rea 700 spell out how agents must disclose their role to every consumer they interact with in a real estate transaction. These are not suggestions. They are mandates backed by enforcement power, and the Commission has shown a willingness to act on complaints.

This post breaks down the specific forms, timing requirements, penalties, and audit strategies that agents and brokers in New Hampshire need to follow through 2026 and beyond.

## The Statutory Framework: RSA 331-A and Rea 700 Rules

**New Hampshire's disclosure obligations flow from two sources: the statute (RSA 331-A:25-a through 331-A:25-d) and the NHREC administrative rules (Rea 701 through Rea 703).** The statute defines the types of agency relationships allowed in the state, while the rules prescribe the exact mechanics of disclosure, timing, and documentation.

Under RSA 331-A:25-b, every licensee must disclose their agency relationship at the "earliest reasonable opportunity." The law further requires that this disclosure happen before any confidential information is exchanged and before substantive discussions about a specific property occur. This is not a gray area. The Commission has interpreted "earliest reasonable opportunity" narrowly in enforcement actions.

New Hampshire recognizes several relationship types: seller's agent, buyer's agent, disclosed dual agent, and designated agent (sometimes called appointed agent within a firm). Each of these requires its own specific consent acknowledgment from the consumer. ## The Forms You Must Use

The primary document is the **Agency Disclosure and Informed Consent Form** prescribed by the NHREC under Rea 701.01. This form describes all available relationship types, explains the duties owed under each, and includes signature lines where the consumer acknowledges their chosen relationship.

The New Hampshire Association of Realtors (NHAR) supplements this with **Form 39**, the Brokerage Relationships Disclosure. Form 39 provides additional plain-language explanations and is widely used across the state, though it does not replace the NHREC-mandated form. Agents should treat it as a companion document that reinforces understanding.

For dual agency situations, a separate **Dual Agency Informed Consent** acknowledgment is required under RSA 331-A:25-d. This document must clearly state that the agent will not be able to advocate exclusively for either party and that both the buyer and seller agree to the arrangement. Without this written consent, dual agency is prohibited. The [NHREC official website](https://www.nh.gov/nhrec/) provides current versions of required forms and rule text.

| Form / Document | Source | Required By | When to Deliver |
|---|---|---|---|
| Agency Disclosure and Informed Consent Form | NHREC (Rea 701.01) | All licensees | Earliest reasonable opportunity |
| Form 39 - Brokerage Relationships Disclosure | NHAR | NHAR members (best practice for all) | With or before consent form |
| Dual Agency Informed Consent | NHREC (RSA 331-A:25-d) | Agents entering dual agency | Before acting as dual agent |
| Designated Agency Notification | Brokerage-specific | Firms using designated agency | Before representing both parties within firm |

## Timing: When "Earliest Reasonable Opportunity" Actually Means

The phrase "earliest reasonable opportunity" has created confusion for years. The Commission has clarified through advisory rulings and disciplinary decisions that this means before the first substantive conversation. If a buyer calls about a listing and starts asking about price flexibility or seller motivation, disclosure should already have been made.

**Open houses present a particular trap.** The NHREC expects that agents hosting open houses have the disclosure form available at the door and present it to any visitor who engages in conversation beyond casual greetings. Waiting until a visitor asks to write an offer is too late. The duty triggers the moment the conversation turns substantive.

For listing agents, disclosure must happen at or before the listing presentation. For buyer's agents, the obligation triggers at the first meeting or phone call where the relationship begins to form. Agents who rely on email or text communication should send the form electronically before discussing property details, and they should document the transmission with timestamps.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, License Actions, and Deal Fallout

The NHREC has authority under RSA 331-A:28 to impose disciplinary sanctions for disclosure failures. These include formal warnings, fines of up to $2,000 per violation, mandatory education requirements, license suspension, and in severe or repeated cases, license revocation. The Commission reviews complaints on a case-by-case basis, but a pattern of non-disclosure will almost certainly result in suspension.

Beyond regulatory sanctions, a consumer who was not properly informed of the agency relationship may have grounds to rescind the transaction. New Hampshire courts have recognized that a material failure to disclose can constitute a breach of fiduciary duty, opening the agent and brokerage to civil liability. Damages in such cases can include commission disgorgement, out-of-pocket losses, and in rare instances, punitive damages.

**Errors and omissions insurance may not cover intentional or knowing failures to disclose.** Most E&O policies exclude coverage for willful violations of licensing law, which means the agent and brokerage could bear the full financial exposure. This is not a theoretical risk. Brokerages in New Hampshire have faced six-figure settlements where disclosure lapses were central to the claims.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in New Hampshire

**First, agents routinely delay disclosure until the offer stage.** They assume that casual conversations at open houses or initial phone calls do not trigger the requirement. The Commission disagrees. If you discussed price, condition, or motivation for any specific property, you should have already disclosed.

Second, agents confuse the NHAR Form 39 with the NHREC-mandated form and use only one. These serve different purposes. Form 39 is an educational supplement. The NHREC consent form is the legal document. Using only Form 39 without the official consent form leaves you exposed, even though many agents believe they are covered.

Third, dual agency consent is obtained after the fact. An agent who has been working with a buyer suddenly realizes they also hold the listing on the property the buyer wants. Instead of stopping and obtaining dual agency consent before any further action, they proceed with negotiations and circle back to get signatures later. This is a violation that the NHREC has specifically flagged in enforcement actions.

Fourth, agents fail to retain copies of signed disclosure forms in the transaction file. Even if the form was delivered, inability to produce it during an audit or complaint investigation shifts the burden to the agent. Without documentation, the Commission presumes non-compliance.

Fifth, teams and assistants deliver disclosure forms without proper context. An unlicensed assistant handing a consumer a form at the door without explanation does not satisfy the informed consent requirement. The licensee must ensure the consumer understands the form and has the opportunity to ask questions. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under RSA 331-A:26 for the actions of their affiliated licensees. A broker who fails to implement systems for disclosure compliance may face their own disciplinary proceedings independent of the agent's violations. Passive oversight is not a defense.

**Every brokerage should maintain a written policy specifying when and how disclosure forms are delivered, who is responsible for obtaining signatures, and where signed forms are stored.** This policy should be distributed to all licensees at onboarding and reviewed at least annually. The NHREC has indicated that brokerages with documented compliance programs receive more favorable consideration during enforcement proceedings.

Brokers should audit transaction files on a regular cycle, checking for the presence of signed disclosure forms, correct dates relative to the first substantive contact, and proper dual agency or designated agency documentation where applicable. A quarterly audit of a random sample of files is the minimum standard that most compliance consultants recommend for New Hampshire brokerages. Agents who track [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously are especially prone to documentation gaps that brokers must catch.

The audit checklist should confirm three things for every file: that the disclosure form is dated on or before the first substantive contact, that the consumer's chosen relationship type matches the role the agent actually performed, and that any dual or designated agency situation has separate written consent from all parties.
