## Understanding Florida FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

Florida's international buyer pool is enormous—and it creates a compliance trap that catches agents every single quarter. The Florida firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 remain one of the most misunderstood federal obligations in residential transactions, particularly because Florida has no state income tax layered on top. Agents who assume "no state tax means no withholding" find themselves on the wrong end of an IRS notice faster than they can spell transferee.

FIRPTA—the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act, codified under Internal Revenue Code Sections 1445 through 1445(e)—requires that when a foreign person sells U.S. real property, the buyer must withhold a percentage of the gross sales price and remit it to the IRS. The withholding acts as a prepayment of the foreign seller's U.S. tax liability on the gain. In Florida specifically, the absence of a parallel state mechanism means FIRPTA is the only withholding gate—but it is a hard gate, and missing it shifts full liability to the buyer.

## Who Qualifies as a "Foreign Person" and Why It Matters

**Under IRC Section 1445(f)(3), a "foreign person" includes any nonresident alien individual, foreign corporation, foreign partnership, foreign trust, or foreign estate.** Green card holders and individuals who meet the substantial presence test are generally treated as U.S. persons and fall outside FIRPTA's scope. But residency status is not always obvious at the listing stage.

The determination typically happens through IRS Form 8288-B (Application for Withholding Certificate) or the seller's provision of a non-foreign affidavit under Treasury Regulation 1.1445-2(b). If the seller cannot or will not provide a valid affidavit certifying U.S. person status—including a U.S. taxpayer identification number—the transaction must proceed under FIRPTA withholding assumptions.

Florida agents working in Miami-Dade, Broward, Palm Beach, and Orange counties encounter this constantly. The National Association of Realtors' [2025 International Transactions report](https://www.nar.realtor/research-and-statistics/research-reports/international-transactions-in-u-s-residential-real-estate) found that Florida accounted for 20 percent of all foreign-buyer purchases nationally. That ratio means a significant chunk of Florida resale inventory is owned by foreign persons subject to FIRPTA on exit.

## The 2026 Withholding Rates and Thresholds

The federal withholding rate under FIRPTA has been 15 percent of the gross amount realized since the PATH Act of 2015 increased it from 10 percent. A reduced rate of 10 percent applies when the property will be used by the buyer as a residence and the amount realized is between $300,001 and $1,000,000. Zero withholding applies when the residence-use requirement is met and the price is $300,000 or less.

| Sales Price Range | Buyer Residence Use? | Withholding Rate | Form Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| $300,000 or less | Yes | 0% | Buyer affidavit |
| $300,001–$1,000,000 | Yes | 10% | IRS Form 8288 + buyer affidavit |
| Over $1,000,000 | Yes or No | 15% | IRS Form 8288 |
| Any amount | No (investment) | 15% | IRS Form 8288 |

The closing agent—typically the title company or real estate attorney in Florida—files IRS Form 8288 (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Dispositions by Foreign Persons) and Form 8288-A (Statement of Withholding) within 20 days of closing. Sellers seeking a reduced withholding must file Form 8288-B before closing, and the IRS may take 90 days or more to issue a withholding certificate, which creates serious timeline pressure on your deal.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

**The liability chain in a FIRPTA failure is punishingly clear: the buyer is on the hook first.** Under IRC Section 1445(a), if the transferee (buyer) fails to withhold, the IRS can assess the full 15 percent withholding amount against the buyer, plus interest and penalties under Sections 6651 and 6662. The buyer's agent who knew or should have known the seller was foreign and did nothing faces potential liability under Sections 1445(d) and associated Treasury Regulations.

For the listing agent, ignoring FIRPTA does not technically create direct tax liability—but it opens the door to professional negligence claims. Florida Statute Section 475.25(1)(b) authorizes the Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) to discipline licensees for fraud, misrepresentation, dishonest dealing, or culpable negligence. If an agent actively concealed the seller's foreign status or failed to disclose known withholding obligations, FREC can suspend or revoke the license.

Beyond regulatory exposure, a blown FIRPTA withholding can [kill the deal entirely](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing). Title companies that discover the omission post-closing may refuse to insure, and the buyer's lender may pull funding if the withholding was not addressed in the settlement statement. The cascading financial damage—lost commissions, earnest money disputes, potential lawsuits—dwarfs the effort of getting it right at intake.

## Common Mistakes Florida Agents Make With FIRPTA

The first error is assuming that a seller with a U.S. mailing address is automatically a U.S. person. Foreign nationals own Florida property through domestic LLCs, have U.S. bank accounts, and receive mail at their condo. None of that changes their FIRPTA status. The test is whether the individual beneficial owner meets the definition under IRC 1445(f)(3), not where their HOA dues get sent.

The second mistake is waiting until closing week to raise the question. Form 8288-B applications for reduced withholding take the IRS an average of 90 days to process. If the listing agent does not flag the seller's foreign status at listing—or at minimum during the contract phase—there is no runway left to obtain a withholding certificate. The seller then faces the full 15 percent withheld from proceeds with no recourse until they file a U.S. tax return.

**The third common error involves misapplying the residence-use exemption.** Agents sometimes coach buyers to sign a residence-use affidavit on an investment purchase to drop the rate to 10 percent or zero. This is fraud, and the IRS treats it accordingly. The buyer must genuinely intend to reside in the property for at least 50 percent of the days it is in use during each of the first two 12-month periods after closing.

The fourth mistake is conflating FIRPTA with HARPTA or other state withholding systems and assuming Florida has a parallel form. Florida imposes no state income tax and therefore no state withholding obligation on real property dispositions. Agents who previously worked in Hawaii, California, or Maryland sometimes apply those states' frameworks incorrectly here.

The fifth error is failing to document diligence. Even if the seller provides a non-foreign affidavit and the transaction closes without withholding, the agent and closing agent must retain that affidavit in the transaction file. If the IRS later determines the seller was in fact foreign, having the affidavit on file demonstrates the buyer relied on it in good faith under Treasury Regulation 1.1445-2(b)(2), which may relieve liability.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers supervising agents who list or sell properties involving foreign nationals carry a heightened oversight duty under Florida Administrative Code 61J2-14.012, which requires maintaining transaction records and ensuring compliance with applicable law. A broker who ignores systemic FIRPTA failures in their office risks FREC discipline for failing to supervise under Florida Statute Section 475.25(1)(u).

| Broker Audit Point | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Listing intake form | Does it ask about seller's citizenship/residency? | No foreign-status question at all |
| Non-foreign affidavit | Signed, dated, includes TIN | Missing TIN or unsigned |
| Form 8288-B timeline | Filed at least 90 days before projected close | Filed after contract execution with short close |
| Closing statement review | Withholding line item present when seller is foreign | No withholding on foreign-seller transaction |
| File retention | Affidavit or withholding certificate in file | No documentation of diligence |

Brokers should build a FIRPTA checkpoint into their [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) so that the compliance question is asked before marketing begins, not during closing coordination. This single addition prevents the majority of downstream problems.

Quarterly file audits should sample international-seller transactions specifically. If a brokerage operates in South Florida, the foreign-seller percentage may be 25 percent or more of listings. A random-sample audit of five to ten such files per quarter provides early warning on systemic gaps. Brokers who rely on agents to self-report problems will always find out too late.

## Timeline Management for FIRPTA Withholding

**Agents who miss FIRPTA deadlines do not get a mulligan—the IRS penalty clock starts running at close.** Form 8288 and Form 8288-A must be filed within 20 days of the date of transfer. Late filing triggers penalties under IRC Section 6651, which start at 5 percent of the unpaid withholding per month and cap at 25 percent.

If the seller wants to apply for a withholding certificate to reduce or eliminate the amount withheld—because, for example, the actual gain is far less than 15 percent of the gross proceeds—Form 8288-B must be submitted well before closing. The IRS processing window is 90 days but can stretch longer. Agents who do not raise this at contract stage effectively guarantee their seller will have 15 percent withheld with no reduction.

The practical workaround when a withholding certificate is pending at closing is to withhold the full amount into escrow, close the transaction, and release funds to the IRS only after the certificate is (or is not) issued. This keeps the deal on schedule while protecting the buyer. Coordinate this with the closing agent and document it in the contract addendum. Missing deadlines is [one of the most common reasons deals fall apart](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), and FIRPTA timelines are less forgiving than most.
