## Understanding Michigan Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

Every purchase agreement that moves through a Michigan brokerage touches escrow rules that carry real consequences when mishandled. Michigan earnest money & escrow laws 2026 have not undergone a statutory overhaul this session, but enforcement actions by the Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) have intensified—making compliance awareness more pressing than it was even two years ago. Agents who treat the earnest money deposit as a routine administrative step, rather than a regulated trust obligation, put their licenses and their brokers' reputations at risk.

The governing framework sits primarily in the Michigan Occupational Code, MCL 339.2501 through 339.2518, and the administrative rules under Rule 339.22301 through 339.22315. These statutes define who may hold escrow funds, how quickly funds must be deposited, and what disclosures must accompany each deposit. The Michigan Association of Realtors (MAR) purchase agreement forms—particularly the MAR Purchase Agreement (Form PAR)—build contractual timelines on top of these statutory requirements.

## Deposit Timing and the Two-Banking-Day Rule

**Michigan Administrative Rule 339.22305 mandates that a broker deposit earnest money into an escrow account within two banking days of the date the broker receives the funds.** This is not two calendar days—it is two days on which the bank is open for business. A check received on a Friday afternoon must reach the escrow account by the close of business Tuesday, assuming no bank holidays intervene.

The rule applies to the broker, not to the individual licensee. If a buyer hands a check to the showing agent at 7 p.m. on a Saturday, the clock starts when the broker's office actually receives the funds. Practically speaking, most brokerages require the agent to deliver the check the next business day to preserve the broker's deposit window.

| Scenario | Day Funds Received by Broker | Deposit Deadline |
|----------|------------------------------|-----------------|
| Check received Monday | Monday | Wednesday close of business |
| Check received Friday | Friday | Tuesday close of business |
| Check received Saturday (office closed) | Monday (next open day) | Wednesday close of business |
| Check received day before bank holiday | Day after holiday counts as Day 1 | Two banking days from that point |

Failure to meet the two-banking-day rule can trigger a complaint to LARA's Bureau of Professional Licensing. Repeated violations have resulted in formal administrative actions, including fines ranging from $500 to $5,000 per occurrence and license suspension.

## Escrow Account Requirements Under Michigan Law

Michigan brokers must maintain a non-interest-bearing trust or escrow account at a federally insured financial institution located in the state unless all parties to a transaction provide written consent for interest-bearing arrangements. MCL 339.2512(1) specifically prohibits commingling escrow funds with the broker's operating funds. Even a temporary shortfall caused by bank fees charged to the escrow account can constitute a technical violation.

The account must be titled in the brokerage's licensed name, and the word "escrow" or "trust" must appear in the account designation. LARA auditors verify this during routine and complaint-driven inspections. Brokers who operate under a DBA must ensure the DBA matches the name on file with the Bureau of Professional Licensing.

A broker may hold escrow funds on behalf of multiple transactions in a single pooled account, but the ledger must track each deposit separately. Monthly reconciliation is not just best practice—it is the standard LARA expects when reviewing records during an audit. ## Disbursement Rules and Dispute Resolution

**Disbursement of earnest money in Michigan follows the terms of the purchase agreement, not the verbal direction of one party.** Under Rule 339.22313, a broker may not release escrow funds unless all parties to the transaction provide written authorization, the terms of the contract explicitly permit disbursement upon a specific event, or a court order compels it.

When a deal falls apart and both sides claim the deposit, the broker faces a statutory obligation to hold the funds pending mutual written agreement or a court order. Michigan does not have a statutory interpleader mandate like some states, but most brokers file an interpleader action in circuit court when disputes exceed 30 days without resolution. The MAR Purchase Agreement includes a liquidated damages clause (Section 15 of Form PAR) that, when initialed by both parties, can simplify distribution—but only if the termination reason aligns with the clause's conditions.

Agents who ignore these rules and release funds based on a phone call or a single party's email expose themselves and their broker to civil liability. A wrongful disbursement claim can result in the broker being held personally liable for the full deposit amount, plus the complaining party's attorney fees if the court finds the release was unauthorized.

## The Forms That Matter: MAR Purchase Agreement and Addenda

Michigan agents working under the MAR system rely on a specific set of forms to document earnest money terms. The MAR Purchase Agreement (Form PAR) addresses deposit amount, holder identity, and the timeline for delivery in Sections 3 and 4. The Seller's Disclosure Statement (Form SDS) does not directly govern escrow, but incomplete disclosures can trigger buyer rescission rights that immediately implicate the deposit's return.

| Form | Purpose | Escrow Relevance |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| MAR Form PAR | Purchase agreement | Sections 3-4: deposit amount, holder, delivery timeline |
| MAR Form AMD | Amendment to purchase agreement | Used to modify deposit terms after execution |
| MAR Form REL | Release of earnest money | Mutual authorization for disbursement |
| MAR Form ADR | Dispute resolution addendum | Outlines mediation/arbitration before interpleader |

When agents skip the Release of Earnest Money (Form REL) and instead rely on informal email confirmations, they create an audit trail that fails the regulatory standard. LARA's administrative law judges have consistently held that written release authorization must be on a form or document that clearly identifies the transaction, the amount, and both parties' signatures. ## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Earnest Money in Michigan

Three to five errors account for the majority of LARA complaints related to escrow handling. Each one is avoidable with basic process discipline.

**First, agents hold checks in their car or desk instead of delivering them to the broker immediately.** The two-banking-day clock starts at broker receipt, but LARA has investigated agents who delayed delivery by a week or more, effectively circumventing the rule. Some agents do this intentionally to give buyers time to "get their finances together," which is both a regulatory violation and a material misrepresentation to the seller about the deposit's security.

Second, agents verbally agree to return earnest money to a buyer without obtaining the seller's written consent. Even when a buyer clearly exercised an inspection contingency within the contractual window, the Form REL should still be executed before the broker cuts a check. Skipping this step exposes the broker to a claim from the seller alleging premature disbursement.

Third, agents fail to note the correct escrow holder on the purchase agreement. Section 3 of Form PAR asks who will hold the deposit. If the listing agent writes "ABC Title Company" but the check goes to the brokerage, the contract terms and actual handling diverge—creating a compliance gap that can void the deposit's enforceability in a dispute.

Fourth, agents accept earnest money in cash without issuing an immediate written receipt. MCL 339.2512 and Rule 339.22307 require that a receipt be provided at the time cash is accepted. Without it, the agent has no defensible record if the buyer later disputes the amount.

Fifth, agents attempt to negotiate a larger earnest money deposit after mutual acceptance without executing a formal amendment (Form AMD). Verbal or text-message modifications to deposit amounts are unenforceable and create inconsistencies between the contract file and the escrow ledger.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Broker liability in Michigan is not derivative—it is direct. MCL 339.2512 places the escrow obligation on the broker, not the individual licensee. A broker who fails to supervise escrow handling can face license suspension even if the offending agent acted without the broker's knowledge.

**Monthly reconciliation of all escrow accounts is the single most important audit task a Michigan broker performs.** LARA's [Bureau of Professional Licensing](https://www.michigan.gov/lara/bureau-list/bpl) expects brokers to maintain contemporaneous ledgers showing the date received, amount, parties' names, and disposition of every deposit. During an audit, investigators compare bank statements against the ledger line by line.

Brokers should also enforce a same-day or next-business-day check delivery policy for all agents in the office. A written office policy, signed by each licensee annually, creates an affirmative defense if a single agent violates the rule. Without that policy on file, LARA may attribute the agent's delay to a systemic supervisory failure.

Finally, brokers need to verify that every closed or terminated transaction file contains either a Form REL signed by both parties or documentation of a court order authorizing disbursement. An incomplete file is treated the same as a missing file during a LARA audit. ## Penalties and Enforcement: What Is Actually at Stake

LARA's enforcement actions for escrow violations fall into three tiers. Administrative sanctions include formal reprimands, mandatory continuing education, and probationary license conditions. Financial penalties range from $500 per violation for minor timing infractions to $10,000 or more for misappropriation or commingling. License revocation is reserved for willful conversion of escrow funds, which also triggers referral to the Michigan Attorney General's office for potential criminal prosecution under MCL 750.174 (embezzlement).

Civil exposure runs parallel to regulatory action. A buyer or seller who suffers financial harm from improper escrow handling can sue the agent, the broker, and potentially the brokerage's errors and omissions carrier. Michigan courts have awarded consequential damages in cases where a delayed or wrongful disbursement caused a buyer to lose a replacement property or forced a seller into foreclosure.

The reputational cost is harder to quantify but equally real. LARA publishes disciplinary actions in a searchable public database. A single escrow violation on a broker's record can deter agents from affiliating and buyers from trusting.
