## California Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026: The Statutory and Practical Framework

The question of California who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 trips up agents far more often than it should. Unlike states that codify a rigid split, California leaves most cost allocation to negotiation, local custom, and whatever the parties memorialize in the California Association of Realtors (CAR) Residential Purchase Agreement (Form RPA). That flexibility is a feature for clients, but it creates real compliance risk for agents who leave blanks, rely on verbal understandings, or assume escrow will "figure it out."

This post breaks down the statutory defaults, the specific CAR forms that govern cost allocation, the liability exposure agents face when they get it wrong, and what brokers should be auditing on every file.

## What California Law Actually Says About Closing Costs

California does not have a single statute that assigns all closing costs to one party. Instead, the obligations are scattered across the Civil Code, the Revenue and Taxation Code, and the Insurance Code. The result is a patchwork of defaults that local custom often overrides.

**The documentary transfer tax under Revenue and Taxation Code Section 11911 is the most commonly cited "seller cost," but the statute itself only imposes the tax — it does not specify who pays it.** Custom in most California counties assigns it to the seller. Agents who tell buyers "the seller always pays transfer tax" are stating a custom, not a legal requirement.

Title insurance is governed by California Insurance Code Section 12340 et seq. The seller customarily pays for the owner's policy (CLTA standard coverage), while the buyer pays for the lender's policy (ALTA). Again, these are customs, not mandates. The [California Department of Real Estate](https://www.dre.ca.gov/) does not enforce any particular split; it enforces that agents disclose material terms accurately.

Escrow fees follow county-level customs. South of the Tehachapi line, a 50/50 split is standard. North of it, the buyer typically pays. None of this is statutory, and all of it is subject to what the parties agree to in writing.

## The Forms That Control Cost Allocation

Every cost allocation conversation ultimately lands on the CAR Residential Purchase Agreement (Form RPA, revised 2024). Paragraph 7 of the RPA addresses closing costs explicitly, including who pays escrow fees, title fees, transfer taxes, HOA document fees, and natural hazard zone reports. If an agent leaves these fields incomplete or checks conflicting boxes, the ambiguity becomes a liability mine.

| Cost Category | Typical Buyer Responsibility | Typical Seller Responsibility |
|---|---|---|
| Escrow fee | 50% (SoCal) or 100% (NorCal) | 50% (SoCal) or 0% (NorCal) |
| Owner's title policy (CLTA) | Rarely | Almost always |
| Lender's title policy (ALTA) | Almost always | Rarely |
| Documentary transfer tax | Rarely | Almost always by custom |
| HOA document/transfer fees | Split varies | Often pays Civ. Code 4525 docs |
| County recording fees | Deed of trust recording | Grant deed recording |
| Natural hazard disclosure report | Negotiable | Often pays per RPA default |

CAR Form RLAN (Seller's Request for Repair/Credit) and Form CR (Seller's Response to Request for Repair) handle post-inspection credit negotiations that directly affect net closing costs. Agents who negotiate seller credits verbally and fail to attach a signed CR or RLAN addendum are violating the Statute of Frauds (Civil Code Section 1624).

Form SSA (Statewide Seller Advisory) and the Estimated Seller Net Sheet prepared by the listing agent are also critical. While the net sheet is not a CAR form, DRE Commissioner's Regulation 2785 requires agents to present material facts — and an inaccurate net sheet that omits a cost line item can constitute a material misrepresentation.

## What Happens When Agents Get It Wrong

**Agents who misallocate or fail to document closing cost responsibility face three distinct categories of risk: deal cancellation, civil liability, and DRE disciplinary action.**

Under the RPA's contingency framework, ambiguity in cost allocation can give either party grounds to cancel. If the buyer discovers at the closing table that they owe an unexpected charge — say a $4,200 city transfer tax in Los Angeles under L.A. Municipal Code Section 21.9.3 that the agent never disclosed — the buyer's remedy may include cancellation and return of the deposit under Paragraph 14 of the RPA.

Civil liability attaches under Business and Professions Code Section 10176(i), which identifies "any other conduct which constitutes fraud or dishonest dealing" as grounds for license discipline. A negligence claim does not require intent; simply failing to advise a seller that San Francisco's transfer tax rate jumps to 2.25% on properties over $10 million (under SF Business and Tax Regulations Code Article 12-C) can support a malpractice suit.

DRE disciplinary consequences range from a restricted license to outright revocation. The DRE can also impose fines and require additional education. Brokers become jointly liable under Business and Professions Code Section 10177(h) if they failed to supervise.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Closing Cost Allocation

**Three to five errors show up repeatedly in arbitration cases and DRE complaints.** Agents who eliminate these from their practice dramatically reduce their exposure.

The first mistake is assuming local custom equals legal obligation. An agent in San Diego who tells the seller "you have to pay the owner's title policy" is misstating the law. Custom is the starting point for negotiation, not a mandate. When the seller pushes back and the buyer's agent has already told their client it is guaranteed, the deal fractures.

The second mistake is leaving Paragraph 7 of the RPA partially blank. Escrow officers will call both sides for clarification, but if the parties disagree about who was supposed to pay a particular fee, the contract controls. A blank field means no enforceable allocation, which hands leverage to whichever side is more willing to walk away.

The third mistake is failing to account for city-level transfer taxes on top of county transfer taxes. Los Angeles, San Francisco, Oakland, Berkeley, Richmond, Culver City, and several other municipalities impose their own documentary transfer taxes at rates far exceeding the standard county rate of $1.10 per $1,000. Agents who only disclose the county rate and omit the city rate on the seller's net sheet face negligence claims routinely. This is a critical point in markets [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) over unexpected cost surprises.

The fourth mistake is conflating seller concessions with seller-paid closing costs on the lender's side. A seller credit toward the buyer's closing costs must comply with the buyer's loan program limits (often 3% for conventional loans with less than 10% down). Agents who negotiate a 6% seller credit without verifying the lender's cap create a contract modification problem days before closing.

The fifth mistake is relying on escrow to "fix" allocation issues. Escrow is a neutral stakeholder. The escrow officer follows the contract. If the contract is ambiguous, the escrow officer will demand written instructions from both sides — adding days or weeks to closing.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Business and Professions Code Section 10177(h) and DRE Regulation 2725. A broker who rubber-stamps files without reviewing cost allocation exposes the entire brokerage to vicarious liability.

| Audit Checkpoint | What to Verify | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| RPA Paragraph 7 | All cost allocation boxes completed | Blank or contradictory fields |
| Seller Net Sheet | Matches RPA cost allocation; includes city transfer tax | Missing line items or rounded estimates |
| CR/RLAN Addenda | Signed by all parties before contingency removal | Unsigned or missing after inspection period |
| Loan Program Limits | Seller credit does not exceed lender cap | Credit exceeds 3%/6%/9% without documentation |
| Local Transfer Tax Disclosure | City-specific rate disclosed on net sheet | Only county rate shown in a city with supplemental tax |

Brokers should implement a checklist that flags these items before the contingency removal deadline — not at closing. If your brokerage handles volume and [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) creates blind spots, automated compliance auditing becomes essential rather than optional.

Quarterly file audits should pull a random sample of closed and cancelled transactions, specifically reviewing whether Paragraph 7 was fully completed and whether the seller net sheet matched the final closing statement. Discrepancies between the two documents suggest the agent made promises the contract did not support.

## Negotiation Leverage: How Market Conditions Shift the Split in 2026

California's 2026 market reflects sustained inventory constraints in coastal metros and softer conditions in inland markets. Listing agents in low-inventory areas hold leverage to push more costs to buyers. Buyer's agents in high-inventory areas — parts of the Inland Empire, Sacramento suburbs, and Central Valley — can negotiate seller-paid closing costs more aggressively.

The post-NAR settlement era has introduced buyer broker compensation into the cost allocation conversation. If a buyer is paying their own agent's fee, that expense competes with closing costs for the same pool of buyer cash. Agents who [understand why deadlines get missed](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) during these multi-variable negotiations can structure timelines that prevent last-minute cost disputes from blowing up the transaction.

Sophisticated agents are using the RPA's Paragraph 7 to create clear, enforceable allocations at contract formation rather than deferring hard conversations to the inspection response phase. Deferral creates risk; specificity at the outset creates binding obligations that survive escrow.
