## Understanding Virginia HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026

Virginia's resale disclosure requirements for properties governed by homeowners associations and condominium regimes rank among the most prescriptive in the Mid-Atlantic. The Virginia hoa & condo disclosure rules 2026 framework operates under two separate but structurally similar statutes, and getting them confused is one of the fastest ways to hand a buyer an indefinite cancellation right. Agents who treat the disclosure packet as a box-checking exercise rather than a legal delivery with specific timing consequences are exposing their clients and their own licenses to measurable risk.

The governing statutes split along property type lines. Properties within a property owners' association fall under the Virginia Property Owners' Association Act, codified at Va. Code sections 55.1-1800 through 55.1-1835. Condominium units fall under the Virginia Condominium Act at Va. Code sections 55.1-1900 through 55.1-2000. Both statutes mandate that sellers deliver a resale disclosure packet to buyers before or at ratification, and both grant buyers a statutory cancellation window after receipt.

## The Two Statutes and What They Require

**The Virginia Property Owners' Association Act (POAA) and the Virginia Condominium Act impose parallel but distinct obligations that agents must not conflate.** The POAA applies to single-family homes, townhomes, and lots within subdivisions governed by a recorded declaration of covenants. The Condominium Act applies exclusively to units created under a condominium declaration.

Under Va. Code 55.1-1809, the seller must deliver a resale disclosure packet that includes the association's governing documents, current budget, reserve study disclosures, outstanding special assessments, and any pending litigation. The condominium counterpart at Va. Code 55.1-1990 requires a substantially similar package but adds condo-specific items such as the percentage of units owned by the declarant and the ratio of owner-occupied versus rented units.

Both packets carry a maximum cost cap that the association may charge for preparation. As of 2026, the statutory cap under both acts is $150 for the base packet, with an additional $50 permitted for rush delivery and $50 for electronic updates. These caps are set by statute and cannot be circumvented by management companies adding "administrative" line items above the ceiling.

## Delivery Timing, Cancellation Rights, and the 3-Day Clock

The delivery timeline is where transactions most frequently go sideways. Under both statutes, the buyer receives a 3-day right of cancellation that begins only upon actual receipt of the completed packet. The cancellation period is measured in calendar days, and the count does not include the day of delivery.

| Element | POA Act (55.1-1814) | Condo Act (55.1-1990) |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Cancellation period | 3 calendar days after receipt | 3 calendar days after receipt |
| Maximum base packet fee | $150 | $150 |
| Rush fee allowed | $50 | $50 |
| Who orders | Seller (unless contract states otherwise) | Seller (unless contract states otherwise) |
| If never delivered | Buyer may cancel anytime before settlement | Buyer may cancel anytime before settlement |

**If the packet is never delivered, the buyer's cancellation right never expires.** This means a buyer sitting at the closing table who has not received the packet can still void the deal, demand return of their earnest money, and walk away without legal consequence. The listing agent who failed to ensure delivery has just cost their seller a transaction and potentially triggered a malpractice claim.

Virginia courts have interpreted "receipt" strictly. Email delivery counts only if the buyer or buyer's agent has affirmatively consented to electronic delivery. A packet uploaded to a transaction management portal that the buyer never accessed does not constitute delivery under the statute.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With HOA and Condo Disclosures

Experienced agents get tripped up by these disclosures more often than you would expect. The errors tend to cluster around five recurring failures that show up in DPOR complaint files and E&O claims.

First, agents frequently order the wrong packet type. A property within both a master POA and a sub-association condominium regime requires disclosures under both statutes. Ordering only the condo packet leaves the POA disclosure undelivered, and the buyer retains an open cancellation right on that missing document. Second, listing agents assume the management company will deliver the packet directly to the buyer's agent. The statute places the delivery obligation on the seller, not the management company. If the packet sits in a portal that nobody downloads, delivery has not occurred.

Third, agents fail to document the date of actual receipt. Without a signed acknowledgment or timestamped email confirmation from the buyer, proving that the 3-day clock started becomes nearly impossible in a dispute. Fourth, agents treat the packet as informational rather than as a contract contingency with teeth. They file it away without reviewing it for red flags like underfunded reserves, special assessments in the pipeline, or litigation that could affect value. Fifth, buyer's agents neglect to advise their clients that the 3-day window is a hard deadline. A buyer who discovers a $15,000 pending special assessment on day four has already lost the statutory right to cancel on those grounds.

These errors connect directly to the broader issue of [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) in transaction workflows. The disclosure packet is not a passive document. It is a ticking clock that requires active management from the moment it arrives.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Va. Code 54.1-2130 through the Virginia Real Estate Board's regulations. When an agent under their license fails to ensure proper HOA or condo disclosure delivery, the broker's compliance exposure is direct, not theoretical.

**Every brokerage operating in Virginia should audit three specific elements in every transaction involving an association-governed property.** First, confirm that the correct disclosure packet type was ordered. The transaction file should contain documentation showing whether the property falls under the POAA, the Condominium Act, or both. Second, verify that a delivery receipt exists with a specific date. This can be a signed acknowledgment page, a timestamped email from the buyer confirming download, or a delivery confirmation from the management company sent to the buyer's verified address. Third, confirm that the 3-day cancellation window expired before the transaction moved to the next contingency milestone.

Brokers should also verify that packet costs charged to the seller do not exceed the statutory caps. Management companies occasionally bundle services and charge $300 or more for a "premium" packet. If those charges exceed the ceiling set by Va. Code 55.1-1810 or 55.1-1991, the overage is unenforceable and the seller can demand a refund. Agents who pass along inflated invoices without questioning them are failing their fiduciary duty.

The Virginia Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR) has issued guidance emphasizing that brokers must maintain [transaction files that demonstrate compliance](/blog/virginia-property-condition-disclosures-2026) with disclosure statutes. A missing delivery receipt is not merely sloppy recordkeeping. It is an audit finding that can trigger a board investigation.

## Liability Consequences for Non-Compliance

The penalty structure for failing to deliver HOA or condo disclosures operates on multiple levels simultaneously. At the transaction level, the buyer can cancel the contract and recover their earnest money deposit without showing cause. This is a bright-line statutory right that requires no proof of harm, reliance, or bad faith.

At the licensure level, DPOR can pursue disciplinary action under the regulations governing the Virginia Real Estate Board. Failure to handle required documents constitutes a violation of the standard of practice, and the Board has authority to impose fines, require additional education, place a license on probation, or suspend the license outright. Repeated violations or those involving demonstrated harm to consumers carry escalated penalties.

| Consequence | Who Bears It | Trigger |
|-------------|-------------|---------|
| Contract cancellation | Seller | Packet never delivered |
| Earnest money refund | Seller | Buyer exercises cancellation right |
| DPOR disciplinary action | Agent and/or broker | Failure to deliver or document |
| E&O claim | Agent and brokerage | Buyer or seller suffers financial loss |
| Civil damages | Agent | Negligent misrepresentation or omission |

At the civil liability level, an agent who knew or should have known about a pending special assessment disclosed in the packet and failed to bring it to the buyer's attention could face a negligent misrepresentation claim. Virginia courts have held that agents owe a duty to review material documents, not merely transmit them.

Understanding these risks fits into the broader picture of [Virginia's agency disclosure requirements](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026), where the duty to inform and protect client interests is codified rather than aspirational.

## Virginia HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026: Practical Workflow for Agents

The most reliable approach is to treat the HOA or condo disclosure packet as a critical-path item with the same urgency as the home inspection contingency. On the listing side, order the packet within 24 hours of going active. Do not wait until ratification. Management companies routinely take 10-14 business days to prepare a packet, and a delayed order can push delivery past the buyer's due diligence window.

On the buyer side, confirm receipt in writing on the same day the packet arrives. Review the reserve study summary, check for special assessment announcements, and verify insurance coverage levels. If the reserve fund is below 50 percent funded, flag it for the buyer in writing. If litigation is pending against the association, recommend the buyer consult an attorney before the 3-day window closes.
