## The Statute That Controls Everything

Texas seller disclosure requirements 2026 rest almost entirely on one piece of law: Section 5.008 of the Texas Property Code. This statute mandates that sellers of residential property deliver a written disclosure to prospective buyers before they execute a binding purchase contract. Agents who treat this as a formality rather than a legal obligation are exposing themselves, their brokers, and their clients to serious liability.

The disclosure obligation is not a suggestion embedded in a trade association form. It is a statutory duty backed by specific remedies, including contract termination and damages. The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) has repeatedly emphasized through its enforcement actions that disclosure failures rank among the most common grounds for disciplinary proceedings against licensees.

## The Specific Forms Agents Must Use

**TREC promulgates the Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition form (TREC OP-H), which is the standard instrument for residential transactions in Texas.** This form covers structural components, roof condition, plumbing, environmental hazards, flooding history, and a range of other material facts. Agents should verify they are using the most current version published by TREC, as revisions occur periodically and outdated forms create compliance gaps.

The Texas Association of Realtors (TAR) also publishes its own version—TAR Form 1406—which many brokerages prefer because it includes additional prompts for sellers. Either form satisfies the statutory requirement, but agents must ensure the form used aligns with their brokerage's approved document list. Using a form that omits required categories of disclosure does not relieve the seller of the duty to disclose those items.

Beyond the standard property condition form, agents handling properties within certain jurisdictions may encounter supplemental disclosure requirements. Homes in Municipal Utility Districts require delivery of a separate MUD notice under Section 49.452 of the Texas Water Code. Properties in floodplains trigger additional disclosure duties under Section 5.014 of the Property Code, which were strengthened after Hurricane Harvey and subsequent legislative sessions.

## Exemptions: Who Does Not Have to Disclose

Not every seller in Texas must deliver the Section 5.008 disclosure. The statute carves out specific exemptions, and agents routinely misidentify which transactions qualify. Exempted transfers include foreclosure sales, transfers by fiduciaries in the administration of an estate, transfers resulting from court orders, and transfers by co-owners to other co-owners.

Relocation companies acting as sellers after acquiring the property from an employee are also exempt under certain conditions. However, agents should not assume an exemption applies without verifying the facts against the statutory language. A seller who incorrectly claims an exemption still faces liability if the buyer can demonstrate material nondisclosure.

One exemption that generates persistent confusion involves new construction. Builders selling homes they constructed are exempt from the standard Section 5.008 form, but they are subject to separate warranty obligations under Chapter 430 of the Texas Property Code and applicable building codes. The exemption does not mean "no disclosure at all"—it means a different disclosure framework applies.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**The consequences of disclosure failures in Texas extend well beyond a tense phone call from opposing counsel.** Under Section 5.008(c), a buyer who does not receive the disclosure before executing the contract may terminate the contract for any reason within seven days of receiving it. If the disclosure is never delivered, the right to terminate arguably persists indefinitely until closing.

For agents personally, TREC can initiate disciplinary action under Section 1101.652 of the Texas Occupations Code, which covers misrepresentation, failure to disclose material facts, and negligence. Sanctions range from reprimands and mandatory continuing education to suspension and revocation of the license. Monetary penalties imposed by TREC can reach $5,000 per violation per day under certain circumstances.

Civil liability adds another layer. Buyers who discover undisclosed defects post-closing routinely name the listing agent in fraud, negligent misrepresentation, and DTPA (Deceptive Trade Practices Act) claims. Texas courts have consistently held that agents who know of defects and fail to ensure proper disclosure can be held personally liable, separate from the seller's liability. The [Texas Real Estate Commission's enforcement page](https://www.trec.texas.gov/public/complaint-process) outlines how complaints are filed and investigated.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Disclosures

The first mistake is treating the disclosure as the seller's problem alone. Texas law places the disclosure duty on the seller, but TREC's Standards of Practice make clear that agents have an independent duty to disclose material facts they know or should know. An agent who walks a property, observes water staining on a ceiling, and says nothing because the seller checked "no" on the water damage question is personally at risk.

The second mistake is delivering the disclosure after contract execution and assuming the deal is safe. Section 5.008 contemplates delivery before the contract is signed. When the disclosure arrives late, the buyer gains a statutory termination window that can torpedo a transaction at the worst possible moment. Agents who routinely allow late delivery are building fragile deals.

**The third mistake is allowing sellers to leave questions blank or write "unknown" across the board without follow-up.** While sellers are only required to disclose facts within their knowledge, a disclosure riddled with blanks invites buyer suspicion, extended due diligence demands, and post-closing claims that the seller was coached to withhold information. Agents should have a direct conversation with sellers about the purpose and importance of each section.

The fourth mistake involves failing to update the disclosure when conditions change. If a pipe bursts during the listing period, the disclosure must be amended. Texas courts have found that sellers—and by extension their agents—cannot rely on a stale disclosure that was accurate when signed but materially misleading by the time a buyer acts on it.

The fifth mistake is neglecting supplemental disclosures entirely. Agents listing properties in homeowners associations must deliver a separate [HOA-related disclosure and resale certificate](/blog/texas-hoa-resale-certificate-requirements). Properties with known mold, lead-based paint (for pre-1978 homes under federal law), or prior methamphetamine contamination each carry their own disclosure obligations that the standard form does not fully address.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability for their agents' disclosure failures under Texas agency law. A supervising broker who lacks a system for tracking disclosure delivery is accepting risk that could cascade into errors and omissions claims, TREC complaints, and reputational damage across the brokerage.

At minimum, brokers should audit every active listing file for the presence of a signed, dated seller disclosure that matches the property's actual conditions. File reviews should confirm the disclosure was delivered to the buyer's agent before contract execution, with a signed acknowledgment of receipt. Any file lacking these elements should be flagged immediately for follow-up.

Brokers should also enforce a policy requiring agents to document conversations with sellers about disclosure content. A simple file note stating that the agent discussed specific items—roof age, prior flooding, foundation repairs—provides a defense if a buyer later claims the agent was complicit in concealment. Training on this documentation standard should occur at least annually, tied to [your brokerage's compliance calendar](/blog/texas-brokerage-compliance-calendar).

Policy manuals should address how agents handle the "reluctant seller"—the client who resists completing the form or pressures the agent to minimize known defects. Brokers who do not train for this scenario will eventually face it in a TREC hearing. The correct response is always documented refusal to participate in nondisclosure, even if it means losing the listing.

## The 2026 Regulatory Environment

Texas legislators have shown increasing interest in expanding disclosure obligations since the 2019 and 2021 sessions strengthened flood-related requirements. Agents should monitor proposed bills each session that could broaden what must be disclosed—energy efficiency features, prior insurance claims history, and broadband availability have all surfaced in recent legislative discussions.

TREC's strategic plan includes enhanced enforcement resources and faster complaint resolution timelines. The commission has publicly stated its intent to pursue proactive education alongside enforcement, but agents should not mistake outreach for leniency. Complaint volume against licensees involving disclosure issues has trended upward in every published enforcement report since 2020.

The interplay between disclosure duties and MLS accuracy also deserves attention. Listing data that contradicts the seller's disclosure—such as advertising "no foundation issues" when the disclosure notes prior repair—creates an independent basis for misrepresentation claims. Agents must reconcile their marketing with the disclosure form before listing a property.

They ask follow-up questions, document the answers, and ensure delivery timing meets statutory thresholds. They do not rely on memory or verbal confirmations.
