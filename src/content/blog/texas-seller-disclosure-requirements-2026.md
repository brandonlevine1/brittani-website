## The Legal Foundation Behind Texas Seller Disclosure Requirements 2026

Texas seller disclosure requirements 2026 remain governed by Section 5.008 of the Texas Property Code, one of the most prescriptive disclosure statutes in the country. The law mandates that sellers of residential property provide written notice to buyers of any known material defects or conditions before closing. Failing to meet this obligation exposes sellers—and potentially their listing agents—to post-closing litigation, rescission claims, and disciplinary action from the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC).

The statute applies to most residential transactions involving one-to-four-unit dwellings. It requires that the disclosure be delivered to the purchaser on or before the effective date of a binding contract, though buyers may terminate within a defined period if the disclosure arrives late. Agents who treat this requirement as a mere formality misread the statute's teeth.

**What separates Texas from many other states is the specificity of its mandated disclosure form.** TREC publishes the Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition form (TREC No. OP-H), which has been updated periodically to reflect new environmental and structural concerns. Agents must use the current version and cannot substitute their own forms or rely on outdated editions from prior years.

## Which Transactions Are Exempt—and Which Are Not

Section 5.008(e) of the Texas Property Code lists specific exemptions from the disclosure requirement. These include transfers pursuant to court order, transfers by a fiduciary in the administration of a decedent's estate, and transfers from one co-owner to another. Foreclosure sales and transfers by a mortgagee who acquired the property through foreclosure are also exempt.

Here is where agents frequently make errors: the exemption for new construction only applies when the seller is the builder and the property has never been occupied. A resale of a two-year-old home by the original buyer does not qualify, even if the builder's warranties are still active. Agents must confirm exemption eligibility on a transaction-by-transaction basis.

Additionally, transfers to a spouse or to a person in the lineal line of consanguinity are exempt. But a transfer to a sibling is not exempt, nor is a sale to a trust beneficiary who is not a direct ascendant or descendant. These distinctions trip up agents who rely on general assumptions rather than reading the statute's precise language.

## What the TREC Seller's Disclosure Form Actually Requires

The TREC OP-H form covers structural systems, roofing, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and environmental hazards. It asks sellers to disclose known conditions related to flooding, foundation movement, termite damage, and the presence of hazardous materials such as asbestos, lead-based paint, or urea-formaldehyde insulation. Sellers must answer affirmatively, negatively, or indicate they are unaware.

**The form also requires disclosure of conditions that might not involve physical defects.** Previous fire damage, deaths on the property under certain circumstances, and the presence of a property owners' association with mandatory fees all require affirmative disclosure. Proximity to a military installation that produces noise or vibration must also be noted under current TREC standards.

One area that generates recurring confusion is the relationship between the TREC OP-H form and federal lead-based paint disclosure obligations under 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d. The federal requirement applies to homes built before 1978 and operates independently of state law. Agents must ensure both disclosures are delivered when applicable—one does not substitute for the other.

## Liability When Agents Fail to Ensure Compliance

The listing agent's role is not to complete the seller's disclosure form. That responsibility belongs to the seller. However, TREC's rules of professional conduct—found in Title 22 of the Texas Administrative Code, Chapter 531—impose a duty on licensees to act with honesty and integrity, and to inform clients of their statutory obligations.

If a listing agent knows a seller has failed to deliver the required disclosure and allows the transaction to proceed to closing, the agent faces potential disciplinary action from TREC. Sanctions can include reprimand, mandatory education, suspension, or revocation of license. The commission has broad discretion under Section 1101.652 of the Texas Occupations Code to impose penalties for dishonest dealing or negligence.

> "A license holder shall not be a party to the naming of a false consideration in any document, unless it is the naming of an obviously nominal consideration." — 22 TAC Section 531.1

Beyond regulatory consequences, agents and their brokerages face civil liability. A buyer who discovers undisclosed defects post-closing can pursue claims for fraud, negligent misrepresentation, or violations of the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act. Damages under the DTPA can include treble damages and attorneys' fees, making these claims expensive even when the underlying defect is modest.

## Common Mistakes Texas Agents Make on Seller Disclosures

The first mistake is procrastination. Agents wait until after the contract is fully executed to request the seller's disclosure, creating a scenario where the buyer receives it late and gains an additional termination right under the contract. The [TREC One to Four Family Residential Contract](/blog/trec-contract-forms-guide) specifically addresses the timing of disclosure delivery, and late delivery can destabilize otherwise solid deals.

The second mistake is accepting incomplete forms. When a seller checks "unknown" on every line, the agent should probe further. A seller who has lived in a property for fifteen years and claims to know nothing about its condition raises credibility concerns that could expose the agent to liability if a court later finds the responses implausible.

**The third mistake is conflating the seller's disclosure with an inspection report.** The disclosure captures the seller's subjective knowledge. A clean inspection report does not cure a seller's duty to disclose what they know. If the seller is aware of a recurring plumbing leak that was temporarily repaired before listing, that condition must be disclosed regardless of what an inspector finds or misses.

The fourth mistake involves exemptions. Agents assume that because a transaction involves a trust, the disclosure is automatically waived. As noted above, the exemption applies only to fiduciaries administering a decedent's estate or guardianship—not to every trust-based transaction. Agents must verify the specific statutory basis for any claimed exemption.

The fifth mistake is failing to retain copies. TREC requires that licensees maintain transaction records for four years from the date of closing or termination. If an agent cannot produce the signed disclosure form during a TREC audit, they face discipline regardless of whether it was originally delivered.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Section 1101.803 of the Texas Occupations Code. This means that a broker who fails to reasonably supervise sponsored agents can be held liable for the agent's noncompliance—even if the broker had no direct involvement in the transaction.

A responsible broker should audit for three things at minimum: that the correct version of the TREC OP-H form is being used across all listings, that signed disclosures appear in the file before or concurrent with contract execution, and that the firm's agents are not advising sellers on how to answer specific questions on the form. That last point is a bright line. Agents can explain what the form asks; they cannot coach answers.

Brokerages should also establish a written policy on disclosure procedures and include it in their policy manual. TREC expects brokers to demonstrate that they have [systems in place for file review and compliance](/blog/transaction-compliance-checklist). Annual training refreshers—particularly when TREC updates its forms—reduce risk across the organization.

## Environmental and Flood-Related Disclosures Gaining Attention

Texas has seen increased litigation over flood-related nondisclosure claims following major events in the Gulf Coast region. The TREC OP-H form asks whether the property is located in a 100-year floodplain and whether the seller has filed a claim for flood damage. Given FEMA's ongoing map updates, agents should advise sellers to verify current floodplain status before completing the form.

Senate Bill 339, passed during the 86th Texas Legislature, added disclosure requirements related to whether a property is in a reservoir area. That provision now appears on the TREC form and requires sellers to indicate whether the property has been identified as being in a flood pool or reservoir area. Agents in counties with Army Corps of Engineers dam projects should pay particular attention to this line item.

For a detailed breakdown of how environmental risk zones affect your listings, see [this guide on flood disclosure obligations](/blog/flood-disclosure-texas-agents). TREC's [official forms page](https://www.trec.texas.gov/forms/seller%E2%80%99s-disclosure-notice) also maintains the most current version of the OP-H form and any associated addenda.

## Building Disclosure Compliance Into Your Workflow

The most effective approach is making disclosure delivery a mandatory step in your listing workflow rather than a reactive task triggered by contract execution. Top-producing agents in Texas request the completed seller's disclosure at the listing appointment, before the property hits the MLS. This eliminates timing disputes and gives the agent an opportunity to flag potential issues early.

Automating reminders and document tracking reduces the risk of human error in high-volume practices.
