# Texas Seller Disclosure Requirements 2026: What Every Agent and Broker Must Know

Getting disclosure wrong in Texas does not just kill deals—it invites lawsuits, disciplinary action, and E&O claims that follow agents for years. Texas seller disclosure requirements 2026 remain governed by Texas Property Code Section 5.008, which mandates that sellers of residential real property provide a written disclosure of known material conditions to prospective buyers before closing. The statute has not changed dramatically in its core obligations, but the enforcement environment and buyer expectations have both intensified heading into this year.

This post breaks down the specific statutory framework, the exact forms agents should be using, the liability exposure for noncompliance, and the operational steps brokers need to take to protect their offices.

## The Statutory Foundation: Texas Property Code Section 5.008

Texas Property Code Section 5.008 requires sellers to deliver a written disclosure notice to buyers on or before the effective date of a contract to purchase residential property. The statute applies to transfers of residential real property comprising not more than one dwelling unit. It does not matter whether the transaction involves a listing agent, a buyer's agent, both, or neither—the obligation runs with the seller.

The disclosure must address specific categories of property conditions including structural components, environmental hazards, flooding history, and known defects in systems such as HVAC, plumbing, and electrical. Section 5.008(b) prescribes the statutory form itself, which the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) mirrors in its approved Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition form. Agents who substitute informal disclosures or abbreviated checklists are not satisfying the statutory requirement.

> "A seller shall give to the purchaser of residential real property a written notice as prescribed by this section or a written notice substantially similar to the notice prescribed by this section." — Texas Property Code § 5.008(a)

**The word "substantially similar" has generated litigation, and agents should not test its boundaries.** Using the TREC-prescribed form verbatim eliminates ambiguity and provides a clean defense if a buyer later alleges inadequate disclosure.

## Exact Forms Texas Agents Must Use

TREC publishes the Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition (TREC Form OP-H), which tracks the statutory requirements of Section 5.008. This is the baseline form for residential transactions in Texas. Agents should verify they are using the most recently adopted version, as TREC periodically updates its promulgated and optional forms during its regular revision cycles.

In addition to TREC Form OP-H, transactions involving properties within a Property Owners Association require delivery of a [resale certificate and related HOA disclosures](/blog/texas-hoa-resale-certificate-requirements) under Texas Property Code Section 207.003. The lead-based paint disclosure mandated by federal law (42 U.S.C. § 4852d) applies to properties built before 1978 and requires the EPA-prescribed form—a separate obligation that runs in parallel with the state disclosure.

For transactions involving properties in designated flood zones, sellers must disclose prior flood damage and whether the property is in a 100-year floodplain. Senate Bill 339, which took effect in 2019, added specific flood disclosure questions to the statutory notice, and those questions remain active in 2026. Agents who skip the flood-related sections or allow sellers to leave them blank are exposing themselves to liability.

## Exemptions That Agents Frequently Misapply

Not every residential transfer triggers the Section 5.008 disclosure obligation. The statute carves out specific exemptions in Section 5.008(e), including transfers pursuant to court order, transfers by a fiduciary in the administration of a decedent's estate, and transfers resulting from foreclosure. Sales by entities that acquired property through foreclosure are also exempt.

**Here is where agents routinely make errors: assuming an exemption applies without verifying the facts.** A trustee selling property from a living trust is not the same as a fiduciary administering a decedent's estate. An investor who purchased a property at a foreclosure auction and is now reselling it as a retail flip does not qualify for the foreclosure exemption—that exemption covers the foreclosing lender's initial disposition, not subsequent resales.

The "new construction" exception also deserves attention. Transfers of new residences that have not been previously occupied do not require the seller's disclosure. However, if the builder used the property as a model home or if the property sat completed but unsold for an extended period with known defects emerging, the analysis becomes less clear. Agents representing builders should confirm with their brokers before relying on this exemption.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of noncompliance operate on multiple levels. At the transaction level, a buyer who does not receive the required disclosure before executing a contract may terminate the contract under Section 5.008(c) and recover earnest money. The statute gives buyers seven days after receiving a late disclosure to rescind without penalty.

At the regulatory level, the Texas Real Estate Commission has authority under Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1101 to sanction licensees for failing to ensure proper disclosure delivery. Sanctions range from reprimands and mandatory education to suspension or revocation of licenses. TREC complaint data shows that disclosure-related violations remain among the top categories of consumer complaints each year.

At the civil liability level, agents face exposure under common law fraud and the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA) if they knew about material defects and actively concealed them or advised sellers to omit information. The DTPA allows for treble damages in cases involving knowing or intentional conduct. E&O carriers have made it clear through premium adjustments and policy exclusions that disclosure failures represent a high-frequency, high-severity risk category.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Texas Seller Disclosures

The first mistake is delivering the disclosure after the contract is executed without informing the buyer of their rescission rights. Section 5.008(c) gives buyers a statutory right to terminate within seven days of receiving a late disclosure, and agents who fail to communicate this right create liability for themselves and their brokers.

The second mistake is allowing sellers to check "unknown" on items the agent personally knows to be defective. An agent who conducted a walkthrough, noticed water stains on a ceiling, and then watches the seller mark the roof condition as "unknown" has an independent duty to disclose what they observed. TREC's [canons of professional ethics](/blog/trec-ethics-requirements-agents) require licensees to deal honestly with all parties.

**The third mistake involves treating the disclosure as a one-time event rather than a living document.** If a seller becomes aware of a new defect between the original disclosure and closing—say, a pipe bursts during the option period—the disclosure must be updated and delivered to the buyer. Agents who ignore mid-transaction developments are gambling with their licenses.

The fourth mistake is failing to retain a signed and dated copy of the disclosure in the transaction file. Without proof of delivery and timing, an agent has no defense in a complaint or lawsuit. Digital transaction management platforms generate automatic timestamps, which is one reason electronic delivery has become the standard of care in most Texas brokerages.

The fifth mistake is confusing the seller's disclosure with the property condition inspection. The disclosure captures the seller's knowledge; the inspection captures an inspector's professional findings. They serve different purposes, and one does not substitute for the other. Agents who tell buyers "you'll get the inspection, so the disclosure doesn't matter" are misrepresenting the law.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under Section 1101.803 of the Texas Occupations Code for the acts of their sponsored agents. A failure to implement systems that ensure disclosure compliance is not just a risk management gap—it is a potential basis for the broker's own disciplinary action.

**Brokers should audit three things on every residential listing file before closing.** First, confirm that the signed seller's disclosure (TREC Form OP-H) is in the file with a delivery date that precedes or coincides with contract execution. Second, verify that the flood-related questions are answered completely, not left blank. Third, check that any mid-transaction amendments to the disclosure are documented and delivered with receipts.

Quarterly file audits remain the industry standard, but brokers managing large teams may need to implement checkpoint systems that flag missing disclosures before transactions reach the title company. Training sessions at least twice per year should cover disclosure obligations, exemption criteria, and the consequences of noncompliance. Brokers who delegate this entirely to their agents without verification are accepting personal risk.

The [Texas Real Estate Commission's website](https://www.trec.texas.gov/) publishes updated forms, advisory opinions, and disciplinary orders that brokers can reference when building compliance protocols. Staying current with TREC's form revisions—especially during the commission's scheduled review periods—prevents agents from using outdated documents.

## How the 2026 Enforcement Environment Differs

TREC's complaint volume has increased steadily since 2020, driven in part by buyers who now have greater access to property history databases, flood mapping tools, and public records that reveal prior insurance claims. When a buyer discovers post-closing that the seller's disclosure contradicted publicly available information, the complaint practically writes itself. Agents are caught in the middle because they signed as the listing agent of record.

The practical reality is that disclosure obligations function as a risk filter: agents who handle them correctly at the front of a transaction eliminate the most common post-closing disputes. Agents who treat them as administrative afterthoughts generate the complaints, lawsuits, and E&O claims that inflate costs industry-wide. The standard of care is rising regardless of whether the statute itself changes.
