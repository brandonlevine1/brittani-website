# Texas Seller Disclosure Requirements 2026: What Every Agent and Broker Must Know

Failing to deliver a proper seller disclosure remains one of the fastest ways to kill a deal or invite a lawsuit in Texas residential real estate. The Texas seller disclosure requirements 2026 have not loosened up, and the Texas Real Estate Commission continues to hold licensees accountable for procedural missteps. If you represent sellers—or manage agents who do—this is the framework you need to have memorized.

## The Statutory Foundation: Texas Property Code Section 5.008

Texas Property Code Section 5.008 is the governing statute that mandates sellers of residential real property containing one to four dwelling units provide a written disclosure to the buyer before the execution of a binding contract, or as otherwise specified. The statute lays out the specific content categories that must be addressed, ranging from structural defects to environmental hazards. It also defines who qualifies for exemptions and what happens when disclosures are delivered late or not at all.

The form itself is codified within Section 5.008(b), which prescribes the statutory minimum content a seller must disclose. The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) publishes a corresponding form—[TREC Form OP-H (Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition)](https://www.trec.texas.gov/forms)—that satisfies the statutory requirement when properly completed. Agents should treat OP-H as the floor, not the ceiling, when advising sellers on what to disclose.

**A seller's failure to deliver the required disclosure gives the buyer a specific statutory remedy.** Under Section 5.008(c), a buyer who does not receive the notice before executing the contract may terminate the contract for any reason within seven days of receiving the notice. That seven-day window restarts the buyer's decision clock, creating uncertainty for sellers and their agents alike.

## Who Is Exempt and Who Is Not

Texas law carves out several categories of sellers who are not required to deliver the Section 5.008 disclosure. These include court-appointed fiduciaries such as executors, administrators, guardians, and trustees acting under a court order. Transfers by foreclosure, government entities, and financial institutions selling properties acquired through foreclosure also fall under the exemption umbrella.

Relocation companies, co-owners in a partition action, and spouses transferring property under a divorce decree are similarly exempt. However, agents frequently misapply these exemptions or assume that any estate sale is automatically exempt. Unless a personal representative is acting under court authority—not merely holding a power of attorney—the exemption likely does not apply, and the disclosure must still be delivered.

A new construction sale where the buyer is the first occupant is also exempt, provided the builder has complied with separate statutory warranty obligations. This is sometimes confused with builder resales, where a builder is selling a previously occupied home they've taken back. In that scenario, the disclosure obligation applies.

## Timing of Delivery: Before or After Contract Execution

The preferred sequence is delivery of the completed OP-H form before the buyer signs the contract. When that happens, the buyer's acceptance of the disclosure is embedded in the transaction timeline without creating a separate termination window. This is the cleanest path for both parties and the agents involved.

When a seller delivers the disclosure after the contract is signed—or fails to deliver at all until the buyer demands it—Section 5.008(c) activates. The buyer then has seven days from receipt to terminate, regardless of the inspection period, financing contingency, or option period status. That termination right exists independently of any contractual option period and cannot be waived by contract language.

**Agents listing properties should treat pre-contract disclosure delivery as a non-negotiable step in their listing workflow.** Waiting until after execution creates risk exposure that no option fee or earnest money deposit can fully mitigate. It also invites the perception that the seller had something to hide, which poisons negotiations if a dispute arises.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Seller Disclosures

The first error is allowing sellers to leave sections blank instead of marking "unknown." Section 5.008 requires the seller to disclose conditions that are actually known to the seller. A blank field is ambiguous—it could mean "no defects," "I don't know," or "I skipped this section." Courts and TREC investigators treat blanks differently than affirmative "unknown" responses, and agents who accept incomplete forms expose their clients and themselves to liability.

The second common mistake is coaching the seller on how to answer. An agent who tells a seller to mark "no" on a question about foundation issues—even if the agent believes the foundation is fine—has crossed a line. The disclosure is the seller's statement, not the agent's opinion. TREC Rule 531.19 requires licensees to deal honestly and in good faith, and advising a seller to minimize or omit known defects violates that standard.

Third, agents often fail to retain proof of delivery and receipt. A disclosure that was "definitely emailed" but cannot be located in any sent folder or transaction file is legally equivalent to a disclosure that was never sent. The burden of proving timely delivery falls on the seller and their agent. Digital timestamps, signed acknowledgments, or transaction management platform logs should be preserved for every file.

Fourth, listing agents sometimes rely on a prior disclosure from a canceled transaction without requiring the seller to update it. Conditions change between contracts—a roof leak discovered during the first buyer's inspection must be reflected in any subsequent disclosure. Recycling an outdated form without re-verification is a known source of post-closing claims.

Fifth, agents confuse the seller's disclosure obligation with the agent's own duty to disclose material facts. Even if the seller's form is complete, an agent who independently knows of a material defect must disclose it under TREC Rules and Texas common law. The seller's form does not shield the agent from their own knowledge-based disclosure duty.

## Liability When Agents Fail to Comply

Non-compliance with seller disclosure requirements exposes multiple parties to multiple types of risk. For the buyer, Section 5.008(c) provides the termination remedy already discussed. For the seller and the listing agent, post-closing discovery of a concealed defect can trigger a claim under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA), which allows for treble damages in cases of knowing or intentional conduct.

TREC can also take disciplinary action against the licensee for violating Section 1101.652 of the Texas Occupations Code, which governs grounds for suspension or revocation. Penalties range from informal counseling letters to formal reprimands, administrative fines up to $5,000 per violation, and license suspension or revocation. A pattern of non-compliance—across multiple transactions—can escalate from a single-file issue to a systemic enforcement action against the agent and the broker.

Errors and omissions insurance carriers may deny coverage if the agent's conduct constitutes intentional misrepresentation rather than negligent omission. Carriers increasingly scrutinize disclosure-related claims and may raise premiums or decline renewal for agents with repeated issues. The financial exposure here extends well beyond any commission earned on the transaction in question.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

**Brokers who do not actively audit disclosure compliance in their transaction files are building a liability portfolio.** Under Section 1101.803 of the Texas Occupations Code, supervising brokers bear responsibility for the acts of their sponsored agents. A broker's defense of "I didn't know" does not hold up when TREC determines that reasonable supervision systems were absent.

At minimum, brokers should verify three things in every residential listing file: that a completed OP-H form exists with no blank fields, that proof of delivery to the buyer or buyer's agent is documented with a timestamp, and that the form was delivered before or promptly after contract execution with a corresponding termination-period acknowledgment. These three checks can be automated within a transaction management system or assigned to a compliance coordinator.

Brokers should also conduct periodic audits of closed files—not just pending ones. Post-closing claims often surface six months to two years after the transaction, and the broker who reviewed the file only at the time of closing may have missed a form that was later altered or lost. Retaining original digital versions with metadata intact protects the brokerage if a claim arises years later.

Training is the other enforcement vector. Annual compliance training that specifically walks agents through disclosure scenarios—blank fields, exempt vs. non-exempt sellers, late delivery consequences—reduces the likelihood of unintentional violations. Brokers who document this training create an affirmative defense showing they took reasonable steps to educate and supervise their agents.

## The Role of the Buyer's Agent in the Disclosure Process

Buyer's agents have their own obligations that intersect with the seller's disclosure. A buyer's agent who receives the OP-H form must deliver it to the buyer and confirm the buyer has reviewed it. Simply attaching it to an email with forty other documents does not satisfy the agent's duty to ensure the buyer understands the significance of the disclosure and their right to terminate if it arrives late.

If a buyer's agent knows that the seller has not delivered a disclosure—or suspects the form is incomplete—the agent has a duty to advise the buyer of their rights under Section 5.008. Staying silent to avoid rocking the deal is not a strategy; it is a violation of the agent's fiduciary obligation. The buyer's agent should document in writing any request made to the listing side for a disclosure and any response received.

Buyer's agents also need to understand that the seller's disclosure does not replace a professional inspection. The form captures only what the seller knows and chooses to reveal. Agents who allow buyers to skip inspections because the disclosure "looked clean" are setting their clients up for disappointment and themselves up for a negligence claim.

## Staying Current With Form Updates and Regulatory Changes

TREC periodically updates its promulgated forms, including the OP-H. Agents must confirm they are using the [most current version available on the TREC website](https://www.trec.texas.gov/forms) rather than a saved PDF from three years ago. Using an outdated form can create enforceability questions, even if the substance of the disclosure is accurate.

The Texas legislature also amends Property Code provisions from time to time. The 2025 legislative session introduced clarifications around disclosure obligations for properties with known flood history, and agents should verify whether any additional content requirements have taken effect for transactions closing in 2026. Subscribing to TREC's official bulletin and your local board's legal updates ensures you are not caught off guard by a statutory change mid-transaction.
