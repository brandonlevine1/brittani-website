## The Statutory Foundation: Texas Property Code §5.008

Texas seller disclosure requirements are governed primarily by **Texas Property Code §5.008**, which mandates that sellers of residential real property comprising not more than one dwelling unit deliver a written disclosure statement to prospective buyers. This isn't optional guidance—it's a statutory obligation with real teeth.

The statute requires sellers to disclose known conditions affecting the property's value or desirability. Unlike some states that allow vague or generalized disclosures, Texas requires sellers to address specific categories of property conditions, from structural integrity and roof condition to the presence of hazardous materials and previous flooding.

As a licensed agent, your job isn't to fill out the disclosure for the seller. But it is your job to ensure the seller receives the correct form, understands the obligation, and delivers the completed disclosure to the buyer within the statutory timeframe. Failure to facilitate this process exposes you, your broker, and your client to significant legal and financial risk.

## The Forms: What You Should Be Using

The **Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC)** publishes the standardized **Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition** form, designated as **TREC Form OP-H (currently OP-H revised 2023)**. This is the form referenced in §5.008 and the one your MLS likely requires as an upload at listing entry.

Key sections of the TREC Seller's Disclosure form include:

- **Property condition items** (roof, walls, foundation, plumbing, electrical, HVAC)
- **Environmental hazards** (asbestos, lead-based paint, radon, urea formaldehyde)
- **Flood-related disclosures** (previous flooding, whether the property is in a 100-year floodplain, whether flood insurance is required)
- **Property located in a groundwater conservation district or utility district**
- **Known defects or malfunctions** in appliances, systems, or structural components
- **Previous repairs** and whether permits were obtained

Additionally, agents should be aware of the **TAR Seller's Disclosure Notice (TAR Form 1406)**, published by the Texas Association of REALTORS®, which provides a more detailed alternative that many brokerages prefer for its expanded coverage of potential issues.

For transactions involving properties in designated floodplains, the **TAR Information About Special Flood Hazard Areas (TAR Form 1414)** should accompany the standard disclosure.

Do not confuse the seller's disclosure with the **TREC-promulgated contract addenda** such as the **Addendum for Property Located Seaward of the Gulf Intracoastal Waterway (TREC Form 33-2)** or the **Addendum for Coastal Area Property (TREC Form 34-1)**, which address additional geographic-specific disclosures but serve a different contractual function.

## Timing and Delivery Requirements

Under §5.008, the seller's disclosure must be delivered to the buyer **before execution of a binding contract**. In practice, this means listing agents should have the completed disclosure available at or before the time offers are solicited.

If the disclosure is delivered after the contract is executed, the buyer has the statutory right to terminate the contract within **seven days** of receiving the disclosure. This creates a practical problem: if you're handing the disclosure to the buyer's agent after contract execution, you've just given the buyer an unconditional exit ramp. Smart listing agents avoid this by having the disclosure completed and available in the MLS or delivered with the listing packet before showings begin.

## Exemptions: Know Who Doesn't Have to Disclose

Not every transaction requires a §5.008 disclosure. The statute carves out specific exemptions, including:

- Transfers pursuant to a court order (foreclosures, bankruptcies, probate sales)
- Transfers by a fiduciary in the administration of a decedent's estate or trust
- Transfers from one co-owner to another
- Transfers by a governmental entity
- Transfers to a spouse or direct descendant
- Transfers of new residences that have not been previously occupied (builder sales)

**Critical note for agents:** The exemption for fiduciary transfers does NOT mean the executor or trustee has zero disclosure obligation if they have actual knowledge of material defects. Courts have found fiduciaries liable under common-law fraud when they concealed known conditions, even though §5.008 technically exempted them from the statutory form.

## What Happens When Compliance Fails

### Liability for the Seller

A seller who fails to deliver the required disclosure, or who knowingly provides false or misleading information, faces:

- **Contract rescission** by the buyer (even post-closing in extreme fraud cases)
- **Actual damages** for repair costs, diminution in value, and related expenses
- **Statutory damages** under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA), which can include treble damages for knowing or intentional violations
- **Attorney's fees** awarded to the prevailing party under DTPA §17.50

### Liability for the Agent and Broker

Agents are not required to independently inspect the property or guarantee the accuracy of the seller's representations. However, under **TREC Rules §531.19** and the general duties outlined in **Texas Occupations Code §1101.652**, agents face disciplinary action for:

- Failing to advise sellers of the disclosure obligation
- Knowingly allowing false disclosures to be delivered to buyers
- Misrepresenting property conditions in marketing materials that contradict the disclosure
- Failing to disclose facts known to the agent that materially affect the property's value

TREC disciplinary actions can include **license suspension or revocation**, **administrative penalties up to $5,000 per violation**, and mandatory continuing education. Beyond regulatory penalties, agents and their brokers face civil liability for negligence and, in egregious cases, fraud.

The broker's vicarious liability under §1101.803 means that a single agent's disclosure failure can result in a claim against the entire brokerage.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make

### 1. Accepting "I Don't Know" as a Default Answer

Sellers sometimes mark "unknown" on every line item of the disclosure to avoid potential liability. While a seller genuinely may not know certain conditions, a blanket "unknown" response—particularly from an owner-occupant who has lived in the property for years—raises red flags. Courts and TREC investigators view wholesale "unknown" responses as evidence of intentional concealment. As the listing agent, you should walk the seller through each section and explain that "unknown" is appropriate only when they genuinely lack knowledge.

### 2. Failing to Update the Disclosure After New Information Emerges

A disclosure isn't a one-time document. If the seller discovers a foundation issue after completing the form but before closing, the disclosure must be supplemented. Agents who treat the disclosure as "done" at listing and never revisit it—even when the seller reports new problems during the pending period—create significant liability exposure.

### 3. Using Outdated Forms

TREC and TAR periodically revise their disclosure forms. Using a form version from 2019 when a 2023 revision exists isn't just sloppy—it can result in missing disclosure categories that the current statute requires. Verify you're using the most current version by checking the TREC forms library at www.trec.texas.gov before every new listing.

### 4. Confusing the Seller's Disclosure with the Agent's Duty to Disclose

Even if a seller properly completes the disclosure form, the agent has an independent duty under §1101.652 to disclose material facts known to the agent. If you notice water stains, foundation cracks, or evidence of unpermitted additions during your listing walkthrough, you cannot stay silent simply because the seller didn't mention them on the form. Your license carries its own disclosure obligations separate from the seller's statutory duties.

### 5. Not Documenting Delivery

Agents who hand the disclosure to the buyer's agent without documenting when and how it was delivered lose the ability to prove compliance. Always obtain a signed acknowledgment of receipt, use your transaction management platform's tracking features, or deliver via a method that creates a verifiable timestamp.

## What Brokers Should Know

Brokers carry direct responsibility for ensuring their agents understand and comply with Texas seller disclosure requirements. Under **Texas Occupations Code §1101.803**, a broker is liable for the misconduct of sponsored agents performed within the scope of their agency relationship. This isn't limited to situations where the broker had actual knowledge—constructive knowledge (what the broker should have known through reasonable supervision) is sufficient to establish liability.

Practically, this means brokers should implement written policies requiring disclosure form completion at the time of listing, conduct periodic audits of pending transactions to verify disclosures are on file, and include disclosure compliance in their onboarding training for new agents. Brokers who rely entirely on agents to self-manage disclosure compliance are betting their E&O deductible on individual competence without a safety net.

The brokerage's errors and omissions insurance policy likely covers disclosure-related claims, but repeated violations can lead to policy non-renewal or increased premiums. More critically, TREC can pursue disciplinary action against the broker's license independent of any action against the individual agent, including imposing conditions on the broker's ability to sponsor new licensees.

## Staying Ahead of Compliance in 2026

The Texas legislature continues to expand disclosure obligations, particularly around flooding and environmental risks following recent legislative sessions. Keep your eye on updates from the **Texas Association of REALTORS® Legal Hotline**, TREC's quarterly newsletter, and any interim committee reports that signal forthcoming statutory amendments. What was compliant last year may not be sufficient this year.

Document everything. Train continuously. And never assume your seller is being fully forthcoming without asking pointed, specific questions about the property's history.
