## Understanding Texas Seller Disclosure Requirements 2026

Texas seller disclosure requirements 2026 remain one of the most frequent sources of post-closing disputes, license complaints, and E&O claims filed against agents in the state. The Texas Property Code Section 5.008 mandates that most residential sellers provide a written disclosure of known material conditions before the buyer is bound under a contract. Getting this wrong does not just kill deals—it exposes agents and their brokers to statutory liability that no errors-and-omissions policy wants to cover twice.

The governing statute has not changed dramatically in recent legislative sessions, but enforcement trends and TREC disciplinary patterns have shifted. Agents who last reviewed their disclosure obligations during pre-license education are working with outdated assumptions. This post breaks down what the statute actually requires, which forms you should be using, where agents consistently stumble, and what managing brokers need to be auditing on every file.

## The Statutory Framework: Texas Property Code Section 5.008

Texas Property Code Section 5.008 requires sellers of residential property (one to four units) to deliver a written disclosure notice to prospective buyers on or before the effective date of a purchase contract. The notice must address specific categories of property conditions including structural components, HVAC systems, plumbing, roofing, environmental hazards, and flood damage history. Failure to deliver the notice gives the buyer a statutory right to terminate the contract.

**The statute does not require sellers to conduct inspections or discover defects they genuinely do not know about.** It requires honest disclosure of known conditions. The distinction matters because agents sometimes pressure sellers to investigate issues they have no knowledge of, which creates its own liability exposure.

The seller's obligation is triggered by the transfer itself, not by listing the property. This means the disclosure must be delivered regardless of whether the property is marketed through MLS, sold off-market, or transferred through a for-sale-by-owner arrangement where a buyer's agent is involved. Agents who assume the listing side handles everything miss this point entirely.

## The Forms: TAR 1406 and TREC OP-H

The [Texas Real Estate Commission](https://www.trec.texas.gov/) publishes a statutory seller's disclosure notice that tracks the requirements of Section 5.008. This is commonly referred to by its TREC form designation. The Texas Association of Realtors also publishes TAR Form 1406, the Seller's Disclosure Notice, which many brokerages prefer because it includes additional questions that provide broader liability protection for all parties.

Either form satisfies the statutory requirement, but brokers should adopt a consistent policy on which form their agents use. Mixing forms within a brokerage creates audit confusion and increases the chance that a less thorough version slips into a file where additional disclosures were warranted. The TAR 1406 covers topics like previous flooding even when the property is not in a FEMA-designated flood zone—a question the statutory minimum does not always capture as explicitly.

Agents also need to be aware of supplemental disclosures required in specific circumstances. The MUD (Municipal Utility District) notice, the coastal area disclosure, and the disclosure related to proximity to military installations each carry their own statutory triggers under different sections of the Texas Property Code. These are not optional add-ons; they are independently required by law when the facts apply.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance operate on multiple tracks simultaneously. Under the Property Code, a buyer who does not receive the required disclosure notice may terminate the contract and recover earnest money. This termination right exists regardless of whether the seller actually knew about a defect—the mere failure to deliver the form is enough.

**Beyond deal cancellation, agents face TREC disciplinary action under the Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.652, which authorizes sanctions for failing to disclose material facts or for negligent or incompetent conduct.** Sanctions range from formal reprimands and mandatory continuing education to license suspension and revocation. TREC has publicly disciplined agents for allowing transactions to close without proper disclosures on file, even when the seller was the one who refused to complete the form.

Civil liability adds a third layer. Buyers who discover undisclosed defects after closing routinely name the listing agent, the selling agent, and both brokers in fraud or DTPA (Deceptive Trade Practices Act) claims. Texas courts have allowed these claims to proceed where evidence showed an agent knew about a condition—or should have known—and failed to ensure it appeared on the disclosure. The defense costs alone, even in cases that settle, typically exceed $30,000 before any judgment or payout.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Seller Disclosures

The first and most damaging error is accepting a blank or incomplete disclosure without following up. Sellers frequently leave questions unanswered or check "unknown" on items they clearly have knowledge about—such as whether the property has ever flooded when water stains are visible in the garage. Agents who pass through an incomplete form without discussion are not protecting their clients or themselves.

The second mistake is failing to update the disclosure when new information surfaces between listing and closing. Texas law does not explicitly require an amended disclosure, but TREC's standards of practice and case law both support the position that a seller who learns of a new defect must disclose it. Agents who learn about a foundation issue from a buyer's inspection report, for example, cannot ignore that information if the deal falls through and the property is re-listed. The knowledge now exists, and it must be disclosed to the next buyer.

**Third, agents routinely misidentify which transactions are exempt from the disclosure requirement.** Section 5.008(e) lists specific exemptions including transfers pursuant to court order, foreclosures, transfers by fiduciaries in the administration of an estate, and transfers from one co-owner to another. Agents sometimes incorrectly apply the estate exemption to properties sold by heirs who have completed probate and now hold title individually—those sellers are not exempt.

A fourth common error involves timing. The statute says delivery must occur "on or before" the effective date of the contract. Agents who obtain the disclosure after execution but before the option period expires assume they are safe. Technically, the buyer's termination right under Section 5.008 has already been triggered by late delivery, creating an unnecessary contractual vulnerability that a savvy buyer's agent can exploit during negotiations.

The fifth mistake is conflating the seller's disclosure with the agent's own disclosure obligations. Under TREC rules, agents must disclose material facts they personally know about a property, independent of what appears on the seller's form. If a listing agent noticed foundation cracks during a walkthrough but the seller did not disclose them, the agent has an independent duty to inform prospective buyers. Relying solely on the seller's form does not satisfy this obligation.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers carry vicarious liability for their agents' disclosure failures under Texas agency law and TREC's broker responsibility rules. A broker who does not have a system for reviewing disclosure completeness on every active listing is accepting risk without managing it. The minimum audit should confirm that the disclosure was delivered before contract execution, that all fields are completed, and that any "unknown" responses are plausible given the property's condition and history.

Brokers should also enforce a policy requiring agents to document the date and method of disclosure delivery. A signed acknowledgment of receipt from the buyer—or at minimum, an email delivery confirmation with timestamp—should exist in every transaction file. When disputes arise two years after closing, the agent's recollection of "I'm pretty sure I gave it to them" is worthless without documentation.

**Quarterly file audits should specifically flag transactions where the seller claimed an exemption from disclosure.** The broker should verify that the exemption actually applies by cross-referencing the transfer type against Section 5.008(e). Incorrectly claimed exemptions are a regulatory red flag that TREC investigators look for during brokerage inspections. They signal either agent incompetence or intentional avoidance—neither of which a broker wants associated with their license.

Brokers should also ensure that their agents understand the relationship between seller disclosures and the [lead-based paint disclosure requirements](/blog/lead-based-paint-disclosure-texas) that apply to all pre-1978 residential properties. These are separate federal obligations under 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d, and they require their own form, their own timing, and their own signatures. Missing one while completing the other is a pattern that shows up repeatedly in TREC complaints.

## Flood Disclosure: The Area Getting the Most Attention in 2026

Senate Bill 339, passed during the 86th Texas Legislature, added questions to the statutory disclosure notice specifically addressing flood history and flood insurance claims. The 2026 enforcement environment reflects several years of post-Harvey, post-winter-storm litigation that has made flood history the single most contested disclosure category in Texas residential transactions.

Sellers must now disclose whether the property is located in a 100-year floodplain, whether it has flooded previously, and whether they have ever filed a flood insurance claim. Agents should be aware that buyer reliance on these disclosures has been specifically recognized in several recent Texas appellate decisions. The "I didn't know" defense is increasingly difficult to sustain when public flood claim data and FEMA mapping are readily accessible.

Agents working in the Houston, Beaumont, and San Antonio metros should pay particular attention to properties that experienced flooding from events not officially declared disasters. Sellers sometimes rationalize that a "minor" water event does not constitute flooding. The statute does not define a threshold—any water intrusion the seller knows about should be disclosed. Agents who help sellers minimize these answers are inviting DTPA claims that name them as co-defendants.

## Practical Workflow for Disclosure Compliance

The most effective agents build disclosure completion into their listing appointment process rather than treating it as paperwork to collect later. Handing the seller the TAR 1406 at the listing appointment—with a brief verbal explanation of each section—produces more accurate and complete responses than emailing the form and hoping it comes back filled out. Sellers who complete the form in conversation with their agent tend to remember conditions they would otherwise overlook.

For agents managing high transaction volumes, tracking disclosure status across dozens of active listings becomes an operational challenge. This is where [transaction coordination systems](/blog/transaction-coordinator-checklist-texas) earn their value—not as luxuries, but as compliance infrastructure. A missed disclosure deadline on one file can cascade into a TREC complaint that affects the agent's entire license history.

> "A seller's disclosure notice must be completed to the best of the seller's belief and knowledge as of the date the notice is completed and signed by the seller." — Texas Property Code Section 5.008(b)

The statute's language—"belief and knowledge"—is intentionally broad. Agents should counsel sellers that this standard does not require certainty. If a seller believes the roof leaked three years ago but is not positive, that belief must be disclosed. Omitting it because the seller "isn't sure" misreads the statutory standard and creates liability for everyone involved.
