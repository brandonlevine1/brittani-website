## Understanding Michigan Escrow Regulations 2026 at the Statutory Level

Michigan escrow regulations 2026 remain governed primarily by the Occupational Code, Public Act 299 of 1980, specifically MCL 339.2512d through 339.2512f. These sections spell out the fiduciary obligations brokers carry when holding funds on behalf of buyers and sellers. Agents who treat escrow as a formality rather than a legal mandate expose themselves—and their brokerage—to license action.

The Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) enforces these statutes through its Bureau of Professional Licensing. LARA's [Administrative Rules for Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons](https://www.michigan.gov/lara/bureau-list/bpl/occ/prof/real-estate) (R 339.22301 through R 339.22315) detail everything from account naming conventions to record retention timelines. If you have not reviewed these rules since renewal, 2026 is the year to do it.

**The penalty structure is not abstract.** A single substantiated complaint about escrow mishandling can trigger a formal investigation, and LARA's Enforcement Division has increased audit activity for the 2025-2026 cycle according to its published enforcement reports.

## Deposit Timelines and the Two-Banking-Day Rule

MCL 339.2512d mandates that any earnest money deposit received by a broker or an agent acting on the broker's behalf must be placed into an escrow or trust account within two banking days of the purchase agreement being fully executed. "Fully executed" means signed by all parties—not just the buyer.

This timeline trips up agents more than almost any other rule. Weekends and bank holidays do not count, but the clock starts the moment the last signature lands. If a buyer signs on Thursday evening and the seller countersigns Friday morning, the broker has until end of business Tuesday to deposit.

Holding a check in a desk drawer "until the inspection passes" is a violation, full stop. The statute does not include an exception for contingency periods, and LARA has disciplined brokers who tried to argue otherwise.

## Required Forms and Documentation

Michigan's standard residential purchase agreement—the Michigan Association of Realtors (MAR) Purchase Agreement (Form PAR)—contains specific escrow clauses in Section 4 that define how and where the deposit will be held. Agents must fill out this section completely; leaving it blank creates ambiguity that benefits no one in a dispute.

Beyond the purchase agreement, brokers are required to maintain an escrow ledger that tracks every deposit and disbursement by transaction. LARA Administrative Rule 339.22307 requires this ledger be reconciled at least monthly. The reconciliation must compare the ledger balance to the actual bank statement balance and resolve any discrepancies within five banking days.

| Document | Purpose | Required By |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| MAR Form PAR (Section 4) | Defines escrow terms in purchase agreement | Signed at offer |
| Trust Account Ledger | Tracks all deposits and disbursements | Maintained ongoing |
| Monthly Reconciliation Statement | Verifies ledger matches bank balance | Due by 10th of following month |
| Escrow Disbursement Authorization | Authorizes release of funds | Prior to any disbursement |
| LARA Trust Account Certificate | Certifies account meets statutory requirements | At account opening |

Agents should also be familiar with the MAR Release of Earnest Money Deposit form (Form RED), which both parties must sign before a broker can disburse funds in a disputed cancellation. Without both signatures or a court order, the broker must hold the funds—sometimes indefinitely.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make with Escrow

**First, agents routinely confuse "received" with "deposited."** The two-banking-day rule applies from the moment the agent physically possesses the check or receives a wire confirmation, not from when they hand it to their broker. If a showing agent accepts a check on Saturday and does not deliver it to the brokerage until Wednesday, the broker is already behind schedule through no fault of their own. This is why many brokerages now require same-day delivery to the office or direct electronic deposits.

Second, agents fail to specify the escrow holder in the purchase agreement. Section 4 of Form PAR asks for the name of the entity holding funds. Writing "TBD" or leaving it blank creates a situation where neither brokerage knows who is responsible. In a dispute, this ambiguity can delay resolution by weeks and generate [the kind of blown closing that costs everyone money](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Third, agents release information about escrow account details—including wire instructions—without proper verification protocols. Wire fraud targeting real estate transactions has increased 73% since 2022 according to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center. Every wire instruction should be verified by phone using a number independently obtained, not one provided in an email. If you are not already following a [formal wire instructions verification process](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process), your brokerage is exposed.

Fourth, agents verbally promise buyers that their deposit is "fully refundable" without clarifying the contractual conditions under which that refund applies. Michigan law does not automatically guarantee refund of earnest money; the purchase agreement's contingency language controls. Agents who over-promise face complaints from buyers who feel misled.

Fifth, agents co-mingle personal and client communications about escrow status, leading to missed deadlines and confusion. Tracking [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without a system inevitably results in a deposit that sits un-deposited one day too long.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry the ultimate statutory liability for escrow compliance in Michigan. MCL 339.2512f makes clear that the broker—not the individual salesperson—is the named fiduciary. This means brokers cannot delegate escrow responsibility and then disclaim knowledge when something goes wrong.

**Every Michigan brokerage should conduct internal audits at least quarterly.** The audit should verify that every open transaction has a corresponding ledger entry, that the trust account balance matches the sum of all individual transaction escrow amounts, and that no funds have been held beyond 30 days past closing or cancellation without documented justification.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag |
|------------|-----------|----------|
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | Variance greater than $0 unresolved past 5 days |
| Deposit timing compliance | Per transaction | Deposit later than 2 banking days post-execution |
| Disbursement authorization | Per disbursement | Missing buyer or seller signature on Form RED |
| Account naming compliance | Annual | Account not titled in brokerage name per R 339.22301 |
| Record retention | Annual | Escrow records destroyed before 3-year minimum |

Brokers should also confirm that their trust account is properly titled. Administrative Rule 339.22301 requires the account be in the name of the brokerage as licensed with LARA, include the word "trust" or "escrow" in the account title, and be maintained at a federally insured institution located in Michigan. Accounts titled in an agent's personal name or a DBA not registered with LARA are violations.

LARA has the authority to conduct unannounced audits of trust accounts. Brokers who receive a records request have 10 business days to produce all documentation. Failure to comply is treated as a separate violation and can result in immediate license suspension pending investigation.

## Penalties and Liability for Non-Compliance

The consequence spectrum for escrow violations in Michigan ranges from a formal reprimand to criminal prosecution. LARA's Enforcement Division categorizes violations by severity, and escrow mishandling sits near the top of their priority list because it involves direct harm to consumers.

For administrative penalties, LARA can impose fines up to $10,000 per violation under MCL 339.604. License suspension or revocation is available for repeat violations or cases involving actual loss of client funds. These proceedings are public record and appear on the licensee's LARA profile permanently.

**Civil liability adds another layer.** A buyer or seller who loses earnest money due to agent negligence can sue both the individual agent and the brokerage. Michigan courts have awarded damages including the lost deposit, consequential damages from failed transactions, and in cases of willful misconduct, attorney fees. The E&O insurance that most agents carry may not cover intentional escrow violations or acts that LARA classifies as fraudulent.

Criminal exposure exists in extreme cases. Converting escrow funds to personal use constitutes embezzlement under MCL 750.174. Depending on the amount, this is a felony carrying up to 20 years in prison. Even cases that do not reach criminal thresholds can destroy a career—LARA publishes all disciplinary actions in its quarterly enforcement bulletin.

## Disputed Escrow: The Interpleader Option

When a transaction falls apart and both buyer and seller claim the earnest money deposit, the broker faces a choice. Michigan law permits the broker to file an interpleader action in circuit court, depositing the disputed funds with the court and requesting that a judge determine proper disbursement.

This is not a theoretical option—it is often the only safe path. Brokers who unilaterally release disputed funds to one party without written authorization from both parties (Form RED signed by all) face liability to the other party. The interpleader filing fee is modest relative to the risk of choosing sides.

The timeline for interpleader resolution varies by county, but most Michigan circuit courts resolve these matters within 60 to 120 days. During that period, the broker is discharged from responsibility for the funds once the court accepts the deposit. Agents should inform clients at the outset of any transaction that disputed deposits may be subject to this process.
