## Michigan New Real Estate Laws 2026: The Statute Changes That Matter

The phrase **Michigan new real estate laws 2026** is going to define your compliance calendar for the rest of this year. The Michigan Legislature passed HB 4417 and SB 221 during the 2025 session, and the Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) finalized administrative rules that take effect August 1, 2026. These changes touch seller disclosures, agency relationships, wire fraud prevention duties, and continuing education requirements.

If you haven't read the amended Michigan Occupational Code (MCL 339.2501 et seq.) or reviewed LARA's updated forms library, you're already behind. This post breaks down the specific statutes, form numbers, liability risks, and enforcement changes you need to internalize before your next listing appointment.

The Michigan Association of Realtors has updated its standard forms to align with these changes, but LARA's mandatory state forms take precedence where conflicts exist. Agents who rely solely on association templates without cross-referencing the statutory requirements are playing a dangerous game.

## Seller Disclosure Overhaul: Form SD-1R Replaces SD-1

**The most impactful change for day-to-day practice is the replacement of the Seller's Disclosure Statement.** The existing Form SD-1, which Michigan agents have used since 2014, is being retired. Form SD-1R introduces 14 new line items covering environmental hazards, flood zone reclassification, and prior insurance claims history.

Under the amended MCL 565.957, sellers must now disclose any knowledge of PFAS contamination within one mile of the property, any denied homeowner's insurance claims in the preceding five years, and any FEMA flood map changes that occurred during ownership. These additions reflect Michigan's specific environmental concerns, particularly the PFAS crisis that has affected communities from Oscoda to Parchment.

The form must be delivered to the buyer before the buyer signs the purchase agreement—not simultaneously, as was previously permissible under certain circumstances. Section 7(2) of the amended statute removes the "at or before" language and replaces it with "prior to execution by the buyer." That single-word change creates a hard sequencing requirement that will trip up agents accustomed to bundling documents at the signing table.

## Agency Disclosure Timing: A Tighter Window

Michigan's agency disclosure requirements under MCL 339.2512d have been amended to require delivery of Form AD-1 before any "substantive discussion regarding property preferences, financial capacity, or transaction terms." The old standard allowed disclosure "at the first substantive contact," which courts interpreted loosely.

**The 2026 amendment defines substantive discussion explicitly in the statute for the first time.** Asking a buyer their price range, discussing neighborhoods, or sharing listing information now triggers the duty. Open house conversations count. A DM on social media asking about a property counts. The statute specifically references electronic communications as triggering events.

This matters because the penalty structure has changed. Previously, late agency disclosure was a technical violation that rarely resulted in discipline. Under the new Section 2512d(4), failure to timely deliver Form AD-1 constitutes a per se violation subject to administrative fines of up to $5,000 and creates grounds for the aggrieved party to rescind the purchase agreement within 14 days of discovering the failure.

| Requirement | Pre-2026 Standard | 2026 Standard (Effective Aug. 1) |
|---|---|---|
| Trigger for delivery | First substantive contact | Before any substantive discussion |
| Electronic conversations included | Ambiguous | Explicitly yes |
| Penalty for late delivery | Discretionary discipline | $5,000 fine + rescission right |
| Cure period | None specified | 72 hours after discovery |
| Form version required | AD-1 (2018) | AD-1 (2026 revision) |

Agents who work open houses for other brokers need to pay particular attention. The amended statute clarifies that the hosting agent—not the listing agent—bears the disclosure duty to unrepresented visitors who engage in substantive discussion at the property.

## Wire Fraud Prevention: A New Affirmative Duty

SB 221 adds MCL 339.2512e to the Occupational Code, creating an affirmative duty for licensees to warn clients about wire fraud and direct them to independently verify wiring instructions. This is not a suggestion. It is a statutory obligation with teeth.

The law requires agents to deliver a written wire fraud advisory—LARA has published Form WF-1 for this purpose—at the time earnest money instructions are first discussed and again within 48 hours of the anticipated closing date. The advisory must include specific language prescribed by the statute, and agents may not modify or abbreviate it.

Failure to deliver Form WF-1 at both required intervals exposes the agent to disciplinary action and creates a rebuttable presumption of negligence if the client falls victim to wire fraud. That presumption shifts the burden to the agent to prove they fulfilled their duty, rather than requiring the client to prove the agent failed. ## Common Mistakes Agents Will Make Under the New Laws

Based on early guidance from LARA's enforcement division and conversations with compliance attorneys across the state, here are the specific errors that will generate the most complaints in the first year.

**First, agents will use the old SD-1 form instead of SD-1R.** LARA has confirmed that transactions using the retired form after August 1 will be treated as if no disclosure was provided at all. That triggers the buyer's right to rescind under MCL 565.960 at any point before closing, regardless of how far the transaction has progressed.

Second, agents will deliver agency disclosures after the triggering conversation has already occurred. The temptation to discuss a buyer's needs before "getting to the paperwork" is deeply ingrained. Under the 2026 standard, that sequence is backwards. You must hand over Form AD-1 before you ask the first meaningful question—even at a coffee shop meet-and-greet.

Third, agents will forget the second wire fraud advisory delivery. The initial delivery at earnest money discussion is intuitive. The follow-up delivery 48 hours before closing is not part of any existing workflow most agents or [transaction coordinators](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) use. Without a calendar trigger, it will be missed.

Fourth, agents will assume their brokerage's transaction management software has been updated to include the new forms. Most platforms lag behind statutory changes by months. Verify independently that you're pulling current versions from LARA's website, not from a third-party template library.

Fifth, agents will fail to document delivery. The 2026 amendments require proof of delivery for all mandatory disclosures—signature, electronic acknowledgment, or certified mail receipt. Handing someone a form without getting proof you did so is functionally the same as not delivering it at all.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear heightened responsibility under MCL 339.2512(1), which requires them to "reasonably supervise" licensees and ensure compliance with all statutory obligations. The 2026 amendments add specific audit requirements that move this from a general obligation to a checklist.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency Required | Statute Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Form version verification (SD-1R, AD-1, WF-1) | Every transaction | MCL 565.957(3) |
| Agency disclosure timing documentation | Quarterly review minimum | MCL 339.2512d(5) |
| Wire fraud advisory dual-delivery confirmation | Every transaction | MCL 339.2512e(2) |
| CE compliance tracking (new 2-hour module) | Annual per licensee | Admin Rule 339.22307 |
| Affiliated business disclosure updates | Semi-annual | MCL 339.2512f |

**Brokers who fail to implement a documented audit system face vicarious liability for their agents' violations.** The amended Section 2512(1)(c) states that a broker's failure to maintain written supervisory procedures constitutes an independent violation, separate from whatever the agent did wrong. Fines for broker-level violations start at $10,000 per occurrence.

The new Administrative Rule 339.22307 also requires all licensees to complete a 2-hour continuing education module on the 2026 changes before their next renewal cycle. LARA has approved several providers, but the module must specifically address Forms SD-1R, AD-1 (2026), and WF-1. Generic "legal update" courses that don't cover these forms won't satisfy the requirement.

Brokers should also be auditing [transaction timelines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) to confirm that disclosure sequencing meets the new "prior to" standard rather than the old "at or before" standard. A single deal that closes with improper sequencing won't just generate a complaint—it gives the buyer a rescission right that survives closing in certain circumstances under the amended MCL 565.960(3).

## Continuing Education and the Transition Period

LARA has published a transition guidance memo establishing that transactions with accepted offers before August 1, 2026, may use the existing forms through closing, provided closing occurs before October 1, 2026. Any transaction with an accepted offer on or after August 1 must use the new forms exclusively.

This two-month grace period does not apply to the wire fraud advisory requirement. SB 221's effective date is absolute: Form WF-1 must be used in all active transactions beginning August 1, regardless of when the offer was accepted. The Legislature specifically excluded the wire fraud provisions from the transition window because of the urgency of the fraud risk.

For agents managing multiple active deals across the transition date, tracking which form set applies to which transaction becomes a serious administrative burden. Building a system to [track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) with different compliance requirements is no longer optional—it's the difference between clean closings and rescission exposure.

## Liability Exposure: What's Actually at Stake

The financial exposure under the 2026 amendments is materially higher than what Michigan agents have faced before. Beyond the $5,000 per-violation fines for individual agents and $10,000 for brokers, the rescission rights created by the new statutes give buyers extraordinary leverage.

> Under MCL 565.960(3), a buyer who discovers a disclosure sequencing violation may rescind the purchase agreement and recover earnest money, inspection costs, appraisal fees, and reasonable attorney's fees from the responsible licensee—not from the seller.

That provision—making the agent personally liable for the buyer's transaction costs—is new. It reflects the Legislature's intent to shift enforcement from regulatory complaints to private civil actions. Agents effectively become guarantors of their own compliance, and the cost of a single mistake can exceed the commission earned on the transaction.

The Michigan Court of Appeals has not yet interpreted these provisions, but the plain language is unambiguous. Compliance attorneys across the state are advising licensees to treat these deadlines with the same seriousness as contractual closing dates.
