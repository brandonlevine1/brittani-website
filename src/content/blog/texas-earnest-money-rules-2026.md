## Why Texas Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026 Demand Your Attention

Mishandling earnest money remains one of the fastest paths to a Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) complaint. The rules governing Texas earnest money & escrow laws 2026 have not loosened—if anything, enforcement has intensified as transaction volumes rebound and TREC audits increase. Agents who treat escrow deposits as an administrative afterthought expose themselves, their brokers, and their clients to financial and legal liability that no commission check is worth.

Texas is one of a handful of states where the real estate commission prescribes mandatory contract forms, meaning the escrow provisions aren't negotiable boilerplate drafted by an attorney on a deal-by-deal basis. They are standardized, and TREC expects strict compliance. The governing statutes sit primarily in the Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1101, the Texas Property Code, and [TREC's administrative rules under Title 22 of the Texas Administrative Code](https://www.trec.texas.gov/laws-and-rules/rules).

## The Statutory Framework Agents Must Know

**Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.652 lists specific grounds for disciplinary action, and commingling or misappropriating trust funds is near the top.** A single violation can trigger license suspension or revocation—no warning letter, no slap on the wrist. TREC treats trust fund violations with the same severity as fraud.

The Texas Property Code Section 5.007 governs escrow disputes when parties cannot agree on disbursement. It permits an escrow agent to interplead funds into court, but it also sets expectations about how long an escrow agent may hold contested money before acting. Agents who pressure title companies to release funds without mutual written authorization are inviting liability.

TREC Rule 535.146 is the operational backbone. It mandates that earnest money received by a license holder must be delivered to the escrow agent named in the contract or deposited into the broker's trust account by the close of the second business day after execution of the contract. There is no grace period for "the check is still in my car."

## Forms That Control the Process

Texas agents work with TREC-promulgated forms, and the earnest money provisions live inside several of them. The most commonly used is the One to Four Family Residential Contract (Resale), designated TREC Form 20-17. Paragraph 5 of that contract specifies the earnest money amount, the form of payment, the escrow agent, and the deposit deadline.

When deals go sideways, agents will reach for the TREC Release of Earnest Money form (TREC Form 38-8). Both buyer and seller must sign this form before the escrow agent can disburse. Agents cannot unilaterally instruct a title company to release funds by citing a "verbal agreement" between the parties—written authorization is non-negotiable.

| Form | Number | Primary Function |
|------|--------|-----------------|
| One to Four Family Residential Contract (Resale) | TREC 20-17 | Sets earnest money terms in Paragraph 5 |
| New Home Contract (Incomplete Construction) | TREC 23-18 | Earnest money for new builds |
| Release of Earnest Money | TREC 38-8 | Authorizes disbursement after termination |
| Amendment to Contract | TREC 39-9 | Modifies earnest money amount or escrow agent |

Agents who attempt to substitute their own release language or skip TREC 38-8 entirely risk TREC enforcement action. The promulgated forms exist for a reason: uniformity, consumer protection, and a clear audit trail.

## Deadlines and Deposit Mechanics

**The deposit clock starts ticking the moment the last party executes the contract, not when the agent "gets around to it."** Under TREC Rule 535.146, if the agent receives the earnest money, they must deliver it to the title company or deposit it into the broker's trust account by the close of the second business day. Weekends and state holidays do not count.

If the buyer delivers the earnest money directly to the title company—which is increasingly common—the agent's obligation shifts to confirmation rather than physical delivery. Smart agents document this with a receipt or email confirmation from the escrow officer. Documentation matters when TREC comes asking questions six months later.

The TREC 20-17 contract also specifies that if the buyer fails to deliver the earnest money within the time required, it constitutes a default under Paragraph 15. The seller then gains the right to exercise remedies, which may include terminating the contract and retaining the earnest money as liquidated damages if elected under Paragraph 15B. Agents who fail to communicate this deadline clearly to buyers often find themselves caught in disputes that could have been avoided with a single calendar reminder. ## Common Mistakes That Trigger TREC Complaints

Five errors account for the majority of earnest money complaints filed with TREC. Every one of them is preventable.

**First: holding the check.** Agents who keep earnest money checks in a desk drawer, briefcase, or glove compartment past the two-business-day window violate TREC Rule 535.146. The excuse that the buyer "asked me to hold it until financing was confirmed" does not override the rule. The deposit obligation is absolute once the contract is executed.

Second: depositing into the wrong account. Earnest money must go into the broker's trust account or to the escrow agent named in the contract. An agent depositing funds into their personal operating account—even temporarily—commits commingling under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.652(b)(3). This can result in license revocation on first offense.

Third: releasing funds without TREC Form 38-8. When a deal terminates during the option period, some agents verbally instruct the title company to return the earnest money to the buyer. Without a signed Release of Earnest Money form, the title company should refuse. Agents who bypass this step create ambiguity that festers into disputes and potential lawsuits.

Fourth: failing to specify the escrow agent in the contract. Paragraph 5 of the TREC 20-17 requires naming the title company or attorney who will hold funds. Leaving this blank—or writing "TBD"—creates an unenforceable provision and confusion about where the money should go. TREC expects the escrow agent to be identified at contract execution.

Fifth: not distinguishing between the option fee and earnest money. The option fee (Paragraph 23 of TREC 20-17) is paid directly to the seller and is independent of the earnest money. Agents who conflate these two payments—or who instruct buyers to write a single check for both—create accounting nightmares and potential contract defects. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability for their agents' trust fund handling under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.803. A broker who fails to supervise earnest money procedures is personally exposed—TREC can sanction the broker even when the individual agent committed the violation.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|-------------------|---------------|-----------|
| Trust account reconciliation | Bank statements vs. ledger entries | Monthly |
| Deposit timing compliance | Date of contract execution vs. date of deposit | Every transaction |
| TREC 38-8 on terminated files | Signed release in every dead deal file | At file closure |
| Named escrow agent in contracts | Paragraph 5 completeness | At contract review |
| Option fee vs. earnest money separation | Separate checks, separate documentation | At contract review |

**Brokers should implement a same-day review process for all executed contracts.** Waiting until closing to audit file compliance is too late. If an agent held a check for five days before depositing it, the violation already occurred—and the broker's failure to catch it compounds the liability.

TREC's Broker Responsibility Advisory (revised periodically and available on the TREC website) explicitly states that brokers must establish policies and procedures to ensure compliance with trust account rules. "I didn't know my agent was doing that" is not a defense that holds up before the commission.

## Texas Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026: Penalties and Enforcement Reality

TREC's enforcement arm does not treat earnest money violations as minor infractions. Under the agency's penalty matrix, trust fund mishandling typically results in a minimum $1,000 fine per occurrence, with suspension or revocation on the table for repeat offenders or cases involving consumer harm. The commission publishes enforcement actions on its website—your name, your violation, your penalty, all publicly searchable.

Beyond TREC sanctions, agents face civil liability. A buyer whose earnest money was improperly disbursed can sue the agent and broker for conversion. Sellers who lost deals because of deposit-timing failures can pursue damages for breach of fiduciary duty. Insurance carriers have increasingly carved out trust fund violations from E&O coverage, meaning agents may bear these costs personally.

Criminal exposure also exists in extreme cases. Misappropriating client funds exceeding $2,500 constitutes theft under the Texas Penal Code, a state jail felony carrying up to two years of incarceration. This is not theoretical—TREC has referred cases to county district attorneys.

## Protecting Your License and Your Clients

The best defense is mechanical consistency. Develop a workflow that triggers the moment a contract is executed: earnest money received, documented, and delivered within the two-business-day window every single time. No exceptions for weekends, holidays, or "the buyer said they'd bring it Monday." If the contract is signed, the clock is running.

For agents managing multiple active transactions simultaneously, the risk of a missed deposit deadline multiplies. A [system for tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) is not optional—it is the minimum standard of practice for anyone handling more than a handful of files per month.

Texas earnest money & escrow laws 2026 reward agents and brokers who treat compliance as infrastructure rather than an afterthought. The agents who build reliable systems around deposit deadlines, form execution, and trust account documentation are the ones who never see the inside of a TREC hearing room. That peace of mind is worth far more than any single commission.
