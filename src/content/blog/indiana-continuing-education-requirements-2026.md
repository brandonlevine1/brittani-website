## Indiana Continuing Education Requirements 2026: The Full Breakdown

The Indiana continuing education requirements 2026 cycle brings no dramatic overhauls, but the enforcement posture of the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC) has shifted noticeably tighter over the past two years. Agents who coast on last-minute completions or assume their broker is tracking compliance for them are the ones most likely to find themselves with a lapsed license and an active listing that suddenly has no authorized agent behind it. This guide covers every hour requirement, form number, penalty structure, and broker obligation you need to know for the current renewal period.

Indiana operates under Indiana Code Title 25, Article 34.1, which governs all real estate licensing and education mandates. The Indiana Professional Licensing Agency (IPLA) administers the mechanics of renewal, while IREC sets the substantive education standards. If you have not logged into your [IPLA online portal](https://www.in.gov/pla/) recently, now is the time—your renewal deadline is tied to your original license issuance date, not a universal calendar cutoff.

## Hour Requirements and Core Course Mandates

**Every Indiana licensee must complete 12 hours of approved continuing education during each three-year renewal cycle.** This applies equally to salespersons and brokers. Of those 12 hours, a mandatory core course is prescribed by IREC each cycle and typically runs four hours. The remaining eight hours are elective, drawn from any IREC-approved provider.

The 2024-2027 core course—which applies to anyone renewing in 2026—focuses on updates to Indiana agency law, recent disciplinary case studies, and changes to federal fair housing enforcement. IREC publishes the approved course list annually through a bulletin on the IPLA website. Agents cannot substitute the core course with additional electives, regardless of topic relevance.

| Requirement | Salesperson | Broker |
|---|---|---|
| Total CE hours per cycle | 12 | 12 |
| Mandatory core course hours | 4 | 4 |
| Elective hours | 8 | 8 |
| Renewal cycle length | 3 years | 3 years |
| Post-license education (first renewal only) | 30 hours | N/A |

First-time renewals carry an additional burden. Salespersons renewing for the first time must complete 30 hours of post-license education on top of the standard 12 CE hours. This catches some newer agents off guard—especially those who obtained their license late in 2023 or early 2024 and are hitting their first renewal window now.

## Key Forms and Filing Procedures

Indiana's renewal process centers on Form 49560, the Real Estate License Renewal Application, submitted through the IPLA's online licensing system. You will not find a paper option for CE verification—IREC eliminated that path in 2021. Course completion data is transmitted electronically by approved providers, but agents bear ultimate responsibility for confirming the hours appear in their IPLA profile.

If hours are missing or a provider failed to report, agents must file a CE Verification Request with supporting certificates of completion. Retain every completion certificate for at least four years. IREC audits are random and can surface well after renewal approval.

**Brokers managing affiliated licensees will also interact with Form 46245, the Broker Affiliation/Termination Form.** This form becomes relevant when an agent's license lapses due to CE non-compliance and the broker must officially terminate the affiliation. Filing this form promptly protects the brokerage from vicarious liability for any unauthorized activity by a lapsed licensee.

## What Happens When You Fail to Comply

The penalty structure under IC 25-34.1-8 is not ambiguous. An agent who practices on a lapsed license faces civil penalties of up to $1,000 per violation, and each transaction conducted while lapsed can constitute a separate violation. Beyond fines, any commission earned during a lapsed period is potentially unenforceable—meaning the agent may not be able to collect on closed deals.

IREC can also initiate disciplinary proceedings that result in license suspension or revocation. The Commission publishes disciplinary orders quarterly, and CE violations consistently appear among the most common grounds for action. A 2025 IREC disciplinary summary showed 23% of all enforcement actions involved lapsed licenses tied directly to incomplete continuing education.

For the consumer-facing side, a lapsed license raises serious questions about any purchase agreement signed during that period. While Indiana courts have not universally voided transactions on this basis alone, buyers and sellers have used it as leverage to renegotiate or cancel deals. The risk to your client relationships—and your errors and omissions insurance coverage—is substantial. If you are already dealing with the challenge of [missed deadlines in your pipeline](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), a lapsed license compounds every existing vulnerability.

## Common Mistakes Indiana Agents Make

**Mistake number one: assuming the three-year cycle resets on January 1.** It does not. Your cycle is tied to your individual license expiration date. An agent licensed on March 15, 2024 has until March 15, 2027 to complete CE—not December 31, 2026. Confusing these dates is the single most common cause of accidental lapsing.

Mistake number two: completing courses from non-approved providers. Indiana maintains a specific approved provider list, and courses from other states—even reciprocal states—do not automatically count. Agents who hold licenses in multiple states sometimes complete another state's CE and assume it transfers. Unless the provider holds separate Indiana approval, those hours are worthless for IPLA reporting purposes. Understanding [license reciprocity and portability](/blog/washington-license-reciprocity-portability-2026) in other states does not guarantee the same rules apply in Indiana.

Mistake number three: failing to verify electronic reporting. Providers have 10 business days to report completions to IPLA, but errors happen. Agents who complete courses in the final week before expiration leave zero margin for reporting delays. If the hours do not appear in the system by your expiration date, your license lapses—even if you physically sat through the course.

Mistake number four: skipping the core course in favor of all electives. Some agents rack up 12 or more elective hours and assume they are compliant. Without the specific IREC-mandated core course, however, the renewal application will be rejected. The core course must match the exact title and course number published for your renewal cycle.

Mistake number five: relying solely on your broker to track deadlines. While brokers have supervisory obligations, the legal duty to maintain an active license rests entirely with the individual licensee under IC 25-34.1-3-4. "My broker didn't remind me" is not a defense recognized by IREC.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

876 IAC 1-1-14 places affirmative obligations on managing brokers to supervise licensee compliance. This is not a suggestion—it is an administrative rule with teeth. A broker who permits an affiliated agent to practice on a lapsed license faces independent disciplinary action, including fines and potential suspension of the brokerage license itself.

| Broker Obligation | Regulatory Source | Consequence of Failure |
|---|---|---|
| Verify CE completion before renewal deadline | 876 IAC 1-1-14 | Disciplinary action against broker |
| File Form 46245 upon agent lapse | IC 25-34.1-4-5 | Vicarious liability for agent's unauthorized acts |
| Maintain records of agent compliance | 876 IAC 1-3-11 | Fines up to $1,000; audit failures |
| Report known violations to IREC | IC 25-34.1-6-11 | Potential license suspension |

**Brokers should build a quarterly audit cadence rather than checking compliance only at renewal time.** A simple spreadsheet tracking each agent's expiration date, core course completion status, and elective hours logged provides early warning when someone falls behind. Some brokerages assign this task to their transaction coordinator—and if the administrative burden feels unreasonable, that is exactly the kind of operational gap where [systematized checklist processes](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) prevent costly oversights.

IREC has signaled through recent rulemaking discussions that it may begin requiring brokers to submit annual compliance certifications as early as 2027. Getting ahead of that potential requirement now builds the audit infrastructure you will need regardless.

## Approved Course Delivery Methods and Provider Standards

Indiana permits three delivery formats for continuing education: live classroom, live synchronous webinar, and asynchronous online self-study. All three carry equal credit weight, but the provider must be separately approved for each format. An instructor certified for classroom delivery cannot simply move their course online without obtaining additional IPLA approval.

For online asynchronous courses, Indiana requires timed progression controls—meaning agents cannot fast-forward through modules. Courses must include interim knowledge checks and a final assessment with a minimum passing score of 75%. Any course that allows completion in less than actual instructional time is non-compliant, and hours from such courses can be retroactively disallowed during an IREC audit.

> "The Commission may deny credit for any course that does not meet the minimum contact hour requirements or instructional standards set forth in 876 IAC 1-3." — Indiana Administrative Code, Title 876, Article 1, Rule 3

Agents should confirm their chosen provider displays a current IPLA approval number on all course materials and completion certificates. If that number is absent, treat it as a red flag and verify directly with IPLA before investing time in the coursework.
