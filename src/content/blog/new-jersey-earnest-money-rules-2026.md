## Understanding New Jersey Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

If you hold a license in the Garden State and handle purchase transactions, **New Jersey earnest money & escrow laws 2026** should be burned into your operational memory. Mishandling a deposit—even by a single day—can trigger regulatory action from the New Jersey Real Estate Commission (NJREC), kill the deal, and expose your brokerage to civil liability. The rules are not suggestions; they sit inside N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.25 and related statutes with teeth.

New Jersey is one of the few states that still mandates attorney review as a near-universal contract contingency, which creates an additional escrow-timing complication agents in other states never face. The interplay between attorney review periods and deposit deadlines is where most compliance errors originate. This post breaks down the statutory framework, the forms you need, the mistakes agents actually make, and what brokers must audit to stay clean.

## Statutory Framework: Where the Rules Live

The primary regulation governing earnest money deposits in New Jersey is [N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.25](https://www.njconsumeraffairs.gov/rec/Pages/default.aspx), administered by the NJREC under the Division of Consumer Affairs. This rule requires that all money entrusted to a licensee be deposited into a trust account "promptly"—defined as the next business day after contract ratification unless the parties have agreed otherwise in writing.

N.J.S.A. 45:15-17 further outlines the grounds for license suspension or revocation, including commingling personal funds with trust account funds and failure to account for money belonging to others. Violations carry fines of up to $5,000 per offense, and repeat offenders face permanent revocation.

**The distinction between "receipt" and "acceptance" matters enormously.** An agent may physically receive a check at the time of offer submission, but the deposit clock does not start until both parties execute the contract. Agents who deposit before acceptance risk converting funds without authorization; agents who wait too long after acceptance risk a regulatory violation.

## Key Forms and Documents Agents Must Use

New Jersey Realtors (formerly NJAR) publishes standard contract forms that most agents rely on for residential transactions. Form 118—the Standard Form of Real Estate Contract—includes specific paragraphs addressing the deposit amount, the identity of the escrow holder, and the conditions under which funds are released or returned.

| Form / Document | Purpose | When It Applies |
|---|---|---|
| NJAR Form 118 | Standard residential purchase contract | All standard residential deals |
| NJAR Form 125 | Escrow agreement addendum | When escrow terms differ from standard |
| Trust Account Ledger | Per-transaction deposit tracking | Every deposit received |
| Form CE-200 | Broker trust account registration | Initial registration and annual updates |
| Written Consent to Hold | Authorization to delay deposit | When parties agree to non-standard timing |

The NJAR Form 125 is especially important when the contract directs funds to an attorney escrow rather than the listing broker's trust account. Without this form or equivalent written direction, the listing broker is presumed responsible for depositing and holding the money—even if the buyer's agent verbally communicated a different arrangement.

Agents should also be aware that the trust account itself must be registered with the NJREC. Brokers file Form CE-200 at the time of licensure and must update the Commission if the account number or banking institution changes. Operating without a properly registered account is a separate violation from mishandling individual deposits.

## Attorney Review and Its Effect on Deposit Timing

**New Jersey's attorney review period—typically three business days—creates a timing gap that confuses even experienced agents.** Under standard contract language, either party's attorney may disapprove the contract within this period, effectively voiding the agreement. The question becomes: do you deposit the earnest money during attorney review, or after?

The regulatory answer is clear. N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.25 requires deposit by the next business day after acceptance. The contract is technically "accepted" upon mutual execution, not upon expiration of attorney review. That means the listing broker must deposit the check even though an attorney could disapprove the contract a day later.

If the contract is disapproved during attorney review and the deposit has already been banked, the broker must return the funds to the buyer promptly. Some brokerages address this friction by including language in the contract that specifies the deposit will be held uncashed until attorney review expires—but that written agreement must exist in the contract or an addendum. Relying on a verbal understanding or office custom exposes the broker to a citation from the NJREC.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Escrow Deposits

Five recurring errors show up in NJREC disciplinary actions and civil complaints related to earnest money handling. Each one is avoidable with basic process discipline.

**First, agents hold checks in their desk drawer.** The deposit does not belong to the agent—it belongs to the transaction. Physically retaining a check past the next-business-day deadline, even with good intentions, is a violation of N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.25 regardless of whether the funds are ultimately deposited correctly.

Second, agents fail to get written consent when deposit timing deviates from the statutory default. A buyer who says "please don't cash my check until Friday" over the phone has not provided the kind of written agreement the regulation requires. The agreement must be documented in the contract, an addendum, or at minimum a signed written communication that the brokerage retains.

Third, agents misidentify the escrow holder in the contract. When Form 118 lists the listing broker as escrow agent but the parties intend for an attorney to hold funds, the mismatch creates liability. If the attorney loses the funds or misapplies them, the listing broker may still face claims because the contract named them as responsible.

Fourth, agents on the buyer side fail to confirm that the check was actually deposited. The buyer's agent has a fiduciary duty to their client, and that duty includes verifying that the deposit reached the trust account. A simple confirmation email satisfies this—but many agents never ask.

Fifth, agents release deposits without proper authorization. In a disputed transaction, both the buyer and seller must sign a release before the broker can disburse funds. Releasing to one party based on a verbal claim—even a seemingly clear-cut contract default—opens the broker to liability from the other side. When parties cannot agree, the broker must either hold the funds or interplead them with the Superior Court.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry personal liability for trust account violations committed by their agents. N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.26 requires brokers to maintain complete records of all trust account transactions, including individual ledger cards for every deposit showing the property address, parties' names, amounts, dates of receipt, and dates of deposit.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Regulatory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Trust account bank reconciliation | Monthly | N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.26 |
| Individual ledger card review | Per transaction | N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.26 |
| Deposit timing verification | Per transaction | N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.25 |
| Written consent documentation | As applicable | N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.25 |
| Trust account registration status | Annually | NJREC Form CE-200 |

**A broker who discovers a deposit was held past the deadline cannot simply backdate the entry.** The correct response is to deposit immediately, document the error internally, and determine whether the delay caused harm to any party. Falsifying ledger entries is a separate violation that compounds the original problem.

Monthly reconciliations should match the bank statement balance against the total of all individual ledger cards. Any discrepancy—even a few dollars—must be investigated immediately. Shortages in trust accounts trigger mandatory reporting obligations, and the NJREC has historically treated unexplained shortages as evidence of commingling or conversion.

Brokers reviewing their [transaction handoff processes](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) should build deposit verification into the first checkpoint after contract execution. If the handoff from agent to transaction coordinator does not explicitly confirm deposit status, the brokerage has a systemic gap.

## Liability When Things Go Wrong

When a broker or agent violates New Jersey's escrow rules, the consequences cascade. The NJREC can initiate an administrative complaint under N.J.S.A. 45:15-17, which may result in fines, mandatory education, license suspension, or revocation. These proceedings are public and appear on the licensee's record permanently.

Beyond regulatory action, the aggrieved party—usually the buyer—can pursue civil remedies. If a deposit is lost because the broker failed to maintain proper trust account controls, the broker is personally liable for the amount. Insurance may cover some claims, but E&O policies frequently exclude trust account mishandling from coverage.

Deal cancellation is also a realistic outcome. If a buyer learns their deposit was not properly handled, they may invoke the implied covenant of good faith or argue that the broker's non-compliance constitutes a material breach permitting termination. Courts in New Jersey have sided with buyers in these scenarios, particularly when the agent's failure to deposit created uncertainty about the safety of the funds.

The financial exposure extends further when you consider the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing)—lost commission, damaged client relationships, and potential referral network fallout all compound the regulatory penalty.

## Disputed Deposits and Interpleader Actions

When both parties claim the deposit and refuse to sign a mutual release, the listing broker sits in a difficult position. New Jersey law does not permit the broker to unilaterally decide who gets the money. The broker has two options: continue holding the funds in trust indefinitely while the parties resolve their dispute, or file an interpleader action in Superior Court.

> Under N.J.S.A. 2A:16-11 et seq., an interpleader action allows the stakeholder to deposit contested funds with the court and withdraw from the dispute, leaving the court to determine rightful ownership.

Filing an interpleader costs money—court filing fees, potential attorney fees—and those costs typically come from the escrowed funds unless the contract provides otherwise. Brokers should ensure their contracts include language allocating interpleader costs to the defaulting party or splitting them between the disputants.

The worst response to a disputed deposit is inaction combined with silence. Brokers who fail to communicate with both parties about the status of held funds invite accusations of favoritism or, worse, conversion. Document every communication and hold the funds exactly as received until a signed release or court order directs otherwise.
