## New York Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026

The question of New York attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 trips up even experienced agents who relocate from states where title companies run the entire show. New York operates under a long-established custom backed by case law and bar opinions that treats real estate closings as legal proceedings requiring attorney involvement. Understanding who sits at the table — and what each party is authorized to do — protects your license, your deal, and your commission.

Unlike most of the country, New York does not allow title companies to act as the sole closing agent. This distinction matters because the line between title services and the practice of law is enforced aggressively in the state, and agents who blur it risk more than a slapped wrist.

## The Legal Framework: Why New York Requires Attorneys

**New York Judiciary Law Section 478 prohibits any person from practicing law without admission to the bar.** The statute does not carve out a real estate closing exemption. Courts have repeatedly interpreted document preparation, contract negotiation, and advising parties on legal rights during a transaction as the practice of law.

The landmark opinion in *Matter of New York County Lawyers' Association v. Dacey* (1967) established that preparing legal instruments for others constitutes unauthorized practice. More specifically, the 2009 Joint Report of the New York State Bar Association and the Real Property Law Section clarified that closings require attorney supervision because nearly every step — from reviewing the contract of sale to explaining the mortgage note — involves legal judgment.

The New York State Department of Financial Services (DFS) regulates title insurance companies under Insurance Law Article 64. Title companies may issue policies, conduct title searches, and hold escrow funds. But the DFS has drawn a firm boundary: title agents cannot explain legal documents to buyers or sellers, draft deeds, or give opinions on title defects without attorney involvement.

## What the Title Company Actually Does at Closing

A title company in New York performs a defined set of functions that complement — not replace — attorney services. The title agent orders and reviews the abstract of title, issues the title commitment, and provides the final title insurance policy after recording. They also handle disbursement of funds through escrow accounts regulated under 11 NYCRR 35.

| Function | Attorney | Title Company |
|----------|----------|---------------|
| Contract of sale review | Yes | No |
| Deed preparation | Yes | No |
| Title search and abstract | No (orders it) | Yes |
| Title insurance issuance | No | Yes |
| Document recording | Sometimes | Yes |
| Escrow fund disbursement | Sometimes | Yes |
| Legal advice to parties | Yes | No |
| TRID/closing disclosure review | Yes | Sometimes (lender coordination) |

The title company's closing protection letter (CPL) protects the lender against fraud or mishandling by the title agent. It does not substitute for the attorney's fiduciary obligation to the client. Agents who tell buyers "the title company handles everything" are misstating New York practice and potentially exposing themselves to complaints before the Department of State's Division of Licensing Services.

## What Happens When Agents Get This Wrong

The consequences of ignoring New York's attorney-closing custom fall into three categories: deal disruption, license jeopardy, and civil liability.

**If a closing proceeds without proper attorney involvement and documents contain errors, the entire chain of title can be clouded.** A defective deed — prepared by someone not authorized to practice law — may be challenged years later, and the agent who facilitated that closing could be named in a malpractice suit as a contributing party.

The New York Department of State (DOS) can impose disciplinary action against licensees under Real Property Law Section 441-c for demonstrating untrustworthiness or incompetence. An agent who repeatedly steers clients away from retaining counsel, or who allows a title company to perform attorney functions, creates a record that DOS investigators will review if a complaint is filed.

Fines under RPL 441-c can include license suspension or revocation. In 2024, DOS issued guidance reiterating that agents must not engage in activities that constitute the unauthorized practice of law, including filling in blanks on legal documents beyond standard [NYSCAR forms](https://dos.ny.gov/real-estate) designated for licensee use.

## Forms Agents Are Authorized to Use

New York agents work primarily with forms approved by the New York State Association of Realtors (NYSAR) and local board-specific documents. The key distinction: agents may fill in factual blanks on pre-approved forms but cannot draft legal language or modify contract terms.

The standard Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement (NYSAR Form 100) and the Buyer Agency Agreement (NYSAR Form 110) are agent-authorized documents. The Contract of Sale, however, is an attorney-drafted document in New York — not an agent form. This surprises agents coming from states where the purchase agreement is a Realtor-association form filled out at the kitchen table.

Agents should also be familiar with the Disclosure of Agency Relationships form required under RPL Section 443. This form must be presented to both buyers and sellers at first substantive contact, and failure to provide it triggers a rebuttable presumption that the agent acted as a dual agent. That presumption alone [can kill a deal at the final hour](/blog/where-deals-break-down) if either party raises an objection.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make at New York Closings

**Mistake one: drafting contract riders or addenda without attorney review.** Even a simple "as-is" rider qualifies as a legal modification. Agents who draft these expose themselves to unauthorized practice of law claims and give the opposing party grounds to void the contract.

Mistake two: telling clients they do not need an attorney to save money. While attorney representation is technically elective — no statute says a party must hire one — the practical reality is that lenders, title companies, and opposing counsel all assume attorneys are present. A party without counsel at a New York closing often cannot close because no one will explain the documents to them, and the title company is prohibited from doing so.

Mistake three: confusing the title closer with the closing attorney. Some title companies send a "closer" who coordinates document signing. This person is not providing legal representation. Agents who introduce the title closer as someone who "handles the legal side" create false expectations and potential liability.

Mistake four: failing to coordinate attorney review periods. The standard New York contract includes a three-day attorney review period during which either side's counsel can propose modifications or cancel the contract. Agents who [miss this deadline](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) or pressure clients to waive review create downstream problems that surface at closing.

Mistake five: not verifying attorney escrow handling. In New York, the buyer's down payment typically goes into the seller's attorney escrow account — not to the title company. Agents who direct funds to the wrong party delay closings and raise red flags about [wire fraud protocols](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process).

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers in New York carry heightened responsibility under RPL Section 441(1), which requires brokers to exercise reasonable supervision over their agents' transactions. A broker who does not review closing procedures is personally liable for agents' missteps.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|------------|---------------|-----------|
| Agency disclosure (RPL 443) | Signed form in file before contract | Every transaction |
| Attorney engagement | Confirmation both parties retained counsel | Before contract execution |
| Escrow deposit routing | Funds sent to correct attorney escrow | Within 48 hours of contract |
| Contract preparation | Drafted by attorney, not agent | Every transaction |
| Closing coordination | Attorney confirmed attendance or review | 7 days before closing |

Brokers should maintain a transaction compliance checklist — not just for their own protection, but because DOS auditors review files during routine office inspections. A missing RPL 443 form or evidence that an agent prepared a contract of sale triggers an immediate violation notice.

Quarterly file audits catch patterns before they become systemic violations. The broker's office policy manual should explicitly state that agents are prohibited from drafting contracts, riders, or addenda, and that all closings require confirmed attorney participation for both sides.

## The 2026 Enforcement Landscape

The New York State Bar Association's Real Property Law Section issued updated guidance in late 2025 reinforcing that virtual closings — which became common post-2020 — do not change the attorney requirement. Whether the closing happens via Zoom, at a title company's conference room, or in an attorney's office, the same rules apply. Someone licensed to practice law in New York must supervise the transaction.

DOS has increased its complaint investigation staff heading into 2026, with a stated focus on unauthorized practice facilitation by licensees. The message is clear: agents who play attorney, or who facilitate title companies acting beyond their authority, face real enforcement.
