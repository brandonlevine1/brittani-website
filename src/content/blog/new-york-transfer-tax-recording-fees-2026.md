## Understanding New York Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Getting the numbers wrong on transfer taxes or recording fees does not just embarrass you at the closing table—it can kill a deal outright. The New York transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 reflect a layered system of state, city, and county charges that agents must quote accurately during negotiations, disclose properly on estimated closing statements, and verify before documents hit the county clerk's office. Misquoting a fee by even a few hundred dollars erodes client trust and exposes your brokerage to liability.

New York's transfer tax framework is governed primarily by Article 31 of the New York Tax Law (Sections 1400–1410) and administered by the New York State Department of Taxation and Finance. Recording fees, by contrast, fall under the authority of individual county clerks and are set by Real Property Law Section 291 and County Law provisions. Agents operating in New York City face a second overlay from the NYC Department of Finance under Title 11, Chapter 21 of the NYC Administrative Code.

This post breaks down every rate, form, and filing requirement you need to protect your clients and your license heading into the second half of 2026.

## State Transfer Tax Rates and Thresholds

**New York State imposes a real estate transfer tax (RETT) of $2 per $500 of consideration, which translates to a 0.4% rate on every conveyance.** For residential properties where the consideration exceeds $3 million, an additional "supplemental" transfer tax of 0.65% applies under Tax Law Section 1402-b, bringing the combined state rate to 1.05% on the full amount.

The seller is the statutory obligor under Section 1404, but the buyer becomes secondarily liable if the seller defaults. In practice, purchase contracts in downstate markets often shift portions of the tax burden contractually—something you need to flag to clients early so net sheets stay accurate.

Here is how the state transfer tax stacks up for common sale price tiers:

| Sale Price | Base Tax (0.4%) | Supplemental (0.65%) | Total State Transfer Tax |
|---|---|---|---|
| $500,000 | $2,000 | $0 | $2,000 |
| $1,500,000 | $6,000 | $0 | $6,000 |
| $3,500,000 | $14,000 | $22,750 | $36,750 |
| $5,000,000 | $20,000 | $32,500 | $52,500 |

Note that the supplemental tax kicks in on the entire consideration once the $3 million threshold is crossed—not just the excess above $3 million. This is the single most common miscalculation agents make on high-value listings.

## NYC Transfer Tax and Mansion Tax Additions

Agents working in the five boroughs deal with an entirely separate municipal transfer tax layered on top of the state charges. The NYC Real Property Transfer Tax (RPTT) is set at 1% for residential conveyances of $500,000 or less and 1.425% for amounts above that threshold. Commercial properties carry rates of 1.425% (at or below $500,000) and 2.625% (above $500,000).

Then there is the mansion tax. Under Tax Law Section 1402-a, any residential purchase of $1 million or more triggers a 1% tax paid by the buyer. In 2019 the legislature added a graduated surcharge for NYC properties at or above $2 million, escalating in eight tiers up to 3.9% for purchases of $25 million or more. Those graduated rates remain in effect for 2026 and are codified in Section 1402-b.

**Failing to disclose the mansion tax to a buyer before contract signing is a compliance failure that can result in a TREC complaint or, more immediately, a renegotiation demand that blows your deal timeline.** Agents should address this in the first conversation about offer price, not at attorney review. ## Recording Fees by County

Recording fees in New York are not uniform. Each county clerk sets its own schedule within the parameters allowed by state law. The base structure under Real Property Law Section 333 typically includes a per-page charge, a cover-page filing fee, and various surcharges for capital improvements, cultural education, or document management funds.

| Fee Component | NYC (5 Boroughs) | Westchester | Suffolk | Albany |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| First page (deed) | $77 | $45 | $44 | $40 |
| Each additional page | $5 | $5 | $5 | $5 |
| Cover page (RP-5217) | $175 | $125 | $125 | $125 |
| Mortgage recording (basic) | Varies by amount | $45 first page | $44 first page | $40 first page |
| Cultural education surcharge | Included | $15 | $15 | $15 |

NYC charges are governed by the City Register's office (ACRIS) rather than a county clerk, which creates a separate e-filing workflow. Agents should not assume that upstate fee quotes apply to downstate closings, or vice versa.

**The mortgage recording tax is separate from recording fees and is one of the largest buyer closing costs in New York.** It ranges from 1.8% (loans below $500,000 in NYC) to 1.925% (loans of $500,000 or more in NYC), with slightly lower rates in counties outside the Metropolitan Commuter Transportation District. This tax is split between the buyer and lender by statute, with the borrower responsible for all but 0.25%.

## Forms Agents Must Know

Every transfer in New York requires a package of state and local forms to be filed at closing. The primary forms include:

The TP-584 (Combined Real Estate Transfer Tax Return, Credit Line Mortgage Certificate, and Certification of Exemption from the Payment of Estimated Personal Income Tax) is the single most important document. It accompanies every deed submitted for recording and must be completed with the correct consideration amount, tax calculations, and exemption claims.

The RP-5217 (Real Property Transfer Report) is required by the Office of Real Property Tax Services and serves as the cover page for recording. Without it, the county clerk will not accept the deed. In NYC, this form is filed through the Automated City Register Information System (ACRIS).

The IT-2663 (Nonresident Real Property Estimated Income Tax Payment) applies when a nonresident seller conveys New York real property. If the seller cannot claim an exemption, estimated income tax must be paid at closing—a surprise cost that agents need to surface during listing presentations with out-of-state owners.

For transactions involving cooperative apartments, Form TP-584.1 replaces the standard TP-584 and captures the unique ownership structure of co-op shares and proprietary leases.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make

The first error is quoting transfer tax based on the wrong threshold. Agents routinely apply the supplemental state tax of 0.65% only to the amount above $3 million rather than to the full consideration. This underquotes the seller's costs by tens of thousands of dollars and forces last-minute renegotiation or seller credits.

The second mistake is ignoring local variations in recording fees. An agent who closes most deals in Nassau County and then takes a Manhattan listing may quote $40 for first-page recording when the ACRIS charge is nearly double that. Small discrepancies across multiple line items add up to meaningful errors on a closing statement.

Third, agents forget to address the IT-2663 obligation for nonresident sellers. The estimated income tax payment can equal 8.82% of the gain, and if it is not arranged before closing, the title company will refuse to disburse proceeds. Deals stall for days while the seller scrambles to get a CPA involved.

Fourth, many agents fail to verify which party is contractually responsible for the NYC RPTT. While the seller pays by default under the Administrative Code, co-op and new development contracts frequently shift this to the buyer. If you draft or review a term sheet without confirming this allocation, your client may face an unexpected five-figure charge.

Fifth, agents neglect to confirm ACRIS filing status post-closing. The City Register's office can reject electronic filings for formatting errors or missing attachments, and if nobody monitors the queue, the deed goes unrecorded for weeks. ## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

**Brokers bear supervisory liability under 19 NYCRR 175.21 for the acts of their sponsored agents, which means transfer tax and recording fee errors can land on the brokerage's doorstep.** A consistent audit process reduces risk and protects the firm's reputation.

Start by requiring every agent to submit a pre-closing checklist that includes the specific transfer tax calculation, the recording fee estimate broken down by county, and confirmation of which party pays each charge. This should be reviewed by the brokerage's compliance officer or transaction coordinator at least 48 hours before the scheduled closing.

Brokers should also mandate that agents use the current year's fee schedules from the relevant county clerk's website—not a memorized figure from last year's closing. Fee schedules update periodically without fanfare, and a stale number in your template creates repeat errors across the office.

Finally, audit post-closing files for proof of recording. The deed and mortgage should appear in the county records within 30 days. If your agents are not confirming recording, you have no way to know whether a client's ownership is actually perfected. This is exactly the type of administrative follow-through that [transaction coordinators handle](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) in high-volume brokerages—and where automation is starting to replace manual tracking.

## Liability Exposure for Non-Compliance

If a transfer tax return is not filed or is filed with materially incorrect information, the New York Department of Taxation and Finance can assess penalties of up to 10% of the unpaid tax plus interest under Tax Law Section 1408. The county clerk will refuse to record the deed until a corrected TP-584 and full payment are submitted, which means the buyer does not have legal title.

For agents, the professional consequences are equally serious. The New York Department of State Division of Licensing Services can investigate complaints related to negligent misrepresentation of closing costs. Under Real Property Law Section 441-c, a license can be suspended or revoked for demonstrated incompetence or untrustworthiness. Even without formal disciplinary action, a pattern of fee miscalculations invites malpractice claims that your E&O carrier will not enjoy defending.

> "Any person required to file a return or pay the tax imposed by this article who fails to file such return or pay such tax...shall be subject to a penalty of ten percent of the amount of tax due." — N.Y. Tax Law Section 1408(a)

The [New York State Department of Taxation and Finance](https://www.tax.ny.gov/bus/transfer/trtidx.htm) publishes current guidance, forms, and rate confirmations that should be bookmarked by every practicing agent in the state.
