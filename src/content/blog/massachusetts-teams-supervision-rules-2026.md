## Why Massachusetts Teams & Supervision Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Massachusetts real estate teams have exploded in popularity over the past five years, but the regulatory framework governing them has not kept pace with the marketing. Understanding Massachusetts teams & supervision rules 2026 is no longer optional—it is the difference between a well-run operation and a license at risk. The Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons, operating under the Division of Professional Licensure, has intensified its scrutiny of team structures, advertising practices, and broker oversight responsibilities.

The core statute governing all licensee conduct is Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 112, Sections 87PP through 87DDD. These sections define who may perform brokerage activities, what constitutes proper supervision, and how violations are adjudicated. If you run a team or belong to one, the 2026 regulatory environment requires you to audit every process from advertising copy to file management.

Unlike states that have enacted specific "team" legislation, Massachusetts regulates teams through its existing broker-salesperson hierarchy. That means many agents operating in team structures unknowingly violate rules designed for individual licensees operating under a single broker.

## The Legal Structure of Teams Under Massachusetts Law

**Massachusetts does not recognize "teams" as a separate legal entity under MGL c. 112.** Every salesperson on a team is individually licensed and directly affiliated with a designated broker. The team itself has no license, no independent authority, and no legal standing separate from the brokerage.

This distinction matters enormously for liability. When a team member makes a misrepresentation on a [seller disclosure](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) or fails to present an offer, the supervising broker bears regulatory responsibility alongside the individual agent. The broker cannot delegate this responsibility to a team leader who holds only a salesperson license.

254 CMR 2.00, the Board's primary administrative regulation, reinforces this hierarchy. It requires that the broker "exercise supervision and control" over all affiliated licensees, regardless of internal team arrangements. A team leader who is not a licensed broker cannot sign off on compliance matters, authorize trust account disbursements, or approve advertising on behalf of the brokerage.

## Advertising Rules That Trip Up Team Leaders

Advertising is where most Massachusetts teams first encounter regulatory trouble. MGL c. 112 §87AAA and 254 CMR 7.00 set strict requirements for how licensees identify themselves in marketing materials. Every advertisement—print, digital, social media, yard signs—must prominently display the licensed name of the brokerage.

Team names are permitted, but they cannot appear more prominently than the brokerage name, and they cannot create the impression that the team is itself a licensed entity. A sign reading "The Smith Group" in large font with "ABC Realty" in tiny print underneath violates the regulation. The Board has issued cease-and-desist orders for exactly this type of infraction.

Here is how advertising requirements break down between individual agents, teams, and brokerages:

| Element | Individual Agent | Team | Brokerage |
|---------|-----------------|------|-----------|
| Brokerage name required | Yes | Yes | N/A (is the brokerage) |
| Team name allowed | N/A | Yes, subordinate to brokerage | N/A |
| Personal name allowed | Yes | Yes, for individual members | Yes (DBA if registered) |
| Phone/email can be personal | Yes | Yes, if brokerage identifiable | Yes |
| "Broker" or "Realty" in team name | Prohibited unless team leader is broker | Prohibited unless team leader is broker | Permitted |

The last row catches people off guard. If your team name includes "Realty," "Brokerage," "Real Estate Company," or similar terms, and the team leader is not a licensed broker, the Board considers it deceptive. Agents have faced license suspensions for this violation alone.

## Broker Supervision Duties: What 254 CMR Requires

**The broker's supervision obligation under 254 CMR 2.00 is non-delegable.** A broker can appoint managers, create internal review systems, and assign compliance tasks—but ultimate responsibility stays with the license holder. This is particularly relevant for large brokerages that host multiple teams, each with its own operational style.

Under the regulation, the broker must ensure that all trust funds are properly handled per MGL c. 112 §87AAA(3), that all required disclosures are delivered, and that licensees do not engage in conduct that would constitute a violation of 254 CMR 3.00 (the grounds for discipline). The broker must also maintain records of all transactions for at least three years after closing.

For teams specifically, this means the broker needs visibility into every file the team touches. Internal team structures that route paperwork through a team leader before the broker sees it create compliance gaps. If the [team leader misses a deadline](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) or fails to deliver a mandatory disclosure, the broker cannot claim ignorance as a defense.

The Board has disciplined brokers who claimed they "trusted" their team leaders to handle compliance. Trust is not a regulatory defense. Documentation is.

## Common Mistakes Massachusetts Teams Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in Board disciplinary actions and audit findings. Each one is entirely avoidable with proper systems.

**First, teams fail to include the brokerage name in social media advertising.** Instagram bios, Facebook page names, and TikTok profiles frequently display only the team name. The Board treats social media identically to print advertising under 254 CMR 7.00. A team's Instagram account named "BostonLuxuryTeam" without the brokerage name visible constitutes a violation.

Second, unlicensed team assistants perform licensed activities. Massachusetts law is explicit: only licensed individuals may show property, discuss terms, negotiate offers, or prepare documents that require professional judgment. Unlicensed assistants may schedule appointments, manage databases, and handle purely administrative tasks. The line blurs when an assistant answers a client's question about a contract term or walks a buyer through a property solo.

Third, teams operate separate trust accounts or hold earnest money outside the brokerage's escrow account. Under MGL c. 112 §87AAA(3), all client funds must be deposited into the broker's designated trust account. Teams cannot maintain independent escrow accounts regardless of their internal commission splits.

Fourth, team leaders sign agency disclosure forms as if they were the broker. Massachusetts requires delivery of the [Mandatory Licensee-Consumer Relationship Disclosure](/blog/where-deals-break-down) at first substantive contact. The form must accurately identify the brokerage, and only the broker (or a properly authorized representative) can establish certain agency relationships on behalf of the firm.

Fifth, teams recruit agents from other brokerages without proper transfer documentation. When an agent joins a team, they must formally transfer their license to the team's brokerage through the Board's online licensing system. Operating under one brokerage while marketing as part of a team under another brokerage is a serious violation that can result in license revocation.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers hosting teams should conduct quarterly audits focused on six specific areas. Random spot-checks are not sufficient when the Board investigates a complaint—they want to see systematic oversight.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Frequency |
|------------|---------------|-----------|
| Advertising compliance | All active social media, websites, signs, and print materials | Quarterly |
| Trust account deposits | Every earnest money deposit traced to brokerage escrow | Per transaction |
| License status verification | Active license status for every team member | Monthly |
| Disclosure delivery documentation | Signed consumer relationship disclosures in every file | Per transaction |
| Unlicensed activity review | Role descriptions and task logs for all unlicensed staff | Quarterly |
| File completeness | All required forms present and properly executed | Per transaction |

**Brokers who fail to maintain these audits face personal liability under MGL c. 112 §87AAA.** The Board can impose fines up to $5,000 per violation, suspend or revoke the broker's license, and refer egregious cases to the Attorney General's office for additional civil or criminal proceedings.

The smartest brokerages build these audits into their [transaction coordination workflows](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) rather than treating them as separate compliance events. When file review happens at every milestone, violations get caught before they become Board complaints.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: Fines, Suspensions, and Deal Fallout

The Board of Registration has broad disciplinary authority under MGL c. 112 §87AAA and 254 CMR 3.00. Penalties range from letters of reprimand to full license revocation. Between these extremes, the Board commonly issues monetary fines, mandatory continuing education requirements, and supervised practice periods.

For advertising violations, first offenses typically result in a cease-and-desist order and a fine between $500 and $2,000. Repeated violations or willful disregard escalate to license suspension. The Board publishes all disciplinary actions on its website, which means your violation becomes a permanent public record searchable by consumers and competitors.

Beyond Board action, non-compliance creates transactional liability. If a team member performed licensed activity without proper authorization, the opposing party in a deal may challenge the enforceability of the contract. Buyers have successfully unwound purchase agreements when they discovered the "agent" who negotiated terms was actually an unlicensed team assistant. The brokerage then faces both the Board action and a civil lawsuit for damages.

> "Licensees shall not aid, abet, or conspire with any person to perform any act which would constitute a violation of M.G.L. c. 112, §§87PP to 87DDD½." — 254 CMR 3.00(12)

This provision means that even if you personally did not commit the violation, knowingly allowing it on your team creates individual liability. Team leaders who look the other way when assistants exceed their authority are personally exposed.

## Building Compliant Team Operations in 2026

The path to compliance is not complicated, but it requires intentional structure. Start with written team operating agreements that define roles, specify which tasks require licensure, and establish the broker's review authority over all team activities. These agreements should be signed by every team member and updated annually.

Formalize your advertising approval process. No marketing material—from a Zillow profile to a business card—should go live without broker review confirming brokerage name prominence and accurate licensee identification. Create templates that already include compliant formatting so team members cannot accidentally publish non-conforming content.
