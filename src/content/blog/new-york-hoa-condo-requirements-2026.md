## Understanding New York HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026

Selling a condominium or cooperative unit in New York requires more disclosure paperwork than a standard single-family transaction, and the penalties for getting it wrong have real teeth. The New York hoa & condo disclosure rules 2026 framework draws from multiple statutes, board-level requirements, and Attorney General regulations that together create a layered compliance obligation for both listing and buyer's agents. Missing a single document can stall a closing, expose your brokerage to liability, or hand the buyer a contractual exit they never should have had.

New York does not have a single unified "HOA disclosure act" the way some Sun Belt states do. Instead, agents must piece together requirements from Real Property Law (RPL) Sections 462-a and 462-b, the Martin Act (General Business Law Article 23-A), the condominium offering plan filed with the New York Attorney General, and the proprietary lease or bylaws governing co-ops. Each source imposes different obligations depending on whether the property is a condo, co-op, or planned community governed by a homeowners association.

**Failure to understand which disclosure regime applies to a given unit is the single most common source of deal delays in New York's multi-family market.** Agents who treat all common-interest communities the same will eventually find themselves on the wrong side of a contract dispute or Department of State complaint.

## The Statutory Framework: RPL 462-a, 462-b, and the Martin Act

RPL Section 462-a established the Property Condition Disclosure Statement (PCDS), which applies to residential sales including condos and co-ops. Sellers must complete the 48-question form or credit the buyer $500 at closing. But this is only the baseline layer of disclosure for common-interest properties.

RPL Section 462-b addresses the separate obligation to provide association-level financial and governance documents. For condominiums, this includes the most recent audited financials, the declaration, bylaws, house rules, insurance certificates, and any pending or anticipated special assessments. The managing agent or board of managers is typically responsible for compiling this resale package, but the listing agent must ensure it reaches the buyer's attorney or agent in a timely manner.

The [Martin Act](https://ag.ny.gov/resources/organizations/real-estate-finance-bureau) layers additional requirements for newly constructed or recently converted condominiums still operating under a sponsor-held offering plan. The Attorney General's Real Estate Finance Bureau regulates these offering plans, and any material amendments—including changes to common charges, reserves, or building systems—must be disclosed to purchasers through formal plan amendments filed with the AG's office.

| Document | Condos (RPL 462-b) | Co-ops (Proprietary Lease) | HOA/Planned Communities |
|----------|--------------------|-----------------------------|--------------------------|
| Audited Financials | Required | Required by board/managing agent | Required if bylaws mandate |
| Offering Plan or Amendments | Required if still in effect | Required for sponsor units | N/A |
| Declaration/Bylaws | Yes | Proprietary lease equivalent | CC&Rs required |
| Insurance Certificate | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Special Assessment Notice | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Transfer/Flip Tax Schedule | Per offering plan | Per proprietary lease | Per CC&Rs |

## What Agents Must Actually Deliver: Forms and Timelines

New York does not have a state-issued "HOA Resale Certificate" form the way Virginia or Pennsylvania does. Instead, the content of the disclosure package is governed by the building's offering plan, bylaws, and the requirements of RPL 462-b. Most managing agents in New York City produce a standardized resale package that includes a questionnaire completed by the board or managing agent, but the format varies.

**For transactions involving co-ops, the board application process effectively serves as both a disclosure and approval mechanism, but it does not eliminate the seller's independent obligation to deliver the PCDS.** Agents frequently conflate the board package with statutory disclosure—these are separate requirements with separate timelines and consequences.

The PCDS (DOS Form 1614) must be delivered to the buyer before contract execution. If the seller opts for the $500 credit instead of completing the form, this must be reflected in the contract of sale. Regardless of the PCDS election, the association-level resale package should be ordered as soon as the unit goes into contract and delivered during the attorney review period, which in New York typically runs 3-7 business days.

For agents managing [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), tracking which units require offering plan disclosures versus standard resale packages is a critical workflow distinction that many agents overlook until a deal is already in jeopardy.

## Liability Risks When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance depend on which disclosure obligation was missed and at what stage it surfaces. A failure to deliver the PCDS (or credit) before contract signing gives the buyer a potential rescission right under RPL 462-a(2). Courts have upheld buyer cancellations where the PCDS was delivered late or not at all, even in cases where the buyer had already waived inspection contingencies.

For condo transactions governed by an offering plan still under AG oversight, failure to deliver required plan amendments can trigger enforcement action by the Real Estate Finance Bureau. Sponsors and their agents face potential civil penalties, rescission obligations, and in extreme cases, referral for criminal prosecution under the Martin Act's anti-fraud provisions.

| Violation | Potential Consequence | Who Bears Liability |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| PCDS not delivered or credited | Buyer rescission right; DOS complaint | Seller and listing agent |
| Resale package withheld by board | Closing delay; buyer may extend contingency | Managing agent; seller indirectly |
| Offering plan amendment not filed | AG enforcement; buyer rescission | Sponsor; potentially listing agent |
| Flip tax not disclosed pre-contract | Contract renegotiation; potential misrepresentation claim | Listing agent |
| Special assessment concealed | Fraud claim; license discipline | Seller and listing agent |

The New York Department of State's Division of Licensing Services can impose fines, require additional continuing education, or suspend/revoke licenses for agents who demonstrate a pattern of non-compliance. Individual complaints may result in mediation or formal disciplinary proceedings under 19 NYCRR Part 175.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in HOA and Condo Disclosures

**First, agents routinely assume the co-op board package replaces independent disclosure obligations.** A board application is a screening tool for the association—it is not a substitute for the PCDS or for providing the buyer with financial statements and governance documents before contract execution. The board package flows from buyer to board; statutory disclosures flow from seller to buyer. Confusing the direction of information flow creates gaps that surface during attorney review.

Second, listing agents fail to order the resale package early enough. Many managing agents in New York take 5-10 business days to compile the package, and some charge rush fees of $500 or more. If the package arrives after the attorney review period closes, the buyer's attorney will either demand an extension or advise their client to walk. Agents who have [experienced blown closings](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) over timing issues know that this is entirely preventable with proper sequencing.

Third, agents neglect to disclose pending or anticipated special assessments that have been discussed at board meetings but not yet formally voted on. Under New York law, material facts known to the seller must be disclosed. If a seller serves on the board and knows a $50,000-per-unit facade repair assessment is coming, silence is actionable misrepresentation.

Fourth, agents in planned communities outside New York City forget that HOAs in suburban and exurban markets still require disclosure of CC&Rs, assessment schedules, and reserve fund status, even though these transactions feel more like single-family sales. The same RPL provisions apply regardless of property density.

Fifth, buyer's agents fail to confirm receipt of disclosures and instead assume the seller's attorney handled everything. This creates a [transaction handoff gap](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) where no one verifies that the buyer actually received and reviewed the package before signing.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers of record carry supervisory liability under RPL Section 441 and 19 NYCRR 175.21 for the acts of their affiliated agents. A broker who fails to implement systems ensuring disclosure compliance is personally exposed when a transaction goes sideways.

**Every brokerage operating in markets with condos, co-ops, or HOA-governed properties should maintain a written policy specifying when the resale package must be ordered, who is responsible for confirming delivery, and how the PCDS election is documented in the transaction file.** This policy should be part of onboarding and reinforced during annual compliance reviews.

Brokers should audit at least three elements on every common-interest property transaction. First, confirm that the PCDS was either delivered to the buyer's side or that the $500 credit is reflected in the contract. Second, verify that the resale package or offering plan disclosure was ordered within 48 hours of executed contract. Third, confirm that any flip tax, transfer fee, or move-in deposit schedule was communicated to the buyer before offer submission so that net sheet calculations are accurate.

## Distinguishing Condos, Co-ops, and HOAs: Disclosure Differences That Matter

New York agents must internalize that condos and co-ops are legally distinct ownership structures with different disclosure regimes. A condominium unit is real property—the buyer receives a deed and is subject to the condominium declaration filed with the county clerk. A co-op unit is personal property—the buyer receives shares in a corporation and a proprietary lease. This distinction affects not only financing but also which disclosures are triggered and who produces them.

For condominiums, the offering plan (if still effective) is the master disclosure document. Any purchaser is entitled to receive the plan and all filed amendments before closing. The plan contains the building's financial projections, unit allocation percentages, and sponsor obligations. When a condo has exited sponsor control, the board of managers takes over disclosure responsibilities through the managing agent.

For co-ops, the proprietary lease and house rules function as the governance disclosure. Financial statements must still be provided, and many co-op boards produce an annual report or financial summary for shareholders. However, co-op transfers also involve board approval, and the board may reject a buyer without stating a reason (subject to fair housing law). Agents must counsel buyers that receiving all disclosures does not guarantee board approval.

HOA-governed planned communities—more common on Long Island, in Westchester, and in the Hudson Valley—follow a framework closer to other states. The HOA must provide a resale disclosure certificate covering assessments, reserves, pending litigation, and rule violations against the unit. While New York does not mandate a specific state form for this certificate, the content requirements under RPL 462-b and common law apply.

## Keeping Current as Rules Evolve in 2026 and Beyond

The New York legislature has considered multiple bills in recent sessions that would standardize resale disclosure requirements across all common-interest communities, potentially creating a unified form similar to what exists in Virginia or Colorado. Agents and brokers should monitor the New York State Senate and Assembly housing committee calendars for any movement on these proposals.

Additionally, the Attorney General's Real Estate Finance Bureau periodically updates its guidance on offering plan disclosure. Agents representing sponsor units or units in newly converted buildings should subscribe to the Bureau's bulletin updates to stay ahead of filing requirements.

The landscape around New York hoa & condo disclosure rules 2026 will continue to shift as consumer protection priorities evolve and more transactions involve aging condo inventory with deferred maintenance obligations. Agents who build disclosure compliance into their standard workflow—rather than treating it as an afterthought—will protect their clients, their licenses, and their deal pipelines from entirely avoidable disruption.
