## Understanding Delaware Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

If you sell residential property built before 1978 in Delaware, you are bound by both federal and state law to disclose known lead-based paint hazards. The Delaware lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 have not changed in their core obligations from prior years, but enforcement patterns and penalty amounts have shifted enough that agents who coast on outdated habits are exposed. The Delaware Real Estate Commission continues to flag incomplete disclosure packets as a top compliance failure during audits.

The federal foundation here is the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992, codified at 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d, and its implementing regulation at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H (for HUD-related transactions) and 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F (for EPA-regulated sales). Delaware does not have a separate state lead disclosure statute that conflicts with or exceeds the federal framework. Instead, the state enforces federal requirements through the Delaware Real Estate Commission under 24 Del. C. Chapter 29 and the [Delaware Department of Health and Social Services (DHSS)](https://dhss.delaware.gov/dhss/dph/hsp/lead.html) Lead Poisoning Prevention Program, which administers the state's lead hazard control standards.

**What matters practically is that Delaware agents carry individual liability as transaction participants under 40 CFR 745.115(a)(2).** You are not merely a pass-through. If the seller fails to disclose and you knew or should have known, you share responsibility.

## The Specific Forms and Documents You Must Use

The primary form is EPA Form 6100, officially titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards." This is not a Delaware-specific form — it is the federally mandated disclosure document that must accompany every pre-1978 residential sale or lease nationwide. In Delaware transactions, your MLS association or brokerage may bundle this within a state-specific addendum package, but the EPA form itself must be present and properly executed.

The form requires three categories of signatures: the seller (attesting to knowledge or lack thereof about lead-based paint), the buyer (acknowledging receipt of the disclosure and the EPA pamphlet), and the agents (confirming they informed the seller of disclosure obligations and provided the buyer with the required materials). Every agent involved — listing side and buyer side — must sign.

You must also provide buyers with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." The current version was updated in 2021, and using outdated copies from the early 2000s can technically constitute noncompliance. Download the current version directly from the EPA website and keep it in your transaction packet templates.

| Document | Who Provides It | Who Signs It | When It Must Be Delivered |
|----------|----------------|--------------|--------------------------|
| EPA Form 6100 (Lead Disclosure) | Listing agent | Seller, buyer, both agents | Before buyer is obligated under contract |
| EPA Pamphlet ("Protect Your Family...") | Listing agent or buyer's agent | N/A (receipt acknowledged on Form 6100) | Before buyer is obligated under contract |
| Lead inspection contingency language | Drafted in purchase agreement | Buyer (to accept, modify, or waive) | At contract execution |
| Known lead reports/records | Seller | N/A | With Form 6100 |

## The 10-Day Inspection Window and How to Handle It

Federal law mandates that buyers receive a minimum 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense. This is not a home inspection contingency — it is a separate, stand-alone right. Buyers can waive this period in writing, and they can agree to a shorter period, but agents cannot pressure or incentivize buyers to waive without exposing themselves to claims of interference with federally protected rights.

**In practice, most Delaware buyers in competitive markets waive or shorten the lead inspection period, but you must document that waiver on EPA Form 6100 itself.** A verbal waiver has zero legal effect. The form includes a checkbox and signature line specifically for this purpose — use it.

If your buyer does elect to conduct an inspection, they hire a certified lead inspector or risk assessor licensed through Delaware's DHSS Lead Poisoning Prevention Program. The cost typically runs $300 to $600 depending on home size. Results belong to the buyer; the seller has no obligation to remediate unless negotiated in the purchase agreement, but the seller cannot proceed to closing during the inspection window without the buyer's written consent.

## Penalties for Noncompliance: What Agents Actually Face

The consequences of failing to comply with lead paint disclosure requirements in Delaware break down into four categories: federal civil penalties, private civil liability, transaction rescission, and state licensing action.

The EPA's current maximum civil penalty for a knowing violation of 40 CFR 745 Subpart F is $19,507 per violation, adjusted annually for inflation. HUD can impose separate penalties of up to $21,039 per violation in transactions involving federally assisted housing. These numbers are per transaction, per violation — meaning a single deal with multiple deficiencies can generate stacked penalties.

Private liability is often the bigger practical risk. Under 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d(b)(3), buyers can sue for treble damages if they suffer injury from lead-based paint hazards that were known but undisclosed. Delaware courts have upheld these claims against both sellers and their agents. The buyer can also rescind the contract entirely if the disclosure was not provided before the buyer became contractually obligated.

| Consequence | Source of Authority | Maximum Exposure |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| EPA civil fine | 40 CFR 745.118 | $19,507 per violation |
| HUD civil fine (assisted housing) | 24 CFR 35.88 | $21,039 per violation |
| Buyer lawsuit (treble damages) | 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3) | 3x actual damages + attorney fees |
| Contract rescission | 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(2) | Full unwind of transaction |
| License discipline | 24 Del. C. Chapter 29 | Suspension, revocation, or fine |

The Delaware Real Estate Commission has independent authority to discipline licensees for failure to comply with disclosure obligations. Under 24 Del. C. Section 2912, the Commission can impose fines, require additional education, suspend, or revoke a license for violations of federal law incorporated by reference into Delaware's licensing standards.

## Common Mistakes Delaware Agents Make

**First, agents frequently forget to get their own signature on EPA Form 6100.** The form requires the agent's attestation that they informed the seller of disclosure obligations and ensured delivery to the buyer. A missing agent signature renders the form incomplete, and in an audit or lawsuit, it becomes evidence that you did not fulfill your statutory duty. This is the single most common deficiency flagged by the Delaware Real Estate Commission in compliance reviews.

Second, agents provide the disclosure after contract execution rather than before. The federal requirement is explicit: the buyer must receive the disclosure before becoming "obligated under any contract to purchase the housing." Delivering it at closing, or even a few days into a ratified contract, is too late. If your office practice is to include the lead disclosure in the closing package rather than the offer package, you are structurally noncompliant.

Third, listing agents fail to attach known lead inspection reports or remediation records when they exist. The disclosure form asks the seller to check a box indicating whether records or reports are available — but agents sometimes check "yes" without actually attaching the documents. Partial disclosure is treated as nondisclosure under federal enforcement guidance.

Fourth, agents conflate the lead inspection contingency with the general home inspection contingency. When a buyer waives their home inspection contingency, that does not waive the 10-day lead inspection right. These are separate legal mechanisms, and collapsing them into a single contingency clause without explicit language addressing lead can leave the buyer's lead rights technically unwaived. Fifth, dual agents or transaction licensees sometimes sign only once on the form, in one capacity. If you represent both parties, you must sign in both the seller's agent and buyer's agent signature blocks. One signature does not cover both roles.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under both 40 CFR 745.115 and 24 Del. C. Section 2913. If your agents are systematically failing to deliver lead disclosures, the Commission can hold your brokerage responsible even if you personally handled no transactions. Quarterly file audits should check for three specific items in every pre-1978 transaction file.

First, confirm that EPA Form 6100 is fully executed with all required signatures — seller, buyer, listing agent, and buyer's agent — and dated before the contract ratification date. Second, verify that a copy of the current EPA pamphlet delivery is documented, either through the acknowledgment checkbox on the form or a separate receipt. Third, check that any known lead reports referenced on the form are actually present in the file.

Brokers should also confirm that their standard purchase agreement templates include the required lead inspection contingency language as a default for pre-1978 properties, with a clear mechanism for buyers to waive in writing. If your brokerage uses custom forms that omit this language, you are creating liability for every agent under your supervision. Understanding [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) can help you build systems that prevent these oversights from becoming pattern violations.

A documented training protocol matters. The Delaware Real Estate Commission looks favorably on brokerages that can demonstrate written policies, annual compliance training, and audit trails when individual violations occur. Without these, a single agent's failure can trigger broader brokerage-level sanctions.

## Exemptions and Edge Cases in Delaware

Not every property triggers lead disclosure. The exemption list under 40 CFR 745.101 includes housing built after January 1, 1978; housing exclusively for the elderly or persons with disabilities (unless children under six reside there); zero-bedroom units such as studios, dormitories, or efficiency apartments; property sold at foreclosure; and housing that has been certified as lead-free by a certified inspector.

Delaware's DHSS maintains a registry of properties that have received lead-free certifications following abatement. If your seller has documentation of a certified lead-free status, the disclosure requirement does not apply — but the certification must come from a properly licensed lead inspector, not merely a contractor who claims to have painted over old surfaces. "Encapsulation" does not equal "lead-free" unless tested and certified post-work.

Estate sales and probate transactions still require disclosure. Executors and personal representatives step into the seller's shoes for disclosure purposes. If the estate has no knowledge of lead conditions, they check the "no knowledge" box — but they cannot skip the form entirely.
