## Understanding Michigan Continuing Education Requirements 2026

Missing a continuing education deadline in Michigan does not come with a grace period or a gentle reminder. It comes with a lapsed license, lost income, and potential regulatory action from the Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA). The Michigan continuing education requirements 2026 cycle affects every salesperson and broker whose license expires on October 31, 2026, and the rules have not loosened.

Michigan's Occupational Code, specifically MCL 339.2504 and MCL 339.2512e, governs real estate licensing and continuing education mandates in the state. LARA's Bureau of Professional Licensing administers these rules through the Board of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons. If you hold an active Michigan license, you fall under their jurisdiction regardless of how many deals you close annually.

The renewal cycle runs on a three-year schedule. Agents whose licenses expire in 2026 must complete all required coursework before that October 31 date — not after, not "in progress," but done. This is the hard line that catches agents off guard every cycle.

## Hours, Topics, and Approved Provider Rules

**Michigan requires 18 hours of approved continuing education per three-year renewal cycle.** This breaks down into specific categories that agents cannot mix and match at random. The mandatory 2-hour legal update course must be completed each cycle, covering recent statutory changes, case law developments, and regulatory updates specific to Michigan real estate practice.

The remaining 16 elective hours must come from courses approved by LARA. Topics typically include fair housing law, agency relationships, property management, ethics, and transactional practice. LARA maintains a searchable database of approved providers and course numbers on its [licensing portal](https://www.michigan.gov/lara/bureau-list/bpl/occ/prof/real-estate).

Agents cannot double-count hours. A course taken in one renewal cycle does not carry over into the next. Similarly, pre-license education hours do not substitute for CE requirements, a confusion that new licensees frequently bring to their first renewal.

## The Renewal Process: Forms and Filing

Michigan real estate license renewals are processed through LARA's online system. Agents use the MiPLUS (Michigan Professional Licensing User System) portal to submit renewal applications. The system prompts agents to confirm CE completion, but it does not automatically verify transcripts at the time of submission.

| Requirement | Salesperson | Broker |
|---|---|---|
| Total CE Hours | 18 | 18 |
| Mandatory Legal Update | 2 hours | 2 hours |
| Elective Hours | 16 hours | 16 hours |
| Renewal Cycle | 3 years | 3 years |
| Renewal Deadline (2026) | October 31 | October 31 |
| Renewal Fee | $78 | $88 |

Agents should retain certificates of completion from every approved course. LARA conducts random audits, and if selected, you must produce documentation proving you finished your hours before the deadline. The burden of proof sits entirely with the licensee — not the education provider and not your broker.

## What Happens When You Fail to Comply

**A lapsed license means you cannot legally perform any activity that requires licensure.** You cannot show property, negotiate offers, collect referral fees, or hold yourself out as a real estate professional. Any transaction you participate in while unlicensed exposes you and your brokerage to liability under Michigan law.

Under MCL 339.2512, practicing without a valid license can result in civil fines of up to $10,000 per violation. LARA can also refer cases for criminal misdemeanor charges, which carry additional penalties. Beyond the regulatory consequences, any commission earned during a lapsed period is subject to clawback, and clients may have grounds to rescind contracts.

For deals already in the pipeline, a lapsed license creates a [real risk of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing). Title companies routinely verify active license status before disbursing commissions. If your status shows "lapsed" or "expired" in the LARA database at closing, expect delays at minimum and deal cancellation at worst.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make

Every renewal cycle, LARA processes thousands of late applications and deficiency notices. The errors are predictable but preventable.

**First, agents wait until the final month to start coursework.** Eighteen hours might sound manageable, but approved providers often fill up their October sessions weeks in advance. Online courses have technical glitches. Providers sometimes take days to report completion to LARA. Starting in September for an October 31 deadline is gambling with your license.

Second, agents take courses from unapproved providers. National CE platforms frequently offer real estate courses that satisfy other states' requirements but lack Michigan LARA approval. The course title might sound identical to an approved Michigan offering, but if the provider number is not in LARA's system, the hours do not count. Always verify approval status before enrolling.

Third, agents confuse association education with CE credit. Local board training sessions, NAR designation courses, and brokerage in-house training do not automatically qualify as Michigan CE. Some do carry LARA approval, but many do not. Check the specific course listing rather than assuming.

Fourth, agents fail to update their mailing address with LARA. Renewal notices and audit letters go to the address on file. If you moved and did not file a change-of-address notification, you may miss critical deadlines without ever knowing they passed. Michigan law requires address changes to be reported within 30 days.

Fifth, agents assume their broker is tracking their compliance. While brokers have supervisory obligations, the legal responsibility for CE completion rests with the individual licensee. Blaming your broker for not reminding you will not restore a lapsed license.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Michigan carry specific supervisory duties under MCL 339.2512e. This statute requires that associate brokers and salespersons operate under adequate supervision, which includes ensuring compliance with licensing requirements. A broker who allows an unlicensed agent to transact business faces their own disciplinary exposure.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Verify agent CE completion | Annually minimum | LARA MiPLUS database |
| Confirm license expiration dates | Quarterly | Internal tracking system |
| Check address accuracy with LARA | At onboarding and annually | Agent self-report + LARA |
| Review approved provider status for brokerage-offered CE | Before each course | LARA provider database |
| Document supervisory actions | Ongoing | Brokerage records |

Brokers should build a tracking calendar that flags agents entering their final renewal year. Waiting until October to ask agents for proof of CE completion is not supervision — it is a liability. A simple quarterly check-in, cross-referenced against LARA's public license lookup tool, identifies at-risk agents before the deadline arrives.

For brokerages managing large teams, this administrative burden multiplies. Tracking [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) while simultaneously auditing agent compliance across different renewal cycles requires systematic processes that most brokerages build reactively rather than proactively.

## The Legal Update Course: What It Covers in 2026

The mandatory 2-hour legal update course changes each cycle to reflect new legislation, court decisions, and regulatory guidance issued since the previous renewal period. For the 2026 cycle, agents should expect coverage of recent amendments to the Michigan Seller Disclosure Act (MCL 565.951-565.966), updates to agency disclosure practices, and any changes to LARA's administrative rules.

Michigan's Seller Disclosure Act requires sellers to complete a Seller's Disclosure Statement detailing known material defects. Changes to disclosure obligations, including expanded categories and revised form language, typically feature prominently in the legal update curriculum. Agents who skip this course or treat it as a checkbox exercise often [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) related to updated disclosure timing requirements.

The legal update also addresses fair housing enforcement trends, wire fraud prevention protocols, and changes to Michigan's recording and transfer tax statutes. These are not theoretical topics — they directly affect how you draft offers, advise clients, and manage transaction timelines.

## Planning Your CE Strategy Before the Deadline

Treating continuing education as a last-minute obligation is the single most common reason agents end up in compliance trouble. The agents who avoid renewal headaches build CE into their annual schedule, completing six hours per year rather than cramming 18 hours into the final quarter.

LARA-approved online providers offer flexibility, but verify completion reporting timelines. Some providers report to LARA within 24 hours; others take up to two weeks. If you finish your last course on October 28 and the provider does not report until November 5, your renewal application may show incomplete hours at the deadline. Keep your own certificates as backup.
