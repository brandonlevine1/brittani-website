## Understanding Florida Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Every Florida closing depends on two line items most agents barely glance at: documentary stamp taxes and recording fees. Getting the Florida transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 wrong does not just embarrass you at the closing table—it can delay recording, expose you to liability, and trigger Department of Revenue enforcement actions. This post breaks down the current statutory rates, the forms you will actually fill out, the penalties for noncompliance, and the audit steps brokers should be running on every file.

Florida's documentary stamp tax is governed by [Chapter 201 of the Florida Statutes](https://www.flsenate.gov/Laws/Statutes/2025/Chapter201), while recording fees fall under Florida Statute 28.24. The Florida Department of Revenue (DOR) administers the stamp tax, and county Clerks of Court handle recording. Agents do not remit these taxes directly, but they are responsible for accurately estimating them on closing disclosure worksheets and ensuring contract language correctly assigns payment responsibility.

## Statutory Rates: Documentary Stamps and Intangible Tax

**The documentary stamp tax rate on deeds in Florida is $0.70 per $100 of consideration—except in Miami-Dade County, where a surtax pushes the rate to $1.05 per $100.** This rate has not changed for 2026, and there is no legislative movement suggesting it will shift mid-year. The tax applies to the total consideration, including any mortgage assumed by the buyer and any liens taken "subject to."

For notes and mortgages, the stamp tax rate is $0.35 per $100 of the obligation secured. This is separate from the deed stamps and often confuses newer agents who assume one payment covers both instruments. Additionally, a nonrecurring intangible tax of 2 mills ($0.002 per dollar) applies to new mortgages under F.S. 199.133.

| Instrument | Rate | Statute | Who Typically Pays |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deed (all counties except Miami-Dade) | $0.70 per $100 | F.S. 201.02 | Seller |
| Deed (Miami-Dade County) | $0.60 + $0.45 surtax per $100 | F.S. 201.02 + Miami-Dade Ordinance | Buyer (by local custom) |
| Promissory Note / Mortgage | $0.35 per $100 | F.S. 201.08 | Buyer |
| Nonrecurring Intangible Tax | $2.00 per $1,000 of mortgage | F.S. 199.133 | Buyer |

Round-up rules matter here. Under F.S. 201.02, if the consideration is not an exact multiple of $100, you round up to the next $100 before multiplying. A $425,050 sale price gets taxed as $425,100. Missing that rounding creates a shortage that the clerk will catch and reject at recording.

## Recording Fee Breakdown for 2026

Florida Statute 28.24 sets the base recording fee structure. The first page of any instrument costs $10.00 to record. Each additional page costs $8.50. On top of that, most counties add surcharges mandated by other statutes—court technology fees, records modernization trust funds, and affordable housing surcharges under the Sadowski Act.

**A typical two-page warranty deed will cost $18.50 to record before surcharges, but actual out-of-pocket at the clerk's window often lands between $35 and $55 depending on the county.** Agents estimating net sheets must check their specific county clerk's published schedule rather than relying on the statutory minimum alone. Broward, Palm Beach, and Hillsborough each have different surcharge stacks.

Mortgage recording follows the same per-page fee structure but tends to run more pages. A 15-page mortgage instrument can hit $130 or more in recording fees alone, before the documentary stamp and intangible tax are applied. This is why buyer-side cost estimates frequently come in short when agents use flat-rate assumptions instead of actual page counts.

## Forms and Documents Agents Must Know

Florida does not require a separate tax return filing for documentary stamps on most residential transactions—the clerk collects the tax at the time of recording. However, agents working with the Florida Realtors / Florida Bar contracts should be familiar with these specific forms and clauses.

The FR/Bar AS IS Contract (Form ASIS-6) and the FR/Bar Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (Form CR-6) both contain Paragraph 9, which addresses prorations, credits, and closing costs including documentary stamp taxes. Paragraph 9(b)(i) specifically assigns responsibility for deed stamps, and Paragraph 9(b)(ii) covers the buyer's obligation for stamps on the note and intangible tax. If an agent modifies this paragraph without understanding the statutory obligations, the resulting dispute can unravel a deal days before closing.

For transactions involving exemptions—such as transfers between spouses under F.S. 201.02(7)(b) or transfers to revocable trusts—agents should know that DOR Form DR-219 (Declaration of Documentary Stamp Tax) is required to claim the exemption at recording. The clerk will not accept a bare assertion of exemption without this form attached to the deed. Agents who handle trust-related transactions without flagging DR-219 to the closing agent are setting up a recording rejection.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Transfer Taxes and Recording Fees

Five errors appear repeatedly in Florida transaction files, and each one carries real consequences for the agent, the client, or both.

**First, agents routinely miscalculate Miami-Dade documentary stamps by applying the standard $0.70 rate instead of the combined $1.05 rate.** The Miami-Dade surtax under F.S. 201.021 adds $0.45 per $100, and it applies to the full consideration—not just the amount above a threshold. A $600,000 condo sale in Brickell that gets estimated at $4,200 in stamps actually owes $6,300. That $2,100 discrepancy either comes out of someone's pocket at closing or kills the deal.

Second, agents forget the intangible tax when preparing buyer net sheets. The intangible tax of $2 per $1,000 of the new mortgage amount is separate from the mortgage stamps. On a $480,000 loan, that is $960 the buyer was not expecting. This is the kind of [surprise that blows closings](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) when it surfaces on the final CD.

Third, agents incorrectly assume doc stamps are always the seller's responsibility. While that is Florida custom in 66 of 67 counties, the contract controls. If a listing agent writes an addendum shifting stamps to the buyer without clear disclosure, the buyer's agent may not catch it until the title company flags the discrepancy—often too late to renegotiate without friction.

Fourth, agents fail to account for multi-parcel transactions correctly. When a single deed conveys multiple parcels, stamps are calculated on the total consideration for all parcels, not individually. But when separate deeds are used for each parcel (common in portfolio sales), each deed triggers its own minimum tax calculation under DOR Technical Assistance Advisement 03A-008.

Fifth, agents neglect the documentary stamp implications of seller financing. If the seller takes back a purchase money mortgage, the buyer owes $0.35 per $100 on that note plus the $2 per $1,000 intangible tax—even though no institutional lender is involved. Agents who structure creative finance deals without flagging these costs create closing table arguments that [derail transactions at the worst possible moment](/blog/where-deals-break-down).

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under F.S. 475.25 for their agents' transactional errors. While brokers are not responsible for calculating taxes—that falls to the closing agent—they are responsible for ensuring their agents do not make material misrepresentations on estimated cost sheets or contract addenda.

**Every brokerage operating in Florida should audit three things in every file: that Paragraph 9 of the contract has not been improperly modified, that the estimated closing statement reflects the correct county-specific rates, and that any exemption claims are supported by the appropriate DR-219 form reference.** A quarterly file review catches these issues before they become DOR audit findings or FREC complaints.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Contract Paragraph 9 | Unmodified or properly initialed changes | Strikethrough without addendum |
| Net Sheet Accuracy | Correct county rate applied | Flat $0.70 used in Miami-Dade |
| Intangible Tax Inclusion | Buyer estimate includes $2/1,000 on mortgage | Missing line item on buyer worksheet |
| Exemption Documentation | DR-219 referenced in file notes | Trust deed with no exemption form flagged |
| Recording Fee Estimate | County-specific schedule used | Statewide flat estimate used |

Brokers should also track [whether agents are meeting compliance timelines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) on document submissions to title. Late delivery of executed contracts means the title company has less time to calculate accurate figures, which cascades into CD revision delays and closing postponements.

## Penalties and Liability for Noncompliance

The Florida Department of Revenue does not play soft with underpaid documentary stamps. Under F.S. 201.17, the DOR can assess a penalty of up to 100% of the unpaid tax, plus interest at 12% per year from the date the tax was originally due. The clerk may also refuse to record an instrument if the stamps affixed are insufficient, which means the deed never enters the public record—leaving the buyer without recorded title.

For agents specifically, the risk is less about DOR penalties (which hit the taxpayer, typically the seller or buyer) and more about FREC disciplinary action. Under F.S. 475.25(1)(b), a licensee who makes a misrepresentation or fails to disclose a material fact can face disciplinary action including fines up to $5,000, suspension, or revocation. An agent who tells a seller their closing costs will be $8,000 when the actual documentary stamp obligation alone exceeds that figure has arguably made a material misrepresentation.

Beyond regulatory action, agents face civil liability for negligent misrepresentation. Florida courts have held that real estate licensees owe a duty of competence when providing cost estimates, even when those estimates are labeled as approximations. The [cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) goes beyond the immediate deal—it includes the reputational damage and potential E&O claims that follow.
