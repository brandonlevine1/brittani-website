## Georgia Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026: The New Reality

The Georgia commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 have rewritten the playbook for how agents get paid. If you hold a license under the Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) and haven't overhauled your buyer-side compensation workflow, you are exposed to liability right now. The settlement's practice changes, which took effect nationally on August 17, 2024, have now had over a year to settle into Georgia-specific enforcement patterns—and agents who treated this as a minor paperwork update are already facing complaints.

Georgia was never a state that codified commission splits into statute. Compensation has always been negotiable under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-25, which governs brokerage relationships and requires written agreements before an agent can act on behalf of a client. What changed is the mechanism: the MLS can no longer serve as the marketplace for buyer-agent compensation offers, and agents must secure written agreements that spell out exactly what they will be paid before touring a single property.

## How the Settlement Changed Georgia's MLS Practices

**The most immediate operational shift hit Georgia's multiple MLS systems, including GAMLS (Georgia MLS), FMLS (First Multiple Listing Service), and Savannah MLS.** All three eliminated the cooperating compensation field from listing input screens in compliance with the NAR settlement terms. Listing agents can no longer broadcast a blanket offer of compensation to buyer agents through MLS data.

This does not mean sellers cannot pay buyer-agent fees. It means the offer must be communicated off-MLS—through direct agent-to-agent negotiation, seller concession fields where permitted, or separate written agreements. The Georgia Association of REALTORS (GAR) responded by releasing updated forms to handle these scenarios, including GAR Form F725 (Buyer Brokerage Agreement) and updated language in GAR Form F201 (Exclusive Seller Listing Agreement).

The distinction matters because MLS participation rules carry their own penalty structure separate from GREC. An agent who sneaks compensation language into the public remarks field of an FMLS listing, for example, faces fines starting at $1,000 per violation under [FMLS Rules and Regulations](https://www.fmls.com/rules-and-regulations) Section 7, plus potential listing suspension.

## Written Buyer Agreements: Georgia's Specific Requirements

O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-25(a)(2) already required that a buyer brokerage engagement be in writing before an agent could claim to represent a buyer. The NAR settlement layered on additional specificity: the agreement must state a definite compensation amount or rate that is objective and not open-ended (i.e., you cannot write "whatever the seller offers"). It must also be tied to a specific duration rather than left indefinite.

In Georgia, agents typically execute GAR Form F725 (Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement) or GAR Form F726 (Non-Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement) to meet this requirement. The 2025 revisions to these forms added mandatory fields for compensation disclosure that satisfy both GREC standards and MLS participation rules. If you are still using pre-2025 versions of these forms, you are out of compliance.

| Requirement | Pre-Settlement Practice | Post-Settlement Practice |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer compensation disclosure | Often implied via MLS co-op field | Must be stated in written buyer agreement before touring |
| MLS compensation field | Active, visible to all agents | Eliminated from all Georgia MLS systems |
| Seller-paid buyer commission | Offered via MLS | Negotiated off-MLS, documented separately |
| Agreement duration | Sometimes open-ended | Must specify a definite term |
| Touring without agreement | Common informal practice | Prohibited under settlement rules |

## What Happens When Georgia Agents Fail to Comply

**Non-compliance in Georgia triggers a multi-layered penalty structure that can hit an agent from several directions simultaneously.** GREC has authority under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-25 to discipline licensees who fail to maintain proper written agreements. Sanctions range from a formal citation and fine (typically $1,000 to $5,000 per violation) up to license suspension or revocation for repeated offenses.

Beyond GREC, the MLS organizations impose their own penalties. GAMLS and FMLS both operate complaint-driven enforcement systems. If a competing agent reports that you showed property without a signed buyer agreement, or that you embedded compensation offers in listing remarks, the MLS can levy fines, suspend your MLS access, or terminate your participation entirely.

Then there is civil liability. A buyer who signs a purchase contract without understanding they are responsible for their agent's fee—because the agent never properly disclosed it—has grounds for a claim under the Georgia Fair Business Practices Act (O.C.G.A. Section 10-1-393). Deals have already been unwound at closing tables in metro Atlanta over disputed commission obligations that were never documented in a valid buyer agreement. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond a single lost paycheck when regulatory complaints and lawsuits follow.

## Common Mistakes Georgia Agents Are Making Right Now

Eighteen months into the new rules, patterns have emerged. These are the five most frequent errors showing up in GREC complaints and MLS violation reports across Georgia.

**First, agents are executing buyer agreements with vague compensation terms.** Writing "up to 3%" or "to be determined at offer" does not satisfy the settlement requirement for an objective, specific amount. GREC has flagged these as deficient, and at least two formal citations were issued in Q1 2026 over this language.

Second, some listing agents are still verbally offering buyer-agent compensation during broker opens or in private showing feedback channels, then failing to document those offers in writing. If the seller later disputes the payment, the buyer agent has no enforceable claim. Verbal promises are worth nothing under Georgia's statute of frauds when it comes to brokerage compensation (O.C.G.A. Section 13-5-30).

Third, agents are confusing seller concessions with commission payments. A seller concession toward closing costs that the buyer then directs toward agent compensation must still be disclosed in the buyer agreement and reflected in contract documents. The GAR Purchase and Sale Agreement (Form F20) has specific addendum provisions for this routing, and skipping them creates audit problems.

Fourth, dual agency situations are being mishandled more than ever. Under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-25(b)(3), Georgia permits disclosed dual agency, but the compensation structure in a dual-agency scenario must be explicitly addressed in both the listing agreement and the buyer agreement. Agents assuming a single commission structure "covers both sides" are creating liability gaps.

Fifth, agents are letting buyer agreements expire mid-transaction. If you signed a 30-day touring agreement and the buyer does not go under contract until day 45, your compensation claim may be unenforceable. Track your [deadlines with the same rigor](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) you bring to contract contingencies.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce in 2026

Georgia brokers bear supervisory responsibility under O.C.G.A. Section 43-40-18. If an affiliated agent violates commission disclosure rules, the broker can face joint disciplinary action from GREC. Broker-level compliance requires more than sending a memo—it requires documented systems.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Action if Deficient |
|---|---|---|
| Signed buyer agreements on file before first showing | Per transaction | Halt touring, execute agreement immediately |
| Compensation terms reviewed for specificity | Per transaction | Reject vague language, require revision |
| Listing agreements checked for prohibited MLS compensation offers | At listing input | Remove non-compliant language before MLS entry |
| Expired buyer agreements flagged | Weekly | Alert agent, suspend showing activity |
| Dual-agency compensation disclosures | Per transaction | Verify both-side documentation exists |

**Brokers who rely solely on agents to self-police are taking on extraordinary risk.** GREC's 2025 enforcement guidance explicitly stated that "ignorance of affiliated licensee activity does not constitute a defense for supervising brokers." Every transaction file should contain a timestamped, signed buyer agreement that predates the first property showing—and your file review system needs to catch gaps before closing.

The operational burden here is real. If you are managing twenty or more agents, manual file reviews become a bottleneck that [slows your entire pipeline](/blog/where-deals-break-down). Automated compliance tracking is no longer a luxury—it is a risk management requirement.

## Negotiating Compensation Off-MLS: Practical Approaches

The settlement did not eliminate buyer-agent compensation. It eliminated the automated delivery system. Georgia agents now negotiate compensation through direct communication with listing agents, seller concession requests written into offers, or buyer-funded payments.

The GAR Purchase and Sale Agreement (Form F20) allows buyers to request that sellers contribute to buyer-agent compensation through the special stipulations section or through GAR Form F753 (Buyer's Agent Compensation Agreement). This form, released in late 2024, creates a binding agreement between the seller and the buyer's agent for a specific compensation amount tied to a specific transaction. It functions as a stand-alone contract separate from both the listing agreement and the buyer agreement.

Some brokerages in Georgia have adopted a practice of submitting a compensation request letter alongside every offer. While not required by GREC or any MLS rule, this approach creates a documentation trail and signals to the listing side that compensation must be addressed before contract acceptance. The key compliance point: whatever compensation structure is agreed upon must be consistent with what was disclosed in the buyer's signed agreement. Any variance requires an amendment to the buyer agreement before closing.
