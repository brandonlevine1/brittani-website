## Tennessee Real Estate License Reciprocity & Portability 2026: The Ground Rules

If you hold a license in another state and want to do business across the Tennessee border, or if you are a Tennessee licensee curious about working in neighboring markets, the rules around **Tennessee real estate license reciprocity & portability 2026** demand your attention. Tennessee does not hand out automatic recognition. The Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC), governed by Tennessee Code Annotated Title 62, Chapter 13, runs a certification-based system that requires deliberate action from every out-of-state agent who wants to transact lawfully in the Volunteer State.

Misunderstanding the distinction between reciprocity, portability, and cooperative agreements has cost agents commissions, triggered disciplinary actions, and even unwound closed transactions. This post breaks down the statutory framework, the forms you need, the penalties for noncompliance, and the audit responsibilities brokers carry.

## How Tennessee Defines Reciprocity vs. Portability

**Tennessee does not participate in any blanket reciprocity compact.** Unlike states that signed onto multi-state licensing agreements through ARELLO, Tennessee maintains its own certification pathway codified in T.C.A. Section 62-13-303(b). This statute allows TREC to waive pre-license education requirements for applicants who already hold an active license in another state—but it does not waive the Tennessee state exam, the background check, or the broker-affiliation requirement.

Portability, on the other hand, refers to the ability of a licensee to conduct limited brokerage activity in a state where they do not hold a license, typically through a cooperative agreement or referral arrangement. Tennessee's position is unambiguous: performing any act of brokerage as defined in T.C.A. Section 62-13-102(4) without a Tennessee license is unlawful. There is no "one-deal pass" or temporary practice allowance.

| Concept | Tennessee's Position | Statutory Basis |
|---------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Full Reciprocity | Not offered with any state | T.C.A. 62-13-303 |
| Certification (Partial Recognition) | Available—waives coursework, not exam | T.C.A. 62-13-303(b) |
| Temporary Practice Permit | Does not exist | T.C.A. 62-13-102(4) |
| Referral-Only Activity | Permitted without TN license | TREC Rule 1260-02-.01 |
| Commercial Exception | None—same rules apply | T.C.A. 62-13-104 |

Agents who confuse referral activity with active representation often find themselves on the wrong side of enforcement. A referral fee paid to an out-of-state licensee who merely introduced a client is lawful. An out-of-state licensee who shows property, drafts offers, or negotiates terms without a Tennessee license is practicing illegally.

## The Certification Application Process

To obtain a Tennessee license through the certification pathway, an out-of-state agent must submit TREC Form CR-1 (Application for Licensure by Certification) along with a license history verification sent directly from the applicant's home-state commission. TREC also requires a completed fingerprint card or LiveScan submission for the TBI/FBI background check, processed through the Tennessee Bureau of Investigation.

The applicant must then pass the Tennessee state portion of the licensing exam, administered by PSI Services. The national portion is waived for certification applicants, but the state section covers Tennessee-specific statutes including the Tennessee Residential Property Condition Disclosure Act (T.C.A. 66-5-201 et seq.), agency disclosure requirements under TREC Rule 1260-02-.12, and trust account regulations.

After passing the exam, the applicant must affiliate with a Tennessee principal broker using TREC Form AB-1 (Affiliation/Transfer Form) before the license activates. No brokerage activity is lawful until TREC processes the affiliation and the license status shows "Active" in the [TREC online verification system](https://verify.tn.gov/).

## Consequences of Practicing Without Proper Licensure

**The penalties for unlicensed practice in Tennessee are both civil and potentially criminal.** Under T.C.A. Section 62-13-112, TREC can impose civil penalties of up to $1,000 per violation. Each transaction—or even each act within a transaction—can constitute a separate violation. An agent who shows three properties, writes an offer, and attends an inspection on a single deal could theoretically face multiple counts.

Beyond TREC's administrative penalties, T.C.A. Section 62-13-301 classifies unlicensed brokerage activity as a Class B misdemeanor, carrying up to six months in jail and a $500 criminal fine. The buyer or seller harmed by the unlicensed activity may also pursue civil remedies including rescission of the contract and disgorgement of any commission paid.

Your home state's commission will likely learn about it too. Most state commissions require licensees to report disciplinary actions taken in other jurisdictions within 10 to 30 days. A Tennessee enforcement action can cascade into discipline in your home state, creating a multi-state licensing crisis from what started as a single cross-border deal. Agents who regularly work border markets near Memphis, Chattanooga, or the Tri-Cities region face heightened exposure here, making compliance non-optional for anyone doing [transaction coordination across state lines](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator).

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Tennessee Portability

Three to five errors show up repeatedly in TREC disciplinary orders and advisory opinions. Understanding them can save you thousands of dollars and your license.

**First, agents assume that holding an ARELLO-certified license in their home state creates automatic recognition in Tennessee.** ARELLO certification relates to education standards; it does not create a license-to-practice in another state. You still need to complete the CR-1 application, pass the Tennessee state exam, and affiliate with a principal broker.

Second, agents conflate "cooperative brokerage" with portability. Tennessee allows brokers from different firms—and even different states—to split commissions on a deal, but the out-of-state agent receiving a co-op split must not have performed licensed activity in Tennessee to earn it. If your involvement went beyond a referral introduction, you needed a Tennessee license.

Third, agents rely on their broker's assurance that "we handle multi-state deals all the time" without verifying that the specific transaction structure is lawful. Brokers sometimes confuse their firm's multi-state corporate registration with individual agent licensing. A firm can be registered in Tennessee while its individual agents remain unlicensed—those agents still cannot practice.

Fourth, agents fail to disclose their out-of-state license status to Tennessee consumers. Even after obtaining a Tennessee license by certification, TREC Rule 1260-02-.12 requires disclosure of any material fact affecting the transaction. If your primary practice is in another state and you are unfamiliar with Tennessee local customs, that unfamiliarity can itself become a disclosure issue if it leads to client harm.

Fifth, agents let their Tennessee certification lapse by missing continuing education deadlines. Tennessee requires 16 hours of CE per two-year renewal cycle under T.C.A. Section 62-13-325, including the mandatory TREC Core Course. Out-of-state agents often forget about their Tennessee renewal because it operates on a different cycle than their home state.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

**Principal brokers in Tennessee carry personal liability for the acts of their affiliated licensees under T.C.A. Section 62-13-312.** This means if an affiliated agent practices on a lapsed certification or allows an unlicensed out-of-state team member to perform brokerage acts, the principal broker faces discipline alongside the agent.

Brokers should audit three areas quarterly at minimum. First, verify that every affiliated agent's license status shows "Active" in the TREC database—not "Expired," "Inactive," or "Pending." Second, confirm that agents working cross-border transactions from neighboring states like Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Kentucky, Virginia, North Carolina, Arkansas, or Missouri have completed their CR-1 certification and hold an active Tennessee affiliation. Third, review team structures to ensure that unlicensed assistants are not performing acts that require licensure, such as discussing contract terms, showing property independently, or negotiating repairs.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Source/Tool |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Agent license status | Monthly | TREC Verify (verify.tn.gov) |
| CE completion tracking | Quarterly | Agent self-report + TREC records |
| Out-of-state agent affiliation forms (AB-1) | At onboarding | TREC file |
| Team member role documentation | Semi-annually | Internal brokerage records |
| Errors & omissions insurance verification | Annually | Carrier confirmation |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-monitor are accepting risk they can easily mitigate. Building a [checklist-based compliance system](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) into your brokerage operations removes the guesswork from multi-state oversight. The cost of a quarterly audit is negligible compared to the cost of a single TREC complaint.

## Cross-Border Scenarios That Trip Up Tennessee Agents

Tennessee agents looking outward face their own portability questions. If you hold a Tennessee license and want to practice in a neighboring state, each state's rules differ dramatically. Kentucky, for example, offers a limited reciprocity pathway for Tennessee licensees under KRS 324.085, but it still requires an application and background check. Georgia requires a full license application with no reciprocity credit for Tennessee education hours.

The practical takeaway: never assume symmetry. Just because Tennessee accepts certification applicants from State X does not mean State X offers a comparable pathway for Tennessee licensees. Research each target state's commission rules independently, or risk the same unlicensed-practice consequences described above—but in another state's jurisdiction.

For agents managing transactions that touch multiple states simultaneously, the compliance burden multiplies.
