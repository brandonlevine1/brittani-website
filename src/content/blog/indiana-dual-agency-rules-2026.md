## Understanding Indiana Dual Agency & Designated Agency Rules 2026

Indiana's agency framework gives brokerages flexibility, but that flexibility creates disclosure obligations that trip up even experienced licensees. The **Indiana dual agency & designated agency rules 2026** are codified primarily in Indiana Code Title 25, Article 34.1, Chapter 10, and enforced by the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC) under the Indiana Professional Licensing Agency. Mishandling agency relationships remains one of the top reasons IREC opens investigations against active licensees.

This post breaks down the statutory requirements, the specific forms you need in your transaction file, and the liability exposure you accept every time you check the "dual agent" box.

## How Indiana Defines Dual Agency vs. Designated Agency

Indiana draws a clear statutory line between these two relationship types, though many agents blur them in practice.

**Dual agency** occurs when a single licensee—or a brokerage without designated agents—represents both buyer and seller in the same transaction. Under IC 25-34.1-10-10, dual agency is permitted only with written, informed consent from both parties, disclosed before any confidential information changes hands. The dual agent becomes a neutral facilitator and may not advocate for either side on price, terms, or negotiation strategy.

**Designated agency**, authorized under IC 25-34.1-10-9.5, allows a managing broker to appoint separate licensees within the same brokerage to represent the buyer and seller independently. Each designated agent owes full fiduciary duties to their assigned client, while the managing broker assumes a supervisory, non-advocating role. The distinction matters because designated agents can advise on price and negotiate aggressively—dual agents cannot.

| Feature | Dual Agency | Designated Agency |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Fiduciary duty to client | Limited (neutral) | Full (advocacy permitted) |
| Confidential info sharing | Prohibited from both sides | Protected within each side |
| Written consent required | Yes, both parties | Yes, both parties |
| Who appoints | N/A—single agent or brokerage acts | Managing broker designates |
| Statutory authority | IC 25-34.1-10-10 | IC 25-34.1-10-9.5 |
| Managing broker's role | May be the dual agent | Supervisory only, no advocacy |

Understanding this table is the difference between running a compliant file and inviting a commission complaint. If your brokerage policy defaults to designated agency, you still need affirmative written consent—automatic designation without disclosure does not satisfy the statute.

## Required Forms and Disclosure Timing

Indiana mandates agency disclosure at "first substantive contact," which IREC interprets as the first meaningful conversation about a specific property or a client's buying/selling needs. Handing someone a business card at an open house is not substantive contact. Discussing their price range or showing a listing is.

The Indiana Association of Realtors (IAR) publishes standardized forms that most brokerages adopt. The key documents include the **Agency Disclosure Form** (presented at first substantive contact to explain all available relationship types), the **Consent to Dual Agency** form, and the **Designated Agency Consent** form. Your brokerage may use custom versions, but they must meet the statutory minimums outlined in IC 25-34.1-10-6 through 10-12.

**Timing failures are the single most common audit finding in Indiana agency files.** If you present the disclosure form at the purchase agreement signing rather than at first substantive contact, you have already violated the statute—even if the client signs without complaint. IREC does not require a specific calendar deadline, but "first substantive contact" is a factual determination, and backdating forms is a separate violation under IC 25-34.1-10-12(a)(2) regarding dishonest dealing.

For agents working across state lines, note that Indiana's timing standard differs from many neighboring states. [Washington's dual agency framework](/blog/washington-dual-agency-rules-2026) operates under different statutory triggers, and agents licensed in multiple jurisdictions need to track which state's clock is running.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

IREC's enforcement authority is broad. Under IC 25-34.1-10-12, the Commission can impose civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation, issue cease-and-desist orders, require additional education, place a license on probation, suspend it, or revoke it entirely. The penalty escalation depends on whether the violation was inadvertent or part of a pattern.

Beyond regulatory action, the aggrieved party has civil remedies. Indiana courts have recognized that undisclosed dual agency can constitute a breach of fiduciary duty, opening the agent and brokerage to compensatory damages. In egregious cases, a buyer or seller may seek rescission of the purchase agreement—unwinding the deal entirely. The brokerage's errors and omissions insurance may or may not cover intentional disclosure failures, depending on the policy language.

> IC 25-34.1-10-10(c): "A licensee shall not act as a dual agent in a real estate transaction without the written consent of all parties to the transaction."

Brokers also face vicarious liability. If an agent under your supervision fails to disclose dual agency, IREC can and does name the managing broker in the complaint. The Commission views supervision failures as independent violations under IC 25-1-11-5, Indiana's general professional licensing enforcement statute.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Indiana Agency Compliance

**First: presenting the disclosure form too late.** Agents routinely wait until the offer stage to present agency disclosures because it feels like the natural "paperwork moment." By then, they have already engaged in substantive contact—sometimes weeks earlier. The form needs to appear the first time you have a real conversation about real estate needs.

Second: failing to obtain a new consent when the relationship changes mid-transaction. An agent who starts as a buyer's agent and then lists the buyer's current home through the same brokerage has created a dual agency or designated agency scenario that requires fresh written consent. Many agents assume the original disclosure covers all future dealings with that client. It does not.

Third: confusing the managing broker's role in designated agency. Some brokers continue advising both sides or sharing offer details between designated agents. Under IC 25-34.1-10-9.5, the managing broker must maintain confidentiality barriers. Acting as an information conduit between designated agents collapses the designation and converts the arrangement into undisclosed dual agency.

Fourth: relying on verbal consent "until we get to the office." The statute says written. Text messages and emails may satisfy the writing requirement in some contexts, but best practice—and what IREC expects to see in an audit—is a signed form. Verbal assurances provide zero protection in a complaint proceeding.

Fifth: not documenting the declination. When a consumer declines agency representation and wants to proceed as a customer, that declination needs written documentation too. Without it, the agent's file looks like a disclosure was never made, which is indistinguishable from a violation during an IREC audit.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers carry independent liability for agency compliance failures in their offices. IREC's investigative staff specifically examines whether brokerages have written policies, training records, and file-review protocols. A broker who cannot demonstrate a supervision system will face personal regulatory exposure alongside the offending agent.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|------------|---------------|-----------|
| Disclosure form timing | Date on form vs. first contact date in CRM or showing records | Every transaction |
| Dual agency consent signatures | Both parties signed before offer presentation | Every dual-agency file |
| Designated agency appointment memo | Managing broker's written designation in file | Every in-house deal |
| Confidentiality firewall documentation | Written policy, training acknowledgment | Annually + each occurrence |
| Declination documentation | Signed customer acknowledgment | Every non-represented party |

**Brokers should audit agency files within 48 hours of ratification, not at closing.** Catching a missing disclosure at closing leaves no meaningful time to cure the defect. By contrast, catching it early allows the agent to present the form (if it was truly an oversight rather than a timing violation) and document the circumstance. Patterns of late disclosures should trigger mandatory retraining and, if repeated, termination of the agent's affiliation.

Indiana's rules around [team structures and supervision](/blog/washington-teams-supervision-rules-2026) share philosophical DNA with agency supervision—principal brokers carry the compliance burden regardless of how autonomously agents operate day-to-day.

## Practical Tips for Staying Compliant in 2026

Build disclosure delivery into your first-contact workflow rather than your offer-preparation workflow. If you use a CRM that logs showing requests or initial consultations, tag that event as the disclosure trigger. Every day between first contact and form presentation is a day of non-compliance.

Keep pre-signed blank forms out of your process. IREC auditors have flagged brokerages where agents pre-print disclosure forms with dates that precede actual client meetings. The fix is simple: generate the form at the moment of contact, not in a batch at the start of the month.

For agents who [struggle with deadline tracking and paperwork sequencing](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), the agency disclosure is often the first ball that gets dropped—because it happens before the transaction file technically exists. The solution is treating the disclosure as the act that creates the file, not a document you add later.
