# The Exact Email Sequence Top TCs Send for Every New Contract

A single missed disclosure deadline costs the average brokerage $4,200 in legal fees before anyone even files a complaint. Multiply that across ten active files, and you are staring at five-figure liability exposure every month. The root cause is almost never malice or incompetence—it is the absence of a repeatable communication system that fires at the right moments. Understanding the exact email sequence top TCs send for every new contract is what separates agents who close predictably from agents who spend Thursday nights chasing signatures in a panic.

The National Association of Realtors' 2025 Member Profile reports that agents handling ten or more concurrent transactions spend an average of 6.3 hours per week on "administrative communication that could be templated." That is 327 hours a year—over eight full work weeks—burned on emails that say nearly the same thing every time. The fix is not working harder. The fix is installing a sequence that runs like clockwork from executed contract to closed file.

## Why Most Agents Lose Control in the First 48 Hours

The window between mutual acceptance and the first contingency deadline is where deals start bleeding. Title orders placed late cascade into delayed preliminary reports, which push back buyer review periods, which threaten financing timelines. One survey from the California Department of Real Estate's [enforcement action database](https://www.dre.ca.gov/Consumers/FileComplaint.html) shows that 38 percent of transaction-related complaints cite "failure to communicate material deadlines" as a contributing factor.

**Top-performing transaction coordinators eliminate this chaos by sending a structured welcome sequence within two hours of executed contract.** They do not wait for the agent to forward documents. They do not wait for the other side to reach out. They initiate, and that single behavior change reduces average days-to-close by four to six days according to internal data from brokerages running 200-plus transactions per year.

The sequence below is not theoretical. It is extracted from interviews with TCs managing 40-plus files per month at firms in Texas, California, and Florida. Every email has a purpose, a trigger, and a specific recipient list.

## Email One: The Intake Confirmation (Sent Within Two Hours)

The first email goes to the representing agent—your own team member or yourself—and accomplishes three things: confirms receipt of the executed contract, lists every document still needed, and establishes the communication cadence for the file.

Subject line format: "[Property Address] – Contract Received – Action Items Inside." The body contains the contract date, the buyer and seller names, the agreed close date, and a numbered list of no more than five missing items. Anything beyond five gets buried and ignored.

This email also states the TC's preferred response window. Something like: "I will send status updates every Tuesday and Friday. If you need something outside that rhythm, reply to any email in this thread." Setting that boundary early prevents the random 9 PM texts that erode work-life separation for everyone on the file.

## Email Two: The Party Introduction (Sent Within Four Hours)

This is the email most agents skip, and skipping it creates a communication vacuum that opposing agents fill with their own narrative. Email two goes to all parties: both agents, the lender, the title or escrow officer, and—depending on state practice—the attorneys.

**The purpose is singular: establish who is managing the file and what each party should expect next.** It reads like a short project brief. "I am coordinating this transaction on behalf of [Agent Name]. Below are the key dates and contacts. Please direct document requests and deadline questions to me at [email]."

Include the earnest money deadline, inspection period end date, appraisal contingency date, and projected closing date. Do not include everything. Four to five dates maximum. The goal is orientation, not overwhelm. This email also functions as a soft accountability mechanism—once everyone sees the dates in writing, claiming ignorance later becomes much harder.

## Email Three: The Earnest Money Tracker (Sent Day One or Two)

Earnest money disputes account for a disproportionate share of post-closing complaints. The TC's third email goes specifically to the party responsible for depositing earnest money and CCs the listing agent and escrow officer.

Subject line: "[Property Address] – EMD Due by [Date] – Deposit Instructions Attached." The body restates the contractual deadline, attaches or links to wiring instructions from the escrow company, and requests a receipt or confirmation once the deposit clears. One sentence at the bottom: "If this deposit is not confirmed by [date minus one day], I will follow up by phone."

That last sentence is not a threat. It is a service promise—and it has prevented more blown deals than any fancy CRM workflow. [Tracking earnest money deadlines](/blog/earnest-money-deadline-tracking) should never be an afterthought.

## Email Four: The Inspection Period Opener (Sent Day Two or Three)

Depending on market and contract terms, the inspection contingency window ranges from five to seventeen days. Email four goes to the buyer's agent (or directly to the buyer if you are the buyer's agent) and establishes the timeline for scheduling inspections, receiving reports, and submitting any repair requests.

The email names specific vendors only if the buyer has not yet selected inspectors. Otherwise, it simply states: "Please confirm your general inspector, and any specialty inspectors, by [date]. Reports should be in hand no later than [date minus three days from contingency expiration] to allow time for review and negotiation."

**This email prevents the single most common inspection-period failure: waiting until day nine of a ten-day window to schedule the inspector.** By day three, the TC has either received confirmation or escalated to the agent. That escalation happens via a short reply-all: "I have not yet received inspector confirmation. Please advise by end of day tomorrow."

## Email Five: The Lender Check-In (Sent Day Seven to Ten)

Lenders operate on their own timelines, and those timelines frequently ignore real estate contract deadlines. Email five goes directly to the loan officer and processor with a simple ask: confirm the appraisal has been ordered, provide the current estimated closing date from the lender's side, and flag any outstanding borrower conditions.

Subject line: "[Property Address] – Loan Status Check – Appraisal and Conditions." Keep it to four sentences maximum in the body. Loan officers receive hundreds of emails daily. Brevity earns responses.

If the lender does not reply within 48 hours, the TC sends a follow-up with the agent CC'd. That CC is deliberate—it signals to the lender that silence has been noticed and that the agent is now aware. Most loan officers respond within an hour of seeing the agent's name appear in the thread.

## Email Six: The Title and Document Review (Sent Day Ten to Fourteen)

By this point, the preliminary title report (or title commitment, depending on jurisdiction) should be available. Email six notifies the buyer's side that the report is ready for review and reiterates any contractual deadline for objecting to title exceptions.

This email also serves as a document checkpoint. The TC lists any outstanding items needed for closing: HOA documents, survey, termite clearance, repair receipts, or signed disclosures. Each item gets a one-line status—"Received," "Requested on [date]," or "Needed from [party] by [date]."

Agents managing [multiple active listings](/blog/managing-multiple-listings-efficiently) find this checkpoint email invaluable because it surfaces problems while there is still runway to fix them. Waiting until the week of closing to discover a missing HOA resale certificate is a self-inflicted wound.

## Email Seven: The Clear-to-Close Notification (Sent Three to Five Days Before Closing)

This is the email that makes everyone exhale. It confirms the lender has issued a clear-to-close, the title company has received all documents, and the closing appointment is scheduled. It includes the date, time, location (or instructions for remote notarization), and a list of what each party needs to bring.

Subject line: "[Property Address] – Clear to Close – Closing Scheduled [Date/Time]." The body breaks down the final settlement statement review timeline: "The Closing Disclosure was sent on [date]. The three-day review period ends [date]. Signing is confirmed for [date] at [location]."

One often-missed detail: reminding buyers to wire funds early and to call the title company directly to verify wiring instructions. Wire fraud losses in real estate exceeded $446 million in 2024 according to FBI IC3 data. A single sentence about verifying instructions by phone is not optional anymore.

## Email Eight: The Post-Close Wrap (Sent Day of Closing or Next Business Day)

The final email goes to all parties and accomplishes three things: confirms the transaction has recorded (or will record on a specific date), provides the recording number when available, and thanks everyone without being saccharine about it. Two sentences of gratitude maximum.

This email also contains a subtle but powerful element—a request for the agent's own file notes. "If there is anything about this transaction you would like documented for your records or for future reference with this client, please reply by [date]." That prompt captures institutional knowledge that otherwise evaporates the moment the commission check deposits.
