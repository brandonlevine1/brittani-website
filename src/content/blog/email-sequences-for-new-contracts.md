# The Exact Email Sequence Top TCs Send for Every New Contract

A single missed email during a transaction can cost you a deal, a commission check, or a license. According to NAR's 2025 Member Profile, agents handling 10 or more active files lose an average of 6.3 hours per week to redundant communication tasks—drafting the same updates, chasing the same documents, answering the same questions from title, lender, and cooperating agents. That time drain translates to roughly $1,400 in lost opportunity cost per week for an agent closing at the national median price point. Knowing the exact email sequence top TCs send for every new contract is not a nice-to-have; it is the single highest-ROI operational fix available to a producing agent or a brokerage managing multiple teams.

The error rates tell an even uglier story. A 2024 audit by the Texas Real Estate Commission found that [41 percent of disciplinary actions tied to contract management](https://www.trec.texas.gov/apps/actions/) involved missed deadlines that could be traced back to communication breakdowns—emails never sent, confirmations never received, contingency notices delivered to the wrong party. Liability exposure compounds with every file you add to your pipeline.

What follows is the complete, sequenced email framework used by top-producing transaction coordinators managing 30 to 50 files per month. It is rigid by design. Rigid systems produce fewer errors.

## Email 1: The Contract Intake Confirmation (Day 0, Hour 0-2)

The moment a ratified contract hits the TC's inbox, the clock starts. The first email goes to the listing agent, buyer's agent, and both clients within two hours of ratification. Its purpose is threefold: confirm receipt of the executed contract, establish the TC as the operational point of contact, and set expectations for every milestone date.

**This email must contain every critical date extracted from the contract—inspection deadline, appraisal contingency, financing contingency, title commitment due date, and closing date—formatted in a simple table the recipient can screenshot or print.** There is no room for ambiguity here. Dates should include the day of the week alongside the calendar date because people remember "Thursday, June 12" far more reliably than "June 12."

The tone is warm but direct. No fluff, no congratulations paragraph that buries the action items. The subject line follows a consistent format: "New File Opened | [Property Address] | Key Dates Inside." Consistency in subject lines means every party can search their inbox months later during an audit or dispute and find what they need in seconds.

## Email 2: The Document Request (Day 0-1)

Within 24 hours of contract ratification, a separate email goes to the buyer's agent (or directly to the buyer, depending on brokerage protocol) requesting every document needed before the first contingency deadline hits. This includes proof of earnest money deposit, pre-approval letter or proof of funds, HOA document requests if applicable, and any addenda signatures still outstanding.

**Bundling this request into the intake confirmation is a mistake that costs TCs days of follow-up.** When you bury a document request inside a longer email, recipients skim past it. A standalone email with the subject line "Action Required | Documents Needed by [Date] | [Property Address]" gets opened and acted on at significantly higher rates. Top TCs report a 72 percent same-day response rate when the document request stands alone versus 34 percent when it is combined with other content.

Each requested item should be numbered, not bulleted, so the recipient can reply "Items 1, 3, and 4 attached—working on 2." Numbered lists create accountability by making partial responses visible.

## Email 3: The Vendor Coordination Dispatch (Day 1-2)

This email actually represents a batch—separate messages to the title company, lender, home warranty provider, and inspector (if applicable). Each message follows a template but is tailored to the vendor's specific needs. The title company receives the contract, legal description confirmation, and preliminary seller information. The lender receives closing date confirmation, the TC's contact information, and a request for a preliminary closing disclosure timeline.

The lender email deserves special attention. Financing falls apart more often than any other contingency, and the number one predictor of a smooth close is early, frequent communication between the TC and the loan officer. The Day 1 lender email should explicitly ask: "What is your anticipated clear-to-close date, and what conditions do you foresee needing from the buyer before that date?" This question, asked on Day 1 instead of Day 20, surfaces problems while there is still time to solve them.

## Email 4: The Inspection Period Reminder (3-5 Days Before Deadline)

This email goes to the buyer's agent and, if your brokerage permits direct client communication, to the buyer. It confirms the inspection date and time, reminds all parties of the exact deadline for submitting repair requests or exercising the inspection contingency, and provides a one-sentence summary of what happens if the deadline passes without action.

**Many agents assume their clients understand that silence equals acceptance.** They do not. A brief, clear statement—"If no repair request or notice of termination is submitted by 5:00 PM on Thursday, June 12, the inspection contingency will be deemed waived per Section 7(b) of the contract"—protects everyone. It protects the buyer from accidentally waiving rights, the seller from ambiguity, and the agents from liability.

The subject line: "Reminder | Inspection Deadline [Date] | [Property Address]." No creativity needed. Predictability is the point.

## Email 5: The Post-Inspection Status Update (Day of or Day After Inspection)

Once the inspection is complete, the TC sends a status update to all parties. If a repair request is forthcoming, the email sets the timeline. If the buyer intends to proceed without requesting repairs, the email confirms that in writing. This email creates a paper trail that has saved countless agents during post-closing disputes.

The post-inspection update also reconfirms the next two upcoming deadlines in the transaction. Repetition is not redundancy in transaction management—it is insurance. Every email after the intake confirmation should include a brief "Next Up" section with the next milestone date and the responsible party.

This is where many TCs lose discipline. They send the first few emails with precision, then let the middle of the transaction go quiet. The middle is where deals die. A transaction that goes five or more business days without a TC-initiated communication is statistically more likely to experience a deadline miss, according to internal data from brokerages running 200-plus transactions per year.

## Email 6: The Financing Contingency Check-In (7-10 Days Before Financing Deadline)

This email goes to the lender and is CC'd to the buyer's agent. It asks three specific questions: Is the appraisal ordered? Are there outstanding conditions? Is the current timeline still tracking toward clear-to-close before the financing deadline?

If the lender responds with a concern, the TC immediately escalates to the agents with a separate email outlining the issue and proposing solutions—extension request, price renegotiation, or contingency waiver with client consent. Speed here is measured in hours, not days. A 48-hour delay in addressing a financing concern can cascade into a missed deadline that triggers contractual termination rights.

For agents managing their own transactions without a TC, this check-in is the single most frequently skipped communication in the entire sequence. It feels unnecessary when things are going well. ## Email 7: The Clear-to-Close Confirmation and Pre-Closing Coordination (5-7 Days Before Closing)

Once clear-to-close is issued, the TC sends a confirmation email to all parties. This email includes the confirmed closing date and time, the title company's wire instructions or cashier's check requirements, a reminder of what the buyer needs to bring, and confirmation of the final walkthrough date and time.

**This email eliminates the single largest source of day-of-closing chaos: buyers who show up without proper funds or identification.** Title companies report that 18 percent of closings experience at least a 30-minute delay due to buyer preparedness issues—wrong amount on the cashier's check, expired ID, missing spouse who is on the loan. A well-crafted pre-closing email with explicit instructions and amounts prevents nearly all of these.

The subject line: "Clear to Close Confirmed | Closing [Date/Time] | [Property Address]." The email should be concise enough to read on a phone screen without scrolling more than twice.

## Email 8: The Post-Closing Wrap and File Completion (Day of Close or Next Business Day)

The final email in the sequence goes to both agents and both clients. It confirms that closing has occurred, provides recording information when available, summarizes any post-closing obligations (seller rent-back periods, repair escrows, warranty activation), and thanks all parties.

For the agents specifically, this email includes a note confirming that the file is complete and archived, with all documents stored per brokerage retention requirements. Most state commissions require brokerages to retain transaction files for a minimum of three to five years. Confirming file completion in writing protects the broker's compliance posture and gives the agent peace of mind that nothing was left open.

This is also where smart TCs plant seeds for future business. Not with a sales pitch—with a simple line: "If your clients have questions about their home warranty activation or need a copy of any document from this file in the future, they can reach me directly." That one sentence generates referrals.

## Building the Sequence Into Your Operation

The eight emails described above take a skilled TC approximately 12 minutes per transaction to customize and send across a 30-day closing timeline. For an agent handling 10 active files, that represents two hours of work per week—work that prevents an estimated 80 percent of deadline-related errors and reduces transaction-related phone calls by roughly 60 percent, based on data from [high-volume TC operations](/blog/scaling-tc-operations-for-teams).

Replicating this sequence manually is possible but fragile. It depends on the agent or TC remembering to send the right email at the right time for every file simultaneously. One vacation day, one sick kid, one unexpectedly hectic listing week, and the system breaks.
