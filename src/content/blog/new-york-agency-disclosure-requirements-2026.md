## Understanding New York Agency Disclosure Requirements 2026

**New York agency disclosure requirements 2026 remain one of the most misunderstood compliance obligations in the state, yet violating them can unravel a transaction weeks after a contract is signed.** The mandate stems from Real Property Law Section 443 (RPL 443), which requires every licensed agent and broker to disclose their agency relationship to all parties before any substantive contact takes place. The New York State Department of State (NYSDOS), Division of Licensing Services, enforces these requirements and has shown no signs of relaxing its audit activity heading into 2026.

The form itself — officially designated DOS-1736, titled "Disclosure Regarding Real Estate Agency Relationships" — is not optional. It is a state-mandated document that explains buyer's agency, seller's agency, broker's agents, and dual agency in plain language so consumers can make informed decisions. Failing to present it at the right time creates liability that falls squarely on the licensee and their supervising broker.

Unlike some states that allow a general verbal explanation followed by written confirmation, New York demands the written form be provided before any confidential information changes hands. This means before you discuss motivation, financial capacity, or property-specific negotiation strategy with a prospective client. The timing distinction matters more than most agents realize.

## The Legal Framework: RPL 443 and DOS-1736

Real Property Law Section 443 was originally enacted in 1996 and has been amended multiple times since. Its core requirement has stayed consistent: a licensee must provide a written disclosure form describing the types of agency relationships available to the consumer at first substantive contact. The [full text of RPL 443 is available through the New York State Legislature's website](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/laws/RPP/443).

The DOS-1736 form itself is two pages. Page one describes the various agency relationships — seller's agent, buyer's agent, broker's agent, and dual agent. Page two contains the acknowledgment signature block where the consumer confirms receipt of the form and may indicate their chosen relationship type.

**A critical distinction: the consumer does not have to select a relationship type at the time they sign the form.** The signature on page two only confirms they received and read the disclosure. Many agents confuse this step with signing a buyer agency agreement, which is a separate obligation that gained renewed importance after the NAR settlement changes. ## Timing: When Disclosure Must Happen

The phrase "first substantive contact" is the trigger point, and it trips up agents constantly. NYSDOS interprets this broadly. Any discussion that goes beyond general factual information about the market constitutes substantive contact. Asking a buyer what price range they are looking in, what neighborhoods they prefer, or whether they have mortgage pre-approval — all of these cross the threshold.

| Scenario | Disclosure Required? | Reasoning |
|----------|---------------------|-----------|
| Open house visitor asks about square footage | Yes | Property-specific discussion initiated |
| Cold call asking if someone wants a market analysis | Yes, upon engagement | Substantive contact begins when seller shares details |
| Buyer calls from a yard sign asking listing price | No (if limited to public facts) | General information only |
| Buyer then asks "Is the seller flexible?" | Yes | Crosses into substantive/confidential territory |
| Referral from another agent, first phone call | Yes | First substantive contact with new licensee |

The safest practice is to present the DOS-1736 before any meaningful conversation begins. Agents who wait until a showing or, worse, until offer submission are already in violation. The statute does not provide a grace period.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Agency Disclosure

Five errors account for the majority of compliance failures that NYSDOS investigators flag during audits and complaint investigations.

**First, agents present the form but fail to obtain a signature.** The statute requires the licensee to make a good-faith effort to obtain the consumer's acknowledgment signature. If a consumer refuses to sign, the agent must note the refusal, the date, and the circumstances on the form — and retain that documented refusal in the file. Simply handing over the form without following up is insufficient.

Second, agents conflate the DOS-1736 with a buyer representation agreement. These are two different documents with different legal functions. The disclosure form explains possible relationships. The representation agreement creates one. Since the 2024 NAR settlement reforms, buyer agency agreements have become standard practice, but they do not replace the state-mandated disclosure obligation.

Third, teams and assistants often handle initial contact without providing the form. If an unlicensed assistant takes a call and begins discussing property details or buyer needs, the supervising agent has already missed the disclosure window. The obligation attaches to the brokerage the moment substantive contact occurs through any representative.

Fourth, agents working dual agency forget that a new, separate disclosure and informed consent is required before proceeding. RPL 443 requires advance informed consent from both parties for dual agency — and this consent must be documented in writing. A general acknowledgment on the original DOS-1736 is not sufficient for dual agency consent.

Fifth, agents relocating from other states assume New York's rules mirror what they knew elsewhere. The timing requirements, form content, and enforcement mechanisms differ significantly from states like [Texas](/blog/texas-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) or [Florida](/blog/florida-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026), where disclosure obligations attach at different transaction stages.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

NYSDOS takes agency disclosure violations seriously, and the consequences extend beyond a slap on the wrist. Under 19 NYCRR 175.7 (the Department's regulations governing real estate licensees), failure to provide timely and proper agency disclosure constitutes demonstrable incompetence or untrustworthiness — grounds for disciplinary action.

| Consequence | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Fine per violation | Up to $1,000 per occurrence |
| License suspension | Possible for repeated violations |
| License revocation | Reserved for egregious or fraudulent conduct |
| Deal rescission | Aggrieved party may void the contract |
| Civil liability | Damages for breach of fiduciary duty |
| Broker liability | Supervising broker shares disciplinary exposure |

The deal rescission risk is what should concern agents most in practical terms. A buyer who was never properly informed of an agent's dual agency status can petition to void the purchase contract. Courts have sided with consumers on this issue repeatedly. The financial fallout — lost commissions, potential damages, legal fees — dwarfs any fine the Department might impose.

Beyond formal discipline, complaints about agency disclosure failures become part of a licensee's permanent record with NYSDOS. Future license renewals, brokerage applications, and even E&O insurance underwriting can be affected. Brokers should understand that their own license is at stake when agents under their supervision fail to comply, a point addressed directly in the next section.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers carry direct liability for disclosure failures committed by their associated licensees. NYSDOS regulation 19 NYCRR 175.21 requires brokers to adequately supervise all licensed activities conducted under their authority. A broker who cannot produce signed DOS-1736 forms for every transaction — and every substantive contact that did not result in a transaction — is exposed.

**Brokers should implement a file-review protocol that checks for the signed disclosure form before any offer is submitted.** This is not just a best practice; it is a risk management imperative. If the form is missing at the offer stage, the brokerage has a narrow window to cure the deficiency before the transaction progresses further.

A quarterly audit of open and closed files should verify three things: the DOS-1736 is present and signed (or refusal documented), the form date precedes any substantive activity noted in the file, and dual agency consent is separately documented where applicable. Agents who consistently produce incomplete files need retraining — not just reminders.

Brokerages managing high transaction volumes should also track disclosure compliance for open house contacts. Every sign-in sheet should be paired with a disclosure acknowledgment process. The operational burden is real, but it pales compared to the cost of a single complaint-driven investigation. ## Dual Agency: The Heightened Disclosure Standard

New York permits dual agency, but only with informed written consent from both the buyer and seller. This goes beyond simply checking a box on the DOS-1736. The licensee must explain in plain terms what dual agency means — specifically, that the agent cannot advocate for either party's interests exclusively and cannot disclose confidential negotiation information from one party to the other.

RPL 443(3) spells out that the dual agent must obtain this consent before the dual agency relationship commences. In practice, this means before the buyer's agent shows a listing held by their own brokerage, or before a listing agent begins working with an unrepresented buyer on their own listing.

**The consent must be knowing and voluntary, which means a buried clause in a representation agreement does not satisfy the statute.** Courts have interpreted this requirement to mean a standalone, clearly identified consent that the consumer can read and understand without legal training. Many brokerages use a supplemental dual agency consent form separate from the DOS-1736, though the Department has not mandated a specific state form for this purpose.

## Practical Workflow for Compliance

The simplest way to stay compliant is to build disclosure into the first sixty seconds of any new contact. Keep printed DOS-1736 forms in your car, your open house kit, and your office. Have a PDF version ready to send via email or text for remote contacts, with e-signature capability through your transaction management platform.

For phone contacts, state your brokerage name and agency status verbally, then immediately follow up with the written form via email. Document the time of the call and the time the form was sent. If the consumer engages in substantive discussion before returning the signed form, note that you provided verbal disclosure and the written form simultaneously — this protects you if a dispute arises later.

Document everything. The agents who face disciplinary action are almost never those who made a single innocent mistake — they are agents who have no documentation at all, leaving investigators to conclude the form was never provided. A signed form with a clear date is your best defense against any future complaint.
