## Texas Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Definitive Rule

The question of "Texas attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026" comes up every cycle, and the answer has remained consistent: Texas does not mandate attorney presence at closing. Unlike states such as New York, Georgia, or South Carolina that require an attorney to supervise or conduct settlement, Texas places that authority squarely on licensed title companies and their escrow officers operating under Texas Insurance Code Title 11, Subtitle B.

That does not mean attorneys play zero role. It means agents need to know exactly where the legal lines sit so they avoid liability, keep deals on track, and protect their licenses. The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) and the Texas Department of Insurance (TDI) each regulate different pieces of the closing puzzle, and misunderstanding their boundaries is one of the fastest ways to blow a transaction or catch an enforcement action.

## Who Can Legally Conduct a Closing in Texas

**Texas law authorizes three categories of parties to conduct a real estate closing.** First, a title insurance company licensed by TDI under Texas Insurance Code Chapter 2501. Second, a title insurance agent operating under a direct or agency relationship with an underwriter. Third, an attorney who is acting as a title agent and has filed the proper appointment with TDI.

An escrow officer employed by the title company physically handles the closing in most residential transactions. That officer must comply with TDI rules on escrow account management (28 TAC Section 2.601 et seq.) and use the promulgated rate manual for title premium calculations. The closing itself—document execution, fund disbursement, recording instructions—falls under the title company's responsibility, not the agent's and not an attorney's unless the parties independently retain one.

| Role | Legal Authority | Regulatory Body | Required at Closing? |
|------|----------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Title Company / Escrow Officer | TX Insurance Code Ch. 2501 | TDI | Yes (one must conduct) |
| Attorney (as title agent) | TX Insurance Code Ch. 2551 | TDI + State Bar | Only if acting as title agent |
| Attorney (for buyer/seller) | Client engagement letter | State Bar of Texas | No — optional |
| Real Estate Agent | TX Occupations Code Ch. 1101 | TREC | No — cannot conduct closing |
| Lender Representative | Federal lending regulations | CFPB / state banking dept. | No — documents sent to title |

The key takeaway from this table: a licensed agent cannot conduct a closing. Period. Your role ends at contract negotiation and delivery of documents to the appropriate party. When agents try to step beyond that boundary, TREC enforcement gets involved.

## What TREC Rules Say About Agent Conduct at Closing

TREC's administrative rules under 22 TAC Section 535.146 explicitly prohibit agents from practicing law. This includes drafting legal documents, interpreting deed language for clients, or advising on title curative matters. The only documents agents may fill in are TREC-promulgated forms and TREC-approved forms—nothing else.

**Agents regularly sit at the closing table as a courtesy or to answer questions about the transaction timeline, but their legal role there is zero.** You cannot explain what a deed of trust clause means. You cannot advise your client to sign or refuse to sign a document based on your interpretation of its legal effect. If a dispute arises at the table, you direct the client to their attorney or to the escrow officer for procedural questions.

The promulgated forms you do control earlier in the transaction matter here because errors in those forms cascade into closing problems. TREC Form 20-16 (One to Four Family Residential Contract) and its addenda—including the Third Party Financing Addendum (TREC Form 40-9) and the Seller's Disclosure Notice—set the terms that the title company must execute. Mistakes on those forms are your liability, not the title company's. ## The Attorney's Optional but Sometimes Critical Role

Texas buyers and sellers have every right to hire an attorney at any point in the transaction. The [Texas State Bar](https://www.texasbar.com/AM/Template.cfm?Section=Lawyer_Referral_Service_702) even operates a referral service for consumers who need real estate counsel. But hiring an attorney is a party decision, not a statutory closing requirement.

When does attorney involvement become practically necessary, even if not legally mandated? Complex commercial transactions, estate sales where probate issues cloud title, 1031 exchanges requiring structured timing, and transactions involving entity formation all benefit from counsel. In these scenarios, the attorney typically reviews the title commitment, negotiates exceptions, and may draft documents outside the TREC promulgated set—something an agent absolutely cannot do.

For standard residential deals, the title company handles everything from title search through policy issuance. The escrow officer collects documents from the lender, prepares the settlement statement (now the TRID Closing Disclosure, coordinated with the lender), oversees execution, records the deed, and disburses funds. No attorney is needed for that workflow unless a party wants one for their own protection.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make Around Closing Authority

**Mistake one: advising clients on title commitment exceptions.** When a client asks what Schedule B Exception 4 means for their property rights, agents routinely explain it instead of directing the client to the title officer or an attorney. This is unauthorized practice of law and creates personal liability if the explanation is wrong.

Mistake two: filling in non-promulgated forms. Some agents draft their own addenda or modify lender-required documents. Under 22 TAC Section 535.146 and TREC's enforcement stance, this exposes the agent to disciplinary action including fines up to $5,000 per violation and potential license suspension.

Mistake three: pressuring a client to close without reviewing documents. The TRID rule gives buyers three business days to review the Closing Disclosure. Agents who push clients to waive that review or who schedule closings that mathematically eliminate the review window create liability for themselves and potentially violate federal Consumer Financial Protection Bureau rules.

Mistake four: accepting or paying referral fees tied to title company selection. RESPA Section 8 prohibits kickbacks. Texas agents who receive marketing fees, office space, or other value from a title company in exchange for directing business face both federal penalties (up to $10,000 per violation) and TREC disciplinary action.

Mistake five: representing that an attorney must be present when one is not required. This misstatement can delay closings, add cost to the client, and in some cases constitutes misrepresentation under TREC's Standards of Conduct (22 TAC Section 531.1). Agents who track deadlines through a reliable system—whether manual or automated—avoid this kind of miscommunication. ## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.803. If an agent under your supervision engages in unauthorized practice of law or mishandles closing-related procedures, you face the same disciplinary exposure.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency | Consequence of Failure |
|------------|--------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Addenda and contract forms | Only TREC-promulgated or approved forms used | Every transaction | Fine up to $5,000; license action |
| Agent conduct at closing | No legal interpretation given to clients | Quarterly review or complaint-triggered | UPL complaint; E&O claim |
| Title company referral practices | No kickbacks; AfBA disclosures filed | Annual compliance audit | RESPA penalties; TREC action |
| Closing timeline compliance | TRID 3-day review honored | Every financed transaction | Federal violation; deal rescission |
| Earnest money handling | Deposited per contract terms; trust account rules followed | Every transaction | Trust account violation; license revocation |

**Brokers should implement a file review checklist that specifically flags non-promulgated documents and any written communication where an agent appears to interpret legal terms.** A quarterly audit of closed files catches patterns before they become enforcement actions. If your brokerage handles volume, consider whether your [transaction handoff process](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) includes a compliance gate before documents reach the title company.

Training new agents on these boundaries from day one reduces risk exposure dramatically. The gray area between "explaining the process" and "giving legal advice" is where most violations occur, and it takes deliberate coaching to keep agents on the right side of that line.

## How 2026 Changes Affect the Closing Landscape in Texas

No new legislation effective in 2026 changes the fundamental attorney-optional structure of Texas closings. However, TDI's ongoing review of title premium rates and TREC's periodic form revisions (the latest round updating several addenda effective January 2026) mean agents must verify they are using current form versions at every contract execution.

TREC's revised Seller's Disclosure Notice and updated language in the Third Party Financing Addendum require agents to pull fresh forms from the TREC website rather than relying on cached versions in their CRM or transaction management software. Using an outdated form version is a citable violation even if the substance is correct.

The federal CFPB has also signaled increased scrutiny of closing cost transparency, which means title companies may request more documentation from agents earlier in the process. Agents who maintain organized transaction files—with every addendum, disclosure, and communication logged—will move through this scrutiny without friction. Those who rely on memory or scattered email threads will find 2026 closings slower and more frustrating.
