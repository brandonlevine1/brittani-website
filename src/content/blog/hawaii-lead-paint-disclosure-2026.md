## Understanding Hawaii Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

The Hawaii lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 affect every residential transaction involving a property built before 1978. Despite Hawaii's tropical climate and reputation for newer resort-style construction, the state has a substantial inventory of older homes, particularly plantation-era residences on the Big Island, Maui, and Oahu's leeward corridors. Federal law mandates specific disclosures and procedures that agents ignore at their own financial peril.

Hawaii does not have a separate state-level lead paint statute that adds to or modifies the federal requirements. Instead, agents must comply with the federal Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulations at 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F. The Hawaii Real Estate Commission (HREC), operating under the Department of Commerce and Consumer Affairs (DCCA), enforces these requirements through its licensing authority.

**Failure to comply can result in federal civil penalties up to $19,507 per violation as of the 2024 EPA adjustment, and HREC can independently discipline your license.** That dual enforcement mechanism means a single missed disclosure could trigger both a federal fine and a state licensing action simultaneously.

## The Federal Framework That Governs Hawaii Transactions

Because Hawaii has not enacted standalone lead paint disclosure legislation, agents operate entirely under the federal framework. The relevant authority is [42 U.S.C. 4852d](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/4852d) and its regulations promulgated jointly by EPA and HUD. The disclosure obligation applies to any sale or lease of "target housing," which means residential housing built before 1978, with limited exceptions for housing exclusively for the elderly, zero-bedroom units, and properties already certified lead-free by an accredited inspector.

The required form is the EPA/HUD standard "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form. In Hawaii, most brokerages use the form distributed through Hawaii Association of REALTORS (HAR) transaction packages, which mirrors the federal template but includes Hawaii-specific formatting. The HAR form is typically referenced as part of the Purchase Contract addenda package rather than bearing a standalone form number like some mainland states assign.

Agents must ensure three things happen before a buyer becomes obligated: the seller discloses known lead-based paint or hazards, the seller provides any existing reports or records, and the buyer receives the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." All three steps must be documented with signatures from seller, buyer, and both agents.

## Form Requirements and Documentation Standards

**The disclosure form requires signatures from four parties: seller, buyer, listing agent, and selling agent.** A missing signature from any party renders the form incomplete and the transaction non-compliant. This is where many Hawaii deals develop problems, particularly in transactions involving mainland buyers who sign remotely or sellers represented by attorneys-in-fact under a power of attorney.

The seller must check one of two boxes on the disclosure form: either they have knowledge of lead-based paint or hazards, or they have no knowledge. There is no option for "decline to disclose." Similarly, the seller must indicate whether any reports or records exist and either attach them or confirm none are available. The buyer then acknowledges receipt of the information and the EPA pamphlet, and either accepts or waives the 10-day inspection opportunity.

| Party | Obligation | Consequence of Non-Compliance |
|-------|-----------|-------------------------------|
| Seller | Disclose known hazards, provide records, deliver EPA pamphlet | Treble damages to buyer, contract rescission |
| Listing Agent | Ensure seller compliance, retain forms 3 years | Federal fine up to $19,507, HREC license discipline |
| Buyer's Agent | Ensure buyer receives pamphlet and 10-day notice, sign form | Federal fine, potential malpractice liability |
| Buyer | Acknowledge receipt, elect/waive inspection period | Loss of rescission rights |

Agents should retain signed copies for three years from the closing date per 40 CFR 745.113. Electronic storage is permissible, but the original signatures must be preserved in a retrievable format.

## Common Mistakes Hawaii Agents Make

The first recurring error is assuming renovated properties are exempt. A home built in 1955 that received new drywall and paint in 2010 still triggers the disclosure requirement. Only a certified lead inspector's determination that the property is lead-free eliminates the obligation. Renovation alone does not qualify as clearance, and agents who skip the disclosure based on a seller's assurance that "everything has been redone" expose themselves to liability.

The second mistake involves the 10-day inspection period. Agents frequently insert language in the purchase contract that compresses or eliminates the buyer's right to conduct a lead inspection without using the specific waiver language on the federal form. The contract timeline and the lead disclosure form operate independently. Even if the buyer agrees to a five-day inspection contingency in the purchase contract, the lead paint disclosure must separately document that the buyer either received the full 10-day opportunity or explicitly waived it on the form itself.

**The third common error is failing to provide the disclosure for lease transactions.** Hawaii's rental market is active, and landlords listing units built before 1978 must comply with the same disclosure framework. Agents who handle property management or rental placements often overlook this requirement because they associate lead paint disclosure exclusively with sales.

The fourth mistake is treating the disclosure as a one-time event. If the seller learns of new information about lead hazards after the initial disclosure but before closing, they must supplement the form. Agents who completed the paperwork at listing and never revisit it may miss material changes that arise during the inspection or repair period.

The fifth error, specific to Hawaii's market dynamics, involves properties sold through living trusts or estate sales. Successor trustees and personal representatives often have no actual knowledge of lead conditions, and they incorrectly mark "no knowledge" without investigating records that may exist from prior inspections. The agent's duty to inform the seller of their obligations includes advising trustees to check estate files for any historical reports.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers-in-charge carry supervisory liability under both federal law and Hawaii Revised Statutes Chapter 467 (Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons). HREC has consistently held that a principal broker's failure to implement compliance systems constitutes a violation independent of any individual agent's error. This means your brokerage must have a documented procedure for lead paint disclosure on every pre-1978 transaction.

**Audit every file for four elements: the completed and fully-signed disclosure form, proof the EPA pamphlet was delivered, documentation of the buyer's election or waiver of the 10-day period, and copies of any reports the seller provided.** If any element is missing, the file is deficient regardless of whether the transaction closed successfully.

Brokers should also verify that their agents are using the current version of the EPA pamphlet. The pamphlet was last updated in 2020, and older versions floating around in brokerage template folders may not satisfy the regulatory requirement. The current version is available directly from EPA's website.

| Audit Item | What to Look For | Red Flag |
|------------|-----------------|----------|
| Disclosure Form | All four signatures present, both seller boxes checked | Missing agent signature, blank seller section |
| EPA Pamphlet | Current 2020 version delivered before ratification | Outdated version, no delivery confirmation |
| 10-Day Period | Buyer waiver or election documented on form | Compressed timeline with no separate waiver |
| Records Attachment | Any known reports attached or "none available" marked | Box left blank, reports referenced but not included |
| Retention | File stored for minimum 3 years from closing | Files purged at 2-year mark or missing entirely |

Consider assigning a transaction coordinator or compliance officer to verify lead paint disclosure completion as a standard checkpoint. The cost of a [dedicated transaction coordinator](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) handling these audit functions is negligible compared to a single federal penalty action. Agents who [manage multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously are particularly vulnerable to missing this step on one or two files.

## Penalties and Liability Exposure in Hawaii

Federal enforcement actions under Section 1018 can result in civil penalties up to $19,507 per violation. The EPA and HUD jointly enforce through their respective regional offices, and Hawaii falls under EPA Region 9 (San Francisco). Violations are identified through buyer complaints, random audits, and referrals from state regulators.

Beyond federal fines, Hawaii buyers can pursue private civil actions under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3), which allows for treble damages. That means if a buyer suffers $50,000 in lead abatement costs because the seller and agents failed to disclose known hazards, the court can award $150,000 plus attorney fees. Both the listing agent and the buyer's agent can be named as defendants if they failed to ensure compliance.

HREC can also impose license sanctions ranging from fines to suspension or revocation under HRS 467-14, which grants authority to discipline licensees for violations of any law related to real estate practice. A federal lead paint violation constitutes grounds for discipline even without a separate state statute specifically addressing lead paint.

The buyer retains the right to rescind the purchase contract if the disclosure requirements were not met before ratification. This rescission right has no expiration and can be exercised even after closing, creating ongoing liability that doesn't disappear at the recording office.

## Practical Compliance Steps for 2026

Start by verifying the construction date of every listing during your initial CMA or listing presentation research. Hawaii's county tax records, accessible through the City and County of Honolulu Real Property Assessment Division or the respective county systems for Maui, Hawaii, and Kauai, include original construction dates. Do not rely solely on the seller's memory.

If the property was built before 1978, prepare the disclosure form as part of your listing package, not as an afterthought during contract negotiation. Having the seller complete their portion at listing appointment time means you have their disclosure on hand for any offer that comes in, eliminating the scramble to get signatures when [deadlines are already tight](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

For buyer's agents, build the lead paint disclosure check into your first contact with the listing side. Ask whether the disclosure has been prepared and request a copy with any seller-provided reports before your client tours the property. Delivering the EPA pamphlet to your buyer at the first showing of a pre-1978 home, rather than at contract, demonstrates diligence and creates a cleaner compliance record.
