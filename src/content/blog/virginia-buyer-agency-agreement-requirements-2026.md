## Virginia Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026: The Rules Have Teeth

The Virginia buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 should be front of mind for every licensee writing offers this year. After the settlement-driven changes that swept through real estate in 2024 and 2025, Virginia's regulatory framework has clarified expectations around written buyer representation in ways that carry real enforcement consequences. Agents who treat these agreements as a formality—or worse, skip them altogether—are exposing themselves and their brokerages to license discipline, commission disputes, and deal-killing liability.

Virginia Code Section 54.1-2138 establishes the statutory foundation for buyer agency relationships in the Commonwealth. The Virginia Real Estate Board (VREB), operating under the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), enforces compliance through its licensing and disciplinary authority. This is not guidance that lives only in a training manual; it shows up in board complaints, arbitration panels, and courtrooms.

## What the Statute Actually Requires

**Virginia Code Section 54.1-2138 mandates that a licensee acting as a buyer's agent must enter into a written brokerage agreement with the buyer before providing any brokerage services.** The statute distinguishes between ministerial acts (providing general market information, opening a door for access) and substantive brokerage services (advising on price, negotiating terms, identifying specific properties based on buyer criteria).

The written agreement must contain specific elements: identification of the parties, the scope of services, the duration of the agreement with a definite termination date, compensation terms, and a description of the duties the agent owes the buyer. Virginia law requires disclosure of whether the agent's compensation will come from the buyer, the seller, or both.

VREB Regulation 18 VAC 135-20-180 further specifies that the agreement must disclose the type of agency relationship being established. This ties directly into the broader [Virginia agency disclosure framework](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) that governs how agents communicate their role to all parties in a transaction.

## The Forms: VAR 320 and 320-A

The Virginia Association of Realtors publishes the standard forms most brokerages adopt. VAR Form 320 is the Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement, which grants one agent or brokerage exclusive authority to represent the buyer during a specified term. VAR Form 320-A is the Non-Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement, which allows the buyer to work with multiple agents simultaneously.

| Feature | VAR Form 320 (Exclusive) | VAR Form 320-A (Non-Exclusive) |
|---------|--------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Agent exclusivity | Yes — one brokerage only | No — buyer may use multiple agents |
| Commission protection | Agent earns fee on any purchase during term | Agent earns fee only on properties they introduce |
| Termination clause | Definite end date required | Definite end date required |
| Compensation source | Must be disclosed (buyer, seller, or both) | Must be disclosed (buyer, seller, or both) |
| Scope of services | Defined geographic area or property type | Typically property-specific |

Both forms require the buyer's acknowledgment of the agent's duties under Section 54.1-2138, including loyalty, obedience, confidentiality, disclosure, reasonable care, and accounting. The forms also include a section addressing dual agency consent, which is still permitted in Virginia with written informed consent from both parties.

Agents affiliated with brokerages that use proprietary forms rather than VAR templates must ensure those forms meet every statutory requirement. DPOR does not pre-approve forms, so the compliance burden falls squarely on the brokerage.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

**Failing to secure a signed buyer agency agreement before providing brokerage services exposes agents to a cascade of consequences that go well beyond losing a commission check.** The Virginia Real Estate Board has authority under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2105 to impose sanctions ranging from reprimand to license revocation.

Specific enforcement actions include fines up to $2,500 per violation, mandatory continuing education, license suspension, and in egregious cases, permanent revocation. The board's published disciplinary actions show that buyer agency violations frequently appear alongside other paperwork failures—agents who skip one compliance step tend to skip several.

Beyond board discipline, agents without a signed agreement lose standing in procuring cause disputes. If a buyer purchases a home without an executed agreement in place, the agent's claim to compensation rests on thin legal ground. Virginia courts have consistently held that the written agreement is the primary evidence of the agency relationship and compensation terms.

There is also the deal-cancellation risk. A buyer who discovers mid-transaction that proper agency disclosures and agreements were not executed may have grounds to rescind or renegotiate. This creates the exact kind of [blown closing scenario](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) that costs everyone money and reputation.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in VREB complaints and brokerage audits. Each one is preventable with proper systems, but each one persists because agents operate under time pressure and faulty assumptions.

**First, agents present the buyer agency agreement after substantive services have already begun.** Showing properties, pulling comps for a specific home, or advising on neighborhoods all constitute brokerage services. The agreement must be signed before these activities start, not at the offer stage. The "I'll get it signed when they're serious" approach is a direct statutory violation.

Second, agents use expired agreements. A buyer's search stretches past the termination date, and the agent continues working without securing a renewal. Every day past expiration is a day without a valid agreement. VREB treats this identically to never having one at all.

Third, agents fail to disclose all possible compensation sources. The 2024 practice changes made this particularly treacherous. If a listing broker offers compensation through a concession and the buyer's agent collects it, that arrangement must be addressed in the buyer agency agreement before the offer is written. Ambiguity here triggers both regulatory and civil liability.

Fourth, agents neglect to provide a signed copy to the buyer. Virginia Code Section 54.1-2138 and VREB regulations require that the buyer receive a copy of the executed agreement at the time of signing. Keeping the only copy in the agent's file does not satisfy this requirement.

Fifth, agents use the wrong form for the relationship type. An agent working with a buyer on a one-property, one-showing basis who presents an exclusive six-month agreement may face claims of overreach. Conversely, an agent providing months of dedicated service under a non-exclusive agreement has no commission protection if the buyer purchases through another agent.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2103 for the acts of their affiliated licensees. A single agent's failure to secure proper buyer agreements can trigger board action against the entire brokerage if the broker's supervision systems are found inadequate.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|------------|---------------|-----------|
| Agreement execution date | Must predate first substantive service | Every new buyer file |
| Termination date | Must exist and must not be expired | Monthly review of active files |
| Compensation disclosure | All sources identified and consistent with offer terms | At offer submission |
| Buyer copy delivery | Confirmation buyer received signed copy | At file opening |
| Form version | Current VAR form or compliant proprietary equivalent | Quarterly form review |
| Dual agency consent | Separate written consent if applicable | At status change |

**Brokers should implement a file-opening checklist that prevents any substantive activity—MLS property alerts, showing scheduling, or CMA preparation—until the signed agreement is uploaded to the transaction management system.** This single gatekeeper step eliminates the most common violation: services rendered before agreement execution.

Regular audits are not optional. VREB's [licensing regulations](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov/Boards/Real-Estate) give the board authority to inspect brokerage records, and the absence of proper buyer agreements in active files is among the first things investigators flag. Brokers who discover compliance gaps internally should document corrective action immediately.

The rise of teams within brokerages adds another layer. When a buyer initially contacts a team's ISA or showing assistant, the brokerage must ensure the agreement is executed before any team member provides substantive services—not just before the lead agent steps in. Team leaders should review how [training and timeline protocols](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) address this handoff point explicitly.

## Duration, Termination, and Renewal Provisions

Virginia law does not prescribe a maximum or minimum term for buyer agency agreements, but it does require a definite termination date. Open-ended agreements violate the statute. The VREB has specifically cited indefinite-term agreements as a basis for disciplinary action in published cases.

Market practice in Virginia typically ranges from 90 days to six months for exclusive agreements. Shorter terms—sometimes as brief as a single showing or a single property—are appropriate for non-exclusive arrangements. The key is that the term must be negotiated, not defaulted.

Termination provisions should address four scenarios: expiration of the term, mutual written agreement to terminate early, breach by either party, and the death or incapacity of either party. VAR Form 320 includes a protection period clause that preserves the agent's commission right for a specified number of days after termination if the buyer purchases a property the agent introduced during the agreement term.

Renewal requires a new signed agreement or a written amendment extending the term. Verbal renewals do not satisfy the statutory writing requirement. Agents who rely on a handshake to continue past the termination date are operating without coverage.
