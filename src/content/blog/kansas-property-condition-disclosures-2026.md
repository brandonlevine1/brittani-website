## Why Kansas Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026 Demand Your Attention

Kansas disclosure law has not undergone a major statutory overhaul since the original Seller's Disclosure Act took effect, but the enforcement environment has shifted. Kansas property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 obligations remain anchored in K.S.A. 58-30,101 through 58-30,110, yet the Kansas Real Estate Commission (KREC) has intensified complaint investigations tied to incomplete or late disclosures. If you treat the disclosure packet as a formality rather than a liability shield, you are gambling with your license.

The stakes are not abstract. KREC disciplinary actions in 2024 and 2025 included license suspensions tied directly to agents who failed to ensure timely delivery of the seller's disclosure or who mischaracterized property conditions in marketing materials. Understanding exactly which forms to use, when to deliver them, and what environmental hazards demand separate treatment is the baseline for practicing safely in Kansas this year.

## The Statutory Framework: K.S.A. 58-30,101 Through 58-30,110

Kansas law requires sellers of residential real property to deliver a written disclosure statement to prospective buyers. The statute applies to sales of one-to-four-unit residential dwellings that are not otherwise exempt. Exemptions exist for transfers pursuant to court order, transfers by a fiduciary administering an estate, transfers resulting from foreclosure, and new construction that has never been occupied.

**The seller's obligation is to disclose all known material defects affecting the property's condition.** "Known" is the operative word—Kansas does not impose a duty to inspect on the seller. However, an agent who personally observes a defect or has actual knowledge of one cannot hide behind the seller's silence; K.S.A. 58-30,106(b) makes clear that the disclosure does not relieve the licensee of any obligation to disclose adverse material facts within the licensee's knowledge.

The Kansas Association of REALTORS (KAR) publishes the standard form used by most agents: the **Seller's Property Condition Disclosure Statement (KAR Form SDS)**. This multi-page form covers structural systems, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, roof condition, water damage, pest infestation, and environmental hazards. While the statute does not mandate using this specific form, KREC expects a disclosure that substantively covers the same categories. Using the KAR form is the safest route because it mirrors statutory expectations and has been tested against KREC complaints.

## Environmental Disclosures: Lead Paint, Radon, and Contamination

Environmental disclosure in Kansas operates on two tracks. The first is federal: the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, 42 U.S.C. 4852d) requires that sellers and their agents disclose known lead-based paint and lead-based paint hazards in any pre-1978 dwelling. Agents must provide buyers with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home," include specific lead-paint disclosure language in the purchase contract, and give buyers a 10-day opportunity to conduct a lead inspection.

The second track is state-level. The KAR Form SDS includes sections asking sellers about known environmental contamination on or near the property, including underground storage tanks (USTs), chemical spills, radon test results, and proximity to landfills or Superfund sites. The Kansas Department of Health and Environment (KDHE) maintains a [public database of contaminated sites](https://www.kdhe.ks.gov/174/Bureau-of-Environmental-Remediation) that agents should cross-reference when listing rural or industrial-adjacent properties.

**Radon disclosure deserves special attention because Kansas has counties with among the highest radon levels in the nation.** While Kansas does not mandate radon testing, sellers who have previously tested must disclose results. Agents working in high-radon zones—particularly in the eastern third of the state—should advise buyers to exercise their inspection rights and recommend radon testing even when prior results are unavailable.

| Disclosure Type | Governing Law | Triggering Condition | Form / Document |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seller Property Condition | K.S.A. 58-30,106 | All residential sales (with exemptions) | KAR Form SDS |
| Lead-Based Paint | 42 U.S.C. 4852d / 24 CFR 35 | Pre-1978 residential dwellings | EPA/HUD Lead Disclosure Form |
| Radon | No state mandate; KAR Form SDS Section | Seller has prior test results | KAR Form SDS (environmental section) |
| Underground Storage Tanks | K.S.A. 65-34,100 et seq. / KAR Form SDS | Seller knowledge of UST on property | KAR Form SDS + KDHE records |
| Contaminated Site Proximity | KDHE database / KAR Form SDS | Known contamination affecting property | KAR Form SDS + separate addendum if needed |

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Liability, Rescission, and Discipline

Failure to comply with Kansas disclosure requirements exposes agents and sellers to three distinct categories of risk. First, the buyer may rescind the contract. Under K.S.A. 58-30,108, if a seller fails to provide the required disclosure before the buyer makes an offer, the buyer can withdraw the offer or terminate the contract without penalty within specified timeframes. This alone can kill a deal days before closing.

Second, civil liability enters the picture. A buyer who discovers an undisclosed material defect after closing can pursue damages against both the seller and the listing agent. Kansas courts have allowed fraud and negligent misrepresentation claims where agents knew of defects or should have known based on observable conditions. The measure of damages typically includes repair costs and, in egregious cases, the difference between the purchase price and the property's actual value.

Third, KREC can bring disciplinary action under K.S.A. 58-3062. Penalties range from formal censure to license suspension or revocation. KREC can also impose civil fines. The Commission has publicly stated that disclosure violations constitute one of the top three complaint categories received annually. Your errors and omissions insurance may cover civil judgments, but it will not restore a suspended license or the reputational damage that follows a KREC order.

## Common Mistakes Kansas Agents Make With Disclosures

Knowing the law is necessary but insufficient if your transaction habits contain gaps. These five errors appear repeatedly in KREC complaints and civil litigation in Kansas.

**First, agents deliver the disclosure after the purchase agreement is already signed.** K.S.A. 58-30,106 contemplates delivery before or at the time the buyer executes the offer. Delivering it three days later at the inspection does not satisfy the statute. Even if the buyer does not object, a post-closing claim can reference late delivery as evidence of bad faith.

Second, agents rely on the seller's verbal assurance that "nothing is wrong" and skip confirming the form is completed in full. A partially completed KAR Form SDS with blank sections is worse than no form at all because it suggests the agent saw the gaps and ignored them. Every line item on the form needs an affirmative response—yes, no, or unknown.

Third, agents fail to provide the federal lead-paint disclosure on pre-1978 properties because the home "looks renovated." Renovation does not eliminate the disclosure requirement. The only way to avoid the lead disclosure is to provide a lead-free inspection report certified by an EPA-accredited inspector. Absent that certificate, the disclosure is mandatory regardless of cosmetic updates.

Fourth, listing agents parrot seller representations in MLS descriptions without independent verification, creating implied warranties that go beyond the disclosure form. Describing a basement as "dry" when the seller merely checked "no" on water intrusion can expose you to separate misrepresentation liability if the buyer later discovers moisture damage.

Fifth, agents neglect to retain signed copies of the disclosure in their transaction files. KREC requires licensees to maintain records for five years under K.A.R. 86-1-5. If a complaint arrives three years after closing and you cannot produce the signed disclosure, the presumption shifts against you. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers carry independent liability under K.S.A. 58-3050 for failing to reasonably supervise their affiliated licensees. A broker who discovers that agents are routinely delivering disclosures late or skipping lead-paint forms cannot look the other way and claim ignorance. KREC has disciplined brokers under a failure-to-supervise theory even when the broker had no direct involvement in the transaction.

| Audit Item | What Broker Should Verify | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| KAR Form SDS completion | All fields marked; seller and buyer signatures present; dated before or concurrent with offer | Every transaction at contract |
| Lead-paint disclosure (pre-1978) | Separate form signed by seller, buyer, and agents; EPA pamphlet delivery confirmed; 10-day waiver or election documented | Every pre-1978 transaction |
| Disclosure delivery timing | Date on disclosure vs. date on purchase agreement; disclosure date must be same or earlier | Every transaction at contract |
| File retention | Signed disclosures stored per K.A.R. 86-1-5 for minimum five years | Quarterly file audit |
| MLS accuracy vs. disclosure | Marketing claims consistent with seller's disclosure responses | Listing launch review |

Brokers should build a checklist into their file review process. If your office handles volume that makes manual review impractical, consider how [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) with systematized workflows prevents disclosure items from slipping through the cracks.

The brokerage compliance review should also flag transactions involving exempt sellers. An exempt sale does not mean zero disclosure—it means the statutory seller disclosure form is not required. Federal lead-paint obligations still apply to pre-1978 properties regardless of exemption status. Agents frequently conflate state exemptions with a blanket pass on all disclosures, and brokers must correct this misunderstanding during training.

## Kansas-Specific Environmental Issues Agents Should Monitor

Kansas properties present environmental conditions less common in coastal or urban markets. Agricultural chemical contamination from decades of farming operations can affect rural residential parcels, especially those near former grain elevators or pesticide storage facilities. The KDHE Bureau of Environmental Remediation tracks these sites, and agents listing rural property should perform a cursory check.

Oil and gas activity adds another layer. In south-central and western Kansas, abandoned wells, brine pits, and orphaned pipelines pose both environmental and safety hazards. While the Kansas Corporation Commission (KCC) regulates oil and gas wells, knowledge of these conditions triggers disclosure obligations on the KAR Form SDS. Agents who list former oilfield properties without inquiring about well status are creating liability they could easily avoid.

Municipal water contamination advisories also warrant attention. If a property's water source has been subject to a boil-water advisory or tested positive for contaminants above EPA action levels, the seller must disclose that history if known. Agents in communities served by smaller water systems should stay current on KDHE drinking water compliance records. Treat the KAR Form SDS and the federal lead-paint disclosure as non-negotiable deliverables that must be completed, signed, and filed before you submit or present any offer.
