## Understanding Michigan Dual Agency & Designated Agency Rules 2026

Michigan dual agency & designated agency rules 2026 remain one of the most misunderstood compliance areas for licensees in the state. The Michigan Occupational Code, specifically Article 25 (MCL 339.2501 et seq.), governs how agents disclose and manage agency relationships, and the Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) enforces those rules with real teeth. Getting the timing wrong on a single form can unravel a deal or trigger a formal complaint.

The distinction between dual agency and designated agency matters more than many agents realize. Dual agency means one licensee represents both buyer and seller in the same transaction. Designated agency means the broker assigns separate licensees within the firm to represent each party independently. Michigan law permits both, but each path comes with distinct disclosure obligations, consent requirements, and fiduciary limitations.

## The Statutory Framework: MCL 339.2512d Through 2512f

Michigan's agency disclosure requirements flow from several interlocking statutes. MCL 339.2512d establishes that a licensee cannot act as a dual agent without the informed, written consent of all parties to the transaction. MCL 339.2512e mandates the timing and content of agency disclosures. MCL 339.2512f authorizes designated agency as an alternative structure.

**The written consent requirement is not optional, not waivable, and not something you can backfill after the purchase agreement is signed.** LARA has repeatedly emphasized that consent must be obtained before the licensee begins acting in the dual capacity. The statute does not provide a grace period.

Under designated agency, the broker retains a supervisory role but does not personally represent either party. Each designated agent owes full fiduciary duties—loyalty, confidentiality, disclosure, obedience, and accounting—to their assigned client alone. The broker, however, must remain neutral and cannot share confidential information between the two designated agents.

## Key Forms Michigan Agents Must Use

The primary document is the **Disclosure Regarding Real Estate Agency Relationships** form, which must be presented at first substantive contact with a prospective buyer or seller. "Substantive contact" means any conversation that goes beyond general factual information about a property—discussing pricing strategy, motivation, or negotiation terms all qualify.

| Form / Document | When It's Required | Who Signs |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure Regarding Real Estate Agency Relationships | First substantive contact | Prospective client acknowledges receipt |
| Consent to Dual Agency (written) | Before dual agency begins | Both buyer and seller |
| Designated Agency Notification | When broker designates agents | Both parties, both designated agents |
| Brokerage Policy on Agency | Listing appointment or buyer consultation | Client acknowledgment |

Most brokerages in Michigan rely on Michigan Association of Realtors (MAR) standardized forms, though LARA does not mandate a specific template as long as the statutory language from MCL 339.2512e is included verbatim. Some independent brokerages draft their own versions, which is permissible but risky if the language falls short of the statutory requirements.

If your brokerage has not updated forms since the [2024 NAR settlement changes](https://www.nar.realtor/the-facts/nar-settlement-faqs), audit them now. Buyer representation agreements are now a prerequisite before showing property, and any dual agency scenario must account for this additional layer of written documentation.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance in Michigan fall into three categories: regulatory, civil, and transactional.

**Regulatory penalties from LARA can include license suspension, revocation, probation, or fines up to $10,000 per violation.** The Michigan Board of Real Estate Appraisers and the Real Estate Commission review complaints filed by consumers or other licensees, and they do not need a court ruling to impose discipline.

On the civil side, a party who was not properly informed of a dual agency can sue for rescission of the contract or damages. Michigan courts have held that undisclosed dual agency constitutes a breach of fiduciary duty, which opens the agent and brokerage to compensatory damages and, in egregious cases, punitive damages. The statute of limitations for fraud-based claims can extend well beyond closing.

Transactionally, a purchase agreement executed under an undisclosed dual agency is voidable at the election of the non-consenting party. That means a buyer who discovers post-closing that their agent was also representing the seller can petition to unwind the entire deal. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) due to compliance failures helps agents recognize these risks before they materialize.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make

Three to five errors account for the majority of dual agency complaints filed with LARA. Each one is preventable with proper systems and training.

**First, agents disclose too late.** They wait until the offer is being drafted to mention that they also represent the seller. By that point, the buyer has already shared confidential negotiation information. The statute requires disclosure at first substantive contact, not first offer.

Second, agents treat verbal acknowledgment as sufficient consent. Michigan law demands written consent, and LARA investigators look for signed documents with dates that precede the dual representation. A phone call where the client says "that's fine" does not satisfy MCL 339.2512d.

Third, agents confuse designated agency with dual agency on their forms. Checking the wrong box or using language that describes one arrangement while practicing another creates a mismatch between the legal relationship and the documented relationship. This discrepancy is exactly what plaintiff attorneys target in litigation.

Fourth, agents in a designated agency arrangement share confidential client information with their colleague in the same office. The firewall between designated agents is not a suggestion—it is a fiduciary obligation. Discussing the buyer's top-dollar figure with the listing agent at the same brokerage is a textbook violation.

Fifth, agents fail to re-disclose when circumstances change. If an agent initially represents only the buyer but later lists a property that buyer wants to purchase, the agency relationship has changed. A new disclosure and new consent are required before proceeding.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under Michigan law. MCL 339.2512 places the responsibility for ensuring proper agency disclosures squarely on the broker's shoulders. A broker who fails to supervise agents' compliance can face the same penalties the agents face—plus additional sanctions for inadequate oversight.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Signed agency disclosures in every file | Every transaction | Missing date, missing signature, or date after first showing |
| Dual agency consent obtained before offer | Every dual agency deal | Consent form dated same day as or after purchase agreement |
| Designated agency firewall documentation | Every designated deal | Email threads showing cross-agent communication about client strategy |
| Buyer representation agreement on file | Every buyer transaction | No signed BRA before first property tour |
| Office policy manual current with 2026 rules | Annually | Last update pre-dates NAR settlement implementation |

**Brokers should conduct quarterly file audits, not annual ones.** Waiting twelve months to catch a pattern of missing disclosures means twelve months of liability exposure. Training alone is insufficient without verification. If your office handles high volume, consider implementing automated compliance tracking to [avoid missed deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) and form gaps before they become complaints.

A well-run brokerage also maintains a written policy on how dual agency and designated agency situations are handled, including who approves them, how the firewall is maintained, and what documentation must be completed before the broker authorizes the arrangement. This policy should be signed annually by every licensee affiliated with the brokerage.

## Designated Agency: The Preferred Risk-Reduction Strategy

Many Michigan brokerages have moved toward designated agency as the default approach when both parties in a transaction are represented within the same firm. The logic is straightforward: designated agency preserves full fiduciary duties to each client, whereas dual agency requires the agent to be neutral and cannot advocate for either side.

From a liability standpoint, designated agency reduces the surface area for complaints because each client has a dedicated advocate. The broker serves as a neutral supervisor rather than a representative of either party. However, this structure only works if the information firewall is genuinely maintained.

Brokers who want to adopt designated agency as standard practice should formalize the process in writing, train all agents on the firewall obligations, and include designated agency language in their listing and buyer representation agreements from the outset. Retroactive designation after a conflict arises looks reactive and may not satisfy LARA's scrutiny. For brokerages onboarding new agents, building [agency disclosure training into early timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) prevents costly errors in the first year of practice.
