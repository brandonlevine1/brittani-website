## Understanding Ohio Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026

Ohio's disclosure framework is deceptively simple on its surface—hand a form to the seller, get it signed, pass it to the buyer. But the details of **Ohio property condition & environmental disclosures 2026** requirements trip up experienced agents every single quarter. Between the state's Residential Property Disclosure Form, federal lead-paint mandates, and environmental red flags tied to old industrial sites, the margin for error is thin and the consequences are real.

The governing statute has not changed its core structure in years, but enforcement attention and buyer litigation have both ticked upward. This post breaks down exactly what the law requires, what forms you must use, where agents keep stumbling, and what brokers should be checking on every file.

## The Statutory Framework: ORC 5302.30 and Beyond

Ohio Revised Code Section 5302.30 is the backbone of residential property disclosure in the state. It requires the owner of residential real property to furnish a completed property disclosure form to a prospective buyer before the buyer enters into a purchase contract—or, in practice, as part of the contract itself with a right of rescission.

The form mandated by this statute is the **Ohio Residential Property Disclosure Form** (often referenced internally as the "RPDF"). The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, housed within the Ohio Department of Commerce, oversees the form template and periodically updates its content. Agents should verify they are using the most current version each year, as outdated forms create compliance gaps even when the seller answers honestly.

**Separate from the seller's obligation, ORC 4735.67 imposes an independent duty on licensees.** This section requires agents to disclose material facts actually known to them that affect the property's value or desirability. The two duties run in parallel—your seller completing the RPDF does not shield you if you personally know about a cracked foundation or contaminated well.

> "A licensee shall disclose to any purchaser all material facts of which the licensee has actual knowledge pertaining to the physical condition of the property." — Ohio Revised Code 4735.67

For environmental matters specifically, Ohio does not have a single stand-alone environmental disclosure statute comparable to some states' "Superfund" disclosure laws. However, known environmental contamination—including underground storage tanks, soil contamination from prior industrial use, or proximity to a [Voluntary Action Program](https://epa.ohio.gov/divisions-and-offices/environmental-response-and-revitalization/programs/voluntary-action-program) site—falls squarely within the "material fact" obligation under both ORC 5302.30 and ORC 4735.67.

## Forms You Must Know and Use

Ohio agents work with a handful of specific forms across property condition and environmental disclosure scenarios. Getting the right document in the right hands at the right time is not optional—it is the compliance baseline.

| Form / Document | Who Completes It | When It Is Due | Governing Authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ohio Residential Property Disclosure Form (RPDF) | Seller | Before or at contract execution | ORC 5302.30 / Ohio Dept. of Commerce |
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (federal) | Seller and agents | Before contract execution for pre-1978 homes | 42 U.S.C. 4852d / EPA & HUD |
| EPA "Protect Your Family" Pamphlet | Delivered by listing agent | Before contract execution | 40 CFR 745 |
| Mold / Environmental Addendum (brokerage-specific) | Varies by brokerage | At contract or upon discovery | No state mandate—risk management best practice |

The RPDF itself covers seven sections, including water supply, sewer, structural components, and known hazards. Sellers check boxes and provide written explanations. Missing sections or blank answers do not satisfy the statutory requirement—the form must be completed in its entirety or the seller must affirmatively state they are disclaiming knowledge.

For pre-1978 properties, the federal lead-paint disclosure is a separate document. Many Ohio brokerages bundle it with their purchase agreement, but it is distinct from the state RPDF. Failure to provide it triggers federal penalties of up to $19,507 per violation under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act, adjusted for inflation.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**Noncompliance with Ohio disclosure requirements generates three distinct categories of risk: deal rescission, civil liability, and license discipline.** Understanding all three keeps you from treating disclosure as a checkbox exercise.

First, if the seller fails to provide the RPDF before contract signing, the buyer has the right to rescind the purchase agreement. Under ORC 5302.30(G), the buyer may terminate the contract at any time until the form is actually received or until closing—whichever comes first. That means a missing disclosure can blow up a transaction at the closing table.

Second, civil lawsuits. Buyers who discover undisclosed defects after closing routinely sue both the seller and the listing agent. Under ORC 4735.67, the agent's liability is separate from the seller's. If you knew about a leaking basement and stayed silent, your errors-and-omissions policy may cover the claim, but your deductible, premium increase, and reputational damage are yours alone.

Third, the Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing can take disciplinary action against your license. Violations of disclosure duties fall under ORC 4735.18(A)(6)—dishonest dealing—which can result in license suspension, revocation, or fines up to $2,500 per violation. Repeat offenders face accelerated enforcement timelines.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Ohio Disclosures

Even agents with a decade of closings behind them make predictable errors on disclosure compliance. The following five mistakes show up repeatedly in complaints filed with the Ohio Division of Real Estate.

**Mistake 1: Filling out the RPDF on behalf of the seller.** This happens when agents try to speed up the listing process. They ask questions verbally and transcribe answers onto the form. The moment you hold the pen, you assume liability for the accuracy of those answers. Always hand the blank form to the seller and let them complete it in their own handwriting or digitally under their own login.

Mistake 2: Using last year's version of the form. The Ohio Department of Commerce can update the RPDF template without dramatic announcement. Agents who pull the form from their personal files rather than downloading the current version risk using outdated language that omits newly required disclosures.

Mistake 3: Accepting "unknown" answers without follow-up. A seller checking "unknown" on every line is a red flag, not a safe harbor. While the form permits that response, [agents who fail to flag patterns miss their independent duty](/blog/where-deals-break-down) to disclose material facts they observe during the listing process—like a visible crack covered by fresh paint.

Mistake 4: Skipping lead-paint disclosure because the home was "renovated." Renovation does not eliminate the disclosure requirement. If the structure was built before 1978, the federal lead-paint disclosure must be provided regardless of subsequent updates. The exemption applies only to zero-bedroom dwellings, housing for the elderly (with no children present), and properties where certified inspectors confirmed total lead-paint removal.

Mistake 5: Ignoring environmental history on commercial-to-residential conversions. Ohio's legacy of manufacturing and mining means many residential lots sit on former industrial land. Agents listing converted lofts in Cleveland, repurposed warehouses in Cincinnati, or farmland near old gas stations must investigate whether the property was enrolled in Ohio EPA's Voluntary Action Program or carries a No Further Action letter.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability for agent disclosure failures under ORC 4735.18(A)(6) and (H). A single agent's oversight can trigger an investigation into the entire brokerage's compliance culture. Proactive auditing is not bureaucratic overkill—it is self-preservation.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| RPDF completion | Every section filled; no blanks masquerading as "unknown" | Every listing file at contract |
| Form version | Date/version identifier matches current Ohio Dept. of Commerce template | Quarterly template check |
| Lead-paint package (pre-1978) | Disclosure signed by all parties; EPA pamphlet receipt confirmed; 10-day waiver or exercise documented | Every applicable file |
| Agent independent disclosure | Written notation of material facts observed; file memo if none | Every listing file |
| Environmental flags | Note in file if property is near known contamination site or has industrial history | Every listing in target ZIP codes |

Brokers should also build a simple intake process for new listings that forces agents to confirm the property's year of construction and prior use before the listing goes active. If your [transaction handoff workflow](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) does not include a disclosure checkpoint, add one immediately.

Training matters too. Annual CE covers disclosure in the abstract, but brokerage-specific training should walk agents through the exact forms, the exact filing sequence, and the exact consequences documented above. New agents especially benefit from [structured onboarding timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) that include live file reviews rather than passive slide decks.

## Environmental Disclosure Nuances for 2026

Ohio's environmental disclosure landscape is shaped by both the Ohio EPA and federal Superfund designations. The state's Voluntary Action Program (VAP) allows property owners to clean up contamination voluntarily and receive a covenant not to sue—but that covenant comes with deed restrictions that transfer to subsequent buyers.

Agents listing a VAP property must disclose those deed restrictions and any ongoing monitoring requirements. A No Further Action letter does not mean the property is pristine—it means the contamination was addressed to a standard acceptable for a specific use category (residential, commercial, or industrial). If the property's NFA letter was issued for commercial use and the new buyer intends residential conversion, the agent must flag the mismatch.

Radon is another persistent environmental issue in Ohio. The state consistently ranks among the highest in the country for elevated radon levels, particularly in the western and central counties. While Ohio does not mandate radon disclosure by statute the way some states do, a known elevated radon test result is a material fact under ORC 4735.67. If the seller disclosed a prior test to you, you must pass that information forward.
