## The Repair Request Black Hole That Costs You Deals

A single missed repair item can blow up a $12,000 commission check 72 hours before closing. Tracking repair requests and credits so nothing falls through is not a nice-to-have skill—it is the operational backbone of every transaction that survives the inspection-to-closing gauntlet. Yet according to NAR's 2025 Member Profile, 32% of delayed closings cite unresolved repair or credit disputes as the primary cause.

**The financial exposure is staggering.** An agent managing 10 active deals might juggle 40 to 80 individual repair line items simultaneously, each with its own deadline, responsible party, and verification requirement. Miss one $1,800 HVAC repair that the seller agreed to but never scheduled, and you are explaining to your buyer at the walkthrough why the system still rattles. That conversation rarely ends with a closed deal.

For brokers managing teams of 5 to 15 agents, the liability compounds. One E&O claim for a repair that "fell through the cracks" averages $18,000 to $35,000 in defense costs alone, according to the [Texas Real Estate Commission's enforcement data](https://www.trec.texas.gov/). The root cause is almost never malice—it is a broken tracking system, or no system at all.

## Why Repair Tracking Fails at Scale

Most agents start their career tracking repairs in their head. That works at two deals per month. At eight or twelve deals per month, memory becomes the weakest link in the chain.

The failure modes are predictable. Verbal agreements never make it into writing. Credit amounts get discussed on a phone call but never reflected in the amendment. A contractor completes three of four items and nobody catches the fourth until the walkthrough reveals a still-leaking faucet.

**Time is the hidden cost nobody calculates.** Agents who lack a system spend an average of 45 minutes per transaction chasing repair status updates—calling sellers, texting listing agents, emailing contractors. Across 10 active deals, that is 7.5 hours per week of reactive firefighting that produces zero new revenue. Compare that to the 12 minutes per transaction an agent with a structured tracking system spends on the same task.

| Metric | No System | Structured System |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Time per transaction on repair follow-up | 45 min/week | 12 min/week |
| Missed items per quarter | 3-5 | 0-1 |
| Closing delays from repair disputes | 32% of deals | Under 5% of deals |

Those numbers should make every broker rethink whether "let agents figure it out" is an acceptable training strategy. If you are [training new agents on timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines), repair tracking deserves its own module.

## Step 1: Centralize Every Repair Item in One Log

The moment an inspection report lands in your inbox, open your tracking log and enter every single deficiency the inspector noted—not just the ones your buyer cares about. Buyers change their minds. Attorneys add items. You need the full universe documented before negotiation begins.

Your log needs seven columns minimum: item description, inspection report page number, resolution type (repair, credit, or waived), dollar amount or scope, responsible party, deadline, and verification status. A shared Google Sheet works. A project management board works. A legal pad does not.

**Assign each item a status from day one: open, negotiating, agreed, in progress, verified, or disputed.** This status field is what prevents items from disappearing into the void. If something sits at "agreed" for more than 48 hours without moving to "in progress," that is your trigger to pick up the phone.

## Step 2: Convert Verbal Agreements to Signed Documents Within 24 Hours

The most dangerous moment in repair negotiation is the gap between "the seller said yes on the phone" and "the seller signed the amendment." That gap kills deals. A seller who verbally agreed to a $4,500 roof credit on Tuesday can legally deny it ever happened on Thursday if nothing is in writing.

Build a 24-hour rule into your workflow: any verbal agreement on repairs or credits must be reflected in a signed amendment, repair addendum, or written counter within one business day. No exceptions. Set a calendar reminder at the moment the verbal agreement occurs.

This is especially critical in states with strict disclosure and repair timelines. If you handle [property condition disclosures](/blog/texas-property-condition-disclosures-2026) in Texas or similar markets, the contractual clock on repair resolution periods waits for nobody. One unsigned document and the entire timeline collapses.

## Step 3: Separate Credit Tracking from Repair Tracking

Credits and repairs are fundamentally different animals that require different verification processes. A $3,000 credit at closing needs lender approval, HUD-1 or CD line-item confirmation, and title company coordination. A $3,000 repair needs contractor scheduling, completion verification, receipt collection, and potentially a re-inspection.

Mixing them in one undifferentiated list creates confusion. Instead, split your tracking log into two sections or use color coding that makes the distinction impossible to miss. Credits get their own deadline pathway: buyer requests credit, seller agrees, lender approves credit structure, title company reflects on settlement statement, agent verifies at final CD review.

| Tracking Element | Credit Path | Repair Path |
|-----------------|-------------|-------------|
| Initial documentation | Amendment with dollar amount | Amendment with scope of work |
| Third-party approval needed | Lender (for closing cost credits) | None (unless permit required) |
| Verification method | Review closing disclosure line item | Receipt, photo, or re-inspection |
| Common failure point | Lender rejects credit structure | Contractor completes partial work |
| Final confirmation timing | 3 days before closing (CD review) | Pre-closing walkthrough |
| Responsible party for follow-up | Buyer's agent + lender | Listing agent + seller |

This separation alone eliminates the most common tracking error: assuming a credit is "handled" because the seller agreed to it, when in reality the lender has not approved the credit structure and nobody checked.

## Step 4: Build a 48-Hour Check-In Cadence

Repairs do not track themselves. Sellers procrastinate. Contractors no-show. Listing agents forget to follow up. You need a forcing function that surfaces stale items before they become emergencies.

**Every 48 hours, scan your repair log for any item that has not changed status.** If a roof repair has been "in progress" since last Tuesday and nobody has provided a completion photo or receipt, that is a phone call—not a text, not an email. Phone calls create accountability that written messages do not.

For brokers: build this 48-hour cadence into your team's weekly accountability meeting. Ask each agent to name their oldest unresolved repair item and what specific action they are taking in the next 24 hours to move it forward. This single question, asked consistently, will surface problems that [break deals down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) weeks before closing instead of days.

## Step 5: Verify Completion Before the Walkthrough

The pre-closing walkthrough should confirm what you already know—not reveal what you failed to track. If you arrive at a walkthrough and discover incomplete repairs, you have failed operationally, even if you eventually negotiate a resolution.

Request completion documentation from the listing agent or seller 5 to 7 days before closing: receipts, contractor invoices, photos, permit sign-offs, or lien waivers depending on the scope. Log each piece of documentation in your tracking sheet with the date received.

For items over $2,000 in value, consider requesting a re-inspection by the original inspector or a specialist. The $150 to $300 re-inspection fee is cheap insurance against a buyer who discovers shoddy work after closing and blames you for not catching it. Document your recommendation to the buyer in writing regardless of whether they accept it.

## Step 6: Protect Yourself With a Paper Trail

Every repair-related communication—every single one—needs to exist in writing somewhere retrievable. If a conversation happens by phone, follow it with a confirming email within the hour: "Per our call at 2:15 PM, seller has agreed to complete the electrical panel replacement by June 20 using a licensed electrician. Please confirm."

This is not paranoia. This is the standard of care that [E&O insurers expect](https://www.trec.texas.gov/) and that state licensing boards evaluate when complaints arise. An agent who can produce a timestamped email trail showing diligent follow-up on every repair item is nearly bulletproof in a disciplinary hearing. An agent relying on memory is exposed.

**Store all repair-related documents in the transaction file—not in a separate email thread, not in a text chain on your personal phone.** Your broker, your TC, and your future self during an audit all need access to the same paper trail. If you are unclear on how this fits into your broader [transaction handoff process](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist), build repair documentation into your handoff checklist as a required section.

## Making This System Scale Across 10+ Active Deals

The system above works for one deal. The challenge is maintaining it across a portfolio of 10, 12, or 15 simultaneous transactions without spending your entire week on administrative tracking. This is where the difference between a manual system and an automated one becomes measurable in hours, not minutes.

A manual spreadsheet requires you to remember to open it, update it, scan it, and act on it. That discipline breaks down during a busy week when three new listings hit the market and two offers come in simultaneously. The spreadsheet goes untouched for four days and suddenly you have stale items everywhere.
