## Florida Buyer Contingency Deadlines 2026: The Calendar Dates That Kill Deals

A single missed date on a residential contract can unravel weeks of work, expose your brokerage to liability, and cost your client their earnest money deposit. Understanding Florida buyer contingency deadlines 2026 is not optional knowledge—it is the baseline competency the Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) expects from every active licensee. The forms updated for 2026 transactions carry the same structural risk they always have: rigid timelines that punish agents who rely on memory instead of systems.

Florida's standard contracts, published jointly by Florida Realtors and The Florida Bar, remain the dominant transaction instruments statewide. The two residential forms agents encounter daily—the FR/BAR Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (Form CR-6) and the FR/BAR AS IS Residential Contract (Form ASIS-6)—each contain buyer contingency deadlines that operate independently of one another. Missing one does not necessarily blow up the entire deal, but it can strip your buyer of the right to walk away with their deposit intact.

## The Contingency Framework in FR/BAR Contracts

**Every contingency in a Florida Realtors contract functions as a clock that starts ticking the moment the Effective Date is established.** The Effective Date is defined as the date the last party signs or initials the final counteroffer—and that date controls every downstream deadline in the transaction. If your buyer signed on June 3 but the seller countered and your buyer accepted the counter on June 5, the Effective Date is June 5.

The major buyer contingencies embedded in the 2026 FR/BAR forms include the Inspection Period, Loan Approval Date, Appraisal Contingency (when elected), and the Closing Date itself. Each one carries a default number of days that agents often leave blank or fill in without calculating whether the timeline is actually achievable given lender processing times, title search timelines, or local inspection availability.

Paragraph 18 of the standard CR-6 contract governs the Computation of Time. Calendar days control unless the contract explicitly states otherwise. When the final day of any period falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or national legal holiday, the deadline extends to 5:00 p.m. of the next business day. Agents who assume "business days" apply across the board are already operating on a flawed assumption.

## Inspection Period: The Deadline That Breaks the Most Deals

Under the standard CR-6 contract, Paragraph 12 grants the buyer a negotiated number of days—commonly 15—to conduct inspections, obtain repair estimates, and deliver a written request for repairs or a cancellation notice. If the buyer fails to deliver that notice before 11:59 p.m. on the final day of the Inspection Period, the buyer is deemed to have accepted the property's condition. There is no grace period. There is no "but my inspector was backed up" exception.

The AS IS contract (Form ASIS-6) works differently. The buyer receives an Inspection Period, but the only remedy is cancellation. The buyer cannot demand repairs. If the buyer discovers defects and wants out, they must deliver written notice to the seller before the Inspection Period expires. Silence equals acceptance.

| Feature | CR-6 (Standard Contract) | ASIS-6 (AS IS Contract) |
|---------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Buyer can request repairs | Yes, within Inspection Period | No |
| Buyer can cancel for defects | Yes, if repair negotiation fails | Yes, before deadline only |
| Default Inspection Period | Negotiated (commonly 15 days) | Negotiated (commonly 15 days) |
| Effect of missed deadline | Buyer accepts condition | Buyer accepts condition |
| Seller response required | Yes, within 5 days of repair request | N/A |

This is where deals fall apart. If you represent buyers and you are not calendaring the inspection deadline on day one, you are gambling with their deposit. ## Financing and Appraisal Contingency Timelines

**The Loan Approval Date in Paragraph 8 of the CR-6 contract is not the closing date—it is the date by which the buyer must obtain loan approval or provide written notice that financing has failed.** Agents routinely confuse these two dates. The Loan Approval Date is typically set 20 to 30 days after the Effective Date, well before closing. If the buyer cannot obtain approval by that date and fails to notify the seller in writing, the contingency is waived and the buyer proceeds at their own risk.

The Appraisal Contingency, when elected on the CR-6 form, ties to the lender's appraisal. If the property appraises below the purchase price, the buyer must deliver written notice within the specified period. The contract provides a framework for renegotiation, but again—silence past the deadline kills the buyer's leverage.

Florida Statute 475.25(1)(b) makes it a violation of licensure standards to engage in conduct that constitutes "culpable negligence" in a real estate transaction. FREC has historically interpreted an agent's failure to communicate material deadlines to their client as potential grounds for discipline under this statute. The [Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/intentions2.asp?chession=&catid=&divid=) maintains enforcement records that confirm this interpretation in practice.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Contingency Deadlines

Three to five days before a deadline expires is not the time to realize you never told your buyer about it. Here are the specific errors that generate complaints, arbitration claims, and license investigations in Florida.

**First, agents leave contingency day-count fields blank on the contract.** When Paragraph 12's Inspection Period line is left empty, the contract's default language may apply—or worse, the parties may dispute what was agreed upon. An ambiguous deadline is an invitation to litigation. Fill in every blank or strike the provision entirely.

Second, agents calculate deadlines using business days instead of calendar days. The FR/BAR contract's Computation of Time clause in Paragraph 18 explicitly states calendar days control. An agent who tells their buyer "you have 15 business days" when the contract says 15 calendar days has just given their client a deadline three to four days later than reality. That error alone can forfeit a deposit.

Third, agents deliver cancellation or contingency notices verbally instead of in writing. The FR/BAR contracts require written notice. A phone call to the listing agent saying "my buyer wants to cancel" is legally insufficient. The notice must conform to Paragraph 17's requirements for how notices are delivered—personally, by mail, or by electronic means as authorized in the contract.

Fourth, agents fail to confirm the Effective Date with the other side. If the buyer's agent believes the Effective Date is June 5 but the listing agent recorded it as June 4 based on when the seller's counter was communicated, every deadline in the transaction is off by a day. One day is all it takes.

Fifth, agents treat the Closing Date as the only deadline that matters. In reality, a buyer who misses the Loan Approval Date or Inspection Period deadline may have already waived their ability to exit the contract—meaning the Closing Date becomes a trap rather than a finish line. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) means understanding that failures cluster around contingency deadlines, not closing day.

## Florida Buyer Contingency Deadlines 2026: What Brokers Must Audit

Brokers carry vicarious liability under Florida Statute 475.278 for the acts of their sales associates performed within the scope of their employment or agency relationship. A missed contingency deadline that results in a client losing a deposit—or worse, facing a breach claim—creates exposure that flows uphill.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|------------|---------------|-----------|
| Effective Date confirmation | Signed confirmation from both sides within 24 hours | Every new contract |
| Contingency deadlines calendared | Written evidence deadlines were entered into CRM or tracking system | Within 48 hours of Effective Date |
| Written notice delivery | Copies of all contingency-related notices in transaction file | Before each deadline |
| Day-count accuracy | Calendar day calculation verified against contract language | At contract review |
| Blank fields on forms | No contingency period left unnumbered or unstruck | At contract submission |

Brokers should require agents to submit a deadline summary sheet within 48 hours of any executed contract. That sheet should list every contingency, its expiration date, the method of notice required, and confirmation that the client has been informed. If your brokerage lacks a systematic [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist), you are relying on individual agents to self-police—a strategy that fails at scale.

FREC Rule 61J2-14.012 requires brokers to maintain transaction files for at least five years. Those files must contain evidence of contract performance, including notices sent and received. A broker who cannot produce evidence that contingency deadlines were properly tracked during a disciplinary investigation will face an uphill battle defending the licensee.

## Protecting the Deposit: Escrow Implications of Missed Deadlines

When a buyer misses a contingency deadline and then attempts to cancel, the seller will almost always claim entitlement to the earnest money deposit. Under Florida Statute 475.25(1)(d), the broker holding escrow has 15 business days from the date of a deposit dispute to notify FREC or institute one of the settlement procedures outlined in Florida Statute 475.25(1)(d)(1)-(6). Those procedures include mediation, arbitration, interpleader, or requesting a FREC escrow disbursement order.

**The agent whose client missed the deadline will likely face an arbitration or small claims proceeding where the core question is: did the buyer's failure to act before the deadline constitute waiver of the contingency?** In most cases, the answer is yes. The contract language is unambiguous, and Florida courts have repeatedly enforced strict deadline compliance in residential transactions.

This is why deadline tracking is not administrative busywork—it is risk management. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond the commission. It includes potential FREC complaints, E&O claims, and the reputational damage that follows a public arbitration loss.
