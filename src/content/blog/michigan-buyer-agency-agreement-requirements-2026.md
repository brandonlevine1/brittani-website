## Michigan Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026: The Rules Have Teeth Now

The era of handshake buyer representation in Michigan is over. Understanding Michigan buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 is no longer optional knowledge for licensed agents—it is the baseline for keeping your license active and your commission checks defensible. The changes triggered by the NAR settlement agreement, combined with Michigan-specific regulatory enforcement from the Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA), have created a compliance environment where a single missing signature can torpedo a deal and invite a formal complaint.

This post breaks down the exact forms, timing obligations, and liability consequences Michigan agents face. It also covers the audit responsibilities brokers cannot delegate away.

## The Regulatory Framework Driving These Requirements

Michigan's real estate licensing authority is the Bureau of Professional Licensing within LARA, operating under the Occupational Code (MCL 339.2501 et seq.). The specific statute governing agency disclosure and relationships is MCL 339.2512d, which mandates written disclosure of agency relationships before confidential information is exchanged or services are rendered. The [Michigan LARA licensing page](https://www.michigan.gov/lara/bureau-list/bpl/occ/prof/real-estate) provides the current administrative rules.

**Post-settlement MLS rules, effective August 2024 and refined through 2025, now require that compensation offers to buyer agents be decoupled from the listing.** This means the buyer agency agreement is no longer just a disclosure formality—it is the legal instrument that establishes how and how much the buyer's agent gets paid. Without it, there is no enforceable right to compensation.

The Michigan Realtors association updated its standard forms library in late 2025 to reflect these changes. The primary document is the Buyer Representation Agreement (Form BRA-1), which replaced earlier iterations that lacked the specificity now required by both LARA guidance and MLS participation rules.

## Form Numbers and Execution Requirements

Michigan agents primarily work with forms published by Michigan Realtors (formerly the Michigan Association of Realtors). Here is a breakdown of the key documents involved in buyer representation:

| Form | Purpose | When It Must Be Signed |
|------|---------|----------------------|
| BRA-1 (Buyer Representation Agreement) | Establishes agency, defines services, sets compensation terms | Before any touring or offer activity |
| Agency Disclosure (Form AD-1) | Discloses the type of agency relationship available | At first substantive contact |
| Compensation Addendum (Form CA-1) | Specifies alternative compensation arrangements per transaction | Before offer submission |
| Termination of Buyer Agency (Form TBA-1) | Formally ends the relationship | Upon mutual agreement or expiration |

The BRA-1 must include a definite termination date. Agreements without an end date violate MLS participation requirements and expose the broker to LARA complaints. The form also requires a clear statement of the buyer's financial obligation if seller-offered compensation falls short of the agreed rate.

Execution means wet or electronic signatures from all parties. Michigan's Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (MCL 450.831 et seq.) permits e-signatures, but the agent must retain an executed copy in the transaction file per LARA's record-keeping rules (Administrative Rule 339.22301).

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences for non-compliance operate on three levels: regulatory, contractual, and financial.

**LARA can impose fines up to $10,000 per violation, suspend or revoke a license, and require additional education hours.** Complaints can be filed by consumers, other agents, or even a broker who discovers non-compliance during an internal audit. The disciplinary process is outlined in MCL 339.601 and enforced through administrative hearings.

On the contractual level, a buyer who never signed a representation agreement can argue that no agency relationship existed. This means the agent has no legal basis to claim a commission, even if the agent invested months of work and the buyer ultimately closed on a property. Courts in Michigan have historically sided with consumers on this issue when the paperwork trail is incomplete.

From an insurance perspective, most Errors and Omissions policies contain exclusions for claims arising from undocumented agency relationships. If a buyer sues alleging breach of fiduciary duty and the agent cannot produce a signed BRA-1, the E&O carrier may decline to defend the claim. The agent or brokerage absorbs the full cost of litigation—often $50,000 or more before a verdict is reached.

Agents who have experienced [deals falling apart over paperwork failures](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) know the financial pain is not theoretical. It compounds when you factor in lost referrals and reputational damage within your local MLS.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make

Five specific errors show up repeatedly in LARA complaint files and broker audit findings.

**First, agents show property before obtaining a signed agreement.** The logic is usually "I don't want to scare the buyer off before they see value." This reasoning made more sense when buyer agency agreements were optional best practices. Under current rules, touring without a signed BRA-1 means you performed services without an enforceable contract. You have no claim to compensation and you exposed yourself to a regulatory complaint.

Second, agents use outdated form versions. The 2023-era buyer agency forms lack the compensation specificity required under post-settlement rules. Using them creates ambiguity about payment terms that can result in disputes at closing. Always verify your forms library matches the current Michigan Realtors release year.

Third, agents fail to specify a termination date. An open-ended agreement triggers MLS compliance flags and can be challenged by the buyer as unconscionable. The safest practice is a defined term of 60 to 90 days with a clear renewal mechanism.

Fourth, agents do not address the compensation gap scenario. If the seller offers 2% but the BRA-1 states the buyer owes 2.5%, the agreement must explain what happens to the 0.5% difference. Failing to address this creates a surprise at closing that frequently results in complaints to both LARA and the local Realtor association's ethics panel.

Fifth, agents treat the agency disclosure (Form AD-1) and the buyer representation agreement (Form BRA-1) as interchangeable. They are not. The AD-1 discloses what types of relationships are available. The BRA-1 creates the actual relationship. Both must be in the file, signed separately, at their respective trigger points. Mixing them up is a [common reason agents miss critical deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) and face compliance issues.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under MCL 339.2512e. If an agent under your supervision operates without proper buyer agency documentation, you share the regulatory exposure. LARA does not accept "I didn't know" as a defense from a managing broker.

**Every brokerage operating in Michigan should maintain a systematic file review process that verifies the following elements within 48 hours of a buyer's first showing or offer submission.** Here is a framework for that audit:

| Audit Checkpoint | What You Are Verifying | Red Flag If Missing |
|-----------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Signed BRA-1 on file | Agreement executed before services began | Agent showed property without authorization |
| Termination date present | Agreement has a specific end date | Open-ended agreements violate MLS rules |
| Compensation terms clear | Buyer's obligation is stated in dollars or percentage | Ambiguity invites disputes at closing |
| AD-1 signed and dated | Agency disclosure preceded substantive contact | Failure to disclose is a LARA violation |
| Compensation Addendum (if applicable) | Alternative payment terms documented per transaction | Missing addendum when seller compensation differs from BRA-1 rate |

Brokers should not rely solely on agents self-reporting compliance. Implement a [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) that requires document uploads before a transaction coordinator or admin can open a file. This creates a structural gatekeep rather than depending on memory or good intentions.

Quarterly audits of closed and pending files should sample at least 20% of active agents' transactions. Document the audit with dates, findings, and corrective actions taken. These records become your defense if LARA investigates your brokerage.

## Timing: When Exactly Must the Agreement Be Signed

The timing question generates more confusion than any other aspect of Michigan buyer agency agreement requirements 2026. The rule is straightforward in principle but tricky in execution.

The BRA-1 must be signed before the agent provides services that go beyond ministerial acts. Ministerial acts—providing general market information, answering questions about the buying process, or directing someone to publicly available listings—do not trigger the requirement. The moment you schedule a private showing, provide property-specific advice, or begin writing an offer, you have crossed the line into agency services.

Open houses present a gray area. If a buyer walks into your listing's open house and asks general questions, you are not yet providing buyer agency services. If that buyer asks you to show them another property or requests your opinion on whether the listing is fairly priced relative to their needs, you have entered the zone where a signed agreement is required before you proceed.

**The safest practice is to present the BRA-1 at your first substantive meeting with any prospective buyer—typically a buyer consultation—and refuse to schedule showings until it is executed.** Agents who adopt this standard eliminate nearly all timing-related compliance risk.

## Practical Workflow for Staying Compliant

Building compliance into your daily workflow prevents the scramble that leads to errors. The sequence looks like this: initial contact triggers the AD-1, the buyer consultation triggers the BRA-1, and the first offer triggers verification that compensation terms align with what the seller is offering (adding a CA-1 if they do not).

Store every signed document in your transaction management system within 24 hours of execution. Michigan's record-keeping rules require brokers to maintain transaction documents for at least three years after closing or termination. Digital storage satisfies the requirement, but the files must be retrievable upon LARA request.

For agents managing multiple active buyers simultaneously, the risk of a document slipping through the cracks multiplies. Setting up automated reminders—or using AI-assisted transaction tools—to flag files missing a signed BRA-1 before the first showing is scheduled can prevent the most common compliance failure.
