## The Settlement Changed the Game — Pennsylvania Added Its Own Layer

The Pennsylvania commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 represent a fundamental shift in how agents document, disclose, and collect compensation. The National Association of Realtors finalized its landmark settlement in late 2024, but the ripple effects continue to reshape daily practice for the roughly 70,000 licensed agents operating under the Pennsylvania Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), codified at 63 P.S. Section 455.101 et seq. If you hold a Pennsylvania license, understanding the specific forms, timelines, and liability exposure is no longer optional.

The Pennsylvania Association of Realtors (PAR) updated multiple standard forms in response to the settlement's mandates, and the Pennsylvania State Real Estate Commission has signaled heightened scrutiny of commission disclosure practices during routine audits. Agents who treat these changes as a minor paperwork update are exposing themselves and their brokers to serious risk.

## How the NAR Settlement Restructured Commission Flows

**The single biggest change is the prohibition on offers of buyer-agent compensation through any MLS field.** Before the settlement, a listing agent could advertise a cooperative commission split in Bright MLS (the dominant MLS serving Pennsylvania). That field no longer exists. Compensation to buyer agents must now be negotiated outside the MLS ecosystem entirely.

This does not mean sellers cannot pay buyer agents. It means the mechanism for communicating and agreeing to that payment has shifted from a centralized, automated system to a transaction-by-transaction negotiation documented through specific PAR forms. The distinction matters because Pennsylvania's disclosure requirements under RELRA Section 604 still apply — agents must disclose their agency relationship and financial interests before providing services.

## Pennsylvania-Specific Forms and Documents You Must Use

PAR released updated versions of several forms specifically addressing post-settlement commission practices. The key documents include:

| Form | Purpose | When Required |
|------|---------|---------------|
| PAR Form ABR (Buyer Representation Agreement) | Establishes compensation terms with buyer | Before any touring activity |
| PAR Form AOS (Agreement of Sale) | Documents seller concessions including compensation contributions | At offer submission |
| PAR Form SPD (Seller's Property Disclosure) | Updated to remove legacy co-op commission language | At listing |
| PAR Form CND (Commission Negotiation Disclosure) | Discloses all compensation sources to buyer | Before offer submission |

The PAR Form ABR now requires a specific, definite compensation amount — either a flat fee or a percentage — that cannot be open-ended. The settlement explicitly bars agreements that state compensation will be "whatever the seller offers" or similar open-ended language. Pennsylvania agents must fill in a number, and that number becomes a contractual ceiling and floor simultaneously.

**If you fail to execute a proper ABR before showing property, you are violating both NAR settlement terms and Bright MLS participation rules.** The MLS can impose fines starting at $500 for a first offense, with suspension of MLS access for repeated violations.

## Liability Exposure for Non-Compliance

The consequences of ignoring these rules extend well beyond MLS fines. Under RELRA Section 604(a), any licensee who fails to provide required disclosures faces potential disciplinary action by the Pennsylvania State Real Estate Commission, including license suspension, fines up to $10,000 per violation, and mandatory continuing education.

Civil liability adds another layer. A buyer who later discovers their agent collected undisclosed compensation — or collected more than the ABR specified — can pursue damages under Pennsylvania's Unfair Trade Practices and Consumer Protection Law (73 P.S. Section 201-1 et seq.). Courts have historically been sympathetic to consumers in real estate disclosure cases, and the post-settlement environment has made plaintiffs' attorneys acutely aware of these claims.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate practical risk. If a title company or settlement attorney identifies a commission structure that contradicts the buyer representation agreement, they may refuse to disburse funds until the discrepancy is resolved. In a market where timing matters, a 48-hour hold on disbursement can cascade into contract termination under standard PAR contingency timelines.

> "A licensee shall not accept compensation from more than one party to a transaction without the informed written consent of all parties." — 63 P.S. Section 455.606(a)

That statute predates the NAR settlement, but it now carries heightened enforcement significance. The State Real Estate Commission [published guidance in 2025](https://www.dos.pa.gov/ProfessionalLicensing/BoardsCommissions/RealEstateCommission/Pages/default.aspx) emphasizing that dual compensation scenarios will receive additional scrutiny under existing law.

## Common Mistakes Pennsylvania Agents Are Making Right Now

After eighteen months of the new rules being in effect, patterns of non-compliance have emerged across Pennsylvania brokerages. These five errors appear with alarming frequency.

**First, agents are using outdated ABR forms that lack the required specificity on compensation amounts.** Some brokerages still have 2023-era buyer representation templates in their document libraries. These forms reference cooperative compensation from the MLS — a concept that no longer exists operationally. Using them creates an unenforceable agreement and leaves the agent with no contractual right to payment.

Second, listing agents are verbally communicating commission offers to buyer agents during showings or through informal channels, then failing to memorialize those offers in writing before settlement. Pennsylvania law requires written documentation of all compensation agreements. A verbal promise at an open house is not enforceable and creates a dispute that the title company cannot resolve at closing.

Third, agents are neglecting to update the buyer when seller concession amounts change during negotiation. If your ABR specifies 2.5% and the seller's counter-offer only provides 2%, you must immediately disclose this to the buyer and obtain written acknowledgment that they are responsible for the 0.5% gap. Failing to do so violates RELRA Section 604's continuous disclosure obligation.

Fourth, some agents are attempting to build commission into the purchase price without transparent disclosure to both parties. While seller concessions toward closing costs (including buyer-agent compensation) are permitted, structuring the transaction to obscure the compensation source violates both the settlement terms and Pennsylvania's consumer protection statutes.

Fifth, dual agents are failing to execute separate compensation disclosures for each party. Under Pennsylvania's designated agency rules (49 Pa. Code Section 35.331), a dual agent must provide individualized written disclosure to both buyer and seller regarding how compensation flows. The post-settlement environment makes this requirement even more complex, as the traditional MLS-driven split no longer provides automatic documentation.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under RELRA Section 603(a) for the acts of their affiliated licensees. The post-settlement commission structure requires brokers to implement new audit protocols that go beyond traditional file review.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Consequence of Failure |
|------------|-----------|----------------------|
| ABR executed before first showing | Every transaction | MLS fine + potential RELRA violation |
| Compensation amount matches settlement statement | Closing file review | Civil liability + possible disgorgement |
| CND form present and signed | Pre-offer review | Consumer protection claim exposure |
| Dual agency compensation disclosure (both parties) | Every dual agency file | License suspension risk |
| Outdated form identification | Monthly library audit | Unenforceable agreements |

**Brokers should implement a pre-offer compliance checkpoint where a designated reviewer confirms that the ABR, CND, and any seller concession documentation are consistent with each other.** This 15-minute review can prevent six-figure liability exposure. Some brokerages have assigned this function to their transaction coordinators, though the legal responsibility remains with the supervising broker.

The Pennsylvania State Real Estate Commission conducts random audits of brokerage files. Post-settlement, auditors have added commission documentation to their standard checklist. A broker whose files show inconsistent or missing compensation disclosures faces not only individual transaction liability but firm-level sanctions including potential revocation of the brokerage license.

## How This Affects Buyer Negotiations in Practice

The practical effect on Pennsylvania buyer agents is significant. Buyers are now directly confronted with the cost of representation in a way they never were before. When an agent presents a buyer with an ABR requiring a 2.5% commission commitment, the buyer naturally asks: "What do I get for that money?"

This creates both a challenge and an opportunity. Agents who can clearly articulate their value proposition will retain clients. Agents who relied on the old system's opacity to avoid that conversation will lose business. The market is already showing signs of commission compression in certain Pennsylvania metros, particularly in the Philadelphia suburbs and Pittsburgh's East End where inventory competition is fierce.

**Buyer agents should prepare a written service outline — separate from the ABR itself — that documents exactly what services the buyer will receive.** While not legally required, this practice reduces disputes and provides evidence of value delivered if a commission challenge arises later.
