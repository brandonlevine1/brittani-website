## Understanding Pennsylvania Dual Agency & Designated Agency Rules 2026

Pennsylvania handles agency relationships with a specificity that trips up even experienced licensees. The rules governing **Pennsylvania dual agency & designated agency rules 2026** sit primarily within the Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. 455.101 et seq., and the regulations codified at 49 Pa. Code Chapter 35, Subchapter E. Agents who treat these as mere paperwork formalities risk disciplinary action from the Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission (PREC), deal collapse, and personal civil liability.

The state draws a firm line between permitted dual agency and designated agency, and the disclosure timeline is not negotiable. Missing the window or using the wrong form does not just create an awkward conversation at the closing table—it creates grounds for rescission.

## How Pennsylvania Defines Dual Agency

Under RELRA and 49 Pa. Code 35.331, dual agency arises when a single licensee—or when a broker and all affiliated licensees collectively—represents both the buyer and seller in the same transaction. Pennsylvania permits dual agency only with informed, written consent from both parties. The consent must be obtained before the dual agent discloses any confidential information belonging to either party.

**The critical distinction is that consent must be truly informed, not merely signed.** The agent is required to explain the limitations of dual agency: that the agent cannot advocate for one party's interests over the other, cannot disclose motivation or financial information of either party without permission, and cannot advise on price or terms that would favor one side. Verbal explanation alone is insufficient; the written acknowledgment must document that these limitations were communicated.

Pennsylvania does not treat dual agency as a default or fallback position. It is an elected status that requires affirmative steps from the licensee and knowing agreement from both consumers. Silence or failure to object does not constitute consent.

## Designated Agency: The Broker's Alternative

Designated agency under Pennsylvania law allows a broker to appoint individual licensees within the firm to act as exclusive agents for the buyer and seller in the same transaction. This structure preserves each client's right to full advocacy and confidentiality—something dual agency explicitly cannot provide.

The broker appointing designated agents must do so in writing. Each designated agent owes full fiduciary duties to their assigned client, including undivided loyalty, confidentiality, and full disclosure of material facts. The broker who makes the designation steps into a neutral supervisory role and cannot share one client's confidential information with the other designated agent.

| Feature | Dual Agency | Designated Agency |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Number of licensees serving both parties | One licensee (or entire firm) | Two separate licensees in same firm |
| Confidentiality preserved | No—limited for both parties | Yes—each agent maintains full confidentiality |
| Advocacy permitted | No—neutral facilitation only | Yes—full advocacy for assigned client |
| Broker's role | Remains dual agent or supervises dual agent | Neutral supervisor; cannot share confidential info |
| Written consent required | Yes, from both parties | Yes, from both parties |

This comparison matters in practice because many brokerages default to designated agency to reduce liability exposure. If your brokerage operates on a designated agency model, each agent must still confirm the arrangement with their client and document it properly using the appropriate PAR forms.

## Required Forms and Disclosure Timing

Pennsylvania's disclosure framework starts with the **Consumer Notice** form. PREC mandates that every licensee present the Consumer Notice at first substantive contact with any consumer—before discussing needs, motivations, or property details beyond publicly available information. This applies regardless of whether dual agency will ever arise in that relationship.

When dual agency does arise, agents must use the **Dual Agency Disclosure and Consent** form. The Pennsylvania Association of Realtors (PAR) provides Form DAD for this purpose. Both parties must sign acknowledging they understand the limitations and voluntarily consent. For designated agency arrangements, brokerages typically use a separate designated agency addendum or rider, documenting which specific licensees have been assigned and affirming the confidentiality firewall.

**Timing is everything: the disclosures cannot be presented simultaneously with the agreement of sale.** Presenting dual agency consent at the same moment you present an offer creates an inherent pressure that courts have found undermines the "informed" requirement. Best practice—and the safest defensible practice—is to present the dual agency disclosure as soon as the potential for dual agency becomes apparent, which is often when a buyer client expresses interest in a property listed by the same brokerage.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The penalties for non-compliance fall into three categories: regulatory, civil, and transactional.

**Regulatory consequences come from PREC directly.** Under 63 P.S. 455.604, the Commission may impose fines up to $10,000 per violation, suspend or revoke licenses, and require additional education. A pattern of disclosure failures can escalate from fine to suspension faster than most agents anticipate.

Civil liability arises because a party who was not properly informed of dual agency can claim breach of fiduciary duty. Pennsylvania courts have permitted buyers and sellers to seek rescission of the purchase agreement, disgorgement of the agent's commission, and compensatory damages. In *Basile v. H&R Block Mortgage Corp.* and related Pennsylvania case law, courts have scrutinized whether consent was truly informed or merely a signature on a form.

Transactionally, a title company or attorney reviewing the deal file may flag the absence of proper dual agency consent and refuse to close. This does not happen often, but when it does, the resulting delay—or cancellation—falls squarely on the agent who failed to document the relationship. ## Common Mistakes Pennsylvania Agents Make

**First, agents frequently present the Consumer Notice too late.** The statute requires presentation at first substantive contact, but agents often wait until a showing or even until an offer is being prepared. By then, confidential information has already been exchanged, and the disclosure is retroactive rather than protective.

Second, agents confuse dual agency with designated agency and check the wrong box or use the wrong form. This is not a technicality—if you document the relationship as dual agency but your brokerage intended designated agency, the confidentiality protections your client expected never existed on paper.

Third, some agents obtain dual agency consent from the seller at listing time as a blanket authorization but fail to obtain separate consent from the buyer at the point dual agency actually arises. Pennsylvania requires contemporaneous informed consent from both parties, not a preemptive blanket at the listing stage alone.

Fourth, agents in designated agency arrangements share confidential client information with their broker or with the other designated agent, defeating the entire purpose of the firewall. The broker's neutral role means they cannot relay pricing strategies, motivation details, or negotiation positions between the two sides.

Fifth, agents fail to document the oral explanation that accompanied the written form. If a dispute arises, having a file note or email confirming that you verbally explained the limitations of dual agency strengthens your defense significantly.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under RELRA for their affiliated licensees' compliance. A broker who fails to implement systems ensuring timely and correct agency disclosures faces the same disciplinary exposure as the individual agent—and potentially greater civil exposure due to vicarious liability.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | What to Check |
|-------------------|-----------|---------------|
| Consumer Notice in every file | Per transaction | Signed copy dated at or before first substantive contact |
| Dual agency consent forms | When applicable | Both parties signed; dated before offer presentation |
| Designated agency documentation | When applicable | Written broker designation; separate agents confirmed |
| Confidentiality firewall compliance | Ongoing | No evidence of cross-sharing between designated agents |
| Agent training records | Annually | Completion of agency-specific CE or in-house training |

**Brokers should perform random file audits at least quarterly, not just at closing.** Waiting until a transaction closes to review disclosures means the damage is already done if a form is missing. Midstream audits catch errors when they can still be corrected without deal disruption.

Training matters here more than in almost any other compliance area. New agents frequently misunderstand when designated agency applies versus when they have inadvertently entered dual agency. Having a documented [training protocol with specific timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) for onboarding licensees into your brokerage's agency policies is both a best practice and a defensible position if PREC investigates.

Brokers should also maintain a written office policy specifying whether the firm permits dual agency at all, or whether designated agency is the firm's exclusive approach. Some Pennsylvania brokerages have eliminated dual agency entirely to reduce risk. This policy must be communicated to every licensee in writing and revisited annually.
