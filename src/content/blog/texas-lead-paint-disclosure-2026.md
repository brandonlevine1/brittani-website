## Why Texas Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026 Still Trip Up Experienced Agents

The Texas lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain one of the most frequently botched compliance steps in residential transactions. This is not a new rule—it has been federal law since 1996—yet enforcement actions and buyer lawsuits tied to lead paint failures continue to climb. The reason is simple: agents treat it as a formality instead of a legal obligation backed by serious financial penalties.

Under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulation at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H, every seller and landlord of pre-1978 housing must disclose known lead-based paint hazards before a sale or lease closes. The [EPA's lead disclosure rule](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-based-paint-disclosure-rule) applies uniformly across all states, including Texas, where the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) and the Texas Association of Realtors (TAR) provide specific forms to satisfy the requirement.

If your listing was built before 1978, there are no exceptions based on property condition, renovation status, or seller statements like "I never tested for it." The obligation exists regardless.

## The Specific Forms Texas Agents Must Use

**Texas agents have two primary form options for lead paint disclosure, and using neither one is not an option.** The TAR 1906—formally titled "Addendum for Seller's Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and Lead-Based Paint Hazards"—is the industry standard form distributed through Texas Association of Realtors membership. It mirrors the federal EPA/HUD disclosure form but is formatted for attachment to the TAR residential contract.

Alternatively, agents may use the EPA's standard disclosure form (often referred to as the "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" pamphlet acknowledgment paired with the Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint form). Either satisfies federal law. However, mixing and matching partial sections from each form creates gaps that expose agents to liability.

The form must be attached as an addendum to the purchase contract. It is separate from—and does not replace—the [Texas seller's disclosure notice](/blog/texas-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) required under Section 5.008 of the Texas Property Code.

## What the Disclosure Actually Requires

Three distinct obligations arise under the federal rule, and all three must be satisfied before the buyer is bound to perform:

The seller must disclose any known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards, provide any available records or reports pertaining to lead paint in the property, and provide the buyer with the EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." The seller must also provide the buyer with a 10-day opportunity to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at the buyer's expense. This 10-day period can be modified by mutual written agreement—shortened, lengthened, or waived—but it must be explicitly addressed in the contract.

**The 10-day inspection period is not automatic in Texas unless the parties specifically agree to it in writing.** Many agents mistakenly believe that simply including the TAR 1906 addendum satisfies this requirement. It does—but only if the buyer's election on the form is properly completed. A blank checkbox for the inspection waiver or acceptance voids the protection.

| Obligation | Responsible Party | Form Section |
|---|---|---|
| Disclose known lead paint/hazards | Seller | TAR 1906, Section A |
| Provide existing reports/records | Seller | TAR 1906, Section A |
| Deliver EPA pamphlet to buyer | Listing agent | TAR 1906, Section C |
| Offer 10-day inspection opportunity | Both agents + seller | TAR 1906, Section B |
| Confirm buyer received all disclosures | Buyer's agent | TAR 1906, Section D |

The buyer's agent also carries an affirmative duty: they must sign the form certifying they informed the buyer of their rights under the rule. This is not optional participation—it is a regulatory requirement.

## Penalties and Liability for Non-Compliance

The consequences of failing to comply with lead paint disclosure requirements in Texas are not hypothetical. Under 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d, violations can result in civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2025 HUD penalty schedules). Criminal penalties can reach $11,000 per violation. Treble damages—three times the buyer's actual damages—are available in private lawsuits.

**Agents and brokers are individually liable, not just the seller.** HUD and the EPA have pursued enforcement actions against real estate licensees who failed to ensure the disclosure was properly completed, even when the seller was the party who withheld information. The listing agent's duty is to confirm the form was provided and executed—not to verify the truth of the seller's statements, but to ensure the process occurred.

In Texas, TREC can also take disciplinary action against a licensee for failing to comply with federal housing regulations. Under Section 1101.652 of the Texas Occupations Code, TREC may revoke, suspend, or refuse to renew a license for conduct that violates federal law related to real estate transactions. A lead paint disclosure failure can cascade: the buyer may rescind the contract, the seller may sue the agent for causing the deal to collapse, and TREC may open a complaint investigation simultaneously.

| Penalty Type | Maximum Amount | Enforcing Body |
|---|---|---|
| Civil fine per violation | $19,507 | HUD / EPA |
| Criminal fine per violation | $11,000 | DOJ |
| Treble damages (private suit) | 3x actual damages | Buyer via court |
| License action | Revocation/suspension | TREC |
| Contract rescission | Full refund to buyer | Court order |

## Common Mistakes Texas Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosure

Five errors appear repeatedly in TREC complaints and transaction audits related to lead paint. Each one is avoidable with basic diligence.

**First: Agents forget to include the addendum entirely on pre-1978 homes.** This is the most common failure, particularly when agents rely on mental checklists rather than systematic compliance workflows. The TAR 1906 should be automatically triggered whenever MLS data or public records show a construction date before 1978. If you are [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without a system, this is exactly the kind of item that slips.

Second: Agents allow the buyer's inspection election to remain blank. The TAR 1906 requires the buyer to either accept or waive the 10-day inspection opportunity. A form submitted with that section unchecked is incomplete and does not satisfy the regulation. The buyer's agent must confirm this box is marked before execution.

Third: The listing agent fails to retain a signed copy for three years. Federal regulations require that the seller and agent retain the completed disclosure form for no fewer than three years from the closing date. Texas agents who delete transaction files after closing or who rely solely on their brokerage's storage without independent verification are exposed.

Fourth: Agents confuse the lead paint addendum with the general [Texas agency disclosure](/blog/texas-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026). These are entirely separate obligations with different legal foundations. The TAR 1906 is federally mandated; the IABS (Information About Brokerage Services) is a state requirement. Conflating them leads to omissions.

Fifth: Agents rely on the seller's verbal statement that "there's no lead paint" without requiring the written form. Verbal representations carry no weight under 24 CFR 35.88. The disclosure must be in writing, signed by all parties, and retained.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under both TREC rules and federal housing law. A broker who knows—or should have known—that agents in their office are skipping lead paint disclosures faces the same penalties as the agent who failed to deliver the form.

**Every Texas brokerage should have a written policy specifying when the TAR 1906 must be included in a transaction file.** This policy should trigger automatically based on the property's year of construction, not agent memory. Quarterly file audits should check for the presence of the executed TAR 1906 in every pre-1978 transaction closed within the review period.

Brokers should verify three things during audits: that the form exists in the file, that all signature lines are completed (seller, buyer, both agents), and that the buyer's inspection election is clearly marked. A form that is 90% complete is 100% non-compliant. There is no partial credit under federal regulations.

> "Each agent who has a role in the transaction is responsible for ensuring compliance with the disclosure requirements applicable to that agent's role." — 24 CFR 35.86(a)

Training is not sufficient without verification. Brokers who conduct annual compliance training but never audit files are building a defense that will not hold up in an enforcement action. TREC expects active supervision, not passive education.

## Exemptions That Apply in Texas

Not every transaction involving a pre-1978 property triggers the lead paint disclosure. Federal law carves out specific exemptions that Texas agents should understand—but never assume without verification.

The exemption list includes: sales of properties at foreclosure, lease renewals where the disclosure was previously provided, housing exclusively for the elderly or disabled (where no child under age six resides or is expected to reside), zero-bedroom units such as efficiencies and lofts, properties that have been certified lead-free by an accredited inspector, and short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or less. Commercial properties are exempt because the rule applies only to "target housing," defined as residential dwellings.

However, mixed-use properties with residential units are not exempt. A fourplex built in 1955 with a ground-floor retail space still requires disclosure for the residential units above. Texas agents handling investor transactions involving [older multi-family properties](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) must treat each residential unit as subject to the rule.
