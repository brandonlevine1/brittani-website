## Ohio Continuing Education Requirements 2026: The Full Breakdown

**If you hold an active Ohio real estate license, the Ohio continuing education requirements 2026 renewal cycle is not something you can afford to treat as a last-minute checkbox.** The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing (ODREPL), operating under the Ohio Department of Commerce, enforces these requirements with zero flexibility on deadlines. Miss a single mandate, and your license goes inactive—no exceptions, no grace period for pending transactions.

Ohio Revised Code Section 4735.141 establishes the continuing education framework for licensed salespersons and brokers. The statute specifies both the total hour count and the mandatory topic areas that must be completed within each three-year renewal cycle. Understanding the structure of these requirements—and the specific forms tied to them—separates agents who stay compliant from those who scramble at the eleventh hour.

The renewal period operates on a birthday-based cycle. Your renewal date is your birthday in the third year following your last renewal. That means every agent in your brokerage may have a different deadline, which makes firm-wide compliance tracking a genuine operational challenge. If you have ever wondered [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), scattered renewal dates across a team are a top contributor.

## Hour Requirements and Mandatory Topics

Ohio salespersons must complete 30 hours of approved continuing education each three-year cycle. Brokers face the same 30-hour requirement. Within those 30 hours, the state mandates specific topic areas that cannot be substituted.

| Requirement | Salesperson | Broker |
|---|---|---|
| Total CE Hours per Cycle | 30 | 30 |
| Core Law (ORC Updates) | 3 hours | 3 hours |
| Civil Rights / Fair Housing | 3 hours | 3 hours |
| Elective Hours | 24 hours | 24 hours |
| Renewal Cycle Length | 3 years | 3 years |
| Deadline | Birthday in 3rd year | Birthday in 3rd year |

The 3-hour core law course covers updates to ORC Chapter 4735, recent Ohio Real Estate Commission rule changes, and license law amendments. The 3-hour civil rights requirement addresses federal and Ohio fair housing law, including Ohio Revised Code Section 4112.02, which prohibits discrimination in real estate transactions. The remaining 24 elective hours can come from any ODREPL-approved course—contract law, ethics, technology, property management, or specialty areas.

## The Forms That Matter

**Ohio agents interact with several specific forms throughout the renewal and compliance process, and confusing them causes avoidable delays.** The primary form for license renewal is the online renewal application submitted through the ODREPL eLicense Ohio portal. Agents do not submit paper CE certificates directly—instead, approved education providers report completion electronically to the Division.

However, if a provider fails to report your hours, you will need to submit proof using the Division's CE Affidavit form. This situation arises more often than you would expect, particularly with out-of-state online providers who are approved but slow to transmit records. Agents should retain their individual course completion certificates for at least four years as backup documentation.

For brokers managing transfers or reactivations, Form DRE-10 (Application for Transfer of License) and Form DRE-30 (Reactivation Application) both require attestation that CE requirements have been met. Submitting either form without completed education triggers an automatic denial, and resubmission requires starting the application process over with updated documentation.

## What Happens When You Fail to Comply

The consequences of missing Ohio CE deadlines cascade quickly. On the day after your renewal deadline, if your hours are not complete and your renewal is not processed, your license status changes to inactive in the ODREPL system. Inactive means you cannot list properties, show homes, write offers, negotiate contracts, or collect commissions.

Any transaction you are involved in as the agent of record becomes a liability for your brokerage. Ohio Administrative Code Section 1301:5-1-01 makes the broker responsible for ensuring that all affiliated licensees maintain active status. If a deal closes while your license is inactive, the commission may be unenforceable, and the brokerage faces potential disciplinary action from the Ohio Real Estate Commission.

Reactivation requires completing all delinquent CE hours, submitting Form DRE-30, and paying reinstatement fees that increase based on how long the license has been inactive. For licenses inactive more than three years, the agent must retake the state licensing exam. The financial and reputational cost of a [blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) due to an inactive license far exceeds the cost of timely compliance.

> Ohio Revised Code Section 4735.18(A)(6) classifies practicing without an active license as grounds for disciplinary action, including fines up to $5,000 per violation and potential license revocation.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make With CE

Five errors recur constantly among Ohio licensees, and each one is entirely preventable.

**First, agents assume their online course provider automatically reports to ODREPL.** Not all approved providers have seamless electronic reporting. Some smaller or newer providers submit batch reports monthly rather than in real time. If you complete your final course two days before your birthday deadline and the provider reports on a monthly cycle, your hours may not appear in the system in time. Always verify your transcript in the eLicense portal at least 30 days before your renewal date.

Second, agents confuse Ohio approval with general CE approval. A course approved in Indiana or Kentucky does not automatically satisfy Ohio requirements unless it carries specific ODREPL approval. The Division maintains its own list of approved courses and providers. Taking a course that lacks Ohio approval means those hours simply do not count, regardless of the topic's relevance.

Third, agents double-count civil rights hours as electives. The 3 required civil rights hours are carved out separately from the 24 elective hours. If you take a 6-hour fair housing course, only 3 hours count toward civil rights and 3 toward electives—but some agents mistakenly believe a single long course satisfies both requirements simultaneously without reducing their elective bank.

Fourth, agents wait until the final quarter of their cycle. ODREPL data consistently shows a spike in renewal issues among agents who complete all 30 hours in the last 90 days. Course availability, technical glitches in reporting, and provider delays compound when every agent in the state is trying to finish at the same time.

Fifth, agents fail to update their mailing address or email with ODREPL. Renewal notices and compliance alerts go to the address on file. If you moved offices or changed your email without updating Form DRE-5 (Change of Address Notification), you may never receive the Division's 90-day reminder.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Ohio brokers carry personal liability for their affiliated agents' compliance status. Ohio Administrative Code Section 1301:5-1-01 places the burden on the broker to maintain records demonstrating that every agent operating under their license holds active, compliant status.

| Broker Responsibility | Frequency | Source Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Verify agent license status | Monthly minimum | OAC 1301:5-1-01 |
| Track individual renewal dates | Ongoing (birthday-based) | ORC 4735.141 |
| Confirm CE completion before renewal | 60 days pre-deadline | Best practice |
| Maintain copies of completion records | Per cycle (retain 4 years) | ODREPL guidance |
| Report inactive agents to ODREPL | Within 10 days of knowledge | ORC 4735.18 |

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-report their CE status are accepting unnecessary risk.** A better approach is to pull agent license statuses directly from the eLicense Ohio public lookup tool monthly. Cross-referencing active deal rosters against license status catches problems before they reach closing tables. For brokerages managing large teams, this audit function alone can justify dedicated operations support.

The Ohio Real Estate Commission has publicly stated that "I didn't know my agent's license had lapsed" is not a defense to disciplinary complaints. Brokers face the same penalty exposure as agents—fines, license suspension, and civil liability from clients whose transactions were handled by unlicensed individuals.

## How Ohio Compares to Neighboring States

Context matters when agents hold licenses in multiple states. Ohio's 30-hour, three-year cycle sits in the middle of the pack regionally, but the birthday-based deadline and carved-out topic mandates add complexity that flat annual requirements do not.

| State | Total CE Hours | Cycle Length | Mandatory Topics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ohio | 30 | 3 years | Core law (3 hrs), Civil rights (3 hrs) |
| Indiana | 12 | Annually | None specified |
| Kentucky | 6 | Annually | Core law (3 hrs) |
| Michigan | 18 | 3 years | Legal updates (2 hrs) |
| Pennsylvania | 14 | 2 years | None specified |
| West Virginia | 7 | Annually | Core law (3 hrs) |

Ohio's higher total hour count combined with its specific topic mandates means multi-state agents cannot simply apply their Ohio courses elsewhere or vice versa without careful cross-referencing. ## Staying Ahead of the 2026 Deadline

The agents who avoid compliance emergencies share one trait: they treat CE like a quarterly obligation, not an annual crisis. Blocking 8-10 hours of coursework each year of the three-year cycle distributes the load and eliminates the last-minute scramble. It also means that if a provider fails to report or a course gets retroactively de-approved, you have time to substitute.

Set a calendar reminder for 90 days before your birthday to pull your transcript from the [eLicense Ohio portal](https://elicense.ohio.gov/). Verify that all completed hours appear correctly, that your core law and civil rights mandates show as satisfied, and that your total hits 30. If anything is missing, 90 days gives you enough runway to resolve disputes with providers or complete additional coursework.
