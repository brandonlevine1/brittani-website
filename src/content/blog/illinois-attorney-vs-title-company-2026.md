## Illinois Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Rule That Confuses Half the State

The question of "Illinois attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026" generates more confusion among licensed agents than almost any other compliance issue in the state. Illinois occupies an unusual middle ground: it is not a pure "attorney state" by statute, yet decades of case law and industry practice make attorney involvement functionally mandatory for most residential closings. Agents who misunderstand this distinction risk liability exposure, deal cancellations, and regulatory scrutiny from the Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation (IDFPR).

The stakes are higher than a philosophical debate. Getting this wrong means your client's transaction could be challenged, your brokerage could face an unauthorized practice of law complaint, and the deal you spent months putting together could collapse at the finish line.

## The Legal Framework: What Illinois Law Actually Says

Illinois has no single statute that reads "an attorney must attend every real estate closing." Instead, the requirement derives from the intersection of several legal authorities. The Illinois Attorney Act (705 ILCS 205/1) prohibits anyone other than a licensed attorney from practicing law, and the Illinois Supreme Court has repeatedly defined the preparation of deeds, mortgages, and contracts as the practice of law.

The landmark case *Chicago Bar Association v. Quinlan & Tyson, Inc.* established that drafting or modifying real estate documents constitutes legal practice. Title companies operate under a narrow exception: they may issue title insurance commitments, conduct escrow, and prepare standardized HUD/TRID closing disclosures. But the moment a title company employee interprets contract language, advises on title defect remedies, or modifies contractual terms, they cross into unauthorized practice territory.

**The practical result is that northern Illinois (Cook County and the collar counties) treats attorney involvement as a near-universal requirement, while parts of southern Illinois sometimes close without attorneys present.** This geographic split does not reflect different laws — it reflects different enforcement cultures and local custom. The legal risk is identical statewide.

## What a Title Company Can and Cannot Do

Understanding the boundary between permissible title company functions and attorney-only functions is where agents most often stumble. The Illinois Title Insurance Act (215 ILCS 155) authorizes title companies to examine title, issue commitments, and provide insurance policies. It does not authorize them to practice law.

| Function | Title Company | Attorney |
|----------|:---:|:---:|
| Issue title commitment | Yes | N/A |
| Conduct escrow / hold funds | Yes | Yes |
| Prepare deed | No | Yes |
| Draft contract riders or modifications | No | Yes |
| Advise on title defect resolution | No | Yes |
| Prepare closing disclosure (TRID) | Yes | Yes |
| Represent buyer or seller at closing table | No | Yes |

When an agent directs a client to rely solely on a title company for functions that require legal judgment, that agent may be facilitating unauthorized practice of law. The IDFPR has authority under the Real Estate License Act of 2000 (225 ILCS 454) to discipline agents who engage in or facilitate UPL.

## The Attorney Review Period: Where Agents Create Their Own Problems

Illinois residential contracts include an attorney review clause — typically a five-business-day window during which either party's attorney can approve, disapprove, or modify the contract. Under the Multi-Board Residential Real Estate Contract (Form 7.0), Paragraph 5 governs this review period. The Illinois REALTORS standard contract contains a similar provision.

**If an agent fails to inform their client about the attorney review period or pressures them to waive it, that agent creates enormous liability exposure.** The attorney review clause exists precisely because agents cannot provide legal advice under Illinois law. Skipping it does not save time — it transfers legal risk directly onto the agent and the brokerage.

The review period clock starts upon acceptance of the contract, not upon delivery to the attorney. Agents who delay forwarding executed contracts to their client's attorney effectively shorten the review window, which has led to disciplinary complaints and civil suits. This is one of the most common [places where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) in Illinois transactions.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make

**First: Telling clients they do not need an attorney.** This statement, even when phrased as an opinion rather than legal advice, exposes the agent to a UPL facilitation claim. The safe practice is to inform every client that attorney representation is customary in Illinois and that the agent cannot provide legal guidance.

Second: Treating the title company as a substitute for attorney review. Agents sometimes tell buyers that "the title company will handle everything," conflating title insurance issuance with legal representation. These are fundamentally different services, and no title insurance commitment protects a buyer from a poorly drafted contract modification.

Third: Modifying contract terms without attorney involvement. The Illinois REALTORS forms and Multi-Board Form 7.0 include fill-in-the-blank sections that agents may complete. But when an agent drafts custom riders, writes addenda addressing legal contingencies, or modifies boilerplate terms, they are practicing law. The proper procedure is to note the parties' intent and refer the language to an attorney for drafting.

Fourth: Missing attorney review deadlines due to poor tracking. The five-business-day window is unforgiving. Agents who lack a systematic approach to deadline management — relying on memory or informal calendars — [miss deadlines at alarming rates](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines). A missed attorney review deadline means the contract stands as written, regardless of defects either attorney would have caught.

Fifth: Failing to confirm attorney attendance or authorization before closing day. Illinois closings frequently require the physical or virtual presence of an attorney to execute documents. Agents who assume the title company will coordinate attorney attendance often arrive at the closing table without the legal authority needed to close.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers carry supervisory liability under 225 ILCS 454/15-5 for the acts of their sponsored agents. This is not theoretical — the IDFPR has issued fines and suspensions to brokers whose agents repeatedly facilitated closings without proper attorney involvement.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Frequency |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| Attorney review documentation | Signed attorney approval or modification letter in every file | Every transaction |
| Contract form version | Confirm agents use current Multi-Board 7.0 or IL REALTORS form | Quarterly |
| Custom rider drafting | Flag any rider not prepared by a licensed attorney | Every transaction |
| Timeline tracking | Verify attorney review deadlines were met | Every transaction |
| Client communication logs | Confirm written recommendation of attorney representation | Random monthly sample |

Brokers should implement a file review protocol that flags any transaction closed without documentation of attorney involvement. The absence of an attorney approval letter in a transaction file is a red flag that demands immediate inquiry. A solid [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) should include attorney confirmation as a non-negotiable item.

> "No person shall be permitted to practice law in this state who has not obtained a license for that purpose from the Supreme Court of this State." — 705 ILCS 205/1

Brokers who discover patterns of non-compliance should document corrective action in writing. The IDFPR considers a brokerage's internal enforcement efforts when evaluating supervisory liability — proactive auditing is a defense, while willful ignorance is an aggravating factor.

## Title Company Selection and Agent Responsibilities Under RESPA

Agents must also consider the Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA), 12 U.S.C. 2607, which prohibits kickbacks and unearned fees in connection with settlement services. An agent who steers clients to a particular title company based on a referral fee arrangement — rather than on the client's best interest — violates federal law regardless of Illinois-specific requirements.

The Illinois IDFPR has coordinated with the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) on enforcement actions involving Illinois agents who received prohibited compensation from title companies. Agents should disclose any affiliated business arrangement using the CFPB's AfBA disclosure form and ensure clients understand their right to choose their own title company and attorney.

This federal layer sits on top of the Illinois-specific attorney requirement. An agent cannot solve the attorney problem by simply selecting a title company that employs staff attorneys — the attorney must represent the client's interests, not the title company's interests. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond a single lost commission when federal violations compound state-level compliance failures.

## How 2026 Changes Affect the Analysis

The Illinois Real Estate License Act of 2000 has not been amended in 2026 to alter the attorney/title company framework. However, the Illinois Supreme Court's Commission on Professionalism issued updated guidance in late 2025 reaffirming that technology-assisted document preparation (including AI-generated contract language) still constitutes the practice of law when it involves legal judgment applied to specific facts.

This means agents cannot use AI tools to draft custom contract modifications and claim the output is "not legal advice." The attorney requirement applies to the function, not the method. Agents should use technology for tracking, compliance monitoring, and deadline management — areas where tools like [Brittani AI](https://brittani.ai/pricing) provide genuine operational value without crossing into legal practice territory — while reserving legal drafting and interpretation for licensed attorneys.

**The 2026 landscape for Illinois attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 remains functionally unchanged from prior years: attorneys handle legal functions, title companies handle insurance and escrow, and agents who blur those lines face compounding liability.** The Illinois State Bar Association's [Real Property Section](https://www.isba.org/sections/realproperty) continues to publish practice guides reinforcing this framework, and agents should treat those publications as authoritative markers of where their role ends and an attorney's begins.
