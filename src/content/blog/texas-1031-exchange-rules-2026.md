## Texas 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: The State-Level Advantage and Its Hidden Traps

Understanding Texas 1031 exchange state tax implications 2026 starts with a fact that surprises agents from other states: Texas imposes no individual income tax. Article VIII, Section 1 of the Texas Constitution prohibits it outright. That single structural advantage makes Texas one of the most attractive states for 1031 exchange activity in the country.

But "no state income tax" does not mean "no state tax exposure." Agents who coast on that assumption expose their clients — and their own licenses — to risks that stem from the Texas franchise tax, property tax reassessment triggers, and federal compliance failures that ripple into state-level consequences. The Texas Comptroller of Public Accounts administers the franchise (margin) tax under [Texas Tax Code Chapter 171](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/TX/htm/TX.171.htm), and this is where entity-level transactions get complicated fast.

This post breaks down exactly what licensed agents and their managing brokers need to know, enforce, and document when a 1031 exchange is part of a Texas deal in 2026.

## Why "No State Income Tax" Is Not the Whole Story

**Texas agents routinely tell investor-clients that a 1031 exchange in Texas carries zero state tax implications — and that statement is dangerously incomplete.** While individual sellers owe no state income tax on capital gains, the picture shifts when the exchangor is an LLC, LP, or corporation subject to the Texas franchise tax.

Under Texas Tax Code Section 171.002, any taxable entity doing business in Texas must file a franchise tax report if its annualized total revenue exceeds the no-tax-due threshold of $2.47 million (2026 figure, adjusted periodically by the Comptroller). The proceeds from a relinquished property sale can push an entity's total revenue over that threshold, even if the gain is deferred federally under IRC Section 1031.

The franchise tax is calculated on margin — total revenue minus either cost of goods sold, compensation, 30 percent of total revenue, or $1 million — depending on which method the entity elects. Agents do not calculate this tax, but they need to flag the issue so the client's CPA can structure the exchange correctly before closing.

## The Forms and Documents Texas Agents Actually Touch

Real estate agents in Texas do not prepare IRS Form 8824 (Like-Kind Exchanges), and they should never attempt to. That form is the exchangor's responsibility, filed with their federal return. But agents do handle transaction documents that directly affect whether a 1031 exchange survives IRS scrutiny.

| Document | Who Prepares It | Agent's Role |
|----------|----------------|--------------|
| TREC Form 20-16 (One to Four Family Residential Contract) | Listing or buyer's agent | Ensure assignment clauses don't violate exchange structure |
| TAR Form 1801 (Commercial Contract) | Agent or attorney | Confirm cooperation clause with Qualified Intermediary |
| TREC Addendum (Back-Up or Amendment) | Agent | Document exchange-related contingencies and deadlines |
| IRS Form 8824 | Exchangor's CPA | Agent has no preparation role — do not advise on entries |
| Texas Comptroller Form 05-158-A (Franchise Tax Report) | Entity's CPA | Agent should confirm entity has counsel reviewing tax exposure |

**Agents who insert exchange cooperation language into contracts without understanding its effect on title transfer timing create the single most common drafting error in Texas 1031 transactions.** Cooperation clauses must permit assignment of the purchase agreement to the Qualified Intermediary without requiring seller consent — but they must not create an impermissible "parking" arrangement.

The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) does not publish a dedicated 1031 exchange addendum. That means agents either draft custom language (which TREC Rule 537.11 restricts unless the agent is also an attorney) or refer clients to a real estate attorney for exchange-specific contract provisions. Agents acting beyond their authorized scope risk disciplinary action under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.652.

## Common Mistakes Texas Agents Make in 1031 Exchange Deals

Five errors show up repeatedly in TREC complaints and broker audits related to exchange transactions. Each one carries real consequences — from deal cancellation to license suspension.

**First, agents confuse "tax-free" with "tax-deferred."** A 1031 exchange does not eliminate capital gains tax. It defers recognition. When an agent tells a client their exchange is "tax-free," they have provided inaccurate tax advice — a violation of Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.652(b)(1), which prohibits misrepresentation. If the client later discovers they owe taxes (particularly at the federal level), the agent faces a complaint and potential civil liability.

Second, agents fail to track the 45-day identification period and 180-day exchange period on behalf of their clients. While the client ultimately owns these deadlines, an agent who [misses critical dates](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) in a transaction they are facilitating may be found negligent — especially if the listing agreement or buyer representation agreement included advisory duties.

Third, agents recommend specific Qualified Intermediaries without disclosing financial relationships. TREC requires disclosure of any referral fee or kickback under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.652(b)(22). If an agent receives compensation from a QI and fails to disclose it, the disciplinary consequences include fines up to $5,000 per violation and license revocation.

Fourth, agents allow their clients to take constructive receipt of exchange funds. If the seller touches the proceeds — even momentarily — the exchange fails under Treasury Regulation 1.1031(k)-1(f). Agents who structure closings without coordinating with the QI on fund handling create conditions where constructive receipt becomes almost inevitable.

Fifth, agents neglect to account for Texas property tax reassessment on the replacement property. While Texas does not reassess based on sale price the way California does under Proposition 13, county appraisal districts under Texas Tax Code Chapter 23 can and do adjust appraised values when a property changes hands. The new appraisal value affects the client's annual property tax obligation immediately, which has nothing to do with the 1031 deferral but everything to do with the client's actual cost of ownership.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers carry supervisory liability under TREC Rule 535.2. When an agent in your brokerage facilitates a 1031 exchange transaction, your audit obligations extend beyond the standard file review.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Risk If Missing |
|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Exchange cooperation clause | Verify language was drafted or reviewed by attorney | Unauthorized practice of law claim |
| QI referral disclosure | Confirm written disclosure in file if agent recommended QI | TREC fine up to $5,000; RESPA violation |
| Deadline documentation | Written communication showing 45-day and 180-day dates communicated to client | Negligence liability |
| Constructive receipt prevention | Confirm escrow instructions route funds directly to QI | Exchange disqualification; client lawsuit |
| Entity-level tax flag | Note in file that client was advised to consult CPA on franchise tax | E&O claim reduction |

**Brokers who treat 1031 exchange files the same as standard residential closings are accepting risk they have not priced into their E&O coverage.** Most errors-and-omissions policies contain exclusions for tax advice — meaning if your agent gave guidance that crossed the line into tax counsel, your brokerage may be paying out of pocket.

Implementing a [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) specific to exchange deals is the minimum standard. The checklist should require confirmation that a QI is engaged, that an attorney reviewed exchange-specific contract language, and that the client acknowledged in writing that the agent is not providing tax advice.

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When Things Go Wrong

A failed 1031 exchange in Texas does not trigger state income tax liability — but it does trigger federal capital gains tax that the client was not expecting to pay. When an investor suddenly owes 20 percent in federal capital gains plus the 3.8 percent Net Investment Income Tax, the first call goes to their attorney. The second call goes to TREC.

Under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.654, TREC can suspend or revoke a license if the agent engaged in conduct that constitutes dishonesty or untrustworthiness. Providing affirmative misrepresentation about tax consequences qualifies. TREC's enforcement division processed 1,247 complaints in fiscal year 2025, and violations related to misrepresentation consistently rank among the top five categories.

Civil liability adds another layer. Texas courts have held real estate agents to a duty of competence in Kubinsky v. Van Zandt Realtors (Tex. App. 1991), establishing that agents who undertake advisory roles must perform them with reasonable skill. If you told the client the exchange would work and it did not because of a drafting error you made or a deadline you failed to flag, you are exposed. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond the immediate commission loss.

## Texas 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: Franchise Tax, Property Tax, and Federal Interplay

The interplay between federal deferral and Texas-specific taxes deserves its own framework. Here is how the three layers interact for a typical Texas LLC selling a rental property and acquiring a replacement:

The federal layer defers capital gains recognition under IRC Section 1031, provided all identification and closing deadlines are met and the properties qualify as like-kind. The Texas franchise tax layer does not defer in lockstep — the total revenue figure on Form 05-158-A may still include proceeds from the relinquished property sale, depending on the entity's accounting method and the Comptroller's interpretation of "total revenue" under Texas Tax Code Section 171.1011. The property tax layer operates independently: the replacement property's appraised value is determined by the county appraisal district under Chapter 23, and the buyer-entity will receive a new Notice of Appraised Value (Form 50-215) reflecting the district's assessment.

Agents must understand this three-layer structure well enough to identify when a client needs professional tax counsel — and document that referral. Failing to do so is not just bad practice; it is the kind of omission that generates six-figure malpractice claims.
