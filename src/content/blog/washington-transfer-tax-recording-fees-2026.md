Getting the numbers wrong on Washington transfer tax and recording fees does not just embarrass you at closing—it can kill a deal or trigger statutory penalties. The Washington transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 have shifted enough from prior years that relying on muscle memory is a professional hazard. This article breaks down the current rate tiers, the forms you must file, what happens when compliance fails, and how brokers should be auditing their agents' files.

## How Washington's Real Estate Excise Tax (REET) Actually Works

Washington imposes a Real Estate Excise Tax on every sale of real property under RCW 82.45. Unlike states that charge a flat percentage, Washington uses a graduated rate structure that the legislature overhauled in 2020 (ESSB 5998) and has adjusted for inflation since. The tax applies to the full selling price, including assumed debt, and the seller bears primary liability.

**The 2026 REET rate tiers continue the graduated brackets first introduced in 2020, indexed for inflation.** The Department of Revenue publishes updated thresholds each January. Agents must verify the current brackets at the time of closing, not at listing.

The county treasurer collects REET before the deed records. If the tax is unpaid, the county auditor refuses to accept the conveyance instrument—full stop. Interest accrues at 12 percent per annum from the date of sale under RCW 82.45.100, and a five percent penalty attaches after the first month of delinquency.

## Washington Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026: Current Rate Tiers

The graduated REET structure taxes different portions of the selling price at different rates. Here is the schedule agents must reference when estimating seller net sheets:

| Selling Price Portion | REET Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 – $525,000 | 1.10% |
| $525,001 – $1,525,000 | 1.28% |
| $1,525,001 – $3,025,000 | 2.75% |
| Over $3,025,000 | 3.00% |

These thresholds reflect inflation adjustments published by the Washington Department of Revenue for the 2026 fiscal year. The rates are cumulative—meaning a $2,000,000 sale does not pay 2.75 percent on the entire amount; it pays 1.10 percent on the first $525,000, 1.28 percent on the next $1,000,000, and 2.75 percent on the remaining $475,000.

Some municipalities layer additional local REET on top of the state rate. Seattle, for example, collects an additional 0.5 percent under SMC 5.68. Agents working in cities with local REET must confirm the combined rate through the county treasurer's website before preparing any estimated settlement statement.

## Recording Fees: The Other Side of the Closing Ledger

Recording fees in Washington are governed by RCW 36.18.010 and collected by the county auditor's office. The base fee structure remains $10 for the first page and $1 for each additional page, but multiple surcharges stack on top. These surcharges fund housing trust funds, homeless services, and technology upgrades.

| Fee Component | Amount (per document) |
|---|---|
| Base recording (first page) | $10.00 |
| Each additional page | $1.00 |
| Affordable housing surcharge (RCW 36.22.178) | $13.00 |
| Homeless housing surcharge (RCW 36.22.179) | $62.00 |
| Technology surcharge (RCW 36.22.176) | $4.00 |
| Building code/historic surcharge | Varies by county |

A standard two-page warranty deed typically costs between $80 and $120 to record when all surcharges are included. Deeds of trust add similar fees per document. Agents who quote the bare $10 base fee to clients are setting up an unpleasant surprise at the settlement table.

Recording fees are typically assigned to the buyer for the deed and to each party for their respective documents (buyer pays for the deed of trust; seller pays for reconveyance documents). However, the allocation is negotiable and should be reflected clearly in the purchase and sale agreement. ## The Forms You Must Use—and When

**Washington requires a Real Estate Excise Tax Affidavit to be filed with the county treasurer before any conveyance document can record.** The affidavit is completed through the Department of Revenue's electronic REET system (eREET), which replaced paper Form REV 84-0001A for most counties. Agents and title companies submit the affidavit electronically, pay the tax, and receive a confirmation number that the county auditor requires before accepting the deed for recording.

The affidavit demands the full legal description, the selling price, the names of grantor and grantee, and the specific exemption code if the transaction qualifies for an exemption under [WAC 458-61A](https://apps.leg.wa.gov/wac/default.aspx?cite=458-61A). Even transfers between family members, transfers pursuant to court order, or transfers into LLCs require filing if they involve real property—the affidavit just cites the applicable exemption.

For the purchase and sale agreement itself, most Washington agents use NWMLS Form 21 (Residential Purchase and Sale Agreement) or Form 25 (for unimproved property). Section 12 of Form 21 addresses closing costs and specifically allocates excise tax responsibility. Agents must ensure this section aligns with the parties' negotiated terms. Mismatched expectations between the listing agent's Form 21 and the settlement statement prepared by escrow generate last-minute disputes that delay recording.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make on Transfer Tax and Recording Fees

Five errors come up repeatedly in transaction audits and licensing complaints. Each one is preventable with basic diligence.

**First, agents miscalculate REET by applying a single flat rate instead of the graduated brackets.** On a $1.8 million sale, applying 1.28 percent to the entire amount instead of correctly tiering the calculation produces a $6,000+ discrepancy. Sellers who discover the error at closing feel blindsided, and the agent's credibility evaporates.

Second, agents in cities with local REET forget to include the municipal layer. Seattle, Olympia, and several other jurisdictions impose their own excise taxes. The combined rate in Seattle on a sale above $3,025,000 can exceed 3.50 percent. An agent who quotes only the state rate on a Capitol Hill listing is materially misinforming the seller.

Third, agents misidentify who pays recording fees. While custom in many Washington counties assigns the deed recording fee to the buyer, the actual allocation depends entirely on the contract. Agents who tell clients "the buyer always pays recording" without checking the signed agreement create accounting conflicts at escrow.

Fourth, agents fail to disclose excise tax obligations when preparing listing presentations or buyer cost estimates. The Washington Department of Licensing expects licensees to demonstrate competence in estimating closing costs under WAC 308-124C. A persistent pattern of inaccurate estimates can support a disciplinary finding of incompetence.

Fifth, agents delay getting the REET affidavit submitted, assuming escrow will handle everything. While escrow typically prepares the filing, the agent who promised a specific closing date bears reputational risk when a late submission pushes recording to the next business day. In a market where possession dates are tied to recording confirmation, a one-day delay cascades into breach-of-contract territory.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carry supervisory responsibility under RCW 18.85.186. That obligation extends to ensuring affiliated agents accurately represent transaction costs and comply with recording requirements. Here is what the audit process should include.

**Brokers should review every estimated seller net sheet and buyer cost estimate before the documents reach a client.** If a brokerage uses standardized templates for net sheets, those templates must be updated every January to reflect new REET thresholds and any recording fee surcharge changes. A template from 2024 will produce wrong numbers in 2026.

Brokers should verify that Form 21 Section 12 matches the actual negotiated allocation. Agents sometimes fill in the form on autopilot, defaulting to "seller pays excise tax" without confirming whether a negotiated credit or split was agreed to verbally. The written contract controls, and an incorrect form can expose the brokerage to a claim.

Transaction file audits should confirm that a REET confirmation number exists in the file before the closing date. If your agents are not attaching the eREET confirmation to their transaction records, you have no proof that the filing occurred properly. Regulators and E&O carriers both look for this documentation during complaints and claims.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, Liability, and Deal Death

The most immediate consequence of unpaid REET is simple: the deed does not record. No recording means no transfer of legal title. If a buyer has already wired funds and the seller's excise tax obligation is unresolved, the transaction stalls in escrow until someone pays. Contractual deadlines expire, and either party may invoke default remedies.

Beyond the transactional consequences, the Department of Revenue assesses a five percent penalty for late REET payment plus 12 percent annual interest under RCW 82.45.100. The buyer is secondarily liable for the tax if the seller fails to pay, which creates a chain of recourse claims that often ensnare the agents who assured both parties the numbers were correct.

From a licensing perspective, the Washington Department of Licensing can take disciplinary action against agents who demonstrate a pattern of negligent cost representations. WAC 308-124C-140 lists providing inaccurate information to a party as grounds for sanctions ranging from fines to license suspension. A single miscalculation likely will not trigger discipline, but repeated errors—especially combined with client complaints—build a case for incompetence findings.

Errors and omissions insurance may cover resulting damages, but policies typically exclude intentional misrepresentation. If an agent knowingly underquoted excise tax to secure a listing, the E&O carrier may deny the claim. Brokerages left holding the bag often terminate the agent's affiliation and report the circumstances to the DOL.

## Practical Steps for Getting It Right Every Time

Run the graduated REET calculation yourself before the listing appointment. Do not rely solely on escrow's preliminary title report. Pull the current thresholds from the Department of Revenue's published schedule and apply each bracket sequentially. Double-check whether the property falls within a municipality that imposes local REET.

Confirm recording fee totals with the county auditor's office at least five business days before the anticipated closing. Surcharges change periodically, and not every county updates its website promptly. A three-minute phone call prevents a $50 shortage from becoming a last-minute wire delay.
