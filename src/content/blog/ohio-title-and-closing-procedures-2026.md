## Understanding Ohio Title & Closing Procedures 2026

Ohio's closing process sits in a unique position compared to many states: it is not a mandatory attorney-close state, yet title agencies must operate under attorney supervision. If you are a licensed agent working transactions in Ohio, you need a working command of **Ohio title & closing procedures 2026** before you advise clients, draft timelines, or set expectations with lenders. Missteps at this stage are not minor inconveniences—they generate liability, blown closings, and license complaints.

The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, housed under the Ohio Department of Commerce, governs agent conduct through Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Chapter 4735. Title insurance underwriting and agency operations fall under the Ohio Department of Insurance per ORC Chapter 3953. Both regulatory bodies have enforcement teeth, and both are paying attention to procedural failures at the closing table.

This post breaks down exactly what agents and brokers need to know—statutes, forms, common traps, and what your brokerage should be auditing right now.

## The Role of Title Companies and Attorney Supervision in Ohio

**Ohio is often called a "title company state," but that label obscures an important legal requirement.** Under ORC Section 4735.02 and related interpretive guidance from the Ohio Supreme Court's Board of Commissioners on Grievances and Discipline, a title agency conducting closings must be supervised by or operate under the direction of a licensed Ohio attorney. The attorney does not need to be physically present at every closing, but they must be available and must have reviewed the title commitment.

Title agents in Ohio perform the closing, prepare settlement statements, handle escrow disbursements, and issue title insurance policies. They are regulated both as insurance producers (under ORC 3953) and as entities performing acts that border on the practice of law. The practical effect for agents: you cannot simply pick any unlicensed notary to run a closing. You need a title company or attorney operating within this framework.

The Ohio Land Title Association (OLTA) publishes standard endorsement forms and best practices, but the actual title insurance forms used in Ohio follow the American Land Title Association (ALTA) framework with Ohio-specific endorsements filed with the Department of Insurance.

## Key Forms and Documents Agents Must Know

Ohio residential transactions rely on a specific set of documents that agents either prepare, review, or must confirm have been executed. Here is a breakdown of the most transaction-critical forms:

| Form / Document | Who Prepares | Agent's Responsibility |
|---|---|---|
| Ohio Residential Purchase Agreement (OAR Form 101) | Agent | Draft, obtain signatures, deliver to title |
| Seller's Disclosure of Property Condition (ORC 5302.30) | Seller with agent guidance | Ensure completion and delivery to buyer before acceptance |
| ALTA Settlement Statement (replaced HUD-1 for most transactions) | Title company | Review for accuracy, confirm commission split |
| Affidavit of Title | Title company / attorney | Confirm seller signs prior to or at closing |
| Transfer Tax Conveyance Fee Statement (DTE 100 or DTE 100EX) | Title company or attorney | Verify property details match, confirm exemptions if claimed |

The Ohio Association of Realtors (OAR) publishes standardized residential forms—Form 101 for the purchase contract, Form 200 series for various addenda, and Form 305 for the agency disclosure. These are not optional formatting preferences. Using outdated forms or substituting non-OAR documents without broker approval exposes agents to disputes over enforceability and potential disciplinary action under ORC 4735.18(A)(6), which prohibits dishonest or incompetent dealing.

**The DTE 100 (Conveyance Fee Statement) deserves special attention.** Every Ohio real estate transfer requires filing this form with the county auditor's office. It calculates the county conveyance fee (typically $4.00 per $1,000 of sale price, though rates vary by county) and must be filed before the deed is recorded. Agents do not prepare this form, but errors in the sale price or legal description that trace back to agent-supplied data can delay recording and create post-closing headaches.

## How a Typical Ohio Closing Unfolds

A closing in Ohio usually takes place at the title company's office, though remote and hybrid closings have expanded significantly since Ohio enacted HB 264 (effective March 2023), which authorized remote online notarization (RON) under ORC Chapter 147. The sequence matters for agents managing client expectations and coordinating with [your transaction coordination workflow](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist).

The title company orders the title search from the county recorder's office—typically Franklin, Cuyahoga, Hamilton, Summit, or one of Ohio's 88 counties. The search goes back a minimum of 42 years (standard Ohio title examination requirement under the Marketable Title Act, ORC Chapter 5301). Once the commitment issues, the buyer's agent should review all Schedule B exceptions with the client and confirm the lender has received the commitment.

At closing, the title agent conducts the signing, collects funds, and handles disbursement. Ohio follows a "dry funding" custom in most markets—meaning the deed and mortgage record before the seller receives proceeds, though same-day disbursement is standard in metro areas with electronic recording. The deed must include a statement of consideration and reference the auditor's permanent parcel number per ORC 319.20.

Agents should understand that Ohio does not mandate a specific "closing date" grace period. If a buyer cannot close on the contractual date, the other party can declare breach unless the contract includes an extension provision (OAR Form 101, Section 13 addresses time-is-of-the-essence language). This is where [missed deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) turn into canceled deals.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Ohio Closings

Five errors surface repeatedly in Ohio disciplinary cases, title claims, and failed transactions. Each one is preventable.

**First: agents failing to deliver the Residential Property Disclosure Form within the statutory timeframe.** ORC 5302.30 requires the seller to provide this disclosure to the buyer before the purchase agreement is signed or, alternatively, within three days after acceptance. Agents who treat this as a "we'll get to it" document risk giving the buyer a statutory right to rescind under ORC 5302.30(E). That right persists until proper delivery occurs.

Second: providing wire instructions verbally or via unverified email. Wire fraud remains the single largest financial threat in Ohio closings. The Ohio Department of Commerce issued a 2024 advisory urging agents to verify all wiring instructions through a known, confirmed phone number—not a number supplied in an email. Agents who forward unverified wire details face not just civil liability but potential regulatory scrutiny for failing to protect client funds. Your process for [verifying wire instructions](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) should be documented and repeatable.

Third: misrepresenting who pays title insurance premiums. Ohio has no statutory allocation—it is entirely negotiable. Agents who tell buyers "the seller always pays for the owner's policy" without checking local custom or contract terms create disputes that can delay closings or trigger ethics complaints under OAR's Code of Ethics, Standard of Practice 1-3.

Fourth: failing to calendar the title commitment review period. Ohio purchase contracts typically allow the buyer a set number of days to review title exceptions. If the agent does not flag Schedule B items (easements, restrictive covenants, mechanic's lien filings) within that window, the buyer may lose the right to object. Missing this deadline is negligence, full stop.

Fifth: ordering title too late in the contract period. In high-volume counties like Cuyahoga or Franklin, a standard title search can take 7 to 10 business days during peak months. Agents who wait until after inspection to order title risk compressing timelines to the point where closing extensions—or cancellations—become inevitable.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokerage compliance starts with verifiable systems, not vague policies. Ohio brokers have supervisory duties under ORC 4735.18(A)(6) and (H), which impose liability when a broker knows or should have known about an agent's procedural failures. Here is what deserves audit attention.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure delivery timing | Timestamp or signed receipt for ORC 5302.30 form | Every transaction |
| Wire instruction protocol | Written verification procedure in file | Quarterly review + every transaction |
| Form version currency | OAR form revision dates match current year | Semi-annual |
| Title commitment review documentation | Agent notes confirming Schedule B review with buyer | Every transaction |
| Commission instructions to title | Written authorization matches listing agreement | Every transaction |

**Brokers who rely solely on agent self-reporting are exposing themselves to vicarious liability.** The Ohio Division of Real Estate has increased audits of brokerage files, and a 2025 enforcement bulletin reminded brokers that "supervisory responsibility extends to every transaction conducted under the brokerage license, regardless of agent experience level." The cost of a compliance failure—up to $2,500 per violation under ORC 4735.051, plus potential license suspension—dwarfs the cost of building proper audit systems.

If your brokerage handles volume, consider whether your current transaction oversight actually catches these gaps. Training alone does not solve procedural drift—[consistent pipeline review systems](/blog/weekly-pipeline-review-system) do.

## Liability Exposure When Agents Get It Wrong

The consequences of procedural failure in Ohio title and closing matters hit agents on multiple fronts simultaneously. ORC 4735.18 lists over 20 grounds for disciplinary action, several of which apply directly to closing conduct: incompetency (subsection A6), failure to account for funds (subsection A24), and offering services outside the scope of licensure (subsection A9).

Civil liability adds another layer. If an agent's negligence causes a buyer to lose earnest money, miss a contractual deadline, or close on a property with undisclosed title defects that should have been flagged, the agent and brokerage face breach of duty claims. Ohio courts have consistently held that agents owe fiduciary duties that include reasonable diligence in reviewing closing documents—see *Waller v. Johnson*, 2012-Ohio-4570.

> "A real estate licensee who fails to exercise reasonable care in reviewing title matters on behalf of a client may be held liable for resulting damages, including lost earnest money and consequential costs of delayed occupancy." — *Waller v. Johnson*, 2012-Ohio-4570

E&O insurance covers some exposure, but carriers increasingly deny claims where the agent deviated from documented office procedures. The [true financial cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends well beyond the immediate transaction—it includes reputation damage, referral loss, and increased insurance premiums.

## Ohio Title & Closing Procedures 2026: Staying Ahead of Regulatory Shifts

The Ohio Division of Real Estate signaled in its 2025 annual report that enforcement focus would shift toward electronic transaction compliance—specifically, whether agents using digital platforms maintain adequate records under ORC 4735.04's record-keeping requirements. Brokers must retain transaction records for a minimum of three years after closing.

Remote online notarization continues to expand under [ORC Chapter 147](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/chapter-147), but agents should confirm that their title company's RON platform is approved by the Ohio Secretary of State's office. Using an unapproved platform could invalidate the notarization, rendering the deed unrecordable and the closing effectively void.
