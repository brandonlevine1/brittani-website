## Why New York Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026 Remain a Minefield

New York's approach to property condition and environmental disclosures has always been an outlier. Unlike most states that mandate a detailed seller disclosure form as a hard prerequisite to closing, New York gives sellers an escape hatch—a $500 credit that effectively lets them stay silent. Understanding **New York property condition & environmental disclosures 2026** rules is not optional for agents who want to avoid lawsuits, Department of State complaints, and blown deals.

The framework rests on NY Real Property Law Article 14 (Sections 460–467), enacted in 2002 and still the controlling statute for residential one-to-four family transactions. The statute created the Property Condition Disclosure Statement, commonly known by its form designation: the **DOS-1614 PCDS**. But the environmental layer—lead paint, mold, petroleum storage, radon—pulls in federal law and multiple state agencies simultaneously.

This post breaks down what agents and brokers must track, where compliance failures hit hardest, and how to audit your files before they become liability time bombs.

## The DOS-1614 PCDS: Structure, Scope, and the $500 Credit

The Property Condition Disclosure Statement (DOS-1614) is a 48-question form that covers structural systems, mechanical systems, environmental hazards, and land-use issues. Sellers must complete it and deliver it to the buyer or the buyer's agent before the buyer signs a binding contract of sale. If the seller refuses to complete the form, the statute requires a $500 credit to the buyer at closing.

**Most sellers in New York opt for the $500 credit, and this has been the market norm since the statute took effect.** The credit is not a penalty—it is an explicitly permitted alternative under Section 462. However, the credit does not immunize the seller from common-law fraud claims if they actively conceal a known defect.

Agents often misread this dynamic. The fact that a seller chose the credit does not eliminate your duty to disclose material facts you personally know about the property. Section 443-a of the Real Property Law (the agency disclosure statute) still requires agents to act honestly and in good faith.

## Environmental Disclosures That Layer on Top

Beyond the PCDS, several environmental disclosures apply depending on property characteristics. The most common are lead-based paint, underground storage tanks, flood zone status, and proximity to contaminated sites.

| Disclosure Type | Triggering Condition | Governing Law | Form/Document |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lead-Based Paint | Pre-1978 construction | 42 U.S.C. 4852d; 24 CFR 35 | EPA disclosure form + pamphlet |
| Flood Zone | Property in FEMA-designated zone | NY General Obligations Law 5-331 | Seller certification on contract rider |
| Underground Storage Tanks | Known tank on property | PCDS Q10; NYS DEC regs | DOS-1614 Question 10 |
| Mold | Known presence | No standalone NY statute | PCDS Q12 |
| Superfund/Brownfield Proximity | Listed DEC or EPA site | CERCLA; NY ECL Article 27 | No mandated form; material fact doctrine |

For properties built before 1978, the federal lead-based paint disclosure is non-negotiable. Both the listing agent and the seller must provide the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" and complete the lead disclosure addendum before the buyer becomes obligated under a contract. This requirement exists regardless of whether the seller opts out of the PCDS with the $500 credit.

Flood zone disclosure under General Obligations Law Section 5-331 requires the seller to disclose if the property is in a FEMA Special Flood Hazard Area, if the seller has received federal flood disaster assistance, and whether flood insurance is currently in effect. The disclosure must be made in the contract of sale or in a separate document before signing. For agents working in [areas affected by evolving climate risk](/blog/florida-property-condition-disclosures-2026), comparing multi-state flood requirements is increasingly relevant.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The liability exposure for non-compliance varies by which disclosure was missed and who missed it.

**If a seller fails to deliver the PCDS or the $500 credit, the buyer can revoke the contract.** Section 465 gives the buyer the right to rescind prior to closing if neither the completed PCDS nor the credit was provided. Courts have upheld this remedy even where the buyer learned of no actual defect—the statutory violation alone is sufficient.

For lead paint violations, the penalties are federal and severe. The EPA can impose fines up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2025). In private litigation, buyers have recovered treble damages. Both the seller and the agent can be named as respondents.

The New York Department of State (DOS) can also discipline licensees under 19 NYCRR 175.21 for "demonstrating untrustworthiness or incompetence." A pattern of missing disclosures—or a single egregious failure—can result in license suspension, revocation, or fines up to $1,000 per violation.

| Violation | Potential Consequence | Who Bears Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Missing PCDS without $500 credit | Buyer contract rescission | Seller; agent may face DOS complaint |
| Lead paint disclosure omission | Up to $19,507 fine per violation; treble damages | Seller and listing agent jointly |
| Failure to disclose known flood zone | Fraud claim; contract rescission | Seller; agent if aware |
| Concealing known UST | Common-law fraud damages | Seller; agent if complicit |
| DOS license violation | Fine up to $1,000; suspension/revocation | Licensed agent or broker |

> "A real estate licensee who has personal knowledge of a condition that materially affects the value or desirability of residential property has an independent duty to disclose that condition, regardless of whether the seller has elected the $500 credit." — NY DOS Guidance, 19 NYCRR 175.21

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With New York Disclosures

Five errors show up repeatedly in DOS complaints, litigation filings, and transaction audits.

**First, agents assume the $500 credit eliminates all disclosure obligations.** It does not. The credit replaces only the PCDS itself. Federal lead paint disclosures, flood zone disclosures, and the agent's own duty to disclose known material facts survive intact. Agents who treat the credit as a universal waiver are setting up their sellers—and themselves—for fraud claims.

Second, agents forget to secure the lead paint disclosure before contract execution. In New York's attorney-review market, many contracts go out for signature quickly. If the lead disclosure addendum is not attached to the initial contract package—or executed as a standalone document before the buyer signs—the transaction is in technical violation from day one. Review how other states handle similar timing requirements by looking at [Virginia's approach to lead paint disclosure](/blog/virginia-lead-paint-disclosure-2026).

Third, agents rely on the seller's verbal representation about property age rather than verifying construction date through public records. If a property was built before 1978 and the agent fails to provide the lead disclosure because the seller "said it was built in 1980," the agent shares liability for the omission.

Fourth, listing agents fail to document delivery of the PCDS. Section 462 requires delivery to the buyer or buyer's agent, but agents rarely retain proof of delivery. Without a signed acknowledgment of receipt, a buyer can claim they never received the form and seek rescission.

Fifth, agents conflate "as-is" sales with no-disclosure sales. An as-is clause shifts repair responsibility but does not eliminate disclosure requirements. The PCDS (or $500 credit) is still required for residential one-to-four family transactions, and environmental disclosures remain mandatory regardless of as-is language in the contract.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under NY Real Property Law Section 441(1). If an agent in your office consistently fails to secure disclosures, the DOS can charge the broker with failure to supervise—even if the broker never saw the deficient file.

**Every broker should have a file review protocol that checks for three documents before contract execution: the PCDS or $500 credit election, the lead paint disclosure (if applicable), and flood zone certification.** These three items are the minimum compliance floor.

Build a checklist into your transaction management system that flags files missing any of these documents at the contract stage. If you are still relying on agents to self-audit, you are accepting risk you cannot control. Agents managing [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) are especially prone to missing disclosure deadlines simply because volume overwhelms manual tracking.

Brokers should also conduct quarterly spot audits of closed files. Pull ten random transactions per quarter and verify that each file contains a signed PCDS acknowledgment (or documented $500 credit on the closing statement), a lead paint disclosure with buyer and seller signatures for any pre-1978 property, and evidence of flood zone disclosure where applicable.

Train new agents on the distinction between the PCDS credit and the agent's independent disclosure duty. This is the single most misunderstood concept among newly licensed agents in New York, and it creates outsized risk for the brokerage.

## The Intersection of Caveat Emptor and Statutory Disclosure

New York still adheres to the doctrine of caveat emptor in residential real estate—buyer beware. Courts have consistently held that sellers have no common-law duty to disclose defects unless they actively conceal them, make affirmative misrepresentations, or have a fiduciary relationship with the buyer. The PCDS statute was intended to soften this harsh rule, but the $500 opt-out largely preserved the status quo.

**This creates a peculiar compliance environment: statutory disclosure exists on paper, but market practice overwhelmingly favors non-disclosure via the credit.** Agents must understand that even in this caveat emptor framework, active concealment remains actionable fraud. If your seller plasters over a crack to hide foundation damage before showings, both the seller and any agent who knew about it face liability.

The [New York Department of State Division of Licensing Services](https://dos.ny.gov/licensing) publishes updated guidance and disciplinary actions that agents should review at least annually. Pay particular attention to cases where agents were disciplined for knowledge-based disclosure failures—these precedents define the boundary of your obligations.
