## Indiana Building Code Updates 2026: The Shift That Matters for Your Transactions

The Indiana Fire Prevention and Building Safety Commission formally adopted revisions to the state's residential building standards effective January 1, 2026. These Indiana building code updates 2026 incorporate portions of the 2021 International Residential Code (IRC) and introduce tighter requirements for energy efficiency, electrical systems, and egress. For agents and brokers, the changes touch everything from disclosure obligations to inspection objections.

Indiana does not leave code adoption to individual counties the way some states do. Under Indiana Code Title 22, Article 13 (IC 22-13), the Commission holds authority over uniform building standards statewide. That centralized system means one rule change ripples across all 92 counties simultaneously. Agents who assume "my market won't be affected" are already behind.

## What Actually Changed in the 2026 Code Adoption

The Commission's rulemaking under 675 IAC 14 updated several residential construction sections. The most transaction-relevant changes fall into three categories: energy envelope requirements, electrical panel specifications, and basement egress dimensions. Each one has a direct line to your listing descriptions, your inspection negotiations, and your disclosure reviews.

**Energy envelope upgrades now require R-49 attic insulation and R-21 wall insulation for new construction and major renovations in Climate Zone 5, which covers nearly all of Indiana.** Previously, R-38 attic insulation satisfied the standard. Buyers purchasing new-build homes should expect to see these specs reflected in builder warranties, and agents listing renovated properties need to confirm whether the work was permitted under the new or old standard.

Electrical panels must now accommodate 200-amp minimum service in homes over 2,000 square feet—up from the prior 150-amp threshold for comparable homes. Basement egress windows must provide a minimum net clear opening of 5.7 square feet, aligning with the 2021 IRC's updated table R310.2.1. Finished basements that were converted without permits before 2026 may not meet this threshold, creating a disclosure flashpoint.

## How These Updates Connect to Seller Disclosures

Indiana's Residential Real Estate Sales Disclosure Form (State Form 46234, revised by the Indiana Association of Realtors) requires sellers to identify known material defects. The form's Section 5 covers structural components, Section 6 addresses mechanical systems, and Section 8 deals with other known issues. None of these sections explicitly ask "Does this home comply with the 2026 building code?" But that is exactly the wrong question.

The right question is whether the seller knows about unpermitted work that now conflicts with current code. Under IC 32-21-5-7, sellers must disclose conditions they are aware of. Agents who represent sellers have an independent duty under 876 IAC 8-2-4 not to misrepresent or conceal material facts they personally know. If you walked the property and noticed a basement bedroom with a window that clearly falls short of egress standards, you cannot simply stay silent because the seller checked "no" on the form.

This duty intensifies when the property includes recent renovations. A seller who finished a basement in 2025 under the previous code likely pulled a permit that satisfied the old egress standard. But a seller who finished that same basement without a permit in 2025 may now face scrutiny under the updated standard if a buyer's inspector flags the deficiency.

## Indiana Building Code Updates 2026: Common Mistakes Agents Make

Mistakes in this area tend to cluster around five patterns. Recognizing them in advance is far cheaper than defending against them in a licensing complaint or civil suit.

**First, agents frequently confuse "grandfathered" with "compliant."** A home built in 1998 does not need to meet 2026 standards unless a renovation triggers the updated code. But agents sometimes tell buyers "it's grandfathered" without checking whether unpermitted improvements were made after the code changed. That casual reassurance can become Exhibit A in a misrepresentation claim.

Second, agents fail to verify permit status on recent improvements. The Indiana Department of Homeland Security maintains the statewide permit database, but local building departments hold the detailed records. A five-minute call to the local office can confirm whether that kitchen remodel or basement finish was permitted. Skipping that step before listing a property with visible improvements is a preventable error.

Third, listing agents describe features in MLS that imply code compliance without verification. Writing "finished basement with bedroom" implies legal habitable space. If that bedroom lacks compliant egress under the 2026 standard and was finished without a permit, the listing description becomes evidence of misleading marketing under 876 IAC 8-2-4.

Fourth, buyer's agents fail to advise clients that inspection findings related to code may affect insurability or financing. An appraiser who notes non-compliant electrical service may trigger lender requirements that delay or kill closing. Agents who [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) on inspection objections because they underestimated the code issue lose negotiating position entirely.

Fifth, agents confuse state code with local amendments. Cities like Indianapolis, Fort Wayne, and Evansville maintain municipal amendments to the state code under their authority per IC 36-7-2. An agent practicing across county lines must check local amendments, not just the statewide baseline.

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When You Get It Wrong

The Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC) has authority under IC 25-34.1-6-11 to discipline licensees for incompetence, negligence, or failure to disclose. Sanctions range from written reprimand to license revocation, with fines up to $10,000 per violation. Civil liability runs parallel—buyers can sue for rescission under IC 32-21-5-11 if they can show the agent knew about a material code deficiency and concealed it.

| Violation Type | Regulatory Consequence | Civil Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Failure to disclose known code violation | IREC fine up to $10,000; license suspension | Rescission of sale; compensatory damages |
| Misrepresenting permit status in MLS | IREC reprimand or suspension | Fraud claim; punitive damages possible |
| Advising buyer that property is "grandfathered" without verification | IREC finding of negligence | Negligent misrepresentation; buyer's repair costs |
| Failing to recommend inspection for code compliance | IREC finding of incompetence | Breach of fiduciary duty claim |

Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk. Indiana Purchase Agreement Form 44 (IAR Standard Form) includes inspection contingency language that allows buyers to terminate based on material defects discovered during the inspection period. When a code violation surfaces—especially one the listing agent should have flagged—the buyer walks, and the seller's agent faces a furious client asking why this wasn't caught earlier.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under IC 25-34.1-6-11(a)(10) for failing to exercise reasonable supervision over their licensees. That statute does not require the broker to personally inspect every listing, but it does demand systems that catch foreseeable compliance gaps.

**Every managing broker in Indiana should audit three specific areas before Q3 2026.** First, review your office's standard listing checklist to confirm it includes a permit verification step for any property with visible improvements less than 10 years old. Second, update your MLS listing review process to flag descriptions that imply code-compliant bedrooms, electrical upgrades, or energy features without supporting documentation. Third, ensure your agents have completed continuing education on disclosure obligations—the 2026 CE cycle includes an approved course from IAR titled "Material Facts and Code Compliance."

| Audit Area | Action Item | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Listing checklists | Add permit verification for renovated properties | Once (update template) |
| MLS description review | Flag compliance-implying language without documentation | Every listing |
| Disclosure form review | Confirm Section 5, 6, and 8 answers match agent's knowledge | Every listing |
| CE tracking | Verify agents completed code-related CE module | Annual |
| Transaction file review | Spot-check inspection response timelines | Monthly |

Brokers who rely on [checklists rather than memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) will catch these issues at the file-review stage rather than at the complaint stage. The cost of prevention is measured in minutes; the cost of a licensing complaint is measured in months and legal fees.

## Practical Steps for Agents Listing Renovated Properties

When you take a listing on a home with improvements completed after 2020, pull the permit history before you draft the MLS description. Contact the local building department—not just the county assessor—because assessed improvements and permitted improvements are two different datasets. If the seller tells you they "did it themselves," that is your cue to verify whether a permit was required under the old or new code.

Document your verification in the transaction file. A one-line note—"Called Marion County Building Dept 7/15/2026, confirmed no permit on file for basement finish"—protects you if the transaction goes sideways later. The [transaction coordination process](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) should include this step as a standard milestone for any renovated property.

On the buyer side, advise your clients that homes with post-2020 renovations should receive a code-focused inspection, not just a general home inspection. Indiana does not require code compliance inspections for resale transactions, but recommending one—and documenting that recommendation in writing—insulates you from claims that you failed to advise.

## The Intersection of Code Updates and Insurance or Financing

Lenders and insurers are already adjusting their requirements in response to the 2026 code changes. FHA appraisers in Indiana have started flagging non-compliant egress in finished basements, which can trigger a "subject to" appraisal requiring correction before closing. VA appraisals follow similar protocols under the Minimum Property Requirements.

**Agents who discover a code issue mid-transaction need to act within the inspection objection period specified in the IAR Purchase Agreement (typically 10 business days from acceptance unless modified).** Missing that window means the buyer loses the contractual right to request repair or terminate. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) often comes back to timing failures on exactly this kind of issue.

Homeowner's insurance carriers in Indiana are beginning to exclude coverage for losses arising in non-code-compliant spaces. A basement bedroom fire in a room without proper egress could result in a denied claim—and a buyer who later discovers the agent knew about the egress deficiency has a clear path to litigation.
