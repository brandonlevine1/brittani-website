## Understanding Texas Escrow Regulations 2026 at the Statutory Level

If you close transactions in Texas, the rules governing earnest money, trust accounts, and escrow disbursement sit inside a web of statutes, commission rules, and contract language that changed more than most agents realize heading into 2026. Texas escrow regulations 2026 carry enforcement teeth that go well beyond a slap on the wrist—license revocation, civil suits, and deal-killing disputes all trace back to mishandled escrow. This post breaks down exactly where agents trip up, what brokers must audit, and how the regulatory framework actually works in practice.

The primary authority is the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC), operating under the Texas Occupations Code, Title 7, Chapter 1101. Subchapter O, Section 1101.654, prohibits commingling and conversion of escrow funds. TREC Rule 535.146 further specifies the operational requirements for trust accounts, including recordkeeping, authorized signers, and reconciliation frequency.

Agents often assume the title company handles everything. That assumption is dangerous because the listing agent or buyer's agent who drafts contract terms around earnest money bears a separate, independent obligation to ensure those terms comply with commission rules and the promulgated contract forms.

## The TREC Promulgated Contract Forms That Control Escrow

**Every residential deal in Texas starts with a promulgated contract form, and escrow provisions are baked directly into the document.** TREC Form 20-17 (One to Four Family Residential Contract) is the workhorse. Paragraph 5 addresses earnest money amounts and delivery timelines, while Paragraph 18 names the escrow agent and sets out disbursement authority.

TREC Form 38-8 (Addendum for Release of Earnest Money) is the form both parties sign when they agree to release funds after a terminated contract. Without dual signatures on 38-8, the escrow agent has no authority to disburse—period. Agents who pressure a title company to release funds without this form expose themselves to a TREC complaint.

The amendment form, TREC 39-9, can modify escrow terms mid-transaction if both parties consent. Any verbal agreement to change earnest money amounts or deadlines means nothing unless it appears in writing on an approved form or an addendum that complies with Rule 535.146(c)(3).

## Deposit Deadlines and the Two-Day Rule

TREC's position is unambiguous: earnest money must be deposited by the close of the second working day after the escrow agent receives the funds. The contract's Paragraph 5 language reinforces this, and the 2026 updates to the TREC FAQ clarify that "working day" excludes Saturdays, Sundays, and federal holidays observed by the depository institution.

| Scenario | Deadline to Deposit | Governing Rule |
|----------|-------------------|----------------|
| Contract executed Monday, funds received Monday | Wednesday close of business | Rule 535.146 |
| Contract executed Friday, funds received Friday | Tuesday close of business | Rule 535.146 |
| Funds received on a federal holiday | Second working day after next business day | TREC FAQ 2026 |
| Parties agree in writing to extend | Per written agreement | Paragraph 5, Form 20-17 |
| Wire transfer pending | Deposit upon receipt of cleared funds | Rule 535.146(b) |

Failing this deadline does not automatically void the contract, but it gives the non-depositing party grounds to terminate under the contract's default provisions. It also triggers potential TREC disciplinary action against the license holder responsible for the trust account.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Texas Escrow

**The errors that generate the most TREC complaints are not exotic—they are mundane failures of process.** Here are five specific mistakes that surface repeatedly in enforcement actions.

First, agents hold checks in their desk drawer instead of delivering them immediately to the escrow agent. Even one extra day of holding a check can violate the deposit timeline once the escrow agent receives it late. The agent's duty is to transmit the funds, not warehouse them.

Second, agents use the wrong form to document an earnest money dispute. When a deal falls apart and the parties disagree about who gets the earnest money, some agents draft a freeform letter instead of using TREC Form 38-8. That freeform letter creates ambiguity the title company cannot act on, which stalls the funds and can lead to interpleader litigation—costing everyone time and money.

Third, agents fail to specify the correct escrow agent in the contract. If the named entity in Paragraph 18 does not match the actual title company holding funds, the mismatch can delay closing or create a cloud over disbursement authority. This happens most often when agents copy a previous contract template without updating the escrow agent field.

Fourth, agents verbally instruct the title company to release funds before receiving the signed release form. TREC considers this an unauthorized direction, and the title company's compliance officer will flag it. The agent's file then contains evidence of a rule violation should any party later file a complaint.

Fifth, agents do not confirm that their broker's trust account meets TREC's reconciliation requirements. If the brokerage holds escrow (rather than a title company), [Rule 535.146 requires monthly reconciliation](https://www.trec.texas.gov/agency-topics/trust-accounts) of the trust account with a written record. Agents who bring deals into a brokerage with a non-compliant trust account inherit that risk.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry the heaviest regulatory burden. TREC Rule 535.2 makes the broker responsible for the acts of their sponsored agents, and escrow handling falls squarely within that scope. A broker who ignores trust-account hygiene is not just risking the firm's license—they are risking personal liability under Section 1101.654.

**Monthly trust-account reconciliations are not optional; they are an express TREC requirement with documentation standards.** The reconciliation must compare the account balance to the sum of individual client ledgers, and any discrepancy must be resolved within one business day of discovery. Brokers should retain reconciliation records for at least four years, matching TREC's statute of limitations for disciplinary proceedings.

Brokers also need to audit contract files for proper form usage. A quarterly review that checks for signed TREC Form 38-8 on every terminated deal, correct escrow agent identification in Paragraph 18, and timely deposit documentation will catch most problems before they become complaints. If your brokerage handles a high volume of transactions, this audit process [can quickly overwhelm even experienced teams](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) without systematic tracking.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | TREC Authority |
|-------------------|-----------|----------------|
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | Rule 535.146 |
| Deposit timeline verification | Per transaction | Rule 535.146(b) |
| Form 38-8 on terminated deals | Per termination | TREC Forms Advisory |
| Authorized signer list update | Quarterly or upon personnel change | Rule 535.146(a) |
| Record retention (4-year minimum) | Ongoing | Section 1101.652(b)(22) |

## Liability When Compliance Fails

The consequences of escrow violations in Texas operate on multiple tracks simultaneously. TREC can impose administrative penalties up to $5,000 per violation under Section 1101.702. Suspension or revocation of a license is on the table for willful or repeated violations, and TREC publishes enforcement actions in its quarterly bulletin—meaning your reputation takes a public hit.

Beyond TREC, the parties to the transaction can pursue civil claims. A buyer who loses earnest money due to unauthorized disbursement can sue the agent and broker for breach of fiduciary duty, conversion, or negligence. Texas courts have awarded actual damages plus attorney's fees in these cases, and some fact patterns support claims for exemplary damages under Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Section 41.003.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate practical consequence. If the escrow agent cannot verify proper authorization or form compliance, they will freeze the funds. A frozen escrow creates a chain reaction: closing cannot occur, lenders pull rate locks, and both parties lose confidence. ## Wire Fraud and Verification Obligations in 2026

Texas escrow practices now intersect with an escalating wire fraud environment. While TREC does not prescribe a specific wire verification protocol, the commission's 2025 advisory bulletin urged all licensees to adopt callback verification procedures when disbursing or receiving wired earnest money. Several title companies in Dallas-Fort Worth and Austin now require agents to confirm wiring instructions through a separate, verified phone number before initiating transfers.

Agents who forward unverified wiring instructions to clients—even by simply passing along an email from the title company—expose themselves to liability if those instructions turn out to be fraudulent. The safest practice is to never transmit wiring details via email and instead direct clients to contact the title company directly using a phone number obtained from a known, verified source.
