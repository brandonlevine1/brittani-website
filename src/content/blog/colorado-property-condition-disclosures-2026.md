# Colorado Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026: What Every Agent Must Get Right

Colorado's approach to property condition and environmental disclosures stands apart from most states. Understanding Colorado property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 requirements is not optional—it is the difference between a clean closing and a license complaint. The state's "buyer beware" doctrine still applies in many respects, but that does not absolve agents of specific statutory and contractual duties.

This post breaks down the exact forms, legal obligations, and enforcement consequences that Colorado licensees face when handling disclosures on residential transactions this year.

## Colorado's Disclosure Framework: Not What You Think

**Colorado is one of the few states without a blanket statutory requirement that sellers complete a property condition disclosure form.** That surprises agents who relocate from states like Virginia or Washington where disclosure is mandated by statute. Colorado instead relies on a combination of common law fraud principles, specific environmental statutes, and contractual provisions built into Colorado Real Estate Commission (CREC) approved forms.

The absence of a mandatory disclosure statute does not mean sellers and agents can stay silent. Under C.R.S. § 38-35.7-101 (the "Stigmatized Property" statute) and C.R.S. § 25-18.5-103 (methamphetamine contamination), specific conditions carry affirmative disclosure duties. Add federal lead-paint requirements under 42 U.S.C. § 4852d, and you have a patchwork system that demands careful attention.

Colorado courts have repeatedly held that a seller's failure to disclose known material defects—even absent a statutory mandate—constitutes fraud or negligent misrepresentation. The landmark case *Clements v. CREC* reinforced that licensees who assist in concealment face discipline under CREC Rule 6.17.

## The Forms That Matter: Numbers Every Colorado Agent Should Know

The Colorado Real Estate Commission publishes standardized forms through its approved forms process. Here are the disclosure-related forms relevant to residential transactions in 2026:

| Form | Number | Purpose | When Required |
|------|--------|---------|---------------|
| Seller's Property Disclosure (Residential) | SPD19 | Property condition disclosure | Contractually referenced; not legally mandated |
| Seller's Property Disclosure (Land/Farm) | SPD45 | Condition disclosure for vacant land | Contractually referenced; not legally mandated |
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (Sale) | LPD19 | Federal lead paint compliance | Pre-1978 homes; federally mandated |
| Square Footage Disclosure | SFD19 | Measurement methodology notice | Best practice; protects against misrepresentation |
| Source of Water Addendum | SWA19 | Well, cistern, or shared water | Required when property lacks municipal water |

The Contract to Buy and Sell Real Estate (CBS) in both its residential (CBS1) and land versions (CBS5) contains built-in provisions referencing these disclosures. Section 10 of the CBS specifically addresses property disclosure, giving buyers a right to receive the SPD and creating a termination right during the inspection period if material information is withheld.

Agents should also know that CREC Commission Rule 6.12 requires licensees to disclose adverse material facts actually known to them, regardless of whether the seller fills out the SPD. This rule functions as the agent's independent disclosure obligation.

## Environmental Disclosures Unique to Colorado

Colorado's geography and history create environmental disclosure issues you will not find in most other states. Mining activity, radioactive soil, methamphetamine labs, and oil and gas operations all generate disclosure obligations that trip up even experienced agents.

**Methamphetamine contamination disclosure is mandatory under C.R.S. § 25-18.5-103.** If a property was used as a meth lab or has tested positive for contamination above allowable levels set by the Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment (CDPHE), the seller must disclose this fact. The property cannot legally be inhabited until it meets CDPHE decontamination standards. Agents who know about contamination and fail to disclose face both CREC discipline and civil liability.

Radon is another Colorado-specific concern. While no state statute mandates radon testing, the EPA identifies large portions of Colorado as Zone 1 (highest potential), and CREC expects agents to recommend testing. The [Colorado Department of Public Health and Environment](https://cdphe.colorado.gov/radon) provides guidance on testing protocols that agents should reference when advising clients.

Oil and gas well proximity disclosures have gained prominence as Front Range development pushes into areas with active drilling. The Surface Use Agreement provisions and Colorado Oil and Gas Conservation Commission (COGCC) setback rules create situations where a buyer's intended use may conflict with mineral rights. Agents should flag COGCC records when properties sit within 2,000 feet of active or planned wells.

## Liability When Disclosures Go Wrong

The consequences for disclosure failures in Colorado fall into three categories: regulatory discipline, civil liability, and transactional collapse.

**CREC can suspend or revoke a license under C.R.S. § 12-10-217 for failure to disclose adverse material facts.** The Commission's disciplinary actions over the past three years show that disclosure violations rank among the top five complaint categories. Fines can reach $2,500 per violation, and the Commission has authority to require additional education or supervised practice.

Civil liability exposure is where the real financial pain hits. Colorado courts award compensatory damages for fraud and negligent misrepresentation tied to disclosure failures. In cases involving environmental contamination, remediation costs alone can exceed six figures. Agents and their employing brokers can be named as defendants under respondeat superior theories.

| Consequence | Trigger | Severity |
|-------------|---------|----------|
| CREC fine | Failure to disclose known adverse material fact | Up to $2,500 per violation |
| License suspension | Pattern of non-disclosure or intentional concealment | 1 month to permanent revocation |
| Civil damages | Buyer suffers loss from undisclosed condition | Actual damages + potential attorney fees |
| Contract rescission | Material misrepresentation discovered post-closing | Full unwinding of transaction |
| E&O claim | Failure to follow standard of care | Policy limits; increased premiums |

Deal cancellation during the inspection period is the most common immediate consequence. When buyers discover undisclosed conditions, they exercise their termination rights under CBS Section 10.6, and the transaction dies. The listing agent then faces a seller who wants to know why their deal fell apart—and often a CREC complaint from the buyer's agent.

## Five Common Mistakes Colorado Agents Make on Disclosures

First, agents assume the seller's refusal to complete the SPD eliminates all disclosure risk. It does not. CREC Rule 6.12 places an independent duty on the licensee to disclose adverse material facts they actually know. If a listing agent observes water damage, foundation cracks, or evidence of contamination during a listing appointment, they must disclose that information regardless of what the seller writes—or refuses to write—on the SPD.

Second, agents conflate "as-is" sales with no-disclosure sales. Selling a property "as-is" shifts repair responsibility but does not eliminate disclosure duties. Colorado courts have been explicit on this point. An as-is clause in the CBS does not override the duty to disclose known material defects.

Third, listing agents fail to update disclosures when conditions change between listing and closing. If a pipe bursts in week three of a 45-day contract period, the seller has a duty to amend the SPD. Agents who know about the new condition and stay silent expose themselves to discipline.

Fourth, agents on both sides mishandle the lead-based paint disclosure timeline. The federal requirement mandates that buyers receive the disclosure and a 10-day opportunity to conduct testing before becoming obligated under contract. Agents who rush this—or allow it to be signed at closing rather than before mutual execution—violate 42 U.S.C. § 4852d. Penalties reach $19,507 per violation under current HUD enforcement guidelines. For a deeper dive into lead paint compliance approaches across states, see our post on [Virginia lead paint disclosure requirements](/blog/virginia-lead-paint-disclosure-2026).

Fifth, agents neglect environmental disclosures for properties in areas with historical mining activity. Counties like Clear Creek, Gilpin, and Teller have extensive mining histories that can result in subsidence, heavy metal contamination, and abandoned mine openings on or near residential properties. The SPD asks about these conditions, but agents often fail to research county records that would reveal known hazards.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Employing brokers carry supervisory liability under C.R.S. § 12-10-201 for their agents' disclosure compliance. A broker who fails to implement reasonable systems for disclosure oversight can face independent discipline—even if the individual agent made the error.

**Brokers should conduct file audits on every transaction to confirm the following documents are present and properly executed.** The SPD (or documented seller refusal) should appear in the file before the inspection deadline passes. The lead-based paint disclosure for pre-1978 properties must be signed and dated prior to contract execution, not stuffed in at closing. Any environmental disclosures specific to the property—meth testing results, radon reports, COGCC proximity notices—should be documented and delivered to the buyer within the inspection timeline.

Training is the other half of the equation. CREC requires 24 hours of continuing education per three-year renewal cycle, but brokers should go beyond the minimum. Quarterly disclosure refreshers that cover recent CREC disciplinary actions give agents concrete examples of what goes wrong. Brokers should also maintain a disclosure checklist specific to their market area. A brokerage operating in the Denver metro needs different environmental flags than one serving mountain communities or the Eastern Plains. Generic compliance templates miss locally relevant hazards—and that is where liability creeps in.
