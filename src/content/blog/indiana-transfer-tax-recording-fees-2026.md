## Understanding Indiana Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Getting the numbers wrong on transfer taxes or recording fees in Indiana does not just embarrass you at closing—it can delay funding, expose your brokerage to liability, and leave your client covering unexpected costs out of pocket. The Indiana transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 remain governed by a patchwork of state statutes and county-level recorder policies that agents must track independently. This article breaks down the current fee structure, the forms you need to file, the penalties for non-compliance, and the mistakes that trip up even experienced licensees.

Indiana's conveyance tax has not changed its base rate since its original enactment, but supplemental county fees and recorder surcharges have shifted in recent years. Agents who rely on closing software defaults without verifying county-specific schedules risk miscalculating seller net sheets and buyer closing cost estimates by hundreds of dollars. That kind of error erodes client trust fast.

## The Statutory Framework: IC 6-9-7 and IC 36-2-7

**Indiana's real estate transfer tax—formally called the "conveyance tax"—is authorized under Indiana Code 6-9-7.** The statute permits each county to impose a tax of up to $1.00 for every $500 of consideration (or fractional part thereof) paid for real property. Every Indiana county currently imposes this tax at the maximum rate, making the effective rate $2.00 per $1,000 of sale price statewide.

Recording fees are governed separately under [IC 36-2-7-10](https://iga.in.gov/laws/2024/ic/titles/36#36-2-7-10), which sets the base fee a county recorder may charge for recording any instrument. The current base is $25 for the first page and $5 for each additional page, though many counties add surcharges authorized under IC 36-2-7-10(b) for infrastructure, document storage, or technology improvements. These surcharges vary by county and can add $5 to $20 per document.

Beyond the per-page charges, Indiana recorders also collect a mortgage recording fee and a deed recording fee that differ slightly in total because of how supplemental charges apply. Agents should confirm exact amounts with the specific county recorder's office at least 48 hours before closing, not rely on last quarter's numbers.

## Key Forms Agents Must Know

Indiana real estate closings require several state-mandated forms beyond the deed itself. Missing any one of them stalls recording.

**State Form 46021 (Sales Disclosure Form)** is required by IC 6-1.1-5.5 for virtually all transfers of real property in Indiana. Both the buyer and seller must sign it, and it must be filed with the county assessor within 30 days of the conveyance date. The assessor uses it for property tax assessment purposes, but agents bear responsibility for ensuring it gets completed at closing.

State Form 21775 (Indiana Real Estate Transfer Tax Receipt) documents that the conveyance tax has been paid. The county auditor issues this receipt, and the recorder will not accept the deed without it. If you are working with a title company, they typically handle this—but if you are managing a FSBO transaction or an attorney closing, you need to confirm it independently.

The combination of these forms, plus the deed itself (most commonly an Indiana Warranty Deed or Special Warranty Deed), constitutes the minimum recording package. Agents involved in [transactions where closing procedures are unfamiliar](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) should maintain a county-specific checklist rather than relying on memory.

## Who Pays What: Customary Allocation in Indiana

Indiana statute does not assign the conveyance tax to a specific party. Custom and contract control. In most Indiana transactions, the seller pays the transfer tax and the buyer pays recording fees for the new deed and mortgage. But this is purely negotiable.

| Cost Item | Customary Payor | Statutory Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Conveyance Tax ($2/$1,000) | Seller | No statutory mandate—contract controls |
| Deed Recording Fee | Buyer | No statutory mandate—contract controls |
| Mortgage Recording Fee | Buyer | No statutory mandate—contract controls |
| Sales Disclosure Filing | Split (both sign) | IC 6-1.1-5.5 requires both parties to sign |
| Satisfaction/Release Recording | Seller's lender | Lender obligation under IC 32-28-1 |

Agents should spell out these allocations explicitly in the purchase agreement. The Indiana Association of Realtors (IAR) purchase agreement form includes a section for closing cost allocation—use it. Leaving it blank or assuming "standard practice" invites disputes at the settlement table.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

**Failing to pay the conveyance tax before recording means the deed gets rejected—full stop.** The county recorder will not accept any conveyance instrument without the auditor's tax receipt (State Form 21775). There is no grace period and no provisional recording.

If a Sales Disclosure Form is not filed within the statutory 30-day window, IC 6-1.1-5.5-3 authorizes a penalty of $50 per day until filed, up to a maximum of $5,000. The county assessor can pursue this against either party, and in practice, the buyer often gets caught holding the penalty because the seller has already left the state or become unreachable.

Beyond statutory penalties, agents face professional liability exposure. The Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC), operating under IC 25-34.1, can take disciplinary action against a licensee who demonstrates incompetence or negligence that causes financial harm to a client. Repeatedly miscalculating transfer taxes or failing to ensure form compliance could trigger an IREC investigation, resulting in fines, mandatory education, or license suspension. Agents who struggle with [deadline tracking across multiple files](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) are statistically more likely to miss the 30-day Sales Disclosure window.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make

The first mistake is using stale fee schedules. Counties update surcharges through ordinance, not through statewide bulletins. An agent who closed a deal in Hamilton County six months ago and assumes the same recording fee applies today may find the title company short at the recorder's window. Always pull current schedules within 72 hours of a scheduled closing.

The second mistake is omitting the fractional rule on transfer tax calculations. The statute says "$1.00 per $500 or fraction thereof." That means a sale price of $250,100 does not round down to $500 of tax—it rounds up to $502. Agents who calculate $250,000 x $2/$1,000 = $500 and stop there shortchange the auditor by $2 and create a rejected deed scenario.

**The third mistake is treating the Sales Disclosure Form as the title company's problem.** While title companies often prepare this form, agents are frequently the last point of contact with both buyer and seller. If the title company sends the form for signatures post-closing and a party ignores it, the $50/day penalty clock starts ticking. The listing agent should confirm filing within two weeks of closing.

The fourth mistake is failing to account for exempt transfers. Not every conveyance triggers the tax. Transfers between spouses, transfers into a revocable trust where the grantor is the beneficiary, and certain court-ordered transfers are exempt under IC 6-9-7. Agents who collect and remit transfer tax on an exempt transaction create a refund headache that can take months to resolve.

The fifth mistake is double-counting recording fees when a buyer is assuming an existing mortgage rather than recording a new one. If there is no new mortgage instrument to record, that fee line should be zero—but it persists on pre-populated settlement templates and confuses buyers who see an unexplained charge.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under IC 25-34.1-10 and IREC's administrative rules (856 IAC 1). If an agent under your brokerage consistently misquotes closing costs or causes penalty assessments on Sales Disclosure filings, the broker's license is exposed alongside the agent's.

**Audit every seller net sheet and buyer cost estimate your agents produce for accuracy against current county fee schedules.** Build a quarterly review process that checks at least a random 10% sample of closed files for proper Sales Disclosure filing confirmation. Request copies of the auditor's transfer tax receipt from your title partners to confirm compliance.

Brokers should also maintain a current fee schedule reference document for every county in which the brokerage operates. This does not need to be complicated—a shared spreadsheet updated quarterly by an admin or [transaction coordinator](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) keeps everyone aligned. Require agents to cite the date they last verified recording fees when submitting closing packages for review.

Finally, consider implementing a post-closing checklist that includes confirmation of Sales Disclosure filing. The 30-day deadline makes this easy to track, but only if someone is actually tracking it. Brokerages that handle high volume across multiple Indiana counties without a systematic compliance process are playing a dangerous game with IREC.

## Preparing for Potential 2026 Legislative Changes

The Indiana General Assembly periodically considers adjustments to the conveyance tax structure. Bills introduced in recent sessions have proposed both increasing the rate and redistributing revenue between counties and the state. While no rate change has been enacted for 2026 as of this writing, agents should monitor the Indiana General Assembly's legislative tracking system and IREC bulletins for mid-session amendments that could take effect after July 1.

County recorders also have authority to adjust surcharges through county council ordinance without state legislative action. Marion County, for example, updated its technology surcharge in late 2025. Agents operating in multiple counties cannot assume uniformity—even adjacent counties in the Indianapolis metro may have different total recording costs.
