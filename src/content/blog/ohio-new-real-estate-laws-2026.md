## Ohio New Real Estate Laws 2026: The Statutory Changes That Matter

The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing finalized several rule amendments in early 2026 that reshape daily practice for every active licensee in the state. These changes touch disclosure timing, form content, wire-fraud protocols, and broker supervision—and they carry real enforcement teeth. If you hold an Ohio license and have not yet read the updated Ohio Revised Code sections, the clock is running: most provisions under Ohio new real estate laws 2026 take effect July 1.

This is not a cosmetic refresh. The amended ORC 5302.30 (Residential Property Disclosure Form), the revised ORC 4735.681 (agency disclosure duties), and new provisions under OAC 1301:5-1-02 (wire instruction verification) collectively impose obligations that did not exist twelve months ago. Agents who treat these as optional paperwork updates will find themselves on the wrong side of a disciplinary hearing—or a lawsuit.

## Revised Residential Property Disclosure Form (RPDF)

**Ohio's Residential Property Disclosure Form has been the backbone of seller-side compliance since 1993, and the 2026 revision is the most substantive rewrite in over a decade.** The updated form—still governed by ORC 5302.30—adds twelve new line items addressing environmental hazards, prior insurance claims, and broadband infrastructure. The Ohio Division published the final version on March 15, 2026, giving agents roughly 100 days to retire old stock.

The new RPDF requires sellers to disclose known PFAS contamination, any mold remediation performed within the past ten years, and whether the property has ever been the subject of a homeowner's insurance non-renewal. These additions respond to litigation trends that caught the prior form off guard. Agents should note that the form number itself has not changed, but a "Rev. 2026" date stamp in the footer distinguishes it from the 2017 edition still circulating in some offices.

Failure to use the correct form version after July 1 does not merely create a paperwork headache. Under ORC 5302.30(G), a buyer who receives a materially incomplete disclosure may rescind the contract up to the point of closing. The listing agent's brokerage may face [vicarious liability if it cannot demonstrate that supervisory systems flagged the outdated form](/blog/where-deals-break-down).

## Agency Disclosure Timing Under Amended ORC 4735.681

The prior statute required agents to provide the Consumer Guide to Agency Relationships "at the earliest practicable time" during a transaction. That phrase invited interpretation—and, predictably, agents pushed it to the limits. The 2026 amendment replaces that language with a hard trigger: the guide must be delivered before the licensee engages in any substantive discussion about property value, pricing strategy, or negotiation positioning.

"Substantive discussion" is defined in the new rule text as any conversation that goes beyond general market statistics or factual property characteristics. The moment you comment on whether a listing is overpriced or advise a buyer on offer strategy, the disclosure must already be in hand. The Ohio Real Estate Commission's guidance memo (OREC-2026-04) explicitly states that a text message or email containing the guide satisfies the delivery requirement if the recipient acknowledges receipt.

**If delivery happens after the substantive discussion, the buyer or seller may void any resulting agreement within five business days of discovering the deficiency.** The agent's brokerage is jointly liable under the amended statute, and the Division may impose a fine of up to $1,000 per occurrence—separate from any civil damages.

## Wire-Fraud Prevention: New Affirmative Duties

Ohio is joining a growing cohort of states that codify wire-fraud prevention rather than leaving it to best-practice recommendations. Under the new OAC 1301:5-1-02, every licensee who coordinates a closing must verify wire instructions through a communication channel different from the one on which instructions were received. If instructions arrive by email, verification must occur by phone call to a known number—not by reply email.

The rule also mandates that brokerages maintain a written wire-fraud prevention policy, train all licensees annually on social-engineering tactics, and document each verification in the transaction file. The [wire instructions verification process](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) is no longer a suggestion from your E&O carrier; it is a regulatory requirement with audit implications.

Non-compliance here carries dual risk. The Division may discipline the licensee's license under OAC 1301:5-1-01 (general conduct standards), and a defrauded consumer may cite the agent's statutory breach as evidence of negligence per se in civil court. Damages in wire-fraud cases routinely exceed $100,000.

**Brokers cannot delegate these responsibilities to individual agents and claim ignorance.** The amended ORC 4735.18(A)(6) explicitly states that a broker's failure to supervise constitutes an independent violation, carrying penalties up to and including license revocation. Quarterly file audits—checking form versions, disclosure timestamps, and wire-verification documentation—should become standard operating procedure by Q3 2026.

The five-year retention rule deserves particular attention. Many Ohio brokerages still purge files at the three-year mark. Under the revised OAC 1301:5-1-01, the Division may request production of any transaction file up to five years after closing. Digital storage is permitted, but files must be stored in an unalterable format (PDF/A is recommended in the Commission's guidance) and be retrievable within 48 hours of a Division request.

## Common Mistakes Agents Will Make Under the 2026 Rules

**First, agents will continue using the 2017 RPDF form past the July 1 cutoff.** This happens every time Ohio updates a mandatory form. Agents who rely on pre-printed stock or outdated templates in their CRM will not notice the missing line items until a buyer's attorney flags the deficiency. The fix is simple: purge every old-version form from your systems before June 30.

Second, agents will misread the substantive-discussion trigger on agency disclosure. Telling a buyer at an open house that "this home is priced well for the neighborhood" constitutes a substantive discussion under the new definition. If you have not already handed them the Consumer Guide, you have violated the statute. The safest practice is to hand the guide to every prospective client at first contact, period.

Third, agents will treat wire-verification as someone else's job. The statute assigns the duty to the licensee coordinating closing—which, in many transactions, is the listing agent or the agent's TC. Delegating to a title company does not relieve the licensee's obligation unless the brokerage's written policy explicitly designates the title company as the verification agent and the title company acknowledges that role in writing.

Fourth, agents will fail to timestamp agency-disclosure delivery. The new rule's five-business-day rescission window starts from the date the consumer discovers the deficiency, not the date of the discussion. Without a written acknowledgment showing exactly when delivery occurred, the agent has no defense. Use email with read receipts or a signed acknowledgment form—Ohio does not prescribe a specific form number for this, so brokerages should create their own.

Fifth, agents will forget that the five-year retention rule applies retroactively to files closed after July 1, 2023. If you closed a deal in September 2023 and shredded the file at the three-year mark (September 2026), you have violated the new rule. Brokerages should immediately flag all files from July 2023 forward for extended retention.

## Practical Steps for Agents Before July 1

The Ohio Division of Real Estate publishes all updated forms and guidance memos on its [official website](https://com.ohio.gov/divisions-and-programs/real-estate-and-professional-licensing). Download the Rev. 2026 RPDF directly from that page—do not rely on third-party template providers who may lag behind the official release.

Build a pre-contact checklist that includes agency-disclosure delivery as step one. Before you discuss pricing, condition, or strategy with any prospective client, confirm in writing that you have delivered the Consumer Guide. This single habit eliminates the most common compliance failure under the new timing rule.

For wire verification, draft a one-page protocol and tape it next to your monitor. The protocol should specify: receive instructions, call the sender at a verified phone number, confirm dollar amount and routing number verbally, and document the call with date, time, and the name of the person who confirmed. If you [manage multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously, systematizing this step prevents the kind of slip that turns a routine closing into a six-figure loss.
