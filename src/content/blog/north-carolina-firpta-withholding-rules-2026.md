## Understanding North Carolina FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

Every year a handful of North Carolina closings blow up—or worse, generate IRS transferee liability notices—because someone at the table failed to address FIRPTA. The North Carolina firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 carry real financial teeth: 15% of the gross sales price withheld at the federal level, plus North Carolina's own 4% nonresident withholding under N.C. Gen. Stat. Section 105-215.1. Agents who treat this as "the attorney's problem" are making a bet that can cost their clients tens of thousands of dollars.

FIRPTA—the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act, codified in Internal Revenue Code Section 1445—requires that when a foreign person disposes of a U.S. real property interest, the buyer must withhold and remit a percentage of the gross amount realized. The IRS does not care whether the buyer knew the seller was foreign. Liability follows the transaction, not the knowledge.

## Federal Withholding Rates and Exemptions

**The default federal FIRPTA withholding rate remains 15% of the gross sales price for 2026 transactions.** Two reduced-rate exceptions apply when the buyer intends to use the property as a personal residence. If the price is $300,000 or less, withholding drops to zero; if the price exceeds $300,000 but does not exceed $1,000,000, withholding drops to 10%.

Sellers can apply to the IRS for a withholding certificate using Form 8288-B before closing, which may reduce the amount withheld to the seller's actual expected tax liability. The application must be filed early enough to receive a determination—typically 90 days, though processing times vary. Agents who discover foreign-seller status two weeks before closing will not have time to obtain a certificate.

| Scenario | Rate | Condition |
|----------|------|-----------|
| Default | 15% of gross price | No exemption or certificate obtained |
| Buyer-residence, price $300K or less | 0% | Buyer signs affidavit of intended use |
| Buyer-residence, price $300K–$1M | 10% | Buyer signs affidavit of intended use |
| Withholding certificate granted | Reduced per IRS determination | Form 8288-B filed and approved pre-closing |
| Seller provides non-foreign affidavit | 0% | Seller certifies U.S. person status on Form W-9 or FIRPTA affidavit |

The remittance happens via IRS Form 8288, which the withholding agent (typically the closing attorney in North Carolina) files within 20 days of closing. Form 8288-A goes to the IRS and seller to document the withheld amount.

## North Carolina's Separate State-Level Withholding

North Carolina adds its own layer. Under [N.C. Gen. Stat. Section 105-215.1](https://www.ncleg.gov/EnactedLegislation/Statutes/PDF/BySection/Chapter_105/GS_105-215.1.pdf), the buyer or closing attorney must withhold 4% of the gross sales price when the seller is a nonresident of North Carolina. This applies to foreign nationals and to domestic sellers who reside in other U.S. states—a distinction many agents miss.

The state withholding is reported and remitted to the North Carolina Department of Revenue using Form NC-1099NRS within 30 days of closing. Sellers can apply for an exemption or reduced withholding by filing Form NC-NRA with the Department of Revenue before the transaction closes.

**Failing to remit the state withholding exposes the buyer to a penalty equal to 10% of the amount required to be withheld, plus interest.** The closing attorney who handled settlement may also face professional discipline if the omission resulted from negligence. North Carolina Real Estate Commission Rule 21 NCAC 58A .0112 requires brokers to account for all funds; ignoring a statutory withholding obligation conflicts with that duty.

## Forms and Filings: A Quick Reference

| Form | Filed With | Purpose | Deadline |
|------|-----------|---------|----------|

| IRS Form 8288-A | IRS + copy to seller | Statement of withholding on disposition | Filed with 8288 |
| IRS Form 8288-B | IRS | Seller's application for reduced withholding | Pre-closing (allow 90 days) |
| NC-1099NRS | NC Department of Revenue | Report NC nonresident withholding | 30 days post-closing |
| Form NC-NRA | NC Department of Revenue | Seller's request for exemption from NC withholding | Pre-closing |
| FIRPTA Non-Foreign Affidavit | Retained by buyer/closing file | Seller certifies U.S. person status | At or before closing |

Agents do not file these forms themselves—that responsibility belongs to the closing attorney or settlement agent. But agents must know which forms exist so they can flag timing issues. A listing agent who fails to surface foreign-seller status until three days before closing has created a problem no attorney can fix quickly. If you want to avoid being the agent who [blows a closing over paperwork failures](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing), treat FIRPTA identification as a listing-intake task, not a closing-week afterthought.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With FIRPTA in North Carolina

**First, agents confuse "nonresident" with "foreign person."** North Carolina's 4% withholding applies to any nonresident seller—including a U.S. citizen living in Virginia. Federal FIRPTA withholding applies only to foreign persons (non-U.S. citizens and non-resident aliens who lack green cards). Conflating these two categories causes agents to either over-flag or under-flag the issue.

Second, listing agents accept a verbal statement from the seller that they are "not foreign" without documenting it. The proper practice is to request a signed Non-Foreign Affidavit (also called a FIRPTA certificate or seller's certification) and deliver it to the closing attorney. Without that document in the file, the buyer's side has no basis to waive withholding.

Third, agents assume a foreign seller will net the same amount regardless of withholding. They list the property, negotiate a price, and only at closing does the seller learn that 19% of gross proceeds (15% federal plus 4% state) will be withheld. Sellers who needed those funds to purchase their next home—or who expected to repatriate proceeds—may refuse to close. Agents who [track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without a system for flagging compliance milestones are most vulnerable to this error.

Fourth, buyer's agents fail to advise their client about transferee liability. If the closing attorney does not withhold and remit, and the seller disappears, the IRS pursues the buyer for the full withholding amount. A buyer's agent who never mentioned FIRPTA risk may face a complaint to the North Carolina Real Estate Commission under N.C. Gen. Stat. Section 93A-6(a)(1) for unworthiness or incompetence.

Fifth, agents wait until after the due diligence period to raise FIRPTA with the other side. By then, the buyer has spent money on inspections and appraisals. Discovering a FIRPTA issue late creates pressure to close without proper compliance—or forces the buyer to walk and absorb sunk costs.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers-in-charge in North Carolina bear supervisory responsibility under Rule 21 NCAC 58A .0110. That includes reviewing transaction files for evidence that FIRPTA status was addressed. A broker who rubber-stamps files without checking for a Non-Foreign Affidavit or evidence of withholding coordination is accepting liability by omission.

**Brokers should require a FIRPTA status field on their internal listing intake form.** This single checkbox—"Is the seller a foreign person or nonresident of North Carolina?"—creates an auditable record that the question was asked. If the answer is yes, the file should contain documentation showing the closing attorney was notified and a timeline for Form 8288-B was discussed.

Quarterly file audits should specifically look for transactions involving sellers with foreign addresses, entities formed outside the U.S., or power-of-attorney closings where the seller did not appear in person. These are the transactions most likely to have FIRPTA exposure. Brokers who [train new agents on timelines and compliance triggers](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) reduce the chance of a missed withholding cascading into a Commission complaint.

Finally, brokers should maintain a written office policy on FIRPTA and nonresident withholding. The North Carolina Real Estate Commission does not mandate a specific form, but it does expect that brokers supervise agents in a manner consistent with competent practice. A written policy, distributed annually and signed by every affiliated agent, provides a defensible record if a complaint arises.

## Liability When Agents Fail to Comply

The IRS does not typically penalize listing or buyer's agents directly for FIRPTA failures—transferee liability falls on the buyer, and reporting obligations fall on the withholding agent (the attorney). But agents face indirect consequences that can be equally damaging.

A blown closing caused by late-discovered FIRPTA issues means lost commission, potential earnest money disputes, and possible arbitration claims. The [true cost of a failed closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends beyond one deal—it damages referral relationships and triggers E&O claims. If the North Carolina Real Estate Commission determines that an agent's failure to identify a known foreign seller constituted incompetence, disciplinary action under N.C. Gen. Stat. Section 93A-6 can include license suspension.

Closing attorneys may also refuse to proceed if they discover the agent withheld information about the seller's status. North Carolina is an attorney-close state, and attorneys who sense compliance exposure will halt a transaction rather than risk their own bar license. Agents who surface the issue early preserve deal momentum.

## Practical Steps for Agents Handling Foreign-Seller Transactions

Start at listing intake. Ask every seller: "Are you a U.S. citizen or permanent resident?" and "Is your primary residence in North Carolina?" Document the answers in writing. If either answer triggers withholding, notify the closing attorney immediately and discuss the timeline for any exemption applications.

During contract negotiations, account for withholding in the seller's net sheet. A foreign seller on a $600,000 transaction faces $90,000 in federal withholding and $24,000 in state withholding—$114,000 held from proceeds. If the seller's mortgage payoff leaves thin equity, the deal may not be viable without a withholding certificate reducing the amount.

Coordinate with the buyer's agent to ensure the buyer understands their transferee liability exposure. The buyer's attorney should confirm in writing that withholding will be handled at closing. This protects the buyer and creates a paper trail that protects you.
