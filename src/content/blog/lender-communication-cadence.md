# The Weekly Lender Check-In: What to Ask and When

A single missed lender update can cost you $4,200 in average commission on a dead deal, plus the 47 hours you already invested in that transaction. Mastering the weekly lender checkin what to ask and when is not optional if you are managing more than a handful of active files. According to NAR's 2024 Member Profile, 32% of delayed closings trace back to financing issues that the listing or buyer's agent discovered too late to remedy.

The math gets uglier at scale. An agent running 10 concurrent deals who skips structured lender communication loses an average of 6.5 hours per week to reactive fire drills—frantic calls after a condition surfaces 48 hours before closing, scrambling for documents the buyer forgot to upload, or renegotiating timelines because nobody flagged an appraisal gap early enough. For brokers managing teams, multiply that waste across 8 or 12 agents and you are looking at hundreds of billable hours evaporating monthly.

This post gives you a repeatable, time-blocked system for lender communication that protects your pipeline and your sanity.

## Why Most Agents Get Lender Communication Wrong

The default approach is reactive. You call the loan officer when your buyer is nervous, when the closing coordinator pings you, or when something feels off. That pattern means you only get information after problems have already metastasized.

**Reactive communication also creates liability exposure.** If a buyer misses a rate lock expiration or a condition deadline because you failed to follow up, the complaint lands on your E&O policy. The [California Department of Real Estate](https://www.dre.ca.gov/) disciplinary actions database shows dozens of annual cases where agents faced sanctions partly because they did not maintain documented communication with the lending team during escrow.

Structured weekly check-ins flip the dynamic. Instead of discovering a problem Tuesday afternoon that needed solving Monday morning, you surface risks at a fixed cadence and route them to the right person before they compound.

## The Right Day and Time for Your Lender Call

Tuesday or Wednesday between 10:00 AM and 11:30 AM local time outperforms every other slot. Monday mornings are chaos at mortgage offices—rate sheets are publishing, weekend applications are being triaged, and processors are buried. Friday afternoons are dead zones where nobody commits to new action items.

Mid-morning Tuesday gives the lender 24 hours after their Monday triage to have fresh status on your file. It also gives you the rest of the week to act on whatever surfaces. If you manage 10 deals, block 10:00 to 11:30 on Tuesday for back-to-back 8-minute calls. That is 80 minutes for 10 files—compared to the 6.5 scattered hours you would spend all week in reactive mode.

For brokers running team meetings, make Wednesday the internal pipeline review day so agents arrive with lender intel already gathered.

## The Five Questions That Actually Matter

Forget "How's it going?" or "Are we still on track?" Those questions invite vague reassurances. You need specificity. Here are the five questions—asked in order—that extract actionable intelligence in under eight minutes per file.

**Question 1: "What conditions are still outstanding, and which ones are in the borrower's control versus third-party?"** This separates things your buyer can fix tonight from things that depend on an employer, an appraiser, or a title company. It tells you where to apply pressure and where to simply monitor.

**Question 2: "Has anything changed with the rate lock, and what is the expiration date?"** Rate lock expirations are silent killers. A seven-day extension on a $450,000 loan can cost your buyer $1,800 or more. You need the exact date and the cost of extension if the timeline slips.

**Question 3: "Is the appraisal ordered, scheduled, or completed—and are there any value concerns?"** Appraisal gaps kill 11% of purchase transactions according to Fannie Mae's 2024 Loan Quality Report. Early awareness lets you prepare your seller for a renegotiation conversation or start pulling supporting comps.

Question 4: "When do you expect clear-to-close, and what is the single biggest risk to that date?" This forces the LO to commit to a timeline and name the bottleneck. Write that bottleneck down. It becomes the first thing you check next Tuesday.

Question 5: "Is there anything you need from me or my client in the next 72 hours?" This closes the loop. If the answer is nothing, great—you have documented that. If the answer is a pay stub or a gift letter, you can text your buyer before lunch.

## How to Document Each Check-In (Without Creating Extra Work)

Documentation protects you legally and operationally. But it does not need to be a novel. A three-line note per call is enough: the date, the answers to your five questions summarized in one sentence each, and any action items with owners and deadlines.

Store these notes in whatever transaction management system you already use—Dotloop, SkySlope, your CRM's notes field. The point is that they are searchable and timestamped. If a buyer later claims you did not communicate a rate lock risk, your notes prove otherwise.

**For brokers, require agents to log these notes as a condition of pipeline review.** No note, no slot on the team board. This single accountability mechanism drops deal fall-through rates dramatically. One brokerage in Phoenix reported a 23% reduction in financing-related delays within 90 days of implementing mandatory lender check-in documentation.

## Adjusting Frequency Based on Deal Stage

Not every week demands the same intensity. Here is how to modulate your cadence across a typical 30-day escrow without over-communicating or under-communicating.

Days 1 through 7: One check-in on day 3 or 4. Confirm the loan application is complete, disclosures have been issued, and the appraisal management company has received the order. This is triage—you are confirming the file is alive and moving.

Days 8 through 21: Weekly check-ins per the system above. This is the meat of underwriting. Conditions surface, employment verifications happen, and appraisals come back. Your Tuesday call matters most during this window.

Days 22 through close: Increase to twice weekly—Tuesday and Thursday. You are now in the clear-to-close window. A missed condition here means a delayed closing, and a delayed closing means you are rescheduling movers, re-coordinating utilities, and possibly losing the deal if your seller has a contingent purchase that falls apart. ## What to Do When the Lender Goes Silent

Silence is not neutral—it is a red flag. If your Tuesday call goes to voicemail and you do not get a callback by Wednesday noon, escalate immediately. Call the processor directly. If you do not have the processor's number, you have already made a mistake at the start of escrow.

**At deal inception, get three contacts: the loan officer, the processor, and the branch manager.** Store all three in your transaction file. The processor is often closer to the daily movement of your file than the LO. The branch manager is your escalation path when both go dark.

Send a brief email after two unanswered calls. Keep it factual: "I called Tuesday and Wednesday regarding the Smith file, loan number 4457821. I need a status update on outstanding conditions before EOD Thursday to protect our closing date." That email is now part of your paper trail. If the deal blows up, you have evidence of diligence. If you need to file a complaint with your state licensing board, you have documentation.

## Scaling This System Across 10 or More Active Files

At five deals, you can keep this in your head. At ten, you cannot. At fifteen, you are guaranteed to drop a ball without a system. The answer is a simple weekly prep ritual that takes twelve minutes every Monday afternoon.

Pull your active pipeline list. For each file, review your previous week's check-in notes—specifically the "biggest risk to clear-to-close" answer. Rank your files by closing date proximity. The three files closest to closing get priority slots on Tuesday morning. The rest fill in afterward.

Create a one-page call sheet with file name, LO name, LO phone number, last week's risk item, and a blank space for this week's notes. Print it or open it on a tablet. This removes all friction from the actual calling block. You sit down at 10:00 AM Tuesday, start dialing, and move through your list without context-switching to look up contact info.

For brokers building [agent accountability systems](/blog/agent-accountability-frameworks), this call sheet doubles as a coaching artifact. Review it in your one-on-one and you will instantly see which agents are proactive and which are guessing.

## When the Lender Check-In Reveals a Problem

Surfacing a problem early only matters if you act on it fast. Here is the decision tree for the three most common issues uncovered during weekly check-ins.

Appraisal comes in low: Immediately request a copy of the appraisal report. Pull three comparable sales that support the contract price. Brief your client on options—challenge, renegotiate, or cover the gap—within 24 hours. Do not wait for the lender to suggest next steps; they are not your client's advocate.

Condition requires a document the buyer cannot produce: Escalate to the LO with a direct question: "Is there an alternative document that satisfies this condition?" Often there is. A bank statement can substitute for a missing deposit slip. A CPA letter can replace a tax transcript that the IRS is slow to deliver. Your job is to ask the question the buyer does not know to ask.

Rate lock is expiring and clear-to-close is not imminent: Calculate the extension cost and present it to your buyer the same day. Do not let this linger—every day of delay increases the chance the lock expires without a decision. ## Building the Habit Permanently

Systems only work when they become automatic. Block your Tuesday 10:00 AM slot as a recurring calendar event with a 15-minute prep reminder at 9:45. Protect this time like a listing appointment. No showings, no inspections, no team meetings during this window.

After four consecutive weeks, the habit sticks. After eight weeks, you will notice your stress level around financing drops measurably because you are never more than five days from fresh intel on any file.
