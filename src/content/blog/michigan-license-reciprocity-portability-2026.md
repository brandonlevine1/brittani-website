## Why Michigan Real Estate License Reciprocity & Portability 2026 Matters Right Now

Michigan sits at the crossroads of the Midwest real estate market, sharing borders with Ohio, Indiana, and Wisconsin—and separated from Ontario, Canada by only a river. That geography makes **Michigan real estate license reciprocity & portability 2026** a topic that affects far more agents than most realize. Any licensee working referral networks across state lines, managing relocation clients, or eyeing expansion into the Detroit-Toledo or Grand Rapids-South Bend corridors needs to understand exactly where Michigan's regulatory boundaries begin.

The Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA), specifically its Bureau of Professional Licensing, governs all real estate licensure under the Occupational Code, MCL 339.2501 et seq. Unlike states that have adopted the ARELLO Cooperative Reciprocal Agreement or signed bilateral compacts, Michigan maintains an independent licensing framework. That distinction carries real consequences for agents who assume a neighboring state's license grants them any authority to practice here.

## Michigan's Position: No Full Reciprocity, Limited Portability

**Michigan does not maintain reciprocal licensing agreements with any U.S. state as of 2026.** This is a hard-stop fact that catches agents off guard, especially those licensed in states like Ohio or Illinois that do offer partial reciprocity with certain jurisdictions. The Occupational Code does not authorize LARA to recognize another state's license as a substitute for Michigan licensure.

What Michigan does offer is a waiver pathway. Under MCL 339.2504(2), an applicant who holds an active, equivalent license in another state may petition LARA to waive certain pre-licensure education requirements and the national portion of the licensing examination. The operative word is "may"—LARA retains full discretion. The state-specific exam covering Michigan law, agency relationships, and regulatory procedure is never waived.

Portability in the cooperative sense—where an agent can conduct a single transaction in another state under their home license—does not exist in Michigan. If you are not licensed by LARA and affiliated with a Michigan broker, you cannot perform licensed activities in the state. Period.

## The Application Process for Out-of-State Agents

Out-of-state agents seeking a Michigan license submit Form BCC-RE-01 (Real Estate Salesperson Application) or Form BCC-RE-02 (Real Estate Broker Application) to LARA's Bureau of Professional Licensing. The application packet must include a certified license history from the applicant's home state, fingerprint cards processed through the Michigan State Police, and proof of errors and omissions insurance meeting Michigan's minimum coverage requirements.

Applicants requesting an education waiver must also submit Form BCC-RE-09 (Request for Waiver of Pre-Licensure Education), accompanied by transcripts or certificates from their original licensing state. LARA evaluates whether the applicant's completed coursework is substantially equivalent to Michigan's 40-hour salesperson pre-licensure requirement or 90-hour broker requirement. Denials are appealable under the Michigan Administrative Procedures Act, MCL 24.201 et seq., but appeals add months to the timeline.

Once approved for examination, candidates schedule the Michigan state portion through PSI Services, the state's contracted testing vendor. The exam covers Michigan-specific statutes, the Seller Disclosure Act (MCL 565.951-565.966), and LARA's administrative rules at R 339.22101 through R 339.22801. Failing this exam means no license—regardless of how many years you have practiced elsewhere.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The penalties for practicing real estate in Michigan without proper licensure are severe and multi-layered. Under MCL 339.2512, any person who engages in licensed activity without holding a current Michigan license faces civil fines of up to $10,000 per transaction. LARA can also refer violations to the Michigan Attorney General's office for criminal prosecution as a misdemeanor.

Beyond regulatory penalties, unlicensed practice creates transactional fallout. A buyer or seller who discovers their agent was not properly licensed may seek rescission of the purchase agreement under common law fraud theories. Courts have also held that an unlicensed agent forfeits all rights to commission—meaning you could close a $500,000 deal and walk away with nothing if your licensure was defective at the time of the transaction.

Brokers face their own exposure. Under MCL 339.2512a, a supervising broker who knowingly permits an unlicensed individual to perform licensed activities is subject to license suspension or revocation. LARA's enforcement division has increased audit activity in border markets, particularly along the Michigan-Ohio corridor where cross-state practice is most common. If your brokerage handles [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) across state lines, this is a compliance risk that demands attention.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Michigan Reciprocity

**The first and most frequent error is assuming that holding a license in a bordering state provides any legal authority to practice in Michigan.** Ohio, Indiana, Wisconsin, and Minnesota have no reciprocity agreement with Michigan. Agents in markets like Toledo-Monroe, South Bend-Niles, or Duluth-Ironwood regularly cross state lines for showings and open houses without realizing they have crossed a regulatory boundary.

The second mistake involves referral fees. Some agents believe they can collect a referral fee for directing a client to a Michigan agent without holding a Michigan license. Michigan's Occupational Code does not explicitly prohibit this, but LARA's interpretive guidance (published in its 2024 FAQ bulletin) states that soliciting clients for referral to Michigan licensees may constitute "listing or offering to list" property if the out-of-state agent had prior contact with the client about a specific Michigan property. The line is blurry, and agents who guess wrong face enforcement action.

Third, agents relocating to Michigan frequently continue practicing under their former state's license during the application period, believing the pending application provides interim authority. It does not. LARA does not issue provisional or temporary licenses for real estate. Until your license number appears in the LARA License Verification portal, you cannot perform licensed activities. Fourth, some agents file Form BCC-RE-01 but neglect to simultaneously affiliate with a Michigan broker on Form BCC-RE-05 (Sponsoring Broker Certification). Without broker affiliation, the license is issued but immediately placed in inactive status—meaning you still cannot practice. This procedural misstep can cost weeks if you are [trying to meet transaction deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers operating in Michigan's border markets carry a heightened duty of supervision. LARA's administrative rules at R 339.22301 require brokers to verify the licensure status of every agent operating under their supervision before that agent engages in any licensed activity. "I didn't know" is not a defense—MCL 339.2512a imposes strict liability on brokers who fail to verify.

**Every managing broker should implement a quarterly license audit that checks three things: active status in LARA's database, current E&O insurance compliance, and proper broker affiliation on file.** For agents who hold licenses in multiple states, brokers must confirm that the agent's Michigan license is the one being used for Michigan transactions. An agent cannot use an Ohio license number on a Michigan purchase agreement, even if both licenses are active.

| Audit Item | Verification Source | Frequency |
|------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Active license status | LARA License Verification Portal | Quarterly minimum |
| E&O insurance currency | Carrier certificate on file | Annually at renewal |
| Broker affiliation (Form BCC-RE-05) | LARA records | At onboarding + annually |
| Continuing education compliance | LARA CE tracking system | Before each renewal cycle |
| Multi-state license documentation | Agent self-disclosure + state portals | At onboarding |

Brokers should also maintain a written policy addressing what happens when an agent's license lapses during a pending transaction. Michigan's administrative rules provide a 30-day grace period for renewal (R 339.22501), but during that window the agent's authority to practice is legally ambiguous. The safest approach is to [hand off transactions](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) to a fully licensed agent the moment a lapse occurs, rather than gambling on a timely renewal.

## Michigan's Relationship With the ARELLO Cooperative Agreement

The Association of Real Estate License Law Officials (ARELLO) maintains a [mutual recognition agreement framework](https://www.arello.org) that participating states can adopt to streamline cross-border licensing. Michigan has not joined this cooperative as of 2026, though LARA participated in exploratory discussions during the 2023-2024 legislative session. Senate Bill 412 (2024) proposed authorizing LARA to enter limited portability compacts with contiguous states, but the bill died in committee without a floor vote.

The practical effect is that Michigan agents looking to practice in other states must follow those states' individual requirements, and vice versa. Some states—like Georgia and Colorado—offer streamlined pathways for Michigan licensees, but Michigan does not guarantee the same treatment in return. Agents planning multi-state careers should research each target state's requirements independently rather than assuming symmetry.

This regulatory isolation has a silver lining for Michigan licensees: it reduces out-of-state competition in Michigan markets. Border agents from Ohio or Indiana cannot casually pick up a Michigan listing without going through the full application process. For agents already licensed here, that barrier protects market share—but only if you are actually compliant with your own licensing obligations.
