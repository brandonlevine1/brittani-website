## Understanding Michigan Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026

The question of Michigan who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is not merely a negotiation detail. It is a compliance issue that touches statutory obligations, contractual precision, and agent liability. Getting it wrong does not just irritate clients — it can kill deals, trigger LARA investigations, and expose brokerages to lawsuits.

Michigan's closing cost framework operates differently from many other states because certain charges are fixed by statute while others remain fully negotiable between parties. The Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) enforces licensee conduct under the Occupational Code, MCL 339.2501 et seq., and agents who misallocate or misrepresent cost responsibilities risk disciplinary proceedings. This post breaks down who actually pays what, which forms govern the allocation, and where agents consistently make avoidable errors.

## Statutory Costs: What Michigan Law Assigns by Default

Michigan imposes specific transfer taxes that have designated payers under state law. The State Real Estate Transfer Tax, codified at MCL 207.526, levies $7.50 per $1,000 of consideration and is the seller's obligation. The County Real Estate Transfer Tax under MCL 207.505 adds $1.10 per $1,000 in counties that impose it, also defaulting to the seller.

**These statutory defaults are not suggestions — they represent the baseline that applies unless the purchase agreement explicitly reallocates responsibility.** On a $350,000 sale, the seller faces $2,625 in state transfer tax and $385 in county transfer tax before any other closing costs enter the picture. Agents who fail to account for these in their listing presentations or net sheets create immediate credibility problems.

Recording fees for the deed transfer are also customarily the seller's responsibility, while recording fees for the mortgage document fall to the buyer. The Register of Deeds in each Michigan county sets these fees, and they vary — Wayne County charges differently than Traverse City's Grand Traverse County.

## The Negotiable Middle Ground

Beyond statutory obligations, most Michigan closing costs are allocated through the purchase agreement itself. The Michigan Association of Realtors Purchase Agreement (Form MAR-1020) contains specific provisions where buyer and seller responsibilities are designated line by line. Form MAR-1050 (the Addendum to Purchase Agreement) handles overflow terms when standard allocations need modification.

The title search and examination fee sits in a grey zone. Historically in Michigan, sellers provided clear title and absorbed associated costs. In 2025 and into 2026, more buyer-side requests for seller-paid title searches have emerged as part of broader concession negotiations, particularly in markets like Kalamazoo and Lansing where inventory has loosened.

## Seller Concessions and Their Limits

Seller concessions — where the seller agrees to pay a portion of the buyer's closing costs — remain one of the most common negotiation tools in Michigan transactions. These concessions appear in Section 10 of Form MAR-1020 and must specify a dollar amount or percentage. Leaving this section vague invites lender rejection and potential RESPA issues.

**Lender caps on seller concessions override whatever the parties agree to in the purchase agreement.** Conventional loans through Fannie Mae limit seller contributions to 3% for buyers putting less than 10% down, 6% for 10–25% down, and 9% for 25% or more. FHA caps at 6%. VA permits up to 4% in concessions plus payment of discount points. Agents who draft concession terms exceeding these caps [create exactly the kind of deal-breaking errors](/blog/where-deals-break-down) that send transactions sideways at the eleventh hour.

Michigan's Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act compliance (which mirrors federal RESPA) also prohibits structuring concessions as disguised kickbacks or referral fees. A seller concession that functionally reimburses a buyer for choosing a particular service provider can trigger both state and federal enforcement actions.

## Forms and Documentation Agents Must Use

Michigan agents work primarily within the MAR form ecosystem, though some brokerages use proprietary or attorney-drafted agreements. The critical forms governing closing cost allocation include:

Form MAR-1020 (Purchase Agreement) remains the primary document. Lines 116 through 135 in the current version address closing cost responsibilities, concessions, and prorations. Form MAR-1025 (Seller's Disclosure Statement) does not directly address cost allocation but intersects when inspection-related repairs create cost-shifting discussions.

Form MAR-1050 (Addendum) handles non-standard arrangements. If the parties agree that the buyer will pay the transfer tax — perfectly legal though unusual — this deviation from custom must be explicitly stated. A verbal agreement or vague reference will not survive title company review or lender underwriting scrutiny.

The Closing Disclosure (TRID) form, while federally mandated, must align with whatever the Michigan purchase agreement specifies. Discrepancies between the CD and Form MAR-1020 create delays that [blow closings apart](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) and generate complaints to LARA. Agents should compare the CD against the purchase agreement at least 48 hours before closing.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make

**First, agents routinely leave Section 10 of Form MAR-1020 blank or partially completed.** When the concession field reads "seller to contribute to buyer's closing costs" without a specific dollar figure or cap, lenders reject the contract. Title companies flag it. The fix is simple — always insert a number — but the error persists across experience levels.

Second, listing agents fail to calculate transfer taxes in their net sheet presentations. A seller expecting $340,000 net on a $370,000 sale will be short by approximately $3,178 in transfer taxes alone. When this surfaces at the closing table, the agent's competence comes into question and the seller may refuse to close.

Third, buyer's agents confuse customary practice with legal requirement. Telling a buyer "the seller always pays for the owner's title policy in Michigan" is inaccurate — it is customary but negotiable. Representing custom as law creates misrepresentation exposure under MCL 339.2512(f), which prohibits making "substantially inaccurate" representations regarding a transaction.

Fourth, agents neglect to verify whether their closing cost allocations survive the lender's underwriting guidelines. A 5% seller concession on an FHA loan exceeds the 6% cap only narrowly, but if the buyer has also negotiated seller-paid repairs, the combined total may exceed limits. The [timeline tracking discipline](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) required to catch these issues before they mature into crises separates competent agents from those generating E&O claims.

Fifth, dual agents in Michigan (operating under MCL 339.2517's disclosed dual agency provisions) sometimes allocate costs in ways that favor one party without adequate written consent from both. LARA enforcement actions in 2024 and 2025 included multiple cases where dual agents defaulted to seller-favorable cost allocations without documenting buyer acknowledgment.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under MCL 339.2512h for their agents' transactional conduct. Specifically regarding closing costs, brokers should audit three areas on every file.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Risk If Missed |
|---|---|---|
| Purchase Agreement Section 10 | Dollar amount specified, within lender limits | Deal cancellation, LARA complaint |
| Transfer Tax Disclosure | Correct calculation on net sheet/CMA | Seller claim of misrepresentation |
| CD vs. Contract Alignment | All cost allocations match between documents | Closing delay, potential RESPA violation |
| Dual Agency Files | Written consent for cost allocation from both parties | Disciplinary action under MCL 339.2517 |
| Concession Cap Compliance | Total concessions within loan program limits | Loan denial, deal collapse |

## Protecting Your License and Your Client's Interests

The distinction between "customary" and "required" confuses even experienced Michigan agents, but the framework is straightforward once internalized. Statutory costs (transfer taxes, recording fees) have legally designated payers that can only shift through explicit contractual language. Everything else — title insurance premiums, escrow fees, inspection costs, survey charges — lives in negotiation territory governed by Forms MAR-1020 and MAR-1050.

Agents who build [systematic checklists](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) for closing cost verification catch errors before they compound. The checklist approach works because closing cost problems rarely emerge in isolation. A missing concession cap usually accompanies a missing inspection deadline, which accompanies an incomplete seller disclosure. File hygiene is holistic.

The question of Michigan who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 ultimately resolves to this: statute assigns some costs, custom suggests others, and the purchase agreement governs everything else. Your job as a licensee is to document allocations precisely, verify lender compatibility, and ensure your client understands what they owe before they sit down at the closing table.
