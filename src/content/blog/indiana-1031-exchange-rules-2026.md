## Indiana 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: The Ground Rules

Real estate agents in Indiana who handle investment property transactions must understand the Indiana 1031 exchange state tax implications 2026 brings into focus. Federal Section 1031 deferral does not automatically mean a free pass at the state level. Indiana imposes its own withholding requirements, conformity rules, and nonresident filing obligations that can create liability for agents, closing attorneys, and their clients.

**Indiana generally conforms to the federal treatment of like-kind exchanges under IRC Section 1031, but with conditions that matter at the closing table.** The Indiana Department of Revenue (DOR) follows the Internal Revenue Code as adopted by Indiana Code 6-3-1-11, which means the state recognizes deferrals—unless the replacement property sits outside Indiana's borders. That single exception catches agents off guard more than any other rule in this space.

The stakes are real. A mishandled exchange can result in unexpected tax bills for your client, withholding penalties for the closing agent, and professional liability exposure for the brokerage. Understanding the mechanics is not optional—it is part of your fiduciary duty when advising on investment dispositions.

## How Indiana Conforms to Federal 1031 Rules—and Where It Diverges

Indiana adopted its conformity with the IRC through Indiana Code 6-3-1-3.5, which annually updates the state's reference to the federal tax code. For 2026, this means the state recognizes the same like-kind property definitions, the 45-day identification window, and the 180-day exchange period that the IRS enforces. Qualified intermediaries must meet the same standards under both frameworks.

The divergence hits when a taxpayer exchanges Indiana real property for replacement property located in another state. Under Indiana's "clawback" approach, the DOR treats the transaction as a taxable disposition because Indiana will never collect tax on gains tied to the out-of-state replacement property. This is not a penalty—it is the state protecting its revenue base from permanent deferral that migrates across state lines.

A second divergence involves nonresident sellers. Indiana Code 6-3-4-8.5 mandates withholding on the sale of Indiana real property by nonresidents, even when a 1031 exchange is in play, unless a specific exemption affidavit is filed at closing. Agents who assume the exchange itself eliminates withholding obligations create risk for every party at the table.

## Withholding Requirements and the Forms That Matter

**Indiana's nonresident withholding framework is the single most transaction-relevant tax rule agents encounter in 1031 exchanges.** The state requires the buyer (or closing agent acting on the buyer's behalf) to withhold tax at a rate equal to the lesser of the gain or a percentage of the gross sales price. For 2026, that rate remains tied to Indiana's individual adjusted gross income tax rate of 3.05%.

Here are the forms you need to know:

| Form | Purpose | Filed By | When Due |
|------|---------|----------|----------|
| WH-18 | Indiana Nonresident Withholding Remittance | Buyer or closing agent | Within 30 days of closing |
| NRA (Nonresident Real Estate Affidavit) | Exemption certification for 1031 or other exclusions | Seller | At or before closing |
| IT-20SC / IT-40PNR | Annual return reporting gain (if exchange fails) | Seller entity or individual | April 15 following tax year |
| Schedule IN K-1 | Pass-through entity reporting of exchange gain | Partnership/S-Corp | With entity return |

When a seller provides a completed NRA certifying a valid 1031 exchange, the closing agent is relieved of withholding responsibility. However, if the exchange later fails—due to a missed deadline, disqualified property, or constructive receipt—the seller owes the tax, and the DOR may look back at the closing agent's reliance on the NRA. Retaining a copy of the executed NRA in the transaction file is non-negotiable.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Indiana 1031 Transactions

Experienced agents still stumble on these errors. Each one carries financial or regulatory consequences that extend beyond the closing date.

**First, agents confuse federal deferral with state deferral in interstate exchanges.** When a client sells a rental duplex in Indianapolis and buys replacement property in Tennessee, federal deferral applies—but Indiana will tax the gain. Agents who tell clients "it's all deferred" without qualifying the state-level treatment are providing inaccurate tax guidance, which can trigger complaints to the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC) under 876 IAC 6-1.

Second, agents fail to flag the withholding exemption process early enough. The NRA must be completed before or at closing. If a seller arrives at the closing table without it, the closer must withhold and remit on Form WH-18. Retroactively unwinding a withholding after funds have been sent to the DOR is a slow, frustrating process for your client.

Third, agents assume qualified intermediary selection is solely the seller's problem. In reality, if an intermediary lacks proper bonding or commingles funds, the exchange is disqualified from the start. Agents who recommend intermediaries without verifying credentials—or who fail to document that the client made an independent selection—expose themselves to liability. The IRS and DOR both look at whether the intermediary met the "safe harbor" requirements of Treasury Regulation 1.1031(k)-1(g)(4).

Fourth, agents mishandle boot. When exchange proceeds include cash or non-like-kind property (boot), that portion is taxable at both the federal and state level. Agents who structure deals with seller concessions, personal property allocations, or cash-out provisions without flagging the boot consequences leave their clients facing unexpected Indiana adjusted gross income tax.

Fifth, agents overlook the entity-level implications. A 1031 exchange by a partnership or LLC does not defer gain at the partner level if the entity dissolves or distributes proceeds within two years. Indiana follows the federal anti-abuse rules here, and agents who help structure "drop and swap" transactions without involving a tax attorney risk the entire deferral.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers supervising agents who handle investment property transactions carry heightened responsibility under [IREC's supervision standards](https://www.in.gov/pla/professions/real-estate-commission/). The commission expects brokers to maintain systems that prevent agents from providing unauthorized tax advice and to ensure proper documentation in exchange-related files.

**Every transaction file involving a 1031 exchange should contain, at minimum, a copy of the executed NRA or the WH-18 remittance confirmation.** Brokers who cannot produce this documentation during an audit face potential violations of 876 IAC 6-3-1, which governs record retention and supervision standards for Indiana brokerages.

| Audit Checkpoint | What to Verify | Risk If Missing |
|-----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| NRA or WH-18 in file | Executed form with date and signatures | Withholding liability, DOR inquiry |
| QI engagement letter | Named intermediary, bonding confirmation | Exchange disqualification, client claim |
| Written disclosure | Agent advised client to consult tax professional | E&O exposure, IREC complaint |
| Timeline tracking | 45-day and 180-day deadlines documented | Missed deadlines, failed exchange |
| Boot calculation | Any non-like-kind proceeds identified | Unreported taxable gain |

Brokers should also enforce a blanket policy: agents must never state that a transaction "qualifies" for 1031 treatment. That determination belongs to the client's CPA or tax attorney. The agent's role is to facilitate introductions, flag deadlines, and ensure proper forms reach the closing table. Anything beyond that crosses into unauthorized practice.

Quarterly file reviews—particularly for agents with a high volume of investment property transactions—should specifically check for these 1031-related documents. A missing NRA is not a paperwork oversight; it is a potential five-figure liability for the brokerage.

## Interstate Exchanges: The Indiana Clawback in Practice

Consider a common scenario: a client sells a $750,000 commercial property in Fort Wayne and identifies replacement property in Columbus, Ohio. Federally, the exchange qualifies for full deferral. The client's CPA prepares the federal return showing zero recognized gain.

Indiana disagrees. Because the replacement property is in Ohio, Indiana will never have the opportunity to tax the built-in gain when the replacement property is eventually sold. The DOR requires the seller to recognize the Indiana-source gain in the year of the relinquished property's sale. At the 2026 rate of 3.05%, a $200,000 gain produces a $6,100 Indiana tax bill that the client did not expect.

Agents who handle cross-border exchanges without mentioning this risk are failing their clients. The disclosure does not require you to calculate the tax—it requires you to say, clearly and early, that an out-of-state replacement property may trigger Indiana income tax despite federal deferral.

## Deadlines, Penalties, and What Happens When Things Go Wrong

A failed 1031 exchange converts what the client believed was a tax-deferred transaction into a fully taxable sale—retroactively. The Indiana DOR assesses interest from the original due date of the return, not from the date the exchange failed. Penalties for underpayment range from 10% to 20% depending on whether the DOR classifies the shortfall as negligence or substantial understatement.

For closing agents who failed to withhold on Form WH-18 without a valid NRA on file, the penalty is personal liability for the withholding amount plus a 20% penalty under IC 6-8.1-10-2.1. The DOR does not need to prove intent—strict liability applies to the withholding obligation.

Agents themselves face a different kind of exposure. If an IREC investigation determines that an agent provided specific tax advice—such as guaranteeing deferral or instructing a client not to file the NRA—the commission can impose discipline ranging from fines to license suspension under IC 25-34.1-3-11. The standard is not whether the advice was correct but whether providing it exceeded the agent's authorized scope of practice.

Documentation is your defense. When you advise a client to consult a qualified tax professional about their 1031 exchange and memorialize that advice in writing, you have built a protective record.
