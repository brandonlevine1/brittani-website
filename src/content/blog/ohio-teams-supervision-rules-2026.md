## Ohio Teams & Supervision Rules 2026: The Regulatory Framework

Ohio teams & supervision rules 2026 represent one of the most misunderstood areas of state real estate law, and the consequences of getting them wrong have only grown steeper. The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, housed under the Ohio Department of Commerce, has increased enforcement actions against teams operating outside proper supervision structures. Agents and brokers who treat team formation as a branding exercise rather than a regulatory obligation are exposing themselves to license suspension, fines, and civil liability.

The governing statutes sit primarily in Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Chapter 4735 and Ohio Administrative Code (OAC) 1301:5-1-01 through 1301:5-1-12. These rules dictate everything from advertising standards to trust account management to the supervisory duties a principal broker must exercise over every licensee operating within their brokerage—including team members. The Division treats a "team" not as a separate legal entity but as a subgroup of the brokerage, which means the broker's liability umbrella covers every action a team member takes.

**Every Ohio team, regardless of size or production volume, operates as an extension of its brokerage—not as an independent unit.** This distinction matters because team leaders often assume authorities they do not legally possess. Unlicensed team assistants, dual-branded marketing, and unsupervised showings all fall into gray areas that the Division has been clarifying through enforcement opinions issued as recently as early 2026.

## What Ohio Law Actually Says About Team Structure

ORC 4735.01(A) defines a "real estate broker" and ORC 4735.01(B) defines a "real estate salesperson," but neither statute explicitly defines a "team." The absence of a statutory team definition does not mean teams are unregulated. Instead, the Division applies existing licensure and supervision requirements to any group of licensees marketing themselves collectively under a shared brand within a brokerage.

OAC 1301:5-1-02 governs advertising. It requires that all team advertising include the brokerage name in a "clear and conspicuous" manner. The team name cannot be deceptively similar to the brokerage name, and it cannot imply the team is an independent brokerage. Any advertising that features only the team name without the brokerage identification violates the rule and exposes both the team members and the broker to disciplinary action.

The [Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing](https://com.ohio.gov/divisions-and-programs/real-estate-and-professional-licensing) publishes guidance letters that further clarify these requirements. A 2025 guidance letter confirmed that social media profiles, yard signs, and email signatures all constitute "advertising" subject to OAC 1301:5-1-02.

## Supervision Duties: What Brokers Owe Under ORC 4735.18

ORC 4735.18(A)(22) states that a broker may be disciplined for "failure to adequately supervise the activities of the licensees affiliated with the broker." This is not optional language—it imposes an affirmative duty. Brokers cannot delegate supervision to team leaders and then claim ignorance when violations occur.

**The principal broker bears personal liability for every transaction file a team produces, regardless of whether the broker personally reviewed it.** Ohio case law has consistently held that "adequate supervision" means the broker must have systems in place to detect errors, not merely react after a complaint. The Division has issued suspensions to brokers who relied solely on team leaders to ensure compliance.

| Duty | Broker Responsibility | Team Leader Role |
|------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Trust account management | Sole authority under ORC 4735.24 | No independent authority |
| Advertising approval | Must pre-approve all materials | Can draft, cannot publish without approval |
| Transaction file review | Must review or establish review system | Can organize files, cannot self-certify |
| Licensee supervision | Cannot delegate to unlicensed persons | Can mentor but not formally supervise |
| Complaint response | Must respond directly to Division | Can assist with documentation |

Team leaders who sign documents on behalf of the brokerage without explicit written authorization from the broker are in violation. The Division's Form DRE-100 (Licensee Transfer or Cancellation) must reflect the proper brokerage affiliation, and any change in team membership that affects licensure status requires broker involvement.

## Common Mistakes That Trigger Enforcement Action

The Division's annual enforcement reports reveal patterns that repeat across brokerages of all sizes. These are not obscure technicalities—they are the most frequently cited violations involving teams.

**First, team names that omit brokerage identification.** This is the single most common advertising violation in Ohio. A team called "The Smith Group" that operates under ABC Realty must display "ABC Realty" on every piece of marketing. Yard signs reading only "The Smith Group" with a phone number violate OAC 1301:5-1-02. The Division can impose fines up to $1,000 per violation under ORC 4735.051.

Second, unlicensed assistants performing licensed activities. Ohio law under ORC 4735.02 prohibits anyone without a license from negotiating, showing property for the purpose of negotiating, or drafting offers. Team assistants who schedule showings, attend inspections, and communicate material terms to clients are performing licensed acts. The Division draws a clear line: an unlicensed assistant can schedule appointments and handle administrative tasks, but the moment they discuss price, terms, or property condition with a client or another party, they have crossed into licensed activity.

Third, team leaders holding earnest money outside the brokerage trust account. ORC 4735.24 mandates that all trust funds be deposited into the brokerage's designated trust account within two business days of receipt. Some team leaders maintain separate business accounts for "team operations" and inadvertently deposit client funds there. This is a trust fund violation that can result in license revocation.

Fourth, failure to use proper disclosure forms. Ohio's agency disclosure form (Form DIV-2, Agency Disclosure Statement) must be presented at the earliest meaningful contact. Teams with multiple members interacting with the same client sometimes assume the form was already presented by another team member. If no one verifies, the disclosure obligation goes unfulfilled, and the transaction becomes vulnerable to rescission. For more on how [missed deadlines create liability](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), the pattern in Ohio mirrors what happens nationally.

Fifth, dual agency conflicts within teams. When one team member represents the buyer and another represents the seller in the same transaction, Ohio requires informed written consent from both parties using appropriate disclosure. Teams that treat internal referrals as arm's-length transactions without disclosing the shared team structure face discipline under ORC 4735.71.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce in 2026

Brokers who manage teams must go beyond signing annual supervision agreements and hoping for the best. The Division's 2025-2026 enforcement priorities specifically target brokerage-level compliance failures, not just individual agent misconduct.

Start with advertising audits. Every quarter, pull a sample of team social media profiles, websites, yard sign photos, and printed materials. Verify that OAC 1301:5-1-02 requirements are met. Document the audit. If the Division investigates, your documented review process demonstrates "adequate supervision" under ORC 4735.18(A)(22).

| Audit Area | Frequency | Documentation Required |
|------------|-----------|----------------------|
| Advertising compliance | Quarterly | Screenshots, sign photos, dated review notes |
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | Bank statements, deposit records, Form DRE-10 |
| Transaction file completeness | Per-transaction or monthly batch | Checklist against required forms (DIV-2, purchase agreement, amendments) |
| Licensee status verification | Monthly | Active license check via Ohio ELMS portal |
| Unlicensed assistant activity review | Quarterly | Task logs, communication records |

**Trust account reconciliation cannot be delegated to team leaders.** The broker or a designated office manager with direct broker oversight must perform monthly reconciliations per ORC 4735.24. Any discrepancy must be resolved within 30 days and documented.

Transaction file reviews should verify the presence of: the signed Agency Disclosure Statement (Form DIV-2), the Consumer Guide to Agency Relationships, properly executed purchase contracts (commonly the Ohio REALTORS residential purchase agreement form), and all amendments bearing proper signatures. A [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) can systematize this process, but it does not replace broker-level review.

Licensee status verification is simple but often neglected. The Ohio Electronic Licensure Management System (ELMS) allows brokers to confirm every affiliated licensee's active status. If a team member's license lapses mid-transaction, every act they performed after lapse is potentially void, and the broker faces liability for allowing an unlicensed person to transact.

## Penalties and Liability When Teams Fail to Comply

The Division has a graduated enforcement structure, but even first offenses can carry serious consequences depending on the nature of the violation.

ORC 4735.051 authorizes civil penalties of up to $1,000 per violation for advertising infractions. Trust fund violations under ORC 4735.24 can result in license suspension or revocation on first offense if the Division determines willful misconduct. Even negligent trust fund mishandling typically results in a formal citation, a mandatory continuing education requirement, and a probationary period.

For brokers, the stakes are higher. A finding of inadequate supervision under ORC 4735.18(A)(22) can result in suspension of the broker's license—which effectively shuts down the entire brokerage and every team operating under it. The Division can also require restitution to harmed consumers. Civil lawsuits arising from team misconduct name the broker as a defendant under respondeat superior principles, meaning the broker's errors and omissions insurance becomes the first line of defense.

Transaction cancellation is the practical risk that agents feel most immediately. If a buyer's agent failed to present the Agency Disclosure Statement (Form DIV-2) at first meaningful contact, the buyer may have grounds to void the purchase contract. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends well beyond a single commission check—it includes potential legal fees, reputation damage, and Division investigation costs.

## How to Structure Your Team for Compliance

The safest approach is to formalize your team structure in writing with your broker before launching any marketing. This written agreement should address: team name approval, advertising pre-approval workflow, trust fund handling protocols, supervision frequency, and the specific duties each team member may and may not perform.

Ohio does not mandate a specific "team agreement" form, but the Division expects brokers to document their supervisory arrangements. Many brokerages use internal team policy addenda attached to the independent contractor agreement or employment agreement already on file. The key is that the document explicitly assigns responsibilities and creates an accountability chain that the Division can review if questions arise.

Unlicensed assistants must have written job descriptions that clearly delineate permissible tasks. Post that job description visibly. Train the assistant on what questions they can and cannot answer. When a client asks an assistant about price negotiations or contract terms, the assistant must redirect to a licensed team member—every time, without exception.
