## Understanding Nevada Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

**The Nevada lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain rooted in federal law, but the compliance burden falls squarely on the agents handling the transaction.** Under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulation at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H, every seller or lessor of pre-1978 housing must disclose known lead-based paint hazards before a buyer or tenant is obligated under contract. Nevada has not enacted a separate state-level lead paint statute that supersedes or supplements this federal mandate.

That does not mean Nevada agents can treat this as a checkbox exercise. The Nevada Real Estate Division (NRED), which operates under the Nevada Department of Business and Industry, holds licensees to a standard of competence that includes knowledge of all disclosure obligations — federal and state. Violations can trigger disciplinary action under NRS 645.630, which covers unprofessional conduct, in addition to federal penalties imposed by HUD and the EPA.

For agents working the Las Vegas, Reno, and Henderson markets, a significant portion of housing stock predates 1978. Clark County alone has tens of thousands of homes built during the 1950s and 1960s housing booms. If you list or represent buyers in these neighborhoods, this disclosure touches nearly every deal you close.

## The Federal Framework That Governs Nevada Transactions

The disclosure obligation originates from 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d and is enforced through regulations codified at 40 CFR Part 745 (EPA) and 24 CFR Part 35 (HUD). These regulations require three specific actions before a purchaser is obligated under a contract to purchase target housing.

First, the seller must disclose the presence of any known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards. Second, the seller must provide the buyer with any available records or reports pertaining to lead-based paint in the housing. Third, the seller must provide the buyer with the EPA-approved pamphlet, currently titled *Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home*.

The buyer then receives a 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense, unless the parties mutually agree in writing to a different time period or the buyer waives the opportunity entirely. This 10-day window is a default — it can be shortened, lengthened, or waived, but it cannot be eliminated by the seller unilaterally.

## Forms and Documentation Nevada Agents Must Use

**Nevada does not publish a state-specific lead paint disclosure form.** Agents rely on the federal disclosure form, which EPA and HUD jointly created: the *Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards* form. Most brokerages in Nevada use versions embedded within their transaction management platforms or provided through forms libraries such as those offered by zipForms or Skyslope.

The form requires signatures from the seller, the buyer, and both agents. Each agent certifies that they have informed the seller of their obligations and that they have ensured the buyer received the required disclosures and pamphlet. This is not a passive role — the regulation at 24 CFR 35.94 explicitly states that agents must ensure compliance by their clients.

| Required Element | Responsible Party | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Known lead paint disclosure | Seller | Before buyer is obligated under contract |
| Available lead inspection reports | Seller | Before buyer is obligated under contract |
| EPA pamphlet delivery | Seller (agent ensures) | Before buyer is obligated under contract |
| 10-day inspection opportunity | Both parties acknowledge | Written in contract or waiver |
| Signed disclosure form | Seller, buyer, both agents | Before ratification or as part of contract |

Agents must retain copies of all lead paint disclosure documentation for a minimum of three years from the date the transaction closes, per 24 CFR 35.92. This applies even if the transaction falls through — the retention clock starts from the date of completion of the selling activity.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The penalty structure for noncompliance operates at both the federal and state level. At the federal level, [HUD and EPA](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-based-paint-disclosure-rule) can impose civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act). Criminal penalties can reach $19,507 per violation as well, with potential imprisonment in egregious cases involving knowing and willful violations.

At the state level, NRED can pursue disciplinary action against a licensee under NRS 645.630(1)(h) for failure to disclose material facts, and under NRS 645.630(1)(d) for demonstrating negligence or incompetence. Sanctions range from fines to license suspension or revocation. A buyer who was not properly informed can also pursue rescission of the contract and civil damages, including attorney fees.

**Treble damages represent the most significant financial risk.** Under 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d(b)(3), a buyer who suffers actual damages from a seller's failure to comply with the disclosure requirements may recover three times the amount of damages incurred. If a child develops elevated blood lead levels in a home where the seller concealed known hazards, the damage exposure can reach hundreds of thousands of dollars — and the listing agent may be individually liable if they failed to ensure compliance.

## Common Mistakes Nevada Agents Make

These errors appear repeatedly in enforcement actions, E&O claims, and transaction disputes across the state. Each one is avoidable with proper systems in place.

**Mistake one: treating the disclosure as optional because "the seller says there's no lead paint."** The form must be completed regardless of whether the seller has knowledge of lead-based paint. The seller checks a box indicating they have no knowledge, but the form itself — and the pamphlet delivery — still must occur. Skipping the form because the seller verbally states "there's nothing to disclose" exposes both the agent and the seller to full liability.

Mistake two: failing to provide the EPA pamphlet or substituting outdated versions. The current version of *Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home* was last updated by EPA and must be the version provided. Agents who hand buyers a printout from 2003 or a third-party summary document have not met the regulatory requirement. The pamphlet is freely available on the EPA website and should be part of every listing agent's pre-listing packet for pre-1978 homes.

Mistake three: not obtaining the buyer's signature acknowledging receipt before the contract becomes binding. In competitive markets — particularly in Reno and Las Vegas — agents sometimes rush contract ratification and plan to "circle back" for lead paint signatures. The regulation requires that disclosures happen before the buyer is obligated. A retroactive signature does not cure a violation that already occurred.

Mistake four: waiving the 10-day inspection period without the buyer's informed written consent. Some listing agents insert language into counteroffers that eliminates the inspection opportunity. While the buyer can waive this right, the waiver must be the buyer's affirmative choice, documented in writing. An agent who pressures a buyer to waive or who buries the waiver in boilerplate language is creating a liability trail.

Mistake five: failing to confirm whether a property is actually pre-1978. County assessor records in Clark, Washoe, and other Nevada counties sometimes list build dates inconsistently. An agent who relies on MLS data alone — without verifying through assessor records or building permits — may incorrectly assume a property is exempt. When in doubt, disclose.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under both federal regulations and Nevada law. NRS 645.630 and NAC 645.605 impose obligations on brokers to supervise their agents' compliance with all disclosure requirements. A broker who fails to implement systems for lead paint disclosure compliance is exposed to both regulatory discipline and civil liability.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Source of Obligation |
|---|---|---|
| Verify lead disclosure form in file for all pre-1978 sales | Every transaction | 24 CFR 35.92 |
| Confirm EPA pamphlet delivery documented | Every transaction | 24 CFR 35.88 |
| Check for buyer signature date vs. contract ratification date | Every transaction | 24 CFR 35.86 |
| Retain disclosure records for 3 years post-closing | Ongoing | 24 CFR 35.92 |
| Agent training on lead paint obligations | Annual or at onboarding | NRS 645.630, NAC 645.605 |

Brokers should build lead paint compliance checks into their [transaction management workflows](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) rather than relying on individual agents to self-audit. A single missed disclosure in a file that passes through your brokerage's review process exposes the brokerage to the same treble damages exposure as the individual agent.

Consider implementing a hard stop in your file review process: no pre-1978 transaction moves to closing coordination without a completed, signed lead paint disclosure form bearing all four signatures and a date that precedes or matches the contract ratification date. If you are [training new agents](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines), lead paint disclosure should be part of their first-week compliance orientation, not an afterthought covered months into their career.

## Nevada-Specific Considerations for 2026

While Nevada does not layer additional state-specific lead paint requirements on top of the federal mandate, agents must be aware of how the disclosure interacts with Nevada's Seller's Real Property Disclosure Form (SRPD), governed by NRS 113.130. The SRPD asks sellers about known environmental hazards, which includes lead-based paint. An inconsistency between a seller's SRPD responses and their lead paint disclosure form creates a discoverability problem that opposing counsel will exploit in litigation.

Nevada's hot climate also introduces a practical dimension. Lead paint in pre-1978 homes deteriorates faster when exposed to extreme heat and UV radiation, which is the reality for exterior surfaces in Las Vegas, Pahrump, and other southern Nevada communities. Sellers who claim "no knowledge" while living in a home with visibly deteriorating painted surfaces from the correct era are making a claim that may not survive scrutiny. Agents should document the condition of painted surfaces in their listing photos and notes — not to perform an inspection, but to protect themselves if a seller's "no knowledge" assertion later proves false.

A buyer's inspector who identifies deteriorating lead paint in a home where the seller checked "no knowledge" triggers a dispute that can collapse the transaction and generate complaints to NRED.
