## Why Arkansas Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026 Demand Your Attention

The rules governing Arkansas property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 have not loosened, and enforcement actions from the Arkansas Real Estate Commission continue to tick upward each year. Agents who treat disclosure compliance as an afterthought are writing themselves into liability scenarios that can end careers. This post breaks down the exact statutes, form numbers, common pitfalls, and broker-level audit requirements that apply to residential transactions in the state.

Arkansas sits in a unique position among southern states. Its statutory disclosure framework under the Arkansas Residential Property Disclosure Act (Ark. Code Ann. 18-44-101 through 18-44-115) mandates seller-completed disclosure for most residential sales, while federal environmental requirements layer on top. Getting both layers right, every time, is non-negotiable.

## The Statutory Framework: Ark. Code Ann. 18-44-101 et seq.

**The Arkansas Residential Property Disclosure Act requires sellers of residential real property (one to four units) to deliver a completed property condition disclosure form to the buyer before the contract becomes binding.** The form must disclose known material defects and conditions affecting the property. Sellers are not required to conduct inspections—they disclose only what they actually know.

The specific form agents should be using is the AREC Property Disclosure Statement, which tracks the statutory requirements in 18-44-104. While the Arkansas Real Estate Commission does not mandate a single numbered state form the way some states do, the Commission's recommended disclosure form (available on [AREC's website](https://www.arec.arkansas.gov)) is the safest bet. Using a brokerage-created form that omits statutory categories creates instant exposure.

Exemptions exist under 18-44-104(b). Foreclosure sales, transfers by court order, transfers between spouses or co-owners, new construction that has never been occupied, and government-to-private-party sales all qualify. Agents must confirm the exemption applies—assuming it does without verifying the facts is one of the most common errors in the state.

## Environmental Disclosures: Lead Paint and Beyond

Federal law under 42 U.S.C. 4852d and the EPA/HUD lead-based paint disclosure rule (24 CFR Part 35) applies to every Arkansas sale involving a home built before 1978. The required form is the EPA's Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards, and it must be signed by both buyer and seller before the purchase agreement is executed.

Buyers must receive the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" and a 10-day opportunity to conduct a lead inspection, unless waived in writing. The listing agent bears independent liability if this disclosure is not delivered—the duty does not rest solely on the seller.

Beyond lead paint, Arkansas properties may trigger additional environmental disclosure obligations depending on location. Properties near former agricultural operations, Superfund sites (there are 11 active NPL sites in Arkansas as of early 2026), or areas with historical mining activity may carry known contamination risks. If the seller discloses knowledge of environmental contamination on the AREC property disclosure form, agents must ensure that information reaches the buyer without alteration or omission.

| Form / Document | Who Provides It | When It Must Be Delivered | Governing Authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| AREC Property Disclosure Statement | Seller (agent facilitates) | Before contract becomes binding | Ark. Code Ann. 18-44-104 |
| EPA Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | Seller and listing agent | Before contract execution | 42 U.S.C. 4852d / 24 CFR 35 |
| EPA Lead Pamphlet | Listing agent | With lead disclosure | 24 CFR 35.88 |
| Radon disclosure (if known) | Seller via property disclosure | With property disclosure form | Ark. Code Ann. 18-44-104 |
| Flood zone disclosure | Seller via property disclosure | With property disclosure form | Ark. Code Ann. 18-44-104 |

Arkansas does not currently have a standalone radon disclosure statute, but the property disclosure form asks whether the seller has knowledge of radon. Agents should not skip this question or mark it "unknown" on the seller's behalf. ## Liability When Compliance Fails

**Failure to deliver required disclosures exposes agents, brokers, and sellers to a cascade of consequences that range from deal cancellation to regulatory discipline and civil litigation.** Here is what is at stake.

Under Ark. Code Ann. 18-44-106, if a seller fails to deliver the property disclosure form, the buyer may rescind the contract within five calendar days of learning about the failure. Earnest money is returned. The deal is dead.

The Arkansas Real Estate Commission's disciplinary authority under Ark. Code Ann. 17-42-311 and AREC Rule 10.10 allows it to impose fines of up to $5,000 per violation, require additional education, suspend licenses, or revoke them entirely. In the 2024–2025 enforcement cycle, AREC issued multiple consent orders involving disclosure failures—some resulting in $2,500 fines plus mandatory coursework.

Federal lead-paint violations carry even steeper penalties. The EPA can assess civil penalties up to $21,017 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2026). That number applies per transaction, meaning a listing agent who habitually skips lead disclosures on pre-1978 listings accumulates exposure with each closing.

Civil lawsuits from buyers remain the most financially devastating risk. Arkansas courts have upheld fraud and negligent misrepresentation claims where sellers and agents knew of defects and failed to disclose. Damages can include cost of repair, diminution in value, and in egregious cases, punitive damages.

## Common Mistakes Arkansas Agents Make

Knowing the rules is one thing. Applying them under the pressure of multiple active transactions is another. These five errors appear repeatedly in AREC enforcement files and E&O claims.

**First, agents fill out disclosure forms on behalf of sellers.** The property disclosure is a seller obligation. When an agent marks answers, even with the seller's verbal guidance, it shifts liability to the agent and raises questions about whether the disclosure reflects the seller's actual knowledge. Hand the form to the seller, explain it, but let them complete it themselves.

Second, agents deliver the disclosure form after the contract is executed rather than before. Ark. Code Ann. 18-44-104 is clear that delivery must happen before the contract becomes binding. Delivering it at the same time as the contract execution—or worse, post-ratification—violates the statute and gives the buyer a rescission window they should not have needed.

Third, agents assume the lead disclosure does not apply because a property has been "renovated." Renovation does not remove the federal requirement. If the structure was originally built before 1978, the lead disclosure must be provided regardless of subsequent remodeling, lead abatement, or encapsulation.

Fourth, agents rely on MLS data to confirm whether a property is exempt from disclosure. A listing marked "bank-owned" in the MLS does not automatically mean the foreclosure exemption applies—the servicer may be selling via standard contract rather than through a court-ordered process. Verify the actual transaction structure before waiving disclosure obligations.

Fifth, agents fail to retain signed copies of the lead disclosure form. The retention requirement is three years from the date of sale under 24 CFR 35.92. If the brokerage file does not contain a signed copy and a complaint arises in year two, the agent has no defense. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Ark. Code Ann. 17-42-311(a)(7) for failure to supervise their licensees. AREC does not accept "I didn't know my agent skipped it" as a defense. A documented audit process is the only shield.

| Audit Point | What to Verify | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Property disclosure delivery | Signed form in file, dated before contract execution | Every transaction |
| Lead-based paint compliance | Signed EPA form + pamphlet receipt for all pre-1978 properties | Every applicable transaction |
| Exemption verification | Written documentation of exemption basis (court order, foreclosure decree, etc.) | Every exempt transaction |
| Agent completion check | Confirm seller—not agent—completed disclosure form | Quarterly random audit |
| Retention compliance | All lead disclosure forms retained minimum three years | Annual file review |

Brokers should require agents to submit disclosure documents within 48 hours of execution, not at closing. Waiting until the file closes to check compliance means the violation has already occurred and the liability has already attached. ## The 2026 Enforcement Climate in Arkansas

AREC's 2025 annual report signaled increased scrutiny on disclosure compliance, particularly for environmental matters related to flood-prone properties in the Arkansas River Valley and the eastern Delta region. With FEMA flood map updates affecting thousands of parcels, agents listing properties in recently remapped zones face heightened duties to ensure sellers accurately represent flood history and insurance requirements on disclosure forms.

The Commission has also issued guidance reminding agents that "as-is" contract language does not eliminate disclosure duties. A seller may sell as-is, but they still must deliver the property disclosure form with honest answers. Conflating contract terms with statutory obligations is a mistake that generates disciplinary complaints every single year.
