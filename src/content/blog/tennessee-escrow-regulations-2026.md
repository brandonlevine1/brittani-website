# Tennessee Escrow Regulations 2026: What Every Agent and Broker Must Get Right

Understanding Tennessee escrow regulations 2026 is non-negotiable if you hold an active license in this state. The Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) treats escrow violations as among the most serious offenses a licensee can commit, and the consequences range from monetary penalties to permanent revocation. This post breaks down the statutes, the forms, the common failure points, and what your broker should be auditing right now.

## The Statutory Framework Governing Escrow in Tennessee

Tennessee's escrow requirements live primarily in the Tennessee Real Estate Broker License Act of 1973, codified at Tenn. Code Ann. Section 62-13-301 et seq. The two sections agents need to memorize are Section 62-13-321, which mandates that all escrow funds be deposited into a trust account maintained by the principal broker, and Section 62-13-312(b)(14), which lists commingling or misappropriation of escrow funds as explicit grounds for disciplinary action.

TREC Rule 1260-02-.09 adds the operational details. It requires that trust accounts be maintained in a federally insured Tennessee depository, that the account be clearly labeled as an escrow or trust account, and that the broker maintain records sufficient to identify each deposit by buyer, seller, property address, and amount.

The [Tennessee Real Estate Commission](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regboards/trec.html) publishes periodic guidance letters clarifying these requirements. Agents should subscribe to TREC's email updates because interpretive guidance can shift without a formal rule change.

## Forms and Contracts That Trigger Escrow Obligations

**The Tennessee Association of Realtors (TAR) Purchase and Sale Agreement, Form RF401, is where escrow obligations begin for most residential transactions.** Paragraph 4 of the RF401 specifies the earnest money amount, the holder (typically the listing broker's firm or a title company), and the deadline for deposit.

The RF401 also incorporates a mutual release provision via TAR Form RF751, which both parties must sign before escrow funds can be returned after a failed transaction. Without that signed release—or a court order—the escrow holder is legally prohibited from disbursing funds to either party under Tenn. Code Ann. 62-13-321(b).

| Form Number | Purpose | Escrow Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| RF401 | Purchase and Sale Agreement | Sets earnest money amount, holder, deposit deadline |
| RF751 | Mutual Release of Purchase and Sale Agreement | Required before escrow disbursement after contract failure |
| RF301 | Exclusive Listing Agreement | Identifies default escrow holder for the brokerage |
| RF656 | Amendment to Purchase and Sale Agreement | Can modify escrow terms, deposit amounts, or holder |

Agents frequently underestimate the RF751's importance. If one party refuses to sign it, the funds sit frozen in escrow until TREC's interpleader process or a court resolves the dispute. This can take months, and the broker holding the funds is stuck as custodian the entire time.

## Deposit Timelines and the Three-Business-Day Rule

TREC does not independently codify a deposit deadline in its administrative rules, which is why the contractual deadline in the RF401 controls. The standard language in the 2026 RF401 requires earnest money to reach the escrow account within three business days of the binding agreement date. Parties can modify this by written addendum, but the default catches most agents off guard when weekends and holidays compress the window.

**Missing the deposit deadline does not automatically void the contract in Tennessee, but it does expose the buyer to potential breach claims and gives the seller grounds to terminate.** The seller must still provide written notice of the failure and allow a cure period if one is specified in the contract. Agents who assume a late deposit is harmless are playing a dangerous game with their client's position.

The distinction between "received by the escrow holder" and "deposited into the trust account" also matters. TREC expects the broker to physically deposit the check or initiate the wire transfer into the trust account promptly after receipt—typically within one additional business day. Holding a check in a desk drawer for a week is a recordkeeping violation even if the overall timeline appears met on paper.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Violations of Tennessee's escrow statutes carry escalating consequences depending on severity and intent. TREC's disciplinary matrix, published in its annual enforcement report, distinguishes between negligent errors and willful misconduct.

| Violation Type | Potential Consequence | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Late deposit (first offense, no harm) | Letter of warning or civil penalty up to $1,000 | Tenn. Code Ann. 62-13-312(b)(1) |
| Commingling funds | License suspension, civil penalty, required audit | Tenn. Code Ann. 62-13-312(b)(14) |
| Unauthorized disbursement | License revocation, personal liability, potential criminal referral | Tenn. Code Ann. 62-13-321(b) |
| Failure to maintain records | Consent order, mandatory education, civil penalty | TREC Rule 1260-02-.09 |
| Misappropriation | Revocation, criminal prosecution (theft charge) | Tenn. Code Ann. 39-14-103 |

Beyond TREC sanctions, agents face civil liability from aggrieved buyers or sellers. A buyer whose earnest money is improperly disbursed can sue the agent, the broker, and the brokerage firm. Tennessee courts have awarded both actual damages and attorney fees in these cases. The Tennessee Real Estate Education and Recovery Fund (Tenn. Code Ann. 62-13-208) exists precisely because consumers lose money to licensee misconduct—and claims against that fund trigger automatic license revocation for the offending licensee.

Deal cancellation is another practical consequence. When escrow disputes arise, closings stall. Title companies refuse to issue policies when the chain of earnest money handling is unclear. Lenders pull commitments when timelines slip. One escrow mistake can collapse an entire transaction and every contingent deal behind it, a problem we explored in our post on [where deals commonly break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down).

## Five Common Escrow Mistakes Tennessee Agents Make

**First, agents accept earnest money checks made payable to them personally rather than to the brokerage or escrow holder.** This creates an immediate commingling risk the moment the agent touches the funds. Even if the agent immediately endorses the check over to the brokerage trust account, the optics and the paper trail are problematic during a TREC audit.

Second, agents fail to document the earnest money trail in the transaction file. TREC auditors expect to see a copy of the check (front and back), the deposit receipt from the bank, and a ledger entry tying the deposit to the specific transaction. Missing any of these documents during a broker audit is a red flag that can trigger a formal TREC investigation.

Third, agents verbally agree to extend deposit deadlines without putting the extension in writing via RF656. Verbal modifications to the RF401 are unenforceable, and the original three-business-day deadline remains in effect. If the seller later claims breach, the agent who promised a verbal extension has no defense—and may face a complaint from their own client.

Fourth, agents release escrow funds based on one party's verbal instruction without a signed RF751 or written authorization from both parties. Tennessee law is unambiguous: no disbursement without mutual written consent or a court order. Agents who shortcut this process expose their broker to personal liability for the full escrow amount.

Fifth, agents operating on teams sometimes assume the team leader or transaction coordinator handles escrow compliance. TREC holds the individual licensee and the principal broker jointly responsible. Delegation of tasks does not delegate legal accountability. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Principal brokers in Tennessee bear ultimate responsibility for escrow compliance under Tenn. Code Ann. 62-13-321. TREC does not accept "I didn't know my agent did that" as a defense. The Commission has suspended broker licenses for supervisory failures even when the broker had no personal involvement in the misconduct.

**Brokers should conduct monthly reconciliations of their trust account, matching every deposit to a specific open transaction and verifying that disbursements correspond to signed releases or closing statements.** TREC Rule 1260-02-.09 requires that these reconciliations be documented and retained for at least three years.

A quarterly file audit is the minimum standard of care. During these audits, brokers should verify that each transaction file contains a copy of the earnest money instrument, proof of timely deposit, the signed escrow receipt (often included in the RF401), and either a closing statement or a signed RF751 showing how the funds were ultimately disbursed. Files missing these documents need immediate remediation.

Brokers should also verify that their trust account signature cards are current. If a broker changes banks, adds a branch office, or brings on a new office manager with signatory authority, TREC must be notified. Failure to update signature cards has triggered consent orders in recent enforcement actions.

The relationship between broker oversight and [daily transaction coordination](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) is tighter than most firms acknowledge. Every task that touches money—collecting deposits, tracking deadlines, filing releases—needs a documented workflow with clear accountability. Firms that treat this as administrative noise are the ones that end up in front of a TREC hearing panel.

## Disputed Funds and the Interpleader Process

When buyer and seller cannot agree on who gets the earnest money after a failed transaction, Tennessee law provides a specific resolution path. Under Tenn. Code Ann. 62-13-321(b), if the escrow holder cannot obtain mutual written release within 21 days of a written demand from either party, the holder must either interplead the funds into court or continue holding them until receiving a court order or a signed release.

> "No licensee shall disburse any funds from an escrow or trustee account without the written consent of all parties having an interest therein, or by court order." — Tenn. Code Ann. 62-13-321(b)

The interpleader process protects the broker from liability to either party, but it costs money. Filing fees, attorney fees, and court costs come out of someone's pocket—often the brokerage's if neither party reimburses. Smart brokers build this contingency into their risk management planning and carry errors and omissions insurance that covers escrow disputes.

Agents can reduce interpleader risk by writing clear, specific contingency language in the RF401 and by counseling clients on what triggers earnest money forfeiture versus return. Ambiguous contract terms are the leading cause of escrow disputes in Tennessee according to TREC's 2025 enforcement data.

## Staying Compliant Through the Rest of 2026

The Commission added two new investigators in fiscal year 2025-2026 specifically assigned to trust account audits. Random audits—not just complaint-driven investigations—are now part of TREC's annual enforcement plan.
