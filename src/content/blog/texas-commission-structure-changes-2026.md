## Texas Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026: The Ground-Level Reality

The phrase "Texas commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026" has become a search term that nearly every agent in the state has typed at least once since the practice changes took effect. The settlement between the National Association of Realtors and plaintiffs in the Sitzer/Burnett litigation rewrote how commission flows between parties, and the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) has layered its own enforcement expectations on top. Agents who operated on autopilot before August 2024 are now dealing with form requirements, disclosure obligations, and negotiation protocols that carry real consequences for noncompliance.

This is not a theoretical compliance exercise. Deals are falling apart over unsigned agreements and vague compensation language, and TREC investigators are actively reviewing complaints tied to the new rules.

## How the Settlement Changed Commission Mechanics in Texas

Before the settlement, the typical Texas transaction routed buyer-agent compensation through the MLS cooperative compensation field. A listing agent entered a percentage or flat fee, the buyer's agent accepted it by showing the property, and the commission split was baked into the listing agreement from day one.

**That mechanism no longer exists on any Texas MLS.** The Houston Association of Realtors, MetroTex Association of Realtors, Austin Board of Realtors, and San Antonio Board of Realtors all removed cooperative compensation fields by August 2024 as required by the settlement terms. What replaced it is a system that demands written buyer agreements with explicit compensation terms before any property tour begins.

The structural shift means buyer agents must negotiate their compensation directly with their client—or negotiate it as part of the purchase contract with the seller. Either way, the days of passive commission collection are finished.

## Written Buyer Agreements: The TREC and TAR Form Requirements

TREC has long required written agreements for representation under the Texas Occupations Code, Section 1101.652(b)(22), which allows disciplinary action for failing to provide a written agreement when representing a buyer. The NAR settlement made this requirement operational nationwide, but Texas already had statutory teeth behind it.

The primary form is TREC No. 24-0 (Buyer/Tenant Representation Agreement). TAR members also have access to TAR Form 2401, which provides additional customization options. Both forms now require that compensation be stated as a specific dollar amount or percentage—not "whatever the listing broker offers" or similarly open-ended language.

| Form | Issuing Body | Key Requirement | When Required |
|------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| TREC 24-0 | Texas Real Estate Commission | Specific compensation amount/rate | Before touring any property |
| TAR 2401 | Texas Association of Realtors | Specific compensation, term limits | Before touring any property |
| TAR 2402 | Texas Association of Realtors | Short-form agreement for single showing | Before that specific showing |
| TREC 20-16 (One to Four Family Contract) | Texas Real Estate Commission | Paragraph 12 broker fee provisions | At offer submission |

The distinction matters because agents using open-ended compensation language—phrases like "to be determined at closing" or "per MLS offering"—are violating both the settlement terms and TREC's expectation of specific written authorization. TAR issued Guidance Memo 2025-03 explicitly warning against this practice.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

TREC enforcement is not hypothetical. Under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.652, the commission can suspend or revoke a license, impose administrative penalties up to $5,000 per violation, or require additional education. Failing to secure a written buyer representation agreement before showing property exposes agents to multiple risk vectors.

**First, the commission claim itself may become unenforceable.** Without a valid written agreement specifying compensation terms, a buyer's agent has no contractual basis to demand payment—from the buyer or from the seller through negotiated contract terms. Texas courts have consistently held that oral commission agreements are unenforceable under the Statute of Frauds (Texas Business and Commerce Code, Section 26.01).

Second, MLS violations carry separate penalties. If an agent or broker attempts to advertise cooperative compensation in listing remarks or data fields, the local MLS association can impose fines ranging from $500 to $15,000 depending on the association and the severity of the violation. Third, buyers who feel misled about compensation obligations may file TREC complaints, triggering investigations that appear on an agent's public license record regardless of outcome.

The risk of [a deal collapsing over paperwork failures](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) is not just financial—it cascades into reputation damage and potential malpractice exposure through errors-and-omissions claims.

## Common Mistakes Texas Agents Are Making Right Now

After reviewing dozens of compliance scenarios reported by Texas brokerages, five errors surface repeatedly.

**Using old language in buyer agreements that references MLS cooperative compensation.** Some agents are still using pre-settlement template language or manually writing in phrases like "compensation per MLS" in the blank fields of TREC 24-0. This creates an agreement that references a mechanism that no longer exists, making the compensation term potentially unenforceable.

Failing to present the buyer agreement before the first showing is the second most common error. The settlement requires the agreement to be signed before touring, not during the offer process or at closing. Agents who show properties first and paper the agreement later are operating without authorization and without a valid commission claim.

Third, agents are neglecting to address what happens if the seller offers less compensation than the buyer agreement specifies. The agreement should clearly state whether the buyer pays the difference, whether the agent absorbs it, or whether the terms are renegotiated. Leaving this ambiguous invites disputes at closing.

Fourth, some agents are verbally promising buyers that "the seller always pays" without disclosing that this is now a negotiated term, not a guarantee. This misrepresentation violates TREC's advertising and disclosure rules under 22 TAC Section 535.154.

Fifth, agents are failing to document compensation negotiations in the contract itself. Paragraph 12 of the TREC One to Four Family Residential Contract (TREC 20-16) addresses broker fees, and agents who leave it blank or check boxes inconsistently create title company confusion that delays closings. Keeping track of these details across [multiple active transactions](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) requires systems, not memory.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.803, which holds them responsible for the actions of their sponsored agents. A single agent's noncompliance can trigger a complaint against the entire brokerage.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency | Risk if Missed |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|----------------|
| Buyer representation agreements | Signed, dated, specific compensation terms | Every transaction file | Commission disputes, TREC complaint |
| MLS listing compliance | No cooperative compensation in remarks or fields | Weekly spot-check | MLS fines, settlement violation |
| Contract Paragraph 12 | Properly completed, consistent with buyer agreement | Every offer submission | Closing delays, commission denial |
| Agent advertising | No claims about "free" representation or guaranteed seller-paid commission | Monthly review | TREC advertising violation |
| Training documentation | Agents trained on post-settlement procedures | Quarterly | Supervisory liability defense |

**Brokers who do not maintain auditable files are building a liability portfolio.** TREC auditors can request transaction files during routine inspections or complaint investigations, and missing buyer agreements are among the first documents they check.

The practical challenge is volume. A brokerage with 40 agents closing 15 transactions per month each generates hundreds of compliance touchpoints. Manual review breaks down quickly, which is why brokerages are increasingly turning to automated transaction management to flag missing documents before closing. The shift toward [systematized oversight rather than ad-hoc file review](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) is no longer optional—it is a regulatory survival strategy.

## Negotiating Compensation in the Purchase Contract

The post-settlement framework creates a new negotiation layer that did not previously exist in most Texas transactions. Buyer agents must now affirmatively request seller-paid compensation through the purchase contract or accept that their client will pay directly.

TREC's One to Four Family Residential Contract (20-16) and the TAR Residential Buyer/Tenant Representation Agreement both contemplate this negotiation. Paragraph 12 of TREC 20-16 allows the parties to specify how and by whom broker fees are paid. Agents can request that the seller pay a specific amount as part of the offer terms, but the seller has no obligation to accept.

This creates a dynamic where buyer agents must articulate their value proposition not only to their client but indirectly to the seller. Offers that include large compensation requests may be less competitive in multiple-offer scenarios. Conversely, buyers who agree to pay their agent directly may submit cleaner offers that appeal to cost-conscious sellers.

The Texas Association of Realtors has published [updated guidance on its member portal](https://www.texasrealestate.com/) addressing how to structure these requests without violating anti-steering provisions. Agents must be careful not to prioritize showing properties where seller-paid compensation is available over properties that better fit the buyer's criteria—doing so constitutes steering and violates both the settlement terms and TREC's fiduciary duty requirements.

## The Broker-to-Broker Compensation Path

Sellers can still authorize their listing broker to offer compensation to a buyer's broker. The difference is that this offer cannot appear in MLS data fields. It can be communicated through the listing broker's website, through direct broker-to-broker communication, or through contractual negotiation.

**This means listing agents now have an additional disclosure obligation.** If the seller has authorized compensation to buyer brokers, the listing agent should document this authorization in the listing agreement and communicate it through permissible channels. The Texas listing agreement forms (TAR 1101 for exclusive right to sell) include provisions for this, but agents must fill them in correctly.

The absence of a centralized compensation database means buyer agents must proactively inquire about available compensation on a property-by-property basis. This adds friction to the transaction process but also creates documentation opportunities—every inquiry and response should be preserved in the transaction file.
