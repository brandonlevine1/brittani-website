## Why Indiana Buyer Contingency Deadlines 2026 Demand More Attention Than Ever

Missing a single contingency deadline can kill a deal, trigger a lawsuit, or land an Indiana agent in front of the Indiana Real Estate Commission. The phrase "Indiana buyer contingency deadlines 2026" is not just a search term—it is the operational reality facing every licensee who writes or manages a purchase agreement this year. The Indiana Association of Realtors (IAR) forms updated for 2026 tightened language around automatic waivers, and the Commission continues to discipline agents who let dates slip without client notification.

Indiana does not have a single statute that defines all contingency periods in one place. Instead, deadlines arise from the contract terms agents select on IAR forms, overlaid by statutory duties found in Indiana Code Title 25, Article 34.1. That dual-source structure means agents bear the responsibility of building a timeline from scratch for every transaction.

Brokers who assume their agents "just know" the dates are taking on risk they cannot afford. This post breaks down the forms, the law, the mistakes, and the audit framework brokers should have in place right now.

## The Forms That Control Indiana Contingency Timelines

The vast majority of residential transactions in Indiana use the IAR Purchase Agreement, commonly referred to as **Form 40** (Residential Real Estate Purchase Agreement) and its companion **Form 46** (Buyer's Inspection Response). These are not state-mandated forms; they are association-provided templates that have become the de facto standard because of their integration with Indiana real estate practice.

**Form 40 requires the parties to fill in specific dates or day-counts for every contingency.** There is no statutory default that rescues a blank field. If an agent leaves the inspection deadline blank, the contract may be unenforceable on that point—or worse, a court may interpret silence as an immediate waiver.

Key contingency fields on Form 40 include the inspection contingency deadline, the appraisal contingency deadline, the financing contingency deadline, and the deadline for the buyer to deliver evidence of loan commitment. Each one must be negotiated, written in, and tracked independently.

| Contingency | Typical IAR Form Field | Common Day-Count Range | Consequence of Missing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inspection | Section 9, Form 40 | 10–14 calendar days | Deemed satisfied; buyer waives objections |
| Financing/Loan Commitment | Section 10, Form 40 | 21–30 calendar days | Buyer may lose right to terminate for financing failure |
| Appraisal | Section 11 (if elected) | Tied to financing deadline | Buyer accepts appraised value as-is |
| Title Objection | Section 6, Form 40 | 10 calendar days from title commitment receipt | Buyer deemed to accept title |
| HOA Document Review | Addendum (Form 54) | 5–10 calendar days | Buyer waives right to cancel based on HOA docs |

Agents should also understand **Form 45** (Counter Offer) because a counter resets acceptance and can shift every downstream deadline unless the parties explicitly recalculate.

## How Indiana Law Creates Agent Liability Around Deadlines

Indiana Code [IC 25-34.1-10-10](https://iga.in.gov/laws/2024/ic/titles/25#25-34.1-10) establishes the duties owed by a licensee to their client, including the obligation to exercise reasonable skill and care. The Indiana Real Estate Commission has interpreted this duty to encompass deadline management—not just advising a client that deadlines exist, but actively ensuring the client is positioned to perform on time.

**A missed deadline is not merely an administrative error; it can constitute a breach of the agent's statutory duty.** The Commission has authority under IC 25-34.1-6-5 to suspend or revoke a license, issue fines up to $10,000 per violation, and require additional education. In practice, most first-offense deadline failures result in a letter of reprimand or a mandatory continuing education order, but repeat offenders face suspension.

Civil liability runs alongside regulatory exposure. If a buyer loses earnest money because their agent failed to communicate an inspection deadline, the buyer can sue the agent and the brokerage for negligence. Indiana's comparative fault system (IC 34-51-2) means a jury can apportion blame, but an agent who had the date in writing and did nothing faces steep exposure.

The listing agent is not off the hook either. If a listing agent accepts a Form 46 inspection response after the deadline without disclosing the timing issue to the seller, the seller may have a claim against their own agent for failing to protect their contractual rights. These situations create [the exact breakdowns that derail closings](/blog/where-deals-break-down).

## Five Common Mistakes Indiana Agents Make With Contingency Deadlines

Errors in deadline management follow predictable patterns. Here are the five that the Indiana Real Estate Commission and E&O carriers see most frequently.

**First, agents fail to calculate the start date correctly.** Form 40 defines "Effective Date" as the date of final acceptance, but agents routinely confuse the date they signed with the date the other party received the signed acceptance. If a counter offer was sent at 11:45 PM and received the next morning, the Effective Date is the receipt date—not the signature date. One day of drift cascades through every contingency downstream.

Second, agents write "10 days" without specifying whether days are calendar or business days. The IAR form's general provisions state that calendar days apply unless otherwise specified, and that deadlines expiring on weekends or holidays roll to the next business day. Agents who verbally tell clients "you have until Friday" without checking whether Friday is a state holiday create avoidable disputes.

Third, agents treat the financing contingency as a passive waiting period. Indiana's Form 40 requires the buyer to deliver a loan commitment letter by the stated deadline—not merely to have applied. An agent who does not follow up with the lender weekly risks missing the moment when a condition cannot be met in time. This is one of the main reasons [agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) across every state.

Fourth, agents send inspection responses via methods that lack verifiable delivery timestamps. Form 40's notice provisions require delivery to the contact information stated in the agreement. An email to an unstated address, or a text message when the form specifies email, may not constitute valid notice—meaning the buyer's timely response could be treated as untimely.

Fifth, agents fail to document deadline extensions in writing. Verbal agreements to "give the buyer two more days" are unenforceable under Indiana's Statute of Frauds (IC 32-21-1-1) because they modify a real estate contract. Without a signed amendment, the original deadline controls and the contingency is waived.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Managing agents who handle dozens of transactions simultaneously requires systemic controls, not trust. Indiana's broker supervisory duties under IC 25-34.1-10-4.5 place the designated broker on the hook for failures in oversight. If an agent's repeated deadline mismanagement shows a pattern, the Commission can discipline the broker directly for inadequate supervision.

**Brokers should audit three areas on a recurring basis: deadline calculation accuracy, client communication logs, and amendment execution.** A quarterly file review is the minimum standard. A monthly spot-check of active transactions is better.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Deadline Calculation | Does the file contain a written timeline from Effective Date? | No timeline document; agent relying on memory |
| Client Communication | Are there timestamped emails/texts confirming deadline notice to the buyer? | Only verbal notes; no written confirmation |
| Amendment Execution | Are all deadline extensions memorialized on a signed IAR Amendment (Form 49)? | Verbal agreements with no paper trail |
| Delivery Method | Does notice match the contact method stated in Form 40? | Texts sent when contract specifies email |
| Lender Follow-Up | Is there documentation of financing status at least weekly? | No lender contact after application |

Brokers who rely on [checklists over memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) for these audits catch problems before they become claims. The goal is not to micromanage agents but to create a system where deadline failures surface during the transaction—not after the deal dies.

Indiana's Commission publishes disciplinary actions in its quarterly bulletin, and a growing percentage of cases in 2024 and 2025 involved deadline-related negligence. Brokers who can demonstrate a documented audit process have a strong defense if one agent's error leads to a complaint.

## Calendar Days, Holidays, and the Weekend Rule

Indiana does not have a statutory "day counting" rule specific to real estate contracts, which is why the IAR form's internal definitions matter so much. Section 19 of the 2026 Form 40 states that all time periods are computed in calendar days beginning the day after the Effective Date, and that any deadline falling on a Saturday, Sunday, or Indiana legal holiday extends to the next business day.

This creates a trap for agents working across state lines or referencing other states' rules. A deadline that would expire on Martin Luther King Jr. Day, Presidents' Day, or even the Friday after Thanksgiving (observed as a state holiday in Indiana) silently extends. Agents who mark their calendar with the raw count and never cross-reference the state holiday schedule hand the other side an unintended gift—or accidentally waive their client's rights.

The safest practice is to build a deadline chart the day a contract goes effective and verify each endpoint against Indiana's official holiday list. Automated transaction management tools eliminate this manual step, but the agent retains responsibility for confirming the output is correct.

## How Inspection Responses Interact With Contingency Deadlines

The inspection contingency in Indiana has a two-step structure that confuses new agents. First, the buyer must complete inspections and deliver a written response (typically on Form 46) by the inspection deadline stated in Form 40. Second, if the buyer requests repairs or credits, the seller has a separate response period—usually stated as a number of days from receipt of Form 46.

**If the buyer delivers Form 46 on time but the seller's response period has not been addressed in the agreement, the deal can enter a gray zone with no clear path to resolution.** Best practice is to ensure Form 40's inspection section explicitly states both the buyer's deadline and the seller's response window.

When Form 46 includes a "Buyer's Right to Cancel" provision, the buyer may terminate if the seller rejects the repair request—but only within the time frame stated. Agents who assume their buyer "can always walk away" after an inspection dispute are wrong unless the contract explicitly preserves that right post-response.
