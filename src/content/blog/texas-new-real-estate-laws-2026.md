## Texas New Real Estate Laws 2026: The Legislative Changes That Hit September 1

The 89th Texas Legislature wrapped its regular session in May 2025 and left behind a stack of real estate bills that reshape how agents operate starting this fall. Understanding **Texas new real estate laws 2026** is not optional — the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) has already updated multiple promulgated forms, and enforcement begins September 1, 2026. Agents who assume last year's practices still work are walking into disciplinary hearings.

This article breaks down the specific statutes, form changes, compliance risks, and broker-level audit requirements you need to address before the effective date. The changes span buyer representation, flood disclosures, property condition reporting, and commission transparency — and every one of them carries real consequences for agents who miss the mark.

## Buyer Representation Agreements Get Teeth Under SB 1184

**Senate Bill 1184 codifies what TREC had previously treated as a best-practice recommendation: written buyer representation agreements are now mandatory before any substantive real estate service.** The statute amends Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.558 and defines "substantive service" broadly — property showings, market analysis preparation, and offer strategy discussions all qualify.

TREC released the updated Buyer Representation Agreement form (TREC Form BRA-2) in June 2026. The new form requires agents to specify the exact compensation structure, including whether the buyer agent's fee comes from the seller, the buyer, or a combination. It also introduces a termination-at-will clause that either party can exercise with 10 days' written notice, replacing the old mutual-consent-only termination language.

The practical impact is significant. You cannot walk a buyer through an open house and then circle back to sign representation paperwork afterward. The timeline is reversed, and TREC has made clear through its June 2026 guidance bulletin that "first substantive contact" includes any showing, whether scheduled or impromptu.

## Expanded Flood and Environmental Disclosures Under HB 2491

House Bill 2491 expands the Seller's Disclosure Notice requirements under Texas Property Code Section 5.008. Sellers must now disclose not only known flooding events but also whether the property sits within a FEMA-designated 100-year or 500-year floodplain, whether flood insurance was ever held on the property, and whether any flood-related insurance claims were filed in the past 10 years.

**The updated Seller's Disclosure Notice (TREC Form OP-H, revised June 2026) adds three new questions in Section 9 addressing these flood history requirements.** Listing agents carry an affirmative advisory duty — you must inform your seller client about these expanded requirements and document that you did so. A simple email confirmation works, but verbal-only advisement puts you at risk.

The bill also adds a new standalone form, the Flood History Addendum (TREC Form FHA-1), required for any residential property located in a designated floodplain. Failure to attach this addendum to the contract gives the buyer a right to terminate within 7 days of receiving the information, without forfeiting earnest money. For agents tracking deadlines across multiple deals, this adds another date-sensitive compliance trigger to every [transaction in your pipeline](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals).

## The Updated IABS Form and Agency Disclosure Timing

TREC revised the Information About Brokerage Services form (TREC Form IABS 2-0) to reflect the new buyer representation mandate and updated compensation disclosure language. The form must be provided at "first substantive contact" — a standard that existed before but is now more strictly defined under the amended Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.558(d).

| Requirement | Previous Rule (Pre-Sept 2026) | New Rule (Post-Sept 2026) |
|---|---|---|
| IABS delivery timing | First face-to-face meeting | First substantive contact (including virtual) |
| Buyer rep agreement | Recommended | Mandatory before services |
| Compensation disclosure | General description | Specific dollar or percentage amount |
| Termination clause | Mutual consent | 10-day unilateral notice |
| Flood addendum | Not required | Required for floodplain properties |

The distinction between "face-to-face meeting" and "substantive contact" matters enormously for agents who begin client relationships over text, Zoom, or phone. A 20-minute phone call where you discuss pricing strategy now triggers the IABS requirement, even if you have never met the person in the same room.

## Common Mistakes Agents Will Make Under the New Laws

Based on TREC's enforcement patterns from prior regulatory changes, here are the errors most likely to generate complaints and disciplinary action after September 1.

**First, agents will continue showing properties before securing a signed BRA-2 form.** The old habit of "let me show you a few homes and then we'll talk about working together" is now a direct violation. TREC's enforcement division has indicated through its spring 2026 CE courses that this will be a priority audit target for the first 12 months.

Second, listing agents will fail to use the new Flood History Addendum (TREC Form FHA-1) on properties that clearly sit in mapped floodplains. The form is not optional for qualifying properties — it attaches to the contract alongside the standard Seller's Disclosure. Missing it gives buyers a termination right and exposes the listing agent to a TREC complaint for inadequate disclosure advisement.

Third, agents will deliver the old IABS 1-0 form instead of the updated IABS 2-0 version. TREC has not announced a grace period for use of outdated forms after September 1. Using the superseded version is treated the same as not providing the form at all — a violation that carries administrative penalties of up to $5,000 per occurrence under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.702.

Fourth, buyer agents will fail to clearly document the compensation source in the BRA-2 form, leaving the field vague or writing "TBD." The new form requires a definitive answer at the time of signing. Ambiguity here exposes agents to breach-of-fiduciary-duty claims from buyers who later discover unexpected out-of-pocket costs.

Fifth, agents will miss the 7-day termination window created by late delivery of the Flood History Addendum. If you deliver this form three days after execution instead of at signing, you start a clock that could blow up your deal — and the [true cost of a transaction falling apart](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) this late is something no agent wants to absorb.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.803 for the acts of their sponsored agents. The September 1 changes demand specific updates to brokerage policies, training programs, and file audit procedures.

| Broker Audit Item | Action Required | Deadline |
|---|---|---|
| BRA-2 form in every buyer file | Verify signed form precedes first showing date | Before September 1, 2026 |
| IABS 2-0 deployment | Remove old IABS 1-0 from all systems and templates | Before September 1, 2026 |
| Flood addendum tracking | Flag all floodplain listings for FHA-1 attachment | Ongoing after September 1 |
| Compensation field audit | Review BRA-2 forms for specificity | Monthly spot-check |
| Agent CE verification | Confirm all agents completed 2026 legal update CE | Before license renewal |

**Brokers should conduct a full form library purge before August 15, 2026.** Any transaction management system, cloud folder, or printed packet that still contains IABS 1-0 or the pre-revision OP-H creates liability. This is not a "when we get to it" task — it requires a documented sweep with agent acknowledgment signatures confirming receipt of updated materials.

Training is equally critical. The 89th Legislature's changes intersect with the NAR settlement requirements that took effect in 2024, and many agents still conflate the two sets of rules. Brokers should run a mandatory compliance meeting specifically addressing the Texas-specific statutory additions that go beyond the NAR-driven changes. ## Penalties and Liability for Non-Compliance

TREC's enforcement authority under Chapter 1101 of the Texas Occupations Code gives the commission multiple tools for addressing violations. Administrative penalties range from $1,000 to $5,000 per violation, and repeated offenses can trigger license suspension or revocation proceedings before the State Office of Administrative Hearings (SOAH).

Beyond TREC action, agents face civil liability. A buyer who can demonstrate that they were not provided the required Flood History Addendum or IABS form has grounds for rescission of the purchase contract and recovery of damages, including earnest money, inspection costs, and potentially attorney's fees under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA), Chapter 17 of the Texas Business and Commerce Code.

The buyer's 7-day termination right under HB 2491 adds deal-cancellation risk to every floodplain transaction where paperwork arrives late. This is not a theoretical risk — Harris County alone contains over 160,000 residential properties in mapped floodplains. Agents working the Houston, Beaumont, and Corpus Christi markets will encounter this form requirement on a significant percentage of their listings.

> "A license holder who fails to provide the flood history addendum required under Section 5.008(g) of the Texas Property Code at the time of contract execution shall be deemed to have committed a violation subject to disciplinary action under Section 1101.652(b)." — TREC Guidance Bulletin 2026-04

For reference, the full text of the updated Texas Occupations Code Chapter 1101 is available through the [Texas Legislature Online](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/OC/htm/OC.1101.htm) portal.

## How to Prepare Your Practice Before September 1

Start with your forms. Download every updated TREC promulgated form from the TREC website and replace every prior version in your transaction files, CRM templates, and any digital signing platform you use. Verify that your dotloop, DocuSign, or SkySlope libraries are current — most platforms require manual updates for state-specific forms.

Next, audit your buyer intake process. Map the exact point at which you currently begin providing "substantive services" and ensure the BRA-2 signing happens before that point. For most agents, this means the buyer representation agreement must be signed before or during the initial consultation, not at the offer stage.

Finally, build a [reliable checklist system](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) that flags floodplain properties automatically. If your MLS provides flood zone data in listing fields, use that trigger to remind yourself about the FHA-1 requirement before you even draft the listing agreement.
