## Understanding California Title & Closing Procedures 2026

California's escrow-based closing system operates differently from attorney-state closings, and agents who treat it casually expose themselves and their clients to real financial harm. The rules governing **California title & closing procedures 2026** are scattered across the Insurance Code, Civil Code, and California Association of Realtors (C.A.R.) form updates, making compliance a moving target for even experienced licensees. This post breaks down what agents and brokers must know—statute by statute, form by form—to keep transactions on track and avoid regulatory exposure.

California is one of the few states where escrow officers, rather than attorneys, manage the closing process. The Department of Insurance regulates title companies, while the Department of Real Estate (DRE) regulates agent conduct during the transaction. Agents who blur the line between facilitating and directing the closing process risk unauthorized practice of law claims under California Business and Professions Code Section 6125.

## The Escrow Timeline and Agent Responsibilities

**The moment a purchase agreement is fully executed, the clock starts running on a series of contractual deadlines that agents must track independently of escrow.** The C.A.R. Residential Purchase Agreement (Form RPA, revised 2024) sets default timeframes: 17 days for the investigation contingency, 21 days for the loan contingency, and 21 days for the appraisal contingency. Missing these deadlines triggers contractual consequences that vary depending on which party defaults.

Agents are responsible for delivering the signed purchase agreement to the escrow holder within three business days of acceptance. California Civil Code Section 1057.3 requires that the buyer's deposit be placed into escrow within the timeframe specified in the contract—typically three business days. Failure to deliver the deposit on time gives the seller grounds to issue a Notice to Buyer to Perform (C.A.R. Form NBP), which starts a two-day cure period before cancellation rights activate.

The preliminary title report (often called a "prelim") must be reviewed by the buyer's agent as soon as it arrives from the title company. Under California Insurance Code Section 12340.11, the preliminary report is not a guarantee of title—it is an offer to issue a policy subject to the stated exceptions. Agents who fail to explain this distinction to buyers risk client complaints and potential DRE disciplinary action for negligence.

## Key Forms and Documents Agents Must Track

California transactions involve a specific document stack that agents are expected to monitor throughout escrow. Knowing the form numbers matters because incorrect or outdated forms can void contingency protections.

| Form Number | Form Name | Purpose | Typical Deadline |
|---|---|---|---|
| C.A.R. RPA | Residential Purchase Agreement | Governs the entire transaction | Day 1 |
| C.A.R. AD | Agency Disclosure | Confirms agency relationships | Before or with offer |
| C.A.R. TDS | Transfer Disclosure Statement | Seller's property condition disclosure | 7 days after acceptance |
| C.A.R. SPQ | Seller Property Questionnaire | Supplements the TDS | Delivered with TDS |
| C.A.R. AVID | Agent Visual Inspection Disclosure | Agent's independent findings | 7 days after acceptance |
| C.A.R. CR | Contingency Removal | Buyer removes investigation/loan contingencies | Per RPA deadlines |

The Agent Visual Inspection Disclosure (Form AVID) deserves special attention. Under California Civil Code Section 2079, both the listing agent and the buyer's agent owe an independent duty to conduct a reasonably competent visual inspection of the property. This is not optional. Agents who skip the inspection or submit a boilerplate AVID expose themselves to personal liability for defects that a reasonable inspection would have revealed.

The Transfer Disclosure Statement (Form TDS), governed by Civil Code Sections 1102-1102.17, must be delivered to the buyer. Once delivered, the buyer has three days (if delivered in person) or five days (if delivered by mail) to rescind the agreement based on the disclosures. Agents often forget that this rescission right is statutory—it exists regardless of what the contract says. For context on how other states handle similar disclosure obligations, see our coverage of [Washington property condition disclosures](/blog/washington-property-condition-disclosures-2026).

## Who Pays for What: Buyer vs. Seller Cost Allocation

**Cost allocation in California closings is governed partly by contract, partly by local custom, and partly by statute—agents who assume "standard" splits without checking their county norms create problems.** The RPA allows negotiation of most closing costs, but certain allocations are deeply entrenched by geographic custom.

| Cost Item | Northern California Custom | Southern California Custom |
|---|---|---|
| Owner's title insurance policy | Seller pays | Seller pays |
| Lender's title insurance policy | Buyer pays | Buyer pays |
| Escrow fee | Split 50/50 | Split 50/50 |
| Transfer tax (county) | Seller pays | Split varies by county |
| City transfer tax | Seller pays (where applicable) | Seller pays (where applicable) |
| Natural Hazard Disclosure report | Seller pays | Seller pays |
| Home warranty | Negotiable | Negotiable |

County-level documentary transfer taxes are governed by California Revenue and Taxation Code Section 11911, assessed at $1.10 per $1,000 of consideration. Some cities—Los Angeles, San Francisco, Oakland, and others—impose additional city transfer taxes with their own rate structures. San Francisco's transfer tax ranges from 0.50% to 6.00% depending on property value, a detail that catches agents from other counties off guard.

California Insurance Code Section 12404 prohibits a seller from conditioning the sale on the buyer purchasing title insurance from a particular company. Agents who steer buyers toward a preferred title company as a contract requirement—rather than a recommendation—violate this statute and expose the transaction to regulatory scrutiny.

## Common Mistakes California Agents Make at Closing

Five recurring errors account for the majority of DRE complaints and transaction failures related to title and closing.

**First, agents routinely confuse contingency removal with contingency expiration.** Under the current RPA, contingency deadlines are not self-executing. If the buyer does not actively remove contingencies by delivering Form CR, the seller must issue a Notice to Buyer to Perform (Form NBP) and wait an additional two days before gaining cancellation rights. Agents who advise sellers that the deal automatically cancels on the contingency date are dispensing incorrect legal guidance.

Second, agents fail to review the preliminary title report for items that affect the buyer's intended use. Easements, CC&Rs, and liens of record appear on the prelim. An agent who never reads the exceptions section cannot advise the buyer to investigate further—and the buyer's agent's duty of visual inspection under Civil Code 2079 extends to reviewing reasonably available documents that affect the property.

Third, agents submit the wrong version of forms after C.A.R. updates its library. C.A.R. revises forms periodically, and using a superseded version may void contractual protections. Agents must verify they are using the current revision date printed on the bottom of each form.

Fourth, agents neglect to confirm that all parties have signed the grant deed before the closing date. In California, the grant deed transfers ownership upon recording with the county recorder's office. If the seller has not signed the deed and delivered it to escrow in time, recording cannot occur on schedule. Escrow cannot force this—the agent needs to track it.

Fifth, agents forget that the California Withholding Law (Revenue and Taxation Code Section 18662) requires withholding 3.33% of the sale price from out-of-state sellers unless an exemption applies. Title companies typically handle this, but the listing agent should confirm compliance to avoid post-closing tax complications for the seller.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under Business and Professions Code Section 10159.2, which requires a designated broker-officer to oversee all transaction activities. The [DRE's enforcement division](https://www.dre.ca.gov/Consumers/EnforcementActions.html) has consistently disciplined brokers who failed to implement file review systems, even when the agent—not the broker—committed the error.

**Brokers should audit every file for five items before the transaction records.** First, confirm that the Agency Disclosure (Form AD) was presented to all parties and signed before or concurrent with the offer. Second, verify that the AVID was completed with property-specific observations—not a checkbox indicating "no defects observed" on every line. Third, check that contingency removal forms were signed and dated within the contractual windows. Fourth, ensure the estimated closing statement from escrow matches the terms negotiated in the RPA and any counter-offers. Fifth, confirm that all required natural hazard and statutory disclosures were delivered within the required timeframes.

Broker liability under California case law extends beyond regulatory fines. In *Salahutdin v. Valley of California, Inc.* (1994), the court held that brokers owe a duty to supervise agents' transaction conduct. A broker who rubber-stamps files without meaningful review cannot claim ignorance as a defense. This matters because agents who handle [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously are statistically more likely to miss deadlines or deliver incomplete disclosures.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

The DRE can impose formal discipline ranging from a citation with a fine (typically $500-$2,500 per violation) to license suspension or revocation. Under Business and Professions Code Section 10176(i), a licensee who demonstrates negligence or incompetence in performing licensed activities faces disciplinary action regardless of whether the client suffered actual damages.

Beyond regulatory exposure, agents face civil liability. A buyer who closes on a property with undisclosed title defects—defects the agent should have caught during prelim review—can sue for damages, rescission, or both. California courts have awarded six-figure judgments against agents who failed to satisfy their Civil Code 2079 inspection duties.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate consequence. When an agent misses a contingency deadline or fails to deliver required disclosures, the opposing party gains leverage to renegotiate or terminate. In competitive markets, a canceled transaction costs clients time, money, and opportunity—and those clients talk. Agents who [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) repeatedly develop a reputation that follows them through escrow and title company circles.
