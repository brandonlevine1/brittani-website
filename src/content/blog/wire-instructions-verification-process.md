## The Cost of Getting Wire Instructions Wrong

Wire fraud cost real estate consumers over $446 million in 2023, according to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center. That figure represents reported losses only — the actual number is almost certainly higher. The wire instructions verification process every TC should use is not optional anymore; it is risk management at its most basic level.

A single compromised wire averages $150,000 to $500,000 in lost funds for a residential buyer. Recovery rates hover below 20% once 72 hours pass. For the agent or brokerage involved, the downstream damage includes E&O claims, regulatory complaints, reputational fallout, and months of legal exposure that no commission check justifies.

If you are managing 5 to 15 active deals simultaneously, the probability that one of your buyers will receive a spoofed wire instruction email during the next 12 months is statistically significant. The American Land Title Association estimates that one in three real estate transactions is now targeted by business email compromise attempts. The question is not whether your process is "good enough" — it is whether your process exists at all.

## Why Traditional Email-Based Workflows Fail

The standard closing workflow looks something like this: the title company sends wire instructions to the buyer via email, sometimes CC'ing the agents. The buyer takes those instructions to their bank and wires the funds. The problem is that email was never designed to be a secure document delivery system.

Business email compromise attacks work by infiltrating one party's inbox — often the agent's or the title company's — and monitoring email threads for weeks before closing. When the wire instructions email goes out, the attacker either intercepts it and replaces the PDF with altered routing numbers, or sends a convincing follow-up email with "updated" instructions from a slightly misspelled domain.

**The attack does not require sophisticated hacking.** A single compromised password, a phished login, or a forwarded email to an unsecured personal account is enough. By the time anyone realizes the wire went to a fraudulent account, the funds have been layered through multiple transfers and withdrawn. This is why [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without a verification protocol is a liability multiplier.

## The Seven-Step Verification Protocol

What follows is a concrete, repeatable system that adds roughly 15 minutes of work per transaction but eliminates the most common failure points in wire instruction handling. Every TC managing files for an agent team should implement this as a non-negotiable checklist item.

**Step 1: Obtain wire instructions exclusively through the title company's secure portal.** Do not accept wire instructions via standard email attachment. If the title company does not offer a secure portal, request that they fax the instructions or provide them in person. Document your request in writing.

**Step 2: Independently verify the title company's phone number.** Look up the number on the title company's official website or your original engagement letter. Never use a phone number that appears in the same email containing the wire instructions.

Step 3: Call the title company and verbally confirm the routing number, account number, and beneficiary name digit by digit. Have the person on the line read the numbers back to you, not the other way around. Note the name of the person who confirmed and the date and time of the call.

Step 4: Send your buyer a secure communication — not standard email — with the verified instructions, along with explicit written language warning them not to trust any "updated" instructions received via email without calling you directly first.

Step 5: Instruct the buyer to call their bank and request a callback verification for any wire over $10,000. Most banks offer this service but do not proactively suggest it.

Step 6: On the day of the scheduled wire, confirm with the title company that funds have not yet been received, then confirm again once the wire lands. If there is a delay beyond the expected window, escalate immediately.

Step 7: Document every step in your transaction file. If a dispute arises months later, your dated notes showing verbal confirmation, the name of the confirming party, and your written buyer warning are your best defense.

## What to Include in Your Buyer Wire Fraud Warning

Every buyer in your pipeline should receive a written warning about wire fraud no later than the ratified contract stage. This is not legal advice — it is operational best practice that reduces your exposure and educates your client before the high-pressure closing week.

Your warning should state in plain language that you will never send updated wire instructions via email. It should include a specific phone number the buyer can call to verify any instructions they receive. It should explicitly say that if they get an email with "corrected" or "updated" wire information, they should stop and call you before acting.

> The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau recommends that all parties in a real estate transaction independently verify wire transfer instructions through a known, trusted phone number before initiating any transfer of funds.

Some brokerages now require this warning as a signed addendum to the buyer representation agreement. Whether yours mandates it or not, adding it to your file protects you in the event of a post-closing claim.

## Red Flags That Should Trigger an Immediate Hold

Not every transaction will present obvious warning signs, but certain patterns should halt the wire process until re-verification is complete. Train your eye — and your buyers' eyes — to catch these before money moves.

**Any email requesting a change to previously provided wire instructions is a red flag, full stop.** Title companies rarely change wire routing mid-transaction. If they do, the change should be confirmed by a phone call you initiate to their independently verified number.

Watch for subtle email address variations: a lowercase "L" replaced with a numeral "1," a domain that adds an extra letter, or a reply-to address that differs from the sender address. These are hallmarks of spoofed communications that slip past distracted readers during closing week chaos.

Pressure to wire immediately "or the closing will be delayed" is another tactic. Legitimate title companies understand that buyers need time to verify. Anyone pushing urgency over accuracy is either compromised or operating with dangerously poor processes.

## Broker-Level Implementation: Making This Protocol Stick

If you manage an agent team, your role is not to verify every wire yourself. Your role is to build the system, train the behavior, and audit compliance. A protocol that lives only in a training manual accomplishes nothing.

Start by adding wire verification to your [new agent training timeline](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) as a required competency, not an elective topic. Role-play the phone verification call. Show agents what a spoofed email looks like versus a legitimate one. Test them with simulated phishing messages — brokerages that do this report 60% higher detection rates among their agents.

Audit a random sample of closed files monthly. Check for documentation of the verbal verification call, the buyer warning, and the day-of confirmation. When you find gaps, address them immediately. The brokerage that discovers its own process failures before a loss event has the opportunity to fix them. The one that discovers them during litigation does not.

## The Liability Landscape for Agents and TCs

The [NAR Code of Ethics](https://www.nar.realtor/about-nar/governing-documents/code-of-ethics/2025-code-of-ethics-standards-of-practice) establishes a general duty to protect client interests, and multiple state courts have found that agents who failed to warn buyers about wire fraud — or who forwarded unverified instructions — breached their fiduciary duty. This is not hypothetical case law. It is happening in courtrooms right now.

E&O insurance policies increasingly contain exclusions or sublimits for wire fraud claims. Your policy may cover defense costs but not the actual loss. Read your policy's wire fraud and cyber liability sections carefully. If you are uncertain, call your carrier and ask specifically what is and is not covered.

The practical reality is that verification takes minutes and litigation takes years. The TC who builds this protocol into every file is not being paranoid — they are being professional. The agent who delegates this step without confirming it was completed is carrying a risk they may not fully appreciate until it materializes.
