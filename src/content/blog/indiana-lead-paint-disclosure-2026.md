## Understanding Indiana Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

If you list or sell pre-1978 residential property in Indiana, the Indiana lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 apply to your transaction regardless of price point, property condition, or whether anyone "thinks" lead is present. The federal Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, codified at 42 U.S.C. § 4852d) sets the floor, and Indiana enforces it through the Indiana State Department of Health (ISDH) and the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC). Ignorance of these rules does not reduce your exposure—it increases it.

Indiana does not maintain a separate state-level lead paint disclosure statute that supersedes the federal mandate. Instead, compliance flows through two channels: the federal EPA/HUD regulation at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H, and Indiana Administrative Code Title 410, Article 32 (lead-based paint programs). Agents must satisfy both or risk disciplinary action from IREC under IC 25-34.1-11-5, which governs license violations tied to material misrepresentation and failure to disclose.

The practical result is straightforward. Every listing agent on a pre-1978 home must deliver the disclosure form, attach the EPA pamphlet, and give the buyer a 10-day inspection window—or document that the buyer waived it. Missing any one of those steps puts your license, your brokerage's E&O policy, and the deal itself at risk.

## Which Properties Trigger the Disclosure

Not every Indiana transaction requires a lead paint disclosure. The trigger is construction date, not property type. Any "target housing" built before January 1, 1978, falls under the mandate when sold or leased.

Exemptions exist but are narrow: zero-bedroom dwellings (studios, lofts without sleeping areas), housing for the elderly or disabled where children under six do not reside, short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or fewer, and foreclosure sales. Agents sometimes assume that "as-is" sales or bank-owned properties are exempt—they are not. HUD made this explicit in its 1996 interpretive guidance, and Indiana follows it without modification.

**If you handle rural farmhouse listings—common across central and southern Indiana—these properties are some of the most likely to contain lead-based paint and the least likely to have prior testing documentation.** Agents working these markets should treat the disclosure as a default rather than an exception.

## The Specific Forms and Documents Indiana Agents Must Use

Indiana does not publish its own state-branded lead paint disclosure form through IREC. Instead, agents use the Indiana Association of Realtors (IAR) disclosure addendum or the equivalent form provided by their MLS or brokerage. The most commonly referenced form is the **IAR Sales Disclosure – Lead-Based Paint Addendum**, which mirrors the federal model disclosure language found in 40 CFR Part 745, Appendix A.

Three documents must be delivered to the buyer before the purchase agreement becomes binding:

The completed and signed Lead-Based Paint Disclosure form, which includes the seller's statement about known hazards and any available reports. The EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" (EPA-747-K-12-001, revised 2021). And a signed acknowledgment from the buyer confirming receipt of both items plus their election to conduct or waive a lead-based paint inspection within 10 calendar days.

| Document | Who Provides It | When It Must Be Delivered | Who Signs |
|----------|----------------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Form (IAR addendum) | Listing agent / seller | Before buyer is obligated under contract | Seller, buyer, both agents |
| EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" | Listing agent | At or before disclosure form delivery | Buyer acknowledges receipt |
| 10-Day Inspection Opportunity Notice | Listing agent | Incorporated into disclosure form | Buyer signs waiver or election |
| Any existing lead inspection reports | Seller (through listing agent) | With disclosure form | N/A (attached as exhibits) |

Agents who work across state lines should note that Indiana's approach differs from states like [Texas](/blog/texas-lead-paint-disclosure-2026) or [Virginia](/blog/virginia-lead-paint-disclosure-2026), which may layer additional state-specific addenda on top of the federal requirement.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The penalties for noncompliance are not theoretical. HUD and the EPA have jointly enforced lead paint disclosure violations since 1996, and fines have only increased. As of 2024, the maximum civil penalty per violation is $19,507 under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act, and that number adjusts annually.

> "Any person who knowingly fails to comply with any provision of this section shall be jointly and severally liable to the purchaser or lessee in an amount equal to 3 times the amount of damages incurred by such individual." — 42 U.S.C. § 4852d(b)(3)

Beyond federal penalties, Indiana's Real Estate Commission can pursue disciplinary action under IC 25-34.1-11-5(a)(10), which authorizes license suspension or revocation for "failing to account for or to remit any moneys or documents coming into the licensee's possession that belong to others" and for conduct demonstrating incompetency or untrustworthiness. A pattern of missed disclosures could easily fall under that umbrella.

**Deal cancellation is the most immediate practical consequence.** A buyer who never received the disclosure can rescind the purchase agreement, recover earnest money, and pursue damages—including inspection costs, moving expenses, and attorney fees. For a brokerage, even one rescission triggered by a compliance gap can cascade into an E&O claim and reputational damage that far exceeds the fine itself.

## Common Mistakes Indiana Agents Make

Five errors show up repeatedly in Indiana transactions involving pre-1978 properties. Each one is preventable with proper systems and awareness.

**First, agents assume the seller's verbal denial of lead paint knowledge is sufficient.** It is not. The disclosure form requires the seller's signature next to a specific written statement—either affirming known hazards and attaching reports, or affirming no knowledge. A verbal "there's no lead paint" during a listing appointment does not satisfy the regulation.

Second, agents deliver the disclosure form at closing instead of before the buyer becomes contractually obligated. The 10-day inspection window must begin before the contract is binding. Handing the pamphlet across the closing table is too late—the buyer's right to a pre-contract inspection opportunity has already been violated.

Third, agents fail to retain signed copies for three years. Under 24 CFR § 35.92, sellers and agents must keep the signed disclosure and acknowledgment forms for no less than three years from the date of sale. Indiana brokers who purge files after closing without archiving these documents expose themselves to enforcement actions years after the fact.

Fourth, dual agents or transaction licensees forget that their independent duty to ensure compliance exists regardless of which party they represent. The regulation places an obligation on "the agent of the seller or lessor"—but HUD guidance makes clear that any agent involved in the transaction who is aware of the property's age has a duty not to facilitate a noncompliant sale.

Fifth, agents incorrectly exempt condominiums. Individual condo units in buildings constructed before 1978 are target housing and require disclosure. The building's age—not the date of the unit's interior renovation—controls.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Indiana law. IC 25-34.1-10-10 requires designated brokers to supervise the activities of licensees associated with the brokerage. When an agent misses a lead paint disclosure, the broker's file review process—or lack of one—becomes part of the enforcement inquiry.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|-------------------|---------------|-----------|
| Disclosure form presence | Signed IAR Lead-Based Paint Addendum in file | Every pre-1978 transaction |
| EPA pamphlet delivery confirmation | Buyer's signed acknowledgment of receipt | Every pre-1978 transaction |
| 10-Day inspection election/waiver | Buyer's written election or waiver on file | Every pre-1978 transaction |

Brokers should build a checklist into their transaction management workflow that flags any listing where the property's construction year precedes 1978. If your brokerage uses a [transaction handoff process](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist), the lead paint disclosure status should be a mandatory field at the handoff stage—not something discovered during a pre-closing scramble.

Quarterly file audits are a minimum standard. Brokerages handling volume in Indianapolis, Fort Wayne, Evansville, or South Bend—cities with large inventories of pre-1978 housing stock—should consider monthly spot checks. The cost of a $19,507 fine per missed disclosure dwarfs the administrative time spent reviewing files.

## The 10-Day Inspection Period: Practical Guidance

The buyer's right to a 10-day lead-based paint inspection is not optional to offer. You can negotiate a different time period (longer or shorter) in writing, or the buyer can waive the inspection entirely, but the opportunity must be presented. Agents cannot simply omit this provision from the contract.

In practice, most Indiana buyers waive the inspection. That waiver must be documented in writing on the disclosure form—a checkbox and signature, not a verbal agreement during negotiations. If the buyer elects to inspect, the 10-day clock starts from the date they receive the disclosure, not the date of mutual acceptance.

**Agents who [manage multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously are the most likely to let this deadline slip past without proper documentation.** Calendar the waiver or inspection election deadline the moment the disclosure is delivered, and confirm the signed waiver is returned before the contract becomes fully executed.
