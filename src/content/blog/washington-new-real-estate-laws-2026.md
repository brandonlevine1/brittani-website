The 2026 legislative session in Olympia produced more real estate reform than any cycle since 2019. If you hold a Washington license and have not reviewed the changes yet, you are already behind. This article breaks down every statute, form update, and enforcement mechanism that shapes **Washington new real estate laws 2026**—so you can adjust your practice before penalties hit.

Governor Ferguson signed three bills between March and May that directly alter transaction workflow for residential agents. Meanwhile, the Washington Department of Licensing (DOL) finalized WAC rule amendments that took effect July 1. The combined result touches buyer representation, seller disclosures, commission transparency, and wire fraud prevention.

## Buyer Agency Agreements Are Now Mandatory Before Services Begin

**HB 1320, codified as an amendment to RCW 18.86.030, removes the previous gray area around when a buyer agency relationship must be formalized.** Before 2026, agents could argue that "casual" showings did not constitute representation. That argument is dead.

The statute now requires a signed written agreement before any "substantive real estate service," which the law defines to include conducting property tours, preparing a comparative market analysis for a buyer, or submitting an offer on a buyer's behalf. The only exception is a single open house interaction where the attending agent is the listing agent.

Washington's Northwest Multiple Listing Service (NWMLS) has released an updated Form 41A—Buyer Agency Agreement—to comply with the new language. The form explicitly states the duration of representation, the compensation terms, and the services included. If you are still using a pre-2026 version, it does not satisfy the statutory requirements.

## Updated Seller Disclosure Requirements Under RCW 64.06

SB 5047 expanded the seller disclosure statement (Form 17) to include three new categories: prior insurance claims related to water intrusion, the presence of any unpermitted accessory dwelling unit (ADU), and known proximity to planned public infrastructure projects. These additions reflect the state's ongoing ADU expansion push and the persistent water-damage litigation clogging Washington courts.

Sellers must complete the updated Form 17 and deliver it to the buyer no later than five business days after mutual acceptance—unchanged from prior law. However, the penalty structure is new. Under the amended RCW 64.06.040, a seller who intentionally omits information on the expanded sections faces treble damages rather than the prior single-damage cap.

Listing agents carry a secondary duty here. The DOL's guidance letter issued in April 2026 clarifies that a listing agent who knows a seller's Form 17 is incomplete and fails to advise the seller to correct it has violated RCW 18.86.050(2)—the duty to deal honestly and in good faith. That violation can trigger a licensing complaint independent of any civil suit.

## Washington New Real Estate Laws 2026: Commission Transparency and Form 22T

**The most contentious change this cycle is the new Form 22T—Transaction Compensation Disclosure—which NWMLS adopted in response to HB 1488.** This form requires both the listing agent and the buyer's agent to disclose, in writing and attached to the purchase and sale agreement, the exact dollar amount or percentage each expects to receive from the transaction.

HB 1488 amends RCW 18.86.120 to require that this disclosure occur no later than the point of mutual acceptance. The buyer and seller must each initial the form acknowledging they have reviewed the other side's compensation arrangement. Failure to attach Form 22T does not void the contract automatically, but it gives either party grounds to rescind within five business days of discovering the omission.

| Requirement | Before 2026 | After July 1, 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| Written buyer agency agreement | Recommended but not required before showings | Required before any substantive service (RCW 18.86.030) |
| Commission disclosure to both parties | Only within the listing agreement and buyer agreement separately | Must be cross-disclosed via Form 22T at mutual acceptance |
| Seller disclosure categories | 35 items on Form 17 | 38 items, including water-intrusion claims, unpermitted ADUs, infrastructure proximity |
| Wire fraud verbal confirmation | Best practice only | Affirmative duty under WAC 308-124 |
| Agency pamphlet delivery timing | Before signing an agreement | Before signing OR before substantive services—whichever is first |

The DOL has stated it will treat missing Form 22T disclosures as a violation of WAC 308-124C-137, which governs advertising and disclosure standards. That means potential license suspension for repeat offenders.

## Wire Fraud Prevention: New Affirmative Duties for Agents

Washington experienced a 38% increase in reported real estate wire fraud attempts between 2023 and 2025, according to the state Attorney General's office. The legislature responded with HB 1612, which amends WAC 308-124 to create affirmative duties for licensees—not just closing agents—around wire instruction verification.

**Under the new rule, any licensee who transmits or facilitates the transmission of wiring instructions to a client must first verbally verify those instructions with the closing agent using a phone number independently obtained—not one from the email containing the instructions.** The rule requires the agent to document the date, time, and name of the person who confirmed the instructions.

Failure to follow this protocol does not automatically create civil liability for the agent if fraud occurs. However, the DOL can pursue disciplinary action under the theory that the agent failed to exercise reasonable skill and care per RCW 18.86.050. The practical takeaway: build a verbal verification step into every closing workflow, and log it.

## Common Mistakes Agents Will Make Under the New Laws

Based on early DOL guidance sessions and conversations with managing brokers across King, Pierce, and Spokane counties, five errors are already surfacing.

**First, agents are using outdated Form 41A templates.** Some brokerages downloaded the NWMLS form in early 2025 for a training session and never updated their transaction management systems. The pre-2026 version lacks the required compensation specificity language and will not satisfy HB 1320.

Second, listing agents are treating Form 22T as optional or "only needed if the buyer asks." The statute does not condition disclosure on a request. It is mandatory at mutual acceptance regardless of whether anyone raises the topic.

Third, agents are confusing the timing requirement for the agency disclosure pamphlet. The amended RCW 18.86.110 says delivery must happen before the consumer signs any agreement OR before the agent provides substantive services. Many agents still hand the pamphlet over at the first showing, which is often too late—because they already sent the buyer a CMA or discussed negotiation strategy by email.

Fourth, agents are failing to log wire verification calls. The WAC amendment requires documentation, not just the call itself. Without a log entry, the agent has no defense if a complaint is filed.

Fifth, some listing agents are not advising sellers about the three new Form 17 categories because the agents themselves have not read the updated form. Ignorance of the update does not shield the agent from a DOL complaint.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce Immediately

Managing brokers carry supervisory liability under RCW 18.86.190. If an affiliated agent violates any of the new statutes, the DOL can pursue the managing broker for failure to supervise—even if the broker personally did nothing wrong.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Form 41A version | Confirm all agents have the 2026-compliant buyer agency agreement loaded in their transaction systems | Immediately and at every quarterly review |
| Form 22T attachment | Verify Form 22T is present in every transaction file at mutual acceptance | Every transaction, pre-closing review |
| Wire verification logs | Confirm documentation exists for every wire instruction transmitted | Every transaction involving wired funds |
| Agency pamphlet timing | Check CRM or email records to confirm pamphlet delivery preceded first substantive service | Random monthly file audits |
| Form 17 completeness | Spot-check seller disclosures for the three new categories | Every new listing |

Brokers should update their office policy manuals before August 1, 2026. The DOL has signaled through its spring newsletter that it intends to begin targeted audits of mid-size brokerages (25–100 agents) in Q4 2026, focusing specifically on buyer agency agreement compliance and Form 22T attachment rates.

Delegation matters here. A broker who assigns transaction file review to an unlicensed assistant without personally spot-checking files is still on the hook. The statute requires the broker to "reasonably supervise," and courts have interpreted that to mean personal involvement in compliance checks—not just assigning them.

## Penalties and Liability: What Noncompliance Actually Costs

The DOL's disciplinary matrix, updated in May 2026, assigns specific penalty ranges to violations of the new statutes. A first offense for missing Form 22T carries a fine between $500 and $2,500 plus mandatory continuing education. A second offense within 24 months can result in a 30-day license suspension.

For buyer agency agreement violations under HB 1320, the penalty is steeper because the legislature classified it as a consumer protection issue. First offense: $1,000 to $5,000 fine. Second offense: suspension up to 90 days. The DOL can also require the agent to refund any compensation earned in a transaction where no valid agreement existed—a provision that has already generated two administrative complaints in Thurston County.

> "A licensee who provides substantive real estate services without a written buyer agency agreement in effect is deemed to have provided those services without authorization, and any compensation received may be subject to disgorgement." — RCW 18.86.030(4), effective January 1, 2026.

Beyond DOL discipline, civil liability looms. A buyer who discovers after closing that their agent never signed a valid buyer agency agreement could argue the agent lacked authority to bind them. The seller could similarly challenge the transaction's validity. These scenarios invite contract rescission claims that cost everyone time, legal fees, and closed deals.
