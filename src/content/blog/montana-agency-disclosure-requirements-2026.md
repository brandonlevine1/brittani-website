## Understanding Montana Agency Disclosure Requirements 2026

Montana agency disclosure requirements 2026 continue to trip up even experienced agents, not because the rules are buried in obscure code, but because the timing and documentation standards leave zero room for shortcuts. The Montana Board of Realty Regulation enforces these requirements under Title 37, Chapter 51 of the Montana Code Annotated (MCA), and the consequences for noncompliance range from deal rescission to license suspension. If you close transactions in this state, you need to know exactly when, how, and with what form you owe a disclosure.

The statutory framework rests on MCA 37-51-313 through 37-51-321, which together establish the duty to disclose agency relationships, define permissible relationship types, and outline remedies for violations. Montana recognizes seller agency, buyer agency, dual agency, and statutory brokerage (sometimes called transaction brokerage). Each relationship carries distinct duties, and the disclosure form must clearly identify which one applies before substantive negotiations begin.

## The Forms You Actually Need to Use

**Montana agents rely primarily on two forms published by the Montana Association of Realtors (MAR) to satisfy their statutory obligations.** MAR Form 9 is the Agency Disclosure and Acknowledgment form, which informs the consumer about the types of agency relationships available under Montana law. MAR Form 10 is the Consent to Agency Relationship, the document that memorializes the consumer's informed choice.

Some brokerages substitute internally drafted forms approved by legal counsel, but those must meet the minimum content requirements outlined in Administrative Rules of Montana (ARM) 24.210.641. The Board has made clear in past enforcement actions that simply describing agency verbally does not satisfy the written disclosure mandate. You need ink on paper or an authenticated electronic signature.

| Form | Purpose | Timing | Parties Who Sign |
|------|---------|--------|-----------------|
| MAR Form 9 | Disclose all available agency types | First substantive contact | Agent and consumer |
| MAR Form 10 | Memorialize chosen agency relationship | Before confidential info exchanged | Agent, consumer, and broker (if dual) |
| Brokerage-specific equivalent | Same as above (must meet ARM standards) | Same statutory deadlines | Same parties |

If you work across state lines, note that Montana's timing requirements differ from those in neighboring states. Oregon, for example, has its own first-contact standard with [different form protocols](/blog/oregon-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) that agents licensed in both states sometimes conflate.

## Timing: When "First Substantive Contact" Actually Means

The phrase "first substantive contact" appears throughout MCA 37-51-313, and it remains the single greatest source of compliance failures. Montana statute defines it as any interaction where confidential information might be disclosed or where the consumer could reasonably believe an agency relationship exists. An open house greeting does not trigger the requirement, but the moment a visitor starts asking about the seller's motivation or you begin discussing pricing strategy, the clock starts.

**A phone call counts.** If a buyer calls you from a Zillow listing and starts discussing their budget, you owe a verbal explanation of agency types followed by written disclosure at the earliest practical opportunity. The Board has disciplined agents who waited until the showing or, worse, until the offer stage.

Montana does not give you a specific number of hours or days. The standard is "before or at the time" substantive discussions begin. In practice, the safest approach is to make the form the very first thing a new prospect sees, right after introductions and before any property-specific conversation.

## Dual Agency and Statutory Brokerage: Montana's Specific Rules

Dual agency remains lawful in Montana, but only with written informed consent from both the buyer and seller obtained before the dual relationship commences. MCA 37-51-314 spells out the limited duties a dual agent owes: confidentiality on pricing strategy, equal treatment, and disclosure of material facts. A dual agent cannot advocate for either party's negotiating position.

Statutory brokerage (transaction brokerage) is the fallback relationship under MCA 37-51-102(18). When neither party has entered into a buyer or seller agency agreement, the licensee functions as a statutory broker with limited duties. This does not eliminate the disclosure obligation. The agent must still present MAR Form 9, explain the relationship, and obtain acknowledgment.

| Relationship Type | Advocacy Permitted? | Written Consent Required? | Key Statute |
|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|-------------|
| Seller Agency | Yes, for seller | Yes (listing agreement) | MCA 37-51-313 |
| Buyer Agency | Yes, for buyer | Yes (buyer-broker agreement) | MCA 37-51-313 |
| Dual Agency | No, neutral facilitation | Yes, from both parties | MCA 37-51-314 |
| Statutory Brokerage | No, limited duties | Acknowledgment required | MCA 37-51-102(18) |

Agents who also operate in states like [Texas](/blog/texas-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) or [South Carolina](/blog/south-carolina-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) should note that Montana's dual agency consent standard is stricter than some jurisdictions that merely require disclosure without affirmative written consent from both sides.

## Consequences of Noncompliance: Fines, Rescission, and Civil Liability

The Montana Board of Realty Regulation has authority under MCA 37-51-321 to impose sanctions including formal reprimand, mandatory continuing education, fines up to $1,500 per violation, license suspension, and license revocation. Repeat offenders face escalating penalties, and the Board publishes disciplinary actions in its quarterly report, making them visible to future clients and brokerages.

Beyond Board action, failure to disclose agency can expose an agent and their brokerage to civil liability. A court may allow the aggrieved party to rescind the transaction if they can show they would not have proceeded had they understood the agent's loyalties. Montana courts have historically applied the "but for" test: but for the undisclosed conflict, would the consumer have entered the contract?

> MCA 37-51-321(2): "The board may impose a civil penalty not to exceed $1,500 for each violation of this chapter."

Errors and omissions insurance may or may not cover agency disclosure failures depending on policy language. Several Montana E&O carriers have added exclusions for "knowing regulatory violations," meaning an agent who deliberately skips disclosure could find themselves personally liable for damages.

## Common Mistakes Montana Agents Make

**The first and most frequent mistake is treating agency disclosure as a closing-table document.** By the time you reach closing, the disclosure should have been signed weeks or months earlier. Agents who collect the form at closing are technically in violation from the moment of first substantive contact forward.

The second error involves dual agency consent that arrives after the dual relationship has already begun. This happens most often when a listing agent receives an offer from an unrepresented buyer and begins negotiating without pausing to secure written consent on MAR Form 10. The negotiation itself creates the dual relationship, and consent obtained afterward does not cure the violation retroactively.

Third, agents frequently fail to update the disclosure when the relationship changes. If a statutory brokerage converts to buyer agency mid-transaction, a new form must be executed. The original MAR Form 9 acknowledgment does not cover a relationship type that did not exist at the time of signing.

Fourth, some agents present the form but do not retain a signed copy. The Board requires licensees to maintain transaction records for a minimum of five years under ARM 24.210.625. A verbal explanation with no signed documentation is functionally the same as no disclosure at all during an audit.

Fifth, teams and assistants sometimes present the form without proper authority. In Montana, only a licensed individual may explain agency relationships to a consumer. An unlicensed assistant handing over a form without a licensee present does not satisfy the statute.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carry supervisory liability under MCA 37-51-304 for the acts of their affiliated licensees. A broker who fails to implement reasonable policies and oversight for agency disclosure can face independent discipline even if they were not personally involved in the transaction.

**Brokers should audit every file for three things: presence of the signed disclosure form, the date on the form relative to first contact, and consistency between the stated relationship type and the actual conduct of the agent.** A signed form dated the same day as closing is a red flag. A dual agency consent dated after the first counter-offer is a liability.

Quarterly file reviews are the minimum standard most compliance attorneys recommend for Montana brokerages. Some firms conduct monthly spot audits, pulling five to ten random files and checking for proper sequencing. The cost of this internal review is negligible compared to the cost of a Board investigation or a rescission lawsuit.

Brokers should also verify that agents who transition between relationship types mid-transaction execute a new consent form. The transaction management system should flag any file where MAR Form 10 is absent or where the relationship type on the form does not match the role the agent played.
