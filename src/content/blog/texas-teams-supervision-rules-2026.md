## Why Texas Teams & Supervision Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) has spent the last several years tightening the regulatory framework around real estate teams, and the rules heading into 2026 leave little room for ambiguity. If you run a team, belong to one, or broker a firm with teams under your roof, understanding Texas teams & supervision rules 2026 is not optional. It is the difference between running a clean operation and facing fines, license actions, or worse.

TREC's authority over teams flows from the Texas Occupations Code, Chapter 1101, and the administrative rules found in Title 22, Part 23 of the Texas Administrative Code. Rule 535.4 specifically addresses advertising, team names, and the broker's supervisory obligations. These are not suggestions buried in guidance documents. They are enforceable regulations with real teeth.

**The core principle is simple: every team operates under the broker's license, and no team structure can obscure that fact from the public.** This means advertising, contracts, signage, and digital presence all must comply. The consequences for getting it wrong range from corrective action letters to fines up to $5,000 per violation under Section 1101.702 of the Occupations Code.

## How TREC Defines a "Team" and Why It Matters

TREC does not define "team" in the same casual way agents use the word at networking events. Under [TREC Rule 535.4](https://www.trec.texas.gov/laws-and-rules/trec-rules), a team is a group of license holders who work together under one sponsoring broker and represent themselves to the public using a collective name. That collective name triggers specific advertising and disclosure requirements.

A team name is treated as an assumed business name. It must be registered with the broker and cannot mislead consumers into believing the team is a separate brokerage. Every piece of advertising that uses the team name must also include the broker's name in at least half the size of the team name or the largest contact information, whichever is greater.

This half-size rule trips up teams constantly. A social media graphic with "The Martinez Group" in bold letters and the brokerage name in tiny footer text violates TREC advertising rules. The same applies to yard signs, business cards, email signatures, and websites.

## Broker Supervision Obligations Under Texas Law

Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.803 places supervisory responsibility squarely on the broker. The broker must ensure that every sales agent and team member conducts business in compliance with the law. Delegation of day-to-day oversight to a team leader does not transfer legal responsibility.

**Brokers cannot use a team leader as a shield against supervisory liability.** If a team member fails to disclose material facts, mishandles earnest money, or runs non-compliant advertising, TREC will look at the broker first. The team leader may face individual discipline, but the broker's license is always on the line.

TREC expects brokers to maintain written policies addressing team operations, advertising review, and transaction file management. These policies should specify how files are reviewed, how advertising is approved before publication, and what happens when a team member leaves the brokerage. Brokers who cannot produce documentation of their supervisory systems during an audit face enforcement action under Rule 535.2.

This supervisory burden becomes especially complex when teams span multiple locations or when a team leader manages agents across different market areas. If your brokerage handles multiple [active deals simultaneously](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), the documentation requirements multiply fast.

## Advertising Rules: The Most Enforced Team Requirement

TREC enforcement data consistently shows that advertising violations are the most common team-related complaints. The rules seem straightforward on paper, but their application across dozens of marketing channels creates friction.

Here is what the advertising rules require for teams in Texas:

| Requirement | Compliant Example | Non-Compliant Example |
|---|---|---|
| Broker name visibility | "Smith Team - ABC Realty" (ABC Realty at 50%+ size) | "Smith Team" with brokerage in fine print |
| License status disclosure | "Jane Smith, Sales Agent" | "Jane Smith, Realtor" with no license type |
| Team name registration | Name on file with broker and TREC | Using an unregistered team name on ads |
| No misleading names | "Downtown Experts Team" | "Downtown Realty" (implies separate brokerage) |
| Digital advertising | Broker name visible without scrolling | Broker name only on "About" page |

Every ad, whether printed or digital, must include the broker's name or the name of the licensed business entity. Rule 535.155 makes clear that this applies to social media posts, video content, podcast descriptions, and any digital platform where the team solicits business.

Agents who fail to include proper identification face a complaint process that begins with TREC's enforcement division. First-time violations on minor advertising issues sometimes result in a warning letter. Repeated violations or willful non-compliance escalate to formal complaints, agreed orders, and fines.

## Common Mistakes Texas Teams Make in 2026

Five errors show up repeatedly in TREC enforcement actions and broker audits. Each one is avoidable with proper systems in place.

**First, teams fail to update advertising when a member leaves.** When a sales agent departs the team or switches brokerages, every piece of marketing featuring that agent must be removed or updated immediately. Websites, Zillow profiles, and social media pages often linger for months with outdated information. TREC views this as a broker supervision failure.

Second, team leaders act as de facto brokers without holding a broker's license. This manifests when a team leader reviews contracts for accuracy, advises newer agents on pricing strategy, or handles client disputes independently. Texas law is unambiguous: only a licensed broker or a designated broker of record can perform supervisory acts. A sales agent leading a team can coordinate tasks and manage workflow, but cannot provide the type of oversight that constitutes broker supervision under Section 1101.351.

Third, teams use separate bank accounts or handle earnest money outside the broker's trust account. All funds must pass through the broker's designated trust account. A team-level account for holding client funds is illegal in Texas, full stop.

Fourth, team members fail to identify their individual license status in communications. Under Rule 535.154, every sales agent must disclose their status as a "sales agent" sponsored by a named broker in their first substantive communication with a party. Many team members default to using just the team name without personal license identification.

Fifth, teams neglect to use the proper TREC forms. Texas requires specific promulgated forms for most residential transactions. The [Information About Brokerage Services form (IABS, TREC No. OP-K)](https://www.trec.texas.gov/forms) must be provided at the first substantive dialogue with a prospective buyer, seller, landlord, or tenant. Teams that assign this task to administrative staff without proper tracking often miss the requirement entirely. This is one of many reasons [agents miss critical deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) in active transactions.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers who sponsor teams should run quarterly compliance audits covering at minimum five areas. Advertising review comes first. Pull every active ad, social media profile, and website page for each team and verify broker name placement and sizing.

Second, audit transaction files for proper form usage. Every file should contain the IABS (TREC No. OP-K), the appropriate representation agreement, and all required addenda. TREC's promulgated contract forms, including the One to Four Family Residential Contract (TREC No. 20-17) and its associated addenda, must be used without modification except in designated blanks.

Third, verify that all team names are registered with TREC as assumed names and that no expired or unauthorized names appear in public-facing materials. Fourth, review trust account records to confirm that no team-level accounts exist and that all earnest money flows exclusively through the brokerage trust account. Fifth, confirm that departed team members have been removed from all advertising and that their listings have been properly transferred or withdrawn.

| Audit Area | Frequency | Documentation Required |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising compliance | Quarterly | Screenshots, printouts, sign photos |
| Transaction file review | Monthly or per-closing | Complete file with all TREC forms |
| Team name registration | Annually and at any name change | TREC filing confirmation |
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | Bank statements, deposit records |
| Departed agent cleanup | Within 72 hours of departure | Ad removal confirmation, listing transfer docs |

Brokers who maintain this audit cadence are far less likely to face enforcement action. Those who delegate without verifying create exposure for themselves and every agent in their office.

## Penalties and Liability When Compliance Fails

TREC's enforcement division handles complaints through a structured process. After investigation, violations can result in agreed orders, administrative penalties, license suspension, or license revocation. Under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.702, administrative penalties can reach $5,000 per violation per day.

**Beyond TREC penalties, non-compliance creates civil liability.** A buyer who discovers that a team misrepresented its structure or failed to disclose agency relationships may have grounds to rescind a contract or pursue damages. Errors and omissions insurance may not cover intentional regulatory violations, leaving agents and brokers personally exposed.

Deal cancellation is a real outcome. If a team member failed to provide the IABS form at the required time, or if advertising misled a consumer about who they were hiring, opposing counsel can use that failure as grounds to challenge the transaction's validity. These issues create the kind of last-minute chaos that leads to [blown closings](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) and shattered client relationships.

License suspension carries collateral damage beyond the immediate loss of income. A suspended agent cannot refer business, collect pending commissions on active deals (in most brokerage agreements), or maintain their position on a team roster. Reinstatement requires additional continuing education, waiting periods, and sometimes retaking the licensing exam.

## Building Compliant Team Operations Going Forward

The agents and brokers who avoid enforcement actions share one trait: they build systems rather than relying on memory or good intentions.
