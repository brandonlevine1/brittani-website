## Why Texas HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Texas has more than 75,000 property owners associations governing an estimated 8 to 9 million residents. If you list or sell property inside one of those communities, the Texas hoa & condo disclosure rules 2026 impose specific obligations that differ significantly from the general seller's disclosure framework. Missing a single document or deadline can hand your buyer a legal exit—or expose your license to disciplinary action from the Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC).

The governing statutes are not optional suggestions. Texas Property Code Chapter 207 (for subdivision information), Chapter 82 (Uniform Condominium Act), and Chapter 209 (Texas Residential Property Owners Protection Act) each carry their own delivery timelines, content requirements, and remedies. Agents who treat HOA paperwork as a low-priority afterthought are the same agents who watch contracts collapse three days before closing.

This post breaks down the statutory framework, the forms you must use, the consequences of non-compliance, and the operational mistakes that keep showing up in broker audits across the state.

## The Statutory Framework: Chapters 82, 207, and 209

**Texas Property Code Chapter 207 is the backbone of HOA resale disclosure for single-family subdivisions.** It requires the property owner to deliver a "subdivision information" document—commonly called the resale certificate—to a prospective buyer before closing. The certificate must include current assessments, outstanding balances, capital expenditure obligations, and any pending special assessments.

Chapter 82, the Texas Uniform Condominium Act, applies specifically to condominium projects. Section 82.157 mandates that the seller provide a resale certificate containing the declaration, bylaws, rules, current budget, reserve study (if one exists), insurance summary, and any pending litigation against the association. The buyer gets a statutory right to cancel within six days of receiving the certificate under Section 82.156.

Chapter 209 governs residential POAs more broadly, covering things like assessment lien authority, foreclosure procedures, and owner access to records. While it does not independently create a resale disclosure obligation, it shapes what information the HOA is required to produce when the owner requests it. Agents who only reference Chapter 207 without understanding how Chapter 209 interacts with the HOA's document production duties are working with an incomplete picture.

## Forms and Addenda Every Texas Agent Must Know

TREC and the Texas Association of Realtors (TAR) each publish forms that implement these statutory requirements at the contract level. The most critical is the TREC-promulgated **Addendum for Property Subject to Mandatory Membership in a Property Owners Association** (TREC Form 36-8). This addendum must be attached to any One to Four Family Residential Contract when the property is in a mandatory HOA.

| Form | Publisher | Purpose | When Required |
|------|-----------|---------|---------------|
| TREC 36-8 | TREC | HOA Addendum for residential contracts | Every mandatory-HOA transaction |
| TAR 1922 | TAR | Addendum for Property in a POA (expanded) | Optional expanded version for TAR members |
| TREC 32-5 | TREC | Condominium Resale Certificate (referenced) | Condo transactions under Ch. 82 |
| TAR 1407 | TAR | Request for HOA Information | Agent request to management company |

The TREC 36-8 addendum sets the contractual clock. It specifies who orders the subdivision information, who pays for it, and when the buyer's termination option expires after receipt. If you leave this addendum off a contract for a property inside a mandatory POA, you have failed to use a promulgated form when one was available—a direct violation of TREC Rules under [22 TAC §537.11](https://www.trec.texas.gov/laws-and-rules/rules).

For condominiums, the resale certificate under Section 82.157 has its own statutory content checklist. Many management companies produce a packet running 50 to 200 pages. Your job is not to review it for accuracy, but you must confirm it was delivered and ensure the buyer's six-day rescission window is tracked precisely.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**Non-compliance creates three simultaneous risk vectors: buyer termination rights, civil liability, and TREC disciplinary action.** Each of these can damage your deal, your brokerage, or your license independently—and they sometimes stack.

If the resale certificate is never delivered, the buyer can terminate under the contract addendum and recover earnest money. Under Chapter 82 for condos, the buyer's six-day right to cancel does not begin until actual delivery, which means the clock never starts if you never hand over the documents. A buyer could theoretically rescind weeks after closing if the certificate was never properly delivered, though this scenario typically triggers litigation rather than a clean unwind.

On the civil liability side, sellers—and by extension their agents—face claims for failure to disclose material information about the association. If the HOA has a pending $2 million special assessment and the buyer never received the resale certificate disclosing it, the listing agent's errors and omissions policy is going to get a workout. Courts have found agents liable when they knew or should have known about HOA issues and failed to ensure proper documentation was delivered.

TREC can impose sanctions ranging from a formal reprimand to license revocation for failing to use promulgated forms, misrepresenting property conditions, or acting negligently. Fines typically range from $1,000 to $5,000 per violation, with repeat offenders facing suspension. The Commission's enforcement actions are public record and appear in license history searches.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With HOA Disclosures

Five errors appear over and over in transaction audits and TREC complaint files. Each one is preventable with basic systems and awareness.

**First, agents forget to attach the TREC 36-8 addendum entirely.** This happens most often with newer agents who do not realize the property is in a mandatory HOA until title work comes back, and by then the contract is already executed without the addendum. The fix is simple: verify HOA status during listing intake, not after contract execution. You can [track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without losing sight of required addenda if your file management system flags HOA properties at intake.

Second, agents order the resale certificate too late. Most HOA management companies take 7 to 10 business days to produce the packet. If your option period is 10 days and you order the certificate on day 4, your buyer may not receive it until after the option expires—eliminating their leverage. Order the certificate the same day you receive an executed contract.

Third, agents confuse the HOA resale certificate with the seller's disclosure notice (TREC Form OP-H). These are completely different documents governed by different statutes. The seller's disclosure under Property Code Section 5.008 covers physical property conditions. The HOA resale certificate under Chapter 207 or 82 covers association-level financial and governance information. Delivering one does not satisfy the obligation to deliver the other.

Fourth, agents fail to document delivery. The statute and contract require that the buyer actually receive the certificate. Uploading it to a transaction management platform without confirming the buyer's agent acknowledged receipt does not constitute delivery under most legal interpretations. Get a signed acknowledgment or at minimum a timestamped email confirmation.

Fifth, agents do not verify the certificate's completeness against the statutory checklist. Section 207.003 specifies exactly what must be included. If the management company omits the reserve fund balance or pending litigation disclosure, the document is deficient. Agents who spot-check against the statute protect their clients—and themselves.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under TREC Rules Section 535.2. If your agents are routinely missing HOA addenda or delivering incomplete resale certificates, the Commission can hold the broker responsible for inadequate supervision. Quarterly file audits should specifically flag three things.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|------------|---------------|----------|
| TREC 36-8 presence | Attached to every contract where MLS shows HOA | Missing addendum on HOA properties |
| Resale certificate delivery date | Timestamp vs. contract execution date | Delivered after option period expires |
| Buyer acknowledgment | Signed receipt or documented email confirmation | No proof of delivery in file |
| Certificate completeness | Matches Section 207.003 or 82.157 checklist | Missing financial statements or litigation disclosure |
| Payment allocation | Addendum specifies who pays for certificate | Blank or contradictory fee allocation |

Beyond file audits, brokers should build HOA verification into their listing intake process. When an agent brings a new listing, the system should prompt them to confirm HOA status, identify the management company, and note the estimated certificate turnaround time. This prevents the downstream scramble that causes missed deadlines and [blown closings](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Training matters here too. A once-a-year compliance meeting is not enough when your agents handle dozens of HOA properties annually. Brokers who build disclosure checklists into their transaction workflows—whether through a TC, a coordinator platform, or automated reminders—see fewer TREC complaints and cleaner files during audits. If you are [training new agents on timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines), HOA disclosure sequencing should be a standalone module in your onboarding curriculum.

## Condominium-Specific Rules Under Chapter 82

Condo transactions carry additional layers that single-family HOA deals do not. Section 82.157 requires the resale certificate to include the declaration, bylaws, and any amendments—documents that can run hundreds of pages for older condo regimes. The association must produce the certificate within 10 business days of receipt of a written request from the unit owner.

The buyer's six-day termination right under Section 82.156 is statutory, not contractual. It cannot be waived by agreement of the parties. Even if the buyer signs a contract addendum attempting to shorten or eliminate this window, the statute overrides. Agents who advise buyers that they have waived their condo rescission right are providing legal advice they are not licensed to give—and incorrect advice at that.

> "A purchaser may cancel a contract of purchase at any time within the six-day period beginning on the date the purchaser receives the resale certificate." — Texas Property Code Section 82.156(b)

Insurance disclosure is another area where condo resale certificates frequently fall short. The certificate must describe what the association's master policy covers and what it excludes. Buyers who assume the master policy covers their interior improvements often discover after a loss that they needed an HO-6 policy. The listing agent's job is not to interpret insurance coverage, but it is to ensure the certificate containing that information reaches the buyer within the statutory window.

## Deadlines That Actually Matter

Timing in HOA and condo disclosure is not forgiving. The contractual deadlines in TREC 36-8 interact with statutory deadlines in Chapters 82 and 207, and agents who conflate them create liability gaps.

Under the standard TREC contract with the HOA addendum, the seller agrees to deliver the subdivision information within a specified number of days after the effective date. If the seller fails, the buyer may terminate before the deadline expires and receive a refund of earnest money. Once the buyer receives the documents, a separate review period—typically three days—begins, during which the buyer may still terminate.

For condos, the six-day statutory window in Section 82.156 runs independently of any contractual option period. A buyer could be past their general option period but still within the six-day condo rescission window if the resale certificate was delivered late. This creates a scenario where the seller believes the deal is locked down but the buyer still holds a statutory exit. Agents on both sides need to track both clocks simultaneously.
