## Why Ohio Buyer Contingency Deadlines 2026 Deserve a Hard Look

Missing a single contingency deadline can collapse a transaction, expose an agent to disciplinary action, and cost a brokerage tens of thousands in legal fees. Ohio buyer contingency deadlines 2026 remain one of the most error-prone areas in residential practice, partly because the Ohio REALTORS standard forms place heavy calendar-counting responsibility on the buyer's agent. The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, operating under Ohio Revised Code Chapter 4735, does not treat missed deadlines as harmless oversights.

**Ohio is an "automatic waiver" state in practice, meaning that when a buyer fails to act within the contingency window, the contingency often evaporates without any affirmative step by the seller.** That default mechanic separates Ohio from states that require seller notification to trigger expiration. Agents who relocate from neighboring states frequently get burned by this distinction.

The stakes are higher in 2026 because inventory pressure is compressing negotiation windows. Sellers are demanding shorter inspection and financing contingencies, leaving agents with even less margin for error on [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals).

## The Forms That Control Contingency Deadlines

Ohio REALTORS publishes the standard residential purchase agreement used in most Board-affiliated transactions. The primary form governing buyer contingencies is **Ohio REALTORS Form 100** (Residential Real Estate Purchase Contract). Additional forms that interact with deadline management include:

| Form Number | Title | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Form 100 | Residential Purchase Contract | Sets all contingency deadlines |
| Form 107 | Contingency Removal Notice | Confirms waiver or satisfaction |
| Form 108 | Amendment to Contract | Extends or modifies deadlines |
| Form 109 | Notice of Termination | Buyer exercises right to cancel |
| Form 110 | Inspection Response | Requests repairs or credits |

Each of these forms carries its own delivery requirements. Under Ohio REALTORS Form 100, Section 17, notices must be delivered in writing and received by the other party or their agent by the stated deadline date. "Received" is the operative word—mailing a notice on the deadline day does not satisfy the requirement if it arrives the following day.

The Ohio REALTORS [forms library](https://www.ohiorealtors.org/) is updated annually, and agents should confirm they are using the 2026 revision. Prior versions may contain different default day counts or delivery language.

## Breaking Down the Standard Contingency Windows

Form 100 establishes several buyer contingency categories, each with a fill-in-the-blank deadline that becomes binding once both parties execute the contract. The most common contingencies and their typical negotiated ranges are:

| Contingency | Default (if blank) | Common Range |
|---|---|---|
| Inspection | 15 calendar days | 7–15 days |
| Financing | 30 calendar days | 21–45 days |
| Appraisal | Tied to financing | Same as financing |
| Sale of Buyer's Property | Negotiated | 30–60 days |
| Title Examination | 15 calendar days | 10–21 days |

**Calendar days, not business days, control every contingency unless the contract explicitly states otherwise.** This distinction trips up agents who mentally skip weekends when counting. A 15-day inspection period that starts on a Monday expires on a Monday two weeks later—not three Mondays later.

When the deadline date falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday recognized under Ohio Revised Code Section 1.14, the deadline extends to the next business day. That extension only applies to the final day, not to intermediate counting.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The consequences of blown contingency deadlines cascade quickly. From a transactional standpoint, a buyer who misses an inspection deadline forfeits the right to request repairs, negotiate credits, or terminate based on inspection findings. The deal moves forward "as-is," and the buyer's earnest money remains at risk.

From a regulatory standpoint, Ohio Revised Code Section 4735.18(A)(6) defines incompetence and negligence as grounds for license suspension, revocation, or fines up to $2,500 per violation. The Ohio Division of Real Estate has historically treated repeated deadline failures as a pattern of negligence rather than isolated mistakes. Brokers who fail to supervise agents on deadline compliance may face separate administrative complaints under Section 4735.18(A)(9).

Civil liability adds another layer. If a buyer loses their ability to terminate and later discovers a material defect, the buyer's agent may face a malpractice claim alleging that proper deadline management would have preserved the buyer's exit rights. Ohio courts have recognized negligence claims against agents in similar fact patterns, and errors-and-omissions carriers are increasingly scrutinizing deadline management procedures during claims investigations.

Agents should also understand that [blown closings carry real financial costs](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) beyond the immediate transaction, including reputational damage and potential Board ethics complaints.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Ohio Contingency Deadlines

**First, agents routinely begin counting from the wrong start date.** Form 100 specifies that contingency periods begin the day after the Effective Date (the date the last party signs or initials acceptance). Counting from the offer date, the listing date, or the date the signed contract was delivered to the buyer's agent produces an incorrect deadline.

Second, agents confuse delivery of notice with creation of notice. Writing a contingency removal letter on Day 14 of a 15-day window does not satisfy the requirement if the letter reaches the listing agent on Day 16. The contract requires receipt, not dispatch. Email delivery adds complexity because Form 100 requires parties to have authorized electronic delivery in the communication section.

Third, agents rely on verbal confirmation rather than written notice. A phone call to the listing agent saying "my buyer waives inspection" does not constitute valid contingency removal under the contract. Without Form 107 or equivalent written documentation, the verbal statement is unenforceable and may lead to disputes about whether the contingency was properly satisfied.

Fourth, agents fail to track the appraisal contingency as separate from the financing contingency. While both often share the same deadline in Form 100, the buyer's rights under each are distinct. An appraised value below the purchase price triggers appraisal contingency rights that are separate from whether the buyer's loan is approved.

Fifth, agents neglect to confirm that contract amendments (Form 108) extending deadlines are fully executed before the original deadline passes. An unsigned extension request is not an extension. If the seller has not signed the amendment before 11:59 PM on the original deadline date, the original deadline controls.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under ORC Section 4735.18(A)(9), which means passive oversight is not a defense. A broker's compliance program for buyer contingency deadlines should include specific, documented procedures that agents must follow on every transaction.

**At minimum, brokers should require agents to submit a completed deadline calendar within 48 hours of contract execution.** This calendar should list every contingency, its start date, its expiration date, and the form required to exercise or waive the contingency. The broker or their designated transaction coordinator should independently verify the calculations.

Brokers should also audit whether agents are using current-year Ohio REALTORS forms. Stale forms create liability because outdated language may reference superseded default periods or delivery requirements. A quarterly file review that checks form version numbers against the current Ohio REALTORS catalog is a straightforward quality control measure.

Supervisory systems that send automated alerts 72 hours and 24 hours before each contingency deadline significantly reduce the risk of missed dates. This is precisely [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) in the first place—they rely on memory rather than systematic reminders. Brokers who implement automated tracking create a defensible record showing that reasonable supervision was in place.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Responsible Party |
|---|---|---|
| Deadline calendar submission | Every new contract | Listing/Buyer agent |
| Form version verification | Quarterly | Broker or TC |
| Written notice confirmation | At each deadline | Agent + broker review |
| File completion check | Within 5 days of closing | Broker or compliance officer |
| Escalation of overdue items | Daily automated | Transaction management system |

## Delivery Methods and Their Pitfalls

Form 100 Section 17 governs how notices must be delivered. The contract allows delivery by personal delivery, mail, facsimile, or electronic transmission—but only if the receiving party has provided the relevant contact information in the contract and has not opted out of electronic delivery.

Agents often assume that texting a photo of a signed Form 107 satisfies electronic delivery. Unless the contract's communication section specifically authorizes text message as an acceptable delivery method, this approach is legally questionable. The safer practice is to use email to the address listed in the contract and retain delivery confirmation (read receipt or reply acknowledgment).

**For time-sensitive notices delivered on the final day of a contingency period, agents should use a method that generates a timestamp.** Certified mail does not work for same-day delivery. Personal delivery with a signed acknowledgment is the most defensible method when the deadline is imminent.

## Building a Deadline Management System That Holds Up

Agents managing more than a handful of active transactions cannot rely on sticky notes or phone calendar reminders. A defensible system requires three elements: accurate initial calculation, automated reminders at multiple intervals, and documented confirmation that each deadline action was completed.

The initial calculation should be performed by referencing the contract's Effective Date, adding the specified number of calendar days, and adjusting for the Saturday/Sunday/holiday rule under ORC Section 1.14. This calculation should be documented in the transaction file, not just stored in a digital calendar.

Automated reminders should fire at 7 days, 3 days, and 1 day before each deadline. The final reminder should specify which form needs to be delivered and to whom.
