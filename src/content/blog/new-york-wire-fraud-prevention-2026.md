## New York Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026: The Rules Have Teeth Now

Real estate wire fraud cost American buyers over $446 million in 2023 according to the FBI's IC3 report, and New York remains one of the top target states. The New York wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 are not optional suggestions—they represent a framework of disclosure obligations, verification protocols, and broker oversight duties that carry real consequences when ignored. If you hold a license issued by the New York Department of State (DOS), you need to understand exactly what is expected of you before your next closing.

New York does not have a single standalone wire fraud statute dedicated to real estate transactions. Instead, compliance flows from a combination of DOS regulatory authority under 19 NYCRR Part 175, industry-standard contract riders published by REBNY and the New York State Bar Association (NYSBA), and fiduciary duty obligations codified in Real Property Law Section 443. The result is a patchwork that agents must treat as a unified obligation.

## The Legal Framework Agents Must Follow

The DOS has authority to discipline licensees under Real Property Law Article 12-A for "demonstrating untrustworthiness or incompetent dealing." Failing to warn a client about wire fraud when the industry has adopted standard protocols fits squarely within that language. Disciplinary actions under RPL Section 441(1) can result in license suspension, revocation, or fines up to $1,000 per violation.

**The REBNY Wire Fraud Advisory Rider (Form RFW-1, updated January 2026) is now standard in all residential transactions processed through REBNY-member firms in New York City.** This rider must be signed by the buyer at or before contract execution. It warns the buyer never to trust wire instructions received solely via email and directs them to verify all wiring details by calling a known phone number.

For transactions outside of REBNY's reach—upstate markets, Long Island, Westchester—the NYSBA/ACREL joint advisory language published in their 2025 residential contract update serves the same function. Agents operating in these markets should confirm with their managing broker that equivalent language is attached to every purchase agreement.

The [wire instructions verification process](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) is not a courtesy—it is an affirmative duty flowing from your fiduciary relationship with the client under RPL Section 443.

## What the Forms Actually Require

Here is what agents need to include or confirm in every transaction file:

| Document | Who Signs | When | Source |
|----------|-----------|------|--------|
| REBNY Wire Fraud Advisory Rider (RFW-1) | Buyer | At or before contract signing | REBNY member portal |
| NYSBA Wire Fraud Advisory (Residential Contract Rider 2025-WF) | Buyer | At or before contract signing | NYSBA forms library |
| Broker Wire Protocol Acknowledgment | Agent | At onboarding or annually | Brokerage compliance office |
| Transaction File Confirmation | Agent | Before wiring instructions are sent | Internal checklist |

The RFW-1 form requires the buyer to initial three specific acknowledgments: that they understand email is not secure, that they will independently verify wire instructions via phone, and that neither the agent nor the brokerage guarantees the security of any third-party communication. The form also includes a hold-harmless clause that protects the brokerage—but only if the form is actually executed and retained in the file.

**Agents should never transmit wire instructions directly.** The DOS has made clear through informal guidance letters that forwarding wire instructions via email—even accurate ones—creates liability exposure. Your job is to direct the buyer to the closing attorney or title company for those details and to document that you gave the warning.

## What Happens If You Fail to Comply

The consequences of skipping wire fraud disclosures in New York fall into three categories, and none of them are hypothetical.

First, DOS disciplinary action. Under 19 NYCRR 175.21, the Department can initiate proceedings against any licensee whose conduct demonstrates incompetence. Multiple DOS complaint investigations in 2024 and 2025 cited failure to provide wire fraud warnings as evidence of inadequate client protection. Penalties range from a formal reprimand to license revocation.

Second, civil liability. If a buyer wires $200,000 to a fraudulent account and your file contains no signed wire fraud advisory, you become a target in the inevitable lawsuit. New York courts apply a reasonable-care standard, and the existence of industry-standard forms you failed to use is devastating evidence. Your E&O carrier may deny coverage if you departed from the protocol your own brokerage adopted.

Third, deal cancellation and reputational harm. When a wire goes to a fraudster, the deal typically collapses. The buyer may lack funds to close, the seller loses patience, and the attorneys start pointing fingers. Your referral network learns about these failures faster than you think. Understanding [the true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) should motivate compliance even when the legal risk alone does not.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in New York

Five errors recur with alarming frequency in DOS complaint files and E&O claims related to wire fraud.

**Mistake one: emailing wire instructions "as a convenience."** Agents receive wiring details from the closing attorney and forward them to the buyer to "save time." This single act creates liability even if the instructions are legitimate because it trains the buyer to trust email for financial details. If a hacker later intercepts the thread and substitutes different instructions, your forwarded email is exhibit A.

Mistake two: failing to re-issue the advisory on delayed closings. The RFW-1 and NYSBA advisory are signed at contract, but closings often slip by weeks or months. By then, the buyer has forgotten the warning. Best practice—and what DOS expects—is a verbal and written reminder within 48 hours of closing when wiring is imminent.

Mistake three: assuming the attorney handles everything. New York is an attorney-state for closings, but that does not relieve agents of their independent fiduciary duty. The DOS has stated in multiple advisory opinions that "delegation does not eliminate responsibility." You must confirm the buyer received the advisory, even if the attorney also covers it.

Mistake four: no documentation that the warning was delivered. Agents verbally mention wire fraud but keep no record. Without a signed form or at minimum a timestamped email confirming the discussion, you have no defense if a claim arises.

Mistake five: using outdated forms. The 2023 REBNY advisory rider lacked language about AI-generated voice spoofing. The 2026 RFW-1 update specifically addresses deepfake phone calls and directs buyers to use a pre-established passphrase with their closing attorney. Using the old form signals that your compliance is stale.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in New York carry supervisory liability under RPL Section 442-c. If an agent in your office fails to deliver wire fraud disclosures and a buyer loses money, the DOS will ask what systems you had in place to prevent that failure.

| Broker Responsibility | Frequency | Evidence Required |
|----------------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Annual wire fraud training for all agents | Yearly, by January 31 | Signed attendance log or LMS completion record |
| Audit of transaction files for RFW-1 / NYSBA rider | Quarterly minimum | Audit checklist with date, reviewer initials, findings |
| Updated wire fraud protocol memo | Annually or upon form update | Dated memo with agent acknowledgment signatures |
| Incident reporting procedure | Ongoing | Written SOP accessible to all agents |
| Vendor communication policy | Ongoing | Written directive restricting email transmission of wire details |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-police this process are accepting risk they do not need to carry. A quarterly file audit takes minimal time and creates a documented pattern of supervisory diligence that the DOS respects in disciplinary proceedings.

The smartest brokerages build wire fraud compliance into their [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) so that no file moves to closing coordination without a confirmed advisory signature. This structural approach eliminates reliance on individual memory.
