## Understanding Ohio Escrow Regulations 2026 at the State Level

Ohio escrow regulations 2026 carry real teeth for agents and brokers who handle earnest money deposits carelessly. The rules governing trust accounts, deposit timelines, and disbursement procedures are codified primarily in Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Chapter 4735 and enforced by the Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing under the Department of Commerce. Getting these wrong does not simply embarrass you at the closing table—it exposes your license, your brokerage, and your client's transaction to serious legal jeopardy.

The Division has signaled increased audit activity heading into 2026, particularly around trust account reconciliation and timely deposit compliance. Agents who treat escrow handling as someone else's problem are making a bet they cannot afford. This post breaks down the specific statutes, forms, deadlines, penalties, and audit triggers every Ohio licensee needs to understand right now.

## The Statutory Framework: ORC 4735 and Administrative Code 1301:5-1

**Ohio's escrow requirements live in two primary sources: ORC Chapter 4735 and Ohio Administrative Code (OAC) 1301:5-1.** ORC 4735.18(A)(21) prohibits comingling client funds with brokerage operating accounts. ORC 4735.24 establishes the deposit timeline, requiring that earnest money reach the broker's trust account by the end of the next business day after acceptance of the purchase contract—unless the contract itself specifies an alternative deadline.

OAC 1301:5-1-02 expands on record-keeping obligations. Brokers must maintain a ledger for each individual transaction within the trust account, and all records must be preserved for a minimum of three years following the closing or termination of the transaction. The rule also mandates monthly reconciliation of the trust account balance against the sum of all individual transaction ledgers.

The Ohio Association of Realtors (OAR) Purchase Agreement form—currently the OAR Residential Purchase Contract, Form 101—includes specific language addressing earnest money deposit amounts, timelines, and the escrow holder's identity. Agents filling out Form 101 must ensure paragraph 3 (Earnest Money) matches the actual handling procedures their brokerage follows. Discrepancies between what the contract says and what actually happens create the exact paper trail that triggers Division complaints.

## Deposit Timelines and Handling Procedures

The deposit timeline is where most compliance failures originate. ORC 4735.24 states the broker must deposit funds into the trust account by the close of the next business day following receipt—not following acceptance, but following the broker's actual receipt of the check or wire. A weekend receipt means Monday deposit. A Friday receipt means Monday deposit.

**Wire transfers and electronic payments have added complexity since many title companies and brokerages now accept ACH or wired earnest money.** The Division treats electronic funds identically to physical checks regarding the next-business-day rule. If your brokerage receives a wire on Tuesday at 4:45 PM, the funds must be posted to the trust account by close of business Wednesday.

Agents who hold a buyer's check in their car console or desk drawer for multiple days are creating a ticking compliance bomb. The statute does not care that you were busy showing properties or that the listing agent had not countersigned yet. Receipt triggers the clock, period.

| Scenario | Clock Starts | Deposit Deadline |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Check received Monday | Monday | Tuesday close of business |
| Wire received Friday at 3 PM | Friday | Monday close of business |
| Check received Saturday at open house | Saturday | Monday close of business |
| ACH initiated by buyer Wednesday | Wednesday (when funds hit brokerage) | Thursday close of business |
| Check received with unsigned counter | Day of receipt regardless | Next business day |

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make With Escrow

Five specific errors appear repeatedly in Division enforcement actions and audit findings. Each one is avoidable with proper training and systems.

**First, agents hold checks pending contract acceptance.** The statute is clear: deposit upon receipt by the broker, not upon full execution. If your buyer writes a check with the offer and the seller has not signed yet, your broker still must deposit it. The only exception is if the purchase agreement explicitly states the deposit will be made upon acceptance—and that language must appear in Form 101's earnest money section.

Second, agents fail to specify the escrow holder on the purchase agreement. Form 101, Paragraph 3 requires identification of who holds the earnest money. Leaving this blank or writing "TBD" creates ambiguity that can derail a transaction when disputes arise over disbursement authority.

Third, brokerages skip monthly reconciliation. OAC 1301:5-1-02 requires reconciliation of trust account bank statements against individual transaction ledgers every single month. Small brokerages with infrequent transactions often let this slide for months, only discovering discrepancies during a Division audit.

Fourth, agents disburse earnest money without written authorization from all parties. When a deal falls apart, the pressure to release funds quickly leads some agents to return deposits based on a phone call or text message. Ohio requires written release instructions—typically via the OAR Mutual Release and Termination of Contract form (Form 540)—signed by both buyer and seller before disbursement occurs.

Fifth, agents use personal accounts as temporary holding accounts. Even briefly routing a client's earnest money through a personal or team checking account violates ORC 4735.18(A)(21). The funds must go directly to the brokerage trust account or the designated escrow holder identified in the contract.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: What Is Actually at Stake

The Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing operates under ORC 4735.051, which grants it authority to investigate complaints, conduct audits, and impose disciplinary action. Penalties for escrow violations range from formal reprimands to license revocation. The Division can also impose civil fines up to $5,000 per violation under ORC 4735.052.

| Violation | Typical Penalty Range |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Late deposit (first offense, no client harm) | Formal warning or reprimand |
| Late deposit (pattern or client harm) | Fine up to $2,500 plus mandatory education |
| Comingling funds | Suspension 30-180 days, fine up to $5,000 |
| Failure to maintain records for 3 years | Fine plus mandatory audit compliance plan |
| Unauthorized disbursement | Suspension or revocation, potential civil liability |
| Using personal account for client funds | Suspension or revocation |

Beyond Division action, agents face civil liability from aggrieved parties. A buyer whose earnest money was improperly disbursed can sue the agent and brokerage for conversion. A seller who lost a deal because of escrow mishandling can pursue damages for breach of fiduciary duty. These lawsuits often exceed the transaction value because they involve attorney fees, consequential damages, and sometimes punitive damages if the court finds willful misconduct.

Errors and omissions insurance may cover some claims, but most E&O policies exclude intentional acts and regulatory violations. An agent who knowingly comingled funds or deliberately delayed a deposit will likely find their insurer denying coverage at the worst possible moment. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under ORC 4735.18(A)(6) for the escrow conduct of every agent in their brokerage. The Division holds the broker responsible even when an individual agent makes the error, which means passive management is itself a compliance risk.

**Brokers should conduct internal trust account audits quarterly at minimum, with monthly reconciliation being the statutory floor.** The audit must verify that every deposit was made within the required timeline, that every active transaction has a matching ledger entry, and that the aggregate trust account balance equals the sum of all individual ledger balances.

A documented policies-and-procedures manual addressing escrow handling is not legally required but functions as a powerful defense during Division investigations. The manual should specify who physically deposits checks, what documentation accompanies each deposit, and who performs monthly reconciliation. Having this in writing demonstrates good-faith compliance efforts.

Brokers should also maintain a log of all earnest money received, noting the date of receipt, date of deposit, transaction address, amount, and form of payment. This log—separate from the bank ledger—provides an independent verification trail that auditors expect to see. Agents who [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) on escrow deposits put the entire brokerage at risk, not just their own license.

Training new agents on escrow procedures should happen during onboarding, not after the first complaint. Building [structured onboarding timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) that include escrow compliance modules prevents the kind of ignorance-based violations that the Division sees most frequently from agents in their first two years of practice.

## Disputed Earnest Money: The Interpleader Option

When buyer and seller disagree about who gets the earnest money after a failed transaction, Ohio agents face a dangerous moment. Releasing funds to either party without mutual written consent exposes the brokerage to liability from the other side. The correct procedure under Ohio law is to either obtain mutual signed release via OAR Form 540 or file an interpleader action in the county court of common pleas.

> ORC 4735.24 provides that when demands are made by both parties and no mutual release is signed within a reasonable time, the broker may deposit the disputed funds with the court and request discharge from further liability through interpleader.

The interpleader filing transfers the dispute from the brokerage to the court, removing the broker from the middle. Filing costs are modest—typically under $300 in court fees—and the expense is far less than defending a conversion claim. Brokers who sit on disputed funds for months without taking action invite Division scrutiny and dual-party lawsuits.
