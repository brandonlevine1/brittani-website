## Understanding North Carolina Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026

The question of North Carolina who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is not purely academic. It dictates how you draft offers, counsel clients, and avoid regulatory exposure with the North Carolina Real Estate Commission (NCREC). Misallocating a single line item — or failing to document the negotiated split — can trigger deal cancellations, earnest money disputes, and formal complaints against your license.

North Carolina is an attorney-state for closings, which shifts responsibilities compared to escrow-state transactions. That distinction affects how costs appear on settlement statements and who has fiduciary authority over disbursements. Every licensed agent in the state needs a current, working knowledge of the standard forms and statutes that govern cost allocation.

## The Default Split: Custom vs. Contract

**North Carolina does not have a statute that assigns most closing costs to a specific party.** What the industry calls "customary" splits are exactly that — customs, not laws. The only cost with a statutory default is the excise tax (revenue stamps) under [N.C. Gen. Stat. § 105-228.30](https://www.ncleg.gov/EnactedLegislation/Statutes/HTML/BySection/Chapter_105/GS_105-228.30.html), which is traditionally the seller's obligation at $1 per $500 of consideration.

Everything else — title search, title insurance, attorney fees, recording fees, survey costs — is determined by the language in the Offer to Purchase and Contract (NCAR/NCBA Standard Form 2-T). Paragraph 8 of Form 2-T is where agents memorialize who pays what, including any seller concessions toward buyer costs. If your contract is silent on a cost, expect a dispute at the closing table.

The Working with Real Estate Agents Disclosure (NCREC Form) establishes agency but does not address cost allocation. Cost allocation lives in the contract, period. Agents who rely on verbal assurances about "who usually pays" without documenting it in Form 2-T are building liability into their files.

## Form 2-T and the Guidelines That Bind You

NCAR/NCBA Standard Form 2-T is the backbone of residential transactions in North Carolina. Paragraph 8 (Prorations and Adjustments) and the related fill-in blanks for seller-paid costs directly address cost allocation. Form 2-A (the Addendum) and Form 2-A14 (Additional Terms Addendum) are used when concessions exceed what Paragraph 8 can accommodate or when the parties agree to non-standard cost splits.

**Agents who modify pre-printed form language without broker approval risk violating 21 NCAC 58A .0104, which prohibits unauthorized practice of law.** You can fill in blanks and check boxes. You cannot draft custom legal provisions. If a deal requires a cost-splitting arrangement that does not fit Form 2-T's structure, the closing attorney should draft supplemental language.

Form 2-T, Paragraph 6 addresses the due diligence fee and due diligence period — both of which interact with closing cost negotiations. A seller concession negotiated during due diligence must be documented via amendment (Form 4-T) before the due diligence deadline expires or you lose contractual leverage. Timing failures here are among the [most common reasons deals fall apart](/blog/where-deals-break-down).

## Liability When Agents Get It Wrong

The NCREC has authority under N.C.G.S. § 93A-6 to discipline licensees for misrepresentation, failure to disclose material facts, and improper handling of transaction funds. A misrepresented closing cost allocation — telling a buyer "the seller always pays that" when the contract says otherwise — constitutes a material misrepresentation.

Consequences escalate quickly. A first offense can result in a reprimand and mandatory continuing education. Repeated violations or those causing financial harm to consumers can lead to license suspension or revocation and fines of up to $5,000 per violation under N.C.G.S. § 93A-6(b1). Civil liability runs alongside regulatory action — an agent who causes a client to unknowingly accept thousands in unexpected costs at closing faces breach-of-fiduciary-duty claims.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk. When a buyer arrives at closing and discovers $4,000 in costs they believed the seller was covering, the buyer may refuse to close. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends well beyond lost commission — it includes reputation damage, potential earnest money litigation, and NCREC complaint exposure.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make on Closing Cost Allocation

**Mistake 1: Relying on verbal agreements about seller concessions.** A listing agent says "my seller will contribute $5,000 toward buyer costs" during a phone call but the offer form reflects no such concession. At closing, the seller denies the agreement. Without Form 2-T or Form 4-T documentation, the buyer's agent has no enforceable position and faces client liability.

**Mistake 2: Exceeding lender caps on seller concessions without checking first.** Conventional loans typically cap seller contributions at 3% for buyers with less than 10% down and 6% for those with more. FHA caps at 6%. An agent who negotiates an 8% seller concession into the contract creates a compliance failure that the lender will reject at underwriting, often days before closing.

**Mistake 3: Failing to account for the excise tax in net sheet calculations.** At $2 per $1,000 of sale price, excise tax on a $450,000 home is $900. Agents who omit this from seller net sheets create a trust gap at closing. It is a small number that causes outsized frustration.

**Mistake 4: Not updating the contract when cost allocation changes during due diligence.** Inspection negotiations often produce seller credits toward buyer costs in lieu of repairs. These must be memorialized in Form 4-T (Amendment to Contract). A handshake agreement or email confirmation is not contractually enforceable under North Carolina's Statute of Frauds (N.C.G.S. § 22-2).

**Mistake 5: Confusing the due diligence fee with closing costs.** The due diligence fee (Form 2-T, Paragraph 1(b)) is a negotiated, nonrefundable payment to the seller at contract execution. It is not a closing cost and should not appear on a buyer's closing cost estimate as though it will be credited at settlement — unless the contract explicitly states it will be applied to the purchase price.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers-in-charge carry supervisory liability under 21 NCAC 58A .0110. If an agent under your supervision consistently misrepresents closing cost obligations, the NCREC can hold you jointly accountable. Proactive auditing is not optional — it is a regulatory expectation.

Review every Form 2-T before ratification to confirm Paragraph 8 is completed accurately and that any seller concession language matches what the agent communicated to the client. Cross-reference the concession amount against loan program limits. A five-second check at contract stage prevents a five-figure problem at closing.

Require agents to produce written lender pre-approval letters that specify maximum allowable seller contributions before submitting offers with concession requests. Establish a firm policy: no verbal concession agreements, no exceptions. Agents who [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) on amendments or fail to document cost changes during due diligence should trigger an immediate file review.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|-------------------|---------------|----------|
| Form 2-T Paragraph 8 | Seller concession amount documented | Blank or "TBD" |
| Form 4-T Amendments | Cost changes during DD period documented | Email-only agreements |
| Lender Pre-Approval | Max seller contribution specified | No letter on file |
| Net Sheets | Excise tax and HOA fees included | Missing line items |
| Closing Disclosure Review | Final allocation matches contract | Discrepancies over $250 |

Brokers should also audit that agents are not inadvertently practicing law by drafting custom cost-allocation provisions outside the standard form checkboxes and blanks. If an agent needs non-standard language, route it to the closing attorney.
