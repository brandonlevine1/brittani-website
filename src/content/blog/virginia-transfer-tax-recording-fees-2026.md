## Understanding Virginia Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Getting the numbers wrong on transfer taxes or recording fees does not just embarrass your brokerage—it stalls recordation, delays funding, and can expose you to liability claims from clients who trusted your settlement figures. Virginia transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 have not changed dramatically from prior years, but the margin for error remains razor-thin because Virginia splits obligations across grantor taxes, grantee taxes, congestion relief fees, and separate circuit court recording charges. Every dollar must land on the correct line of the settlement statement or the clerk's office sends the package back.

This post breaks down the statutory framework, exact fee amounts, form numbers you will encounter, and the compliance failures that get agents and brokerages into trouble. If you handle any part of the closing coordination process—even just reviewing the preliminary HUD or CD—these details belong in your working knowledge.

## The Statutory Framework: Where the Fees Come From

Virginia's transfer tax authority lives primarily in Title 58.1, Chapter 8 of the Virginia Code. Two statutes control the bulk of what agents see at closing. [Virginia Code Section 58.1-801](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title58.1/chapter8/) establishes the state recordation tax (often called the deed recordation tax or grantee's tax in practice), assessed at $0.25 per $100 of consideration—or equivalently, $2.50 per $1,000. Virginia Code Section 58.1-802 establishes the grantor's tax at $0.50 per $500 of consideration, which works out to $1.00 per $1,000 of sale price.

**On top of these base rates, certain localities impose an additional regional congestion relief fee.** Under Virginia Code Section 58.1-802.2, properties located in Planning District 8 (Northern Virginia) or in the Hampton Roads Transportation District trigger an extra $0.15 per $100 of consideration. This surcharge is frequently missed by agents who work across district boundaries.

Recording fees for the deed itself are governed by Virginia Code Section 17.1-275, which sets the circuit court clerk's fee schedule. The base recording fee for a deed is $18.00 for the first two pages, with $5.00 for each additional page. Additional technology trust fund fees and clerk's office fees can bring the total to approximately $35-$60 depending on document length and locality-specific surcharges.

## Fee Breakdown: Who Pays What

Virginia custom—and standard contract language in the Virginia REALTORS residential contract of purchase—assigns the grantor's tax to the seller and the recordation tax to the buyer. But custom is not statute. The parties can negotiate any allocation they want, and agents must confirm allocation in the executed purchase agreement rather than assuming defaults.

| Fee Type | Statutory Citation | Rate | Customary Payor |
|----------|-------------------|------|-----------------|
| State Grantor's Tax | Section 58.1-802 | $1.00 per $1,000 | Seller |
| State Recordation Tax | Section 58.1-801 | $2.50 per $1,000 | Buyer |
| Regional Congestion Surcharge (NoVA/Hampton Roads) | Section 58.1-802.2 | $1.50 per $1,000 | Seller |
| Circuit Court Recording Fee (Deed) | Section 17.1-275 | $18.00 base + page fees | Buyer |
| Circuit Court Recording Fee (Deed of Trust) | Section 17.1-275 | $18.00 base + page fees | Buyer (via lender) |

On a $500,000 sale in Fairfax County, the seller's transfer tax obligation runs to $1,250 ($500 grantor's tax plus $750 congestion surcharge). The buyer's recordation tax hits $1,250 for the deed alone, plus additional recordation tax on the deed of trust based on the loan amount. These are not trivial sums, and misquoting them on a net sheet can swing a negotiation or kill a listing appointment.

## Forms and Documents Agents Must Know

Virginia does not have a standalone "transfer tax form" that agents file separately—the taxes are collected by the circuit court clerk at the time of recordation and remitted to the Virginia Department of Taxation. However, agents interact with several critical documents that incorporate these fees.

The settlement statement—whether a TRID Closing Disclosure (CD) or a HUD-1 for exempt transactions—must itemize transfer taxes on the correct lines. On the Closing Disclosure, grantor's taxes appear in Section H (line items under "Government Recording and Other Charges") and must match the contract allocation. The Virginia REALTORS Form 600 (Residential Contract of Purchase) includes a section specifying responsibility for settlement costs, including transfer taxes and recording fees.

**Agents listing property should verify the locality's specific additional levies before preparing any seller net sheet.** The Virginia REALTORS Form 800 (Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement) creates an expectation of accurate cost estimates. If your net sheet omits a $750 congestion surcharge, you may face a claim that you misrepresented the seller's proceeds.

For deed preparation, the settlement agent or attorney drafts the deed and includes the consideration amount that determines the tax. Virginia is an attorney-settlement state for most transactions, meaning a licensed attorney typically handles recordation. But agents still bear responsibility for ensuring the contract terms correctly reflect what will be charged.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Transfer Taxes and Recording Fees

Errors in this area tend to cluster around five recurring patterns. Understanding them keeps you out of disciplinary proceedings and client disputes.

**First, agents frequently confuse the recordation tax with the grantor's tax and quote a blended "transfer tax" rate that is neither.** When a buyer asks what closing costs to expect, an agent who says "transfer tax is about $3.50 per thousand" without specifying which portion belongs to whom creates a misleading impression. The correct practice is to separate buyer-side recordation tax from seller-side grantor's tax in every written estimate.

Second, agents working in jurisdictions that straddle Planning District 8 boundaries forget the congestion surcharge. A property in Prince William County triggers the surcharge; a property 15 minutes away in Fauquier County does not. Relying on "Northern Virginia" as a geographic shorthand rather than confirming Planning District designation causes miscalculations that surface at the settlement table.

Third, some agents fail to account for the recordation tax on the deed of trust—not just the deed. The buyer's lender will require recordation of the deed of trust, and the recordation tax applies to the secured amount. On a $400,000 loan, that is an additional $1,000 in recordation tax the buyer must bring to closing. Omitting it from a buyer's estimated closing costs creates sticker shock at final walkthrough time.

Fourth, agents occasionally represent that transfer taxes are "negotiable" without clarifying that only the allocation between parties is negotiable—the tax itself is fixed by statute. Telling a buyer "we can get the seller to waive the transfer tax" mischaracterizes how the system works and can constitute a [material misrepresentation that derails a deal](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Fifth, agents neglect to verify exemptions. Certain transfers—between spouses, to revocable trusts, or in foreclosure situations—are exempt under Virginia Code Section 58.1-811. Failing to flag an exemption means your client overpays at closing. Failing to recognize that a transaction is not exempt means the clerk rejects the documents.

## What Happens When Compliance Fails

The Virginia Real Estate Board, operating under the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), does not directly enforce tax collection—that falls to the circuit court clerks and the Department of Taxation. But agents face liability on multiple fronts when transfer tax or recording fee errors occur.

If a deed is presented with insufficient tax payment, the clerk refuses recordation. Title does not transfer. The transaction does not close on time, triggering potential default under the contract. The agent who coordinated the closing or prepared the erroneous estimates may face a claim for the buyer's rate-lock extension costs, temporary housing expenses, or seller's carrying costs during the delay.

**Under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2131, a licensee must disclose all material facts and exercise reasonable care.** Systematically miscalculating closing costs—particularly after being informed of errors—could constitute a violation leading to DPOR disciplinary action. Sanctions range from required continuing education to license suspension. Civil liability through the Virginia Real Estate Transaction Recovery Fund under Section 54.1-2112.1 provides another avenue for harmed consumers to recover damages.

The Department of Taxation can also assess penalties under Section 58.1-813 if taxes are underpaid and the deficiency is discovered post-recording. A 10% penalty plus interest accrues, and while the liability technically falls on the taxpayer (buyer or seller depending on the tax type), the agent who caused the error often ends up absorbing the cost through negotiation or litigation.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers carry an independent obligation under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2130 to ensure that affiliated licensees comply with all applicable laws. This means brokers cannot passively assume their agents are getting transfer tax calculations right. Active file review should flag specific items.

| Audit Checkpoint | What to Verify | Risk if Missed |
|-----------------|----------------|----------------|
| Seller Net Sheets | Grantor's tax + congestion surcharge included | Misrepresentation claim |
| Buyer Cost Estimates | Recordation tax on deed AND deed of trust shown separately | Sticker shock, potential rescission |
| Contract Form 600 | Settlement cost allocation section completed | Ambiguous responsibility at closing |
| Locality Verification | Planning District confirmed for surcharge applicability | Underpayment, rejected recordation |
| Exemption Documentation | Section 58.1-811 exemption cited with supporting docs | Overpayment or rejection |

Brokers should build these checkpoints into their [transaction handoff process](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) and require agents to submit preliminary net sheets for review before delivery to clients. A brokerage that catches a $750 surcharge omission before the listing appointment protects both the agent's credibility and the firm's E&O exposure.

Quarterly training refreshers on fee schedule updates—even when rates have not changed—reinforce awareness. Virginia's General Assembly occasionally proposes adjustments to congestion surcharge boundaries or rate increases, and agents who last studied these numbers during pre-licensing coursework five years ago are operating on outdated assumptions. Brokers who maintain a [structured timeline for training new agents](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) on compliance topics reduce systemic risk across the office.
