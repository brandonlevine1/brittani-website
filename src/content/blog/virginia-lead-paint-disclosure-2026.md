## Why Virginia Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026 Still Trip Up Experienced Agents

Federal lead paint rules have been on the books since 1996, yet HUD enforcement actions against real estate professionals continue to climb. Understanding Virginia lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 is not optional—it is a condition of lawfully selling any residential property built before 1978 in the Commonwealth. The penalties are real, the paperwork is specific, and the margin for error is thinner than agents often assume.

Virginia does not layer a separate state-level lead paint statute on top of the federal mandate the way some states do. Instead, Virginia agents operate directly under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulations at 24 CFR Part 35 and 40 CFR Part 745. The Virginia Real Estate Board, housed within the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), treats violations of federal disclosure law as grounds for disciplinary action under the Virginia Real Estate Transaction Recovery Act and Virginia Code Section 54.1-2131.

This means your liability runs on two tracks simultaneously: federal fines administered by EPA and HUD, plus state-level license discipline from DPOR. A single missed checkbox can trigger both.

## The Specific Forms Virginia Agents Must Use

**The most commonly used form in Virginia transactions is the Virginia Association of Realtors (VAR) Form 600-L, "Lead-Based Paint Disclosure."** This form mirrors the federal requirements codified at 42 U.S.C. 4852d and satisfies the EPA/HUD disclosure mandate when completed correctly. Agents working outside the VAR ecosystem sometimes use the generic EPA disclosure form, which is equally valid as long as it contains all required elements.

Every compliant lead paint disclosure must include three components delivered together. First, the seller's disclosure statement regarding known lead-based paint or hazards. Second, a list of any reports or records pertaining to lead-based paint in the property. Third, the EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" (last updated 2021 edition).

The buyer must then acknowledge receipt of all three items and confirm whether they elect to conduct a lead inspection or waive that right. Both the buyer's and seller's signatures, along with the agents' signatures, must appear on the form. Missing any single signature makes the disclosure legally incomplete.

For agents handling [Virginia seller disclosure obligations](/blog/virginia-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) more broadly, the lead paint form sits alongside—but is legally distinct from—the Virginia Residential Property Disclosure Statement required under Virginia Code Section 55.1-702.

## Buyer vs. Seller Responsibilities at a Glance

Confusion over who owes what to whom is the root of most compliance failures. The table below breaks down the division of duties under federal law as applied in Virginia transactions.

| Responsibility | Seller's Obligation | Buyer's Right | Agent's Duty |
|---|---|---|---|
| Disclose known lead paint or hazards | Must disclose all known information | Receives disclosure before contract ratification | Ensure form is completed and signed |
| Provide existing records/reports | Must attach or make available | May review prior to signing | Confirm records are included with form |
| EPA pamphlet delivery | Must provide (often delegated to agent) | Must receive before becoming obligated | Distribute and document receipt |
| 10-day inspection period | Must allow unless buyer waives | May conduct inspection or waive in writing | Confirm election is documented on form |
| Retain signed disclosure | Must keep copy for 3 years | Should retain copy | Must retain copy for 3 years minimum |

Notice that the agent has an independent obligation. You are not merely a conduit—you are a named party on the form with your own signature line and your own recordkeeping duty.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The penalties for noncompliance are not theoretical. Under 40 CFR 745.118, civil penalties can reach $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2025; the 2026 figure may increase per the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act). Criminal penalties can reach $19,507 per day for knowing and willful violations under certain circumstances, and treble damages are available in private lawsuits.

**At the state level, the Virginia Real Estate Board can suspend or revoke a license under 18 VAC 135-20-300 for failure to comply with applicable federal housing laws.** A DPOR investigation typically begins with a consumer complaint and can result in fines, mandatory education, or license action independent of any federal proceeding.

Buyers who discover undisclosed lead hazards after closing have a private right of action under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3). Successful plaintiffs can recover actual damages, court costs, attorney fees, and up to three times the amount of damages as additional penalty. The three-year retention requirement for signed disclosures exists specifically so that you have a defense if a claim arises years later.

Deal cancellation is also on the table. A buyer who was not given the required 10-day inspection opportunity—or who never received the disclosure at all—may have grounds to rescind the contract. Virginia courts have not extensively litigated rescission on lead paint grounds, but the federal regulatory framework clearly contemplates it.

## Common Mistakes Virginia Agents Make

Five errors account for the vast majority of lead paint disclosure problems in Virginia transactions. Each one is entirely preventable.

**First, agents forget that the disclosure must be delivered before the buyer becomes obligated under the contract.** Handing the form to the buyer at closing—or even at the home inspection—is too late. The disclosure must precede contract ratification. In competitive markets where offers fly within hours of listing, agents sometimes skip the form in the rush and plan to "circle back." That plan creates federal liability the moment the buyer signs.

Second, agents assume the 10-day inspection period is automatic. It is not automatic in the sense that it just happens—it must be affirmatively offered. If the buyer wants to waive the inspection, that waiver must be documented in writing on the disclosure form. Leaving the inspection-period section blank is not a waiver; it is an incomplete form.

Third, listing agents fail to ask the seller about existing records. The disclosure requires the seller to attach or reference any available reports, test results, or inspection records related to lead. Many agents hand the seller the form and accept "none" without probing. If a seller had a lead inspection performed for a prior sale or renovation permit, those records exist—and failing to disclose them creates liability for both seller and agent.

Fourth, agents neglect their own signature line. The form requires both agents to sign, confirming they have informed their respective clients of their obligations. An unsigned agent line does not just look sloppy—it means the disclosure is technically incomplete under 24 CFR 35.92(b).

Fifth, agents destroy or lose the signed form before the three-year retention period expires. If you handle [multiple active deals simultaneously](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), maintaining compliant records across dozens of transactions becomes a systems problem. The three-year clock starts at the date of the last signature, not the closing date.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under both federal law and Virginia Code Section 54.1-2130. A broker who knows—or should have known—that agents under their supervision are failing to deliver lead paint disclosures faces the same penalty exposure as the agent.

The following table outlines a minimum audit framework for Virginia brokerages handling pre-1978 inventory.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Signed VAR Form 600-L in file | Every pre-1978 transaction | Missing form or missing signatures |
| EPA pamphlet receipt confirmation | Every pre-1978 transaction | No buyer acknowledgment |
| 10-day inspection election documented | Every pre-1978 transaction | Blank or ambiguous election section |
| Three-year file retention verification | Quarterly | Files purged before expiration |
| Agent training documentation | Annually | No evidence of lead paint compliance training |

**Brokers should treat a missing lead paint disclosure the same way they treat a missing agency disclosure—as a file deficiency that must be corrected before closing.** The Virginia Real Estate Board's audit standards under 18 VAC 135-20-185 require brokers to maintain transaction records that demonstrate compliance with all applicable laws. A DPOR audit that reveals systematic disclosure failures will not just penalize individual agents—it will land on the broker's license.

One practical enforcement mechanism: require your transaction coordinator or compliance staff to flag every listing with a pre-1978 build year at intake. If the listing file does not contain a completed 600-L before the first showing, it should not go active. This mirrors the kind of workflow that firms managing [agency disclosure obligations](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) already apply to other mandatory forms.

## The 10-Day Inspection Window: How It Actually Works

The 10-day inspection opportunity is the most misunderstood element of the federal rule. Sellers must permit buyers to conduct a risk assessment or inspection for lead-based paint at the buyer's expense. The parties may mutually agree to a different time period—longer or shorter—but absent written agreement, the default is 10 calendar days.

If the buyer elects to inspect, the clock typically starts on contract ratification or the date the disclosure is delivered, depending on your contract language. Virginia contracts often incorporate this timeline into the home inspection contingency, but the lead inspection right is legally separate from a general home inspection contingency under Virginia Code Section 55.1-704.

Buyers who waive the inspection must do so in writing on the disclosure form. A verbal waiver is insufficient. A contract clause stating "buyer waives all inspections" does not satisfy the lead-specific requirement—the waiver must appear on the lead disclosure form itself.

## Exemptions: When the Disclosure Does Not Apply

Not every Virginia residential sale triggers lead paint disclosure. The [EPA's regulatory framework](https://www.epa.gov/lead/lead-paint-disclosure-rule) exempts several categories of transactions: housing built in 1978 or later, housing certified as lead-free by an accredited inspector, zero-bedroom units (studios, dormitories, lofts), short-term lease renewals of 100 days or fewer, and sales of housing at foreclosure.

The most common trap here involves the "certified lead-free" exemption. A seller who says "we had all the lead paint removed" is not the same as a property certified lead-free by a Virginia-licensed lead inspector. Unless there is a written certification from a licensed professional on file, the disclosure requirement still applies.

Senior housing and housing for persons with disabilities are also exempt, but only if no children under six reside or are expected to reside there—a condition that is nearly impossible to guarantee in a sale context. Agents should default to providing the disclosure unless a clear, documented exemption applies.
