## Virginia New Real Estate Laws 2026: The Statutory Changes That Matter

The Virginia Real Estate Board (VREB) and the General Assembly passed a series of bills during the 2025-2026 legislative session that rewrite portions of the Virginia Real Estate Transaction Recovery Act and amend key sections of the Virginia Residential Property Disclosure Act. If you hold an active license in the Commonwealth, these Virginia new real estate laws 2026 changes affect how you take listings, represent buyers, handle earnest money, and document agency relationships starting July 1, 2026.

This is not a minor update cycle. The amendments touch Virginia Code Title 54.1, Chapter 21 in at least six separate sections, and they directly impact the forms published by the Virginia REALTORS association. Agents who rely on last year's templates risk deal cancellations, disciplinary complaints, and personal liability that errors-and-omissions insurance may not cover.

## Buyer Representation Agreements Get Teeth

**Virginia Code Section 54.1-2138.1, amended effective July 1, 2026, now mandates a written buyer representation agreement before any substantive real estate activity.** The prior standard allowed oral agreements in limited circumstances. That era is over.

The Virginia REALTORS form VAR-460 (Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement) has been updated to include a mandatory compensation disclosure addendum, now designated VAR-460A. This addendum must specify the exact compensation amount or percentage the buyer's agent expects, who pays it, and the circumstances under which the buyer owes a fee directly.

Agents cannot show property, provide a CMA for a buyer, or discuss specific listing details until the buyer signs this agreement. VREB has clarified in its 2026 guidance bulletin that "substantive real estate activity" includes virtual tours conducted in real time with the agent providing commentary.

## Seller Disclosure Overhaul: New Material Facts Standard

The Virginia Residential Property Disclosure Act (Virginia Code Section 55.1-702 through 55.1-714) now expands what constitutes a material fact that sellers must disclose. The amended statute adds three categories: known insurance claims within the past five years, presence of any private transfer fee covenants, and proximity to planned public infrastructure projects disclosed in county filings.

The updated Virginia Residential Property Disclosure Statement form must be delivered to the buyer or buyer's agent no later than five days after ratification of the contract, unless a shorter period is specified. The previous form version lacked a dedicated section for insurance claim history. The 2026 revision adds Section G to the disclosure, which asks sellers to identify the nature and outcome of any property insurance claims filed since 2021.

**Listing agents bear an independent duty to confirm that sellers have completed every section of the disclosure.** Leaving Section G blank does not satisfy the statute. VREB has stated that a blank section will be treated as a failure to disclose for enforcement purposes, exposing both the seller and the listing agent to liability under Virginia Code Section 55.1-708.

## Commission Structure and Compensation Transparency

| Requirement | Pre-2026 Standard | 2026 Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer agent compensation disclosure | Recommended but not mandated in writing | Required in VAR-460A before showing |
| Listing agreement compensation terms | Stated in MLS, negotiable | Must specify whether seller offers buyer agent compensation on VAR-600 |
| Dual agency commission split | Disclosed at consent | Disclosed at initial agency disclosure, before consent |
| Commission disputes | Handled post-closing via arbitration | Must be addressed in writing before ratification per VREB Rule 18VAC135-20-180 |

The changes here mirror national trends following the NAR settlement but carry Virginia-specific enforcement mechanisms. VREB Rule 18VAC135-20-180, as amended, requires that any compensation arrangement between cooperating brokers be documented in writing and attached to the transaction file before the contract is ratified. Verbal agreements between brokers no longer satisfy the documentation standard.

This intersects directly with the [commission structure changes](/blog/virginia-commission-structure-changes-2026) that went into effect earlier this year and have already generated enforcement actions.

## Agency Disclosure Timing and Electronic Delivery

Virginia Code Section 54.1-2138 governs when and how agents must disclose their brokerage relationship. The 2026 amendment introduces a hard deadline for electronic disclosure delivery: within two business hours of first substantive contact when that contact occurs through digital means (email, text, video call, or messaging platform).

The form itself, VAR-480 (Disclosure of Brokerage Relationship), has been revised to include a timestamp field and an acknowledgment checkbox for electronic receipt. Agents using transaction management software must ensure the platform records the exact time of delivery and the buyer or seller's acknowledgment.

**Failure to deliver VAR-480 within the statutory window creates a rebuttable presumption that no agency relationship exists.** This means the agent has no legal basis to collect a commission from that party and no fiduciary duties are owed. It also means the agent cannot claim agency protections if a dispute arises.

## Common Mistakes Agents Are Already Making

The VREB enforcement docket from Q1 2026 reveals patterns. Here are the errors that are generating complaints and formal investigations.

First, agents are using outdated VAR-460 forms that lack the compensation addendum (VAR-460A). The old form was retired in March 2026. Any buyer agreement executed on the pre-March form after July 1 is considered incomplete, and VREB treats it as a failure to obtain written authorization.

Second, listing agents are not updating the seller disclosure when new material facts emerge between listing and closing. The 2026 amendment to Section 55.1-706 requires supplemental disclosure within three business days of the seller learning new material information. Agents who knew about a roof leak reported after inspection but did not prompt the seller to supplement the disclosure have already faced complaints.

Third, agents are treating the two-business-hour electronic delivery window for VAR-480 as a suggestion rather than a statutory mandate. Three disciplinary actions in Northern Virginia alone involved agents who sent the disclosure form the morning after an evening video consultation with a prospective buyer.

Fourth, buyer's agents are not retaining proof that the compensation discussion occurred before the first showing. The statute requires the discussion to precede the showing, but VREB requires documentation that proves the sequence. A signed VAR-460A with a date after the showing date in MLS records is a red flag that triggers investigation.

Fifth, dual agency consent forms (VAR-490) are being presented at the wrong time. The 2026 amendment requires that dual agency be disclosed and consented to before any offer is drafted, not at the time of offer presentation. Presenting VAR-490 alongside a purchase agreement is now a per se violation.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

| Audit Item | Frequency | Documentation Required |
|---|---|---|
| VAR-460A attached to every buyer file | Per transaction, before first showing | Signed form with date and timestamp |
| VAR-480 delivery timing | Per transaction, at file opening | Electronic delivery receipt with timestamp |
| Seller disclosure completeness (Section G) | At listing and again before closing | Completed disclosure form, initialed by seller |
| Commission documentation per 18VAC135-20-180 | Before contract ratification | Written agreement between cooperating brokers |
| Dual agency consent timing (VAR-490) | Before offer drafting | Signed consent form dated prior to offer date |

Supervising brokers face direct liability under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2105 for failing to supervise agents' compliance with these requirements. VREB has signaled that it will hold brokers accountable for systemic failures even when individual agents are the ones making errors.

Brokers should implement a file review checkpoint that occurs between the signed buyer agreement and the first showing, and again between contract ratification and the delivery of the disclosure package. Waiting until closing to audit files means problems surface when it is too late to correct them.

The [VREB regulatory guidance page](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov/Boards/Real-Estate) publishes updated enforcement bulletins monthly. Brokers should assign a compliance officer or office manager to review each bulletin within a week of publication.

## Earnest Money and Trust Account Amendments

Virginia Code Section 54.1-2108.1, as amended, now requires that earnest money deposits be delivered to the escrow agent within two business days of contract ratification, reduced from the prior five-business-day standard. The holding broker must issue a written receipt within one business day of deposit.

This shorter timeline means agents cannot hold checks over a weekend and deposit on Monday if the contract was ratified on Thursday. Friday ratification means a Monday deposit deadline, period. Agents who hold earnest money past the two-day window expose their broker to trust account violations carrying fines of up to $2,500 per occurrence under VREB Rule 18VAC135-20-250.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

VREB enforcement actions for the violations described above range from formal reprimands to license suspension. The fine schedule published in the 2026 enforcement manual sets the following baseline penalties: failure to obtain written buyer representation before showing, $1,000 fine and mandatory continuing education; failure to deliver disclosure within statutory timeframe, $1,500 fine and possible license probation; trust account timing violations, $2,500 fine per occurrence.

Beyond regulatory penalties, agents face civil liability. A buyer who can demonstrate they were not properly informed of their agent's compensation arrangement before the first showing may void the buyer agency agreement entirely and refuse to pay any commission. A seller whose agent failed to ensure timely supplemental disclosure may face rescission claims that unwind the entire transaction post-closing.

Errors-and-omissions insurance policies typically exclude coverage for regulatory fines and may deny claims where the agent's violation was willful or the result of using outdated forms. The gap between what insurance covers and what an agent owes out of pocket is where careers end.
