## Ohio Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026: The Rules That Changed Everything

The Ohio buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 landscape is not the same one agents operated in two years ago. Between the NAR settlement practice changes that took effect on August 17, 2024, and Ohio-specific statutory mandates under Ohio Revised Code Chapter 4735, agents who fail to secure a proper written buyer agreement before touring now face real consequences. This is not a soft recommendation from your brokerage's compliance officer—it is an enforceable legal obligation backed by the Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing.

Ohio has historically required agency disclosure through the Consumer Guide to Agency Relationships, but the mechanics of buyer representation agreements have tightened significantly. Agents who treat these forms as an afterthought are exposing themselves—and their brokers—to disciplinary proceedings, commission disputes, and deal-killing liability issues.

## The Statutory Framework: ORC 4735 and Division Rules

Ohio's agency law lives in Ohio Revised Code Chapter 4735, specifically Sections 4735.55 through 4735.681. Section 4735.681 mandates that a licensee acting as a buyer's agent must operate under a written agency agreement that defines the scope of representation, the duration of the relationship, and the compensation structure. The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, housed within the Ohio Department of Commerce, enforces these provisions.

**The written agreement requirement is not optional, and verbal understandings do not satisfy the statute.** An agent cannot claim buyer agency status—or the fiduciary duties that come with it—without an executed document. The Division has made clear in its [administrative guidance](https://com.ohio.gov/divisions-and-programs/real-estate-and-professional-licensing) that post-settlement MLS practice changes do not override state law but rather layer additional obligations on top of it.

The Consumer Guide to Agency Relationships (required under ORC 4735.57) is a disclosure document, not an agreement. Agents who confuse the two are making a distinction without a difference in their own minds but a very material one in front of a hearing officer.

## Which Forms Ohio Agents Must Use

Ohio REALTORS (the state association) publishes the standardized forms most brokerages adopt. The two primary buyer agency forms are:

| Form Number | Form Name | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| Ohio REALTORS Form 101 | Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement | Buyer works exclusively with one agent/brokerage for a defined period |
| Ohio REALTORS Form 102 | Non-Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement | Buyer may work with multiple agents simultaneously |
| Ohio REALTORS Form 103 | Agency Disclosure (Consumer Guide) | Disclosure only—does not create an agency relationship |
| Brokerage-Specific | Custom Buyer Representation Agreement | Must comply with ORC 4735.681 and NAR settlement terms |

Form 101 is the most commonly used in practice. It must include a definite expiration date, a clear statement of how the agent will be compensated, and language confirming the buyer's agreement to the terms before touring begins. Form 102 is less common but relevant for buyers who want to interview multiple agents simultaneously without exclusivity.

Post-settlement, both forms were revised in late 2024 to explicitly state that compensation is negotiable, that it cannot be open-ended, and that MLS offers of compensation are no longer guaranteed. If your brokerage is still using pre-August 2024 versions of these forms, you are out of compliance.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of operating without a valid buyer agency agreement in Ohio fall into three categories: regulatory, civil, and transactional.

**Regulatory penalties come from the Ohio Division of Real Estate.** Under ORC 4735.18, the Division can impose disciplinary action for violations including failing to maintain proper agency documentation. Sanctions range from formal reprimand and mandatory continuing education to license suspension or revocation. Fines up to $5,000 per violation are authorized under the administrative code.

Civil liability exposes agents to commission disputes and breach-of-fiduciary-duty claims. Without a written agreement, a buyer can argue that no agency relationship existed, meaning the agent has no enforceable compensation claim. Conversely, if an agent acts as though they represent a buyer without documentation, they may still owe fiduciary duties without the contractual right to be paid—the worst of both worlds.

Transactional consequences are immediate and painful. A listing agent or seller's attorney who discovers the buyer's agent has no signed agreement may challenge the commission split, refuse to negotiate compensation, or question whether the buyer received proper disclosures. Deals fall apart over this. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond the single transaction when it triggers a license complaint.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make With Buyer Agreements

Five errors surface repeatedly in Ohio Division disciplinary cases and brokerage audits. Each one is avoidable.

**First, agents present the agreement after showing properties instead of before.** The NAR settlement rules and Ohio law both require the agreement to be signed prior to touring. Showing three houses and then asking for a signature at the car does not satisfy the requirement, and it creates a factual record that you operated without authorization.

Second, agents use outdated forms that reference MLS-based compensation guarantees. Any form language suggesting the seller "will" pay the buyer's agent commission through MLS is now misleading. Ohio REALTORS updated Forms 101 and 102 to address this, but agents using old PDF versions downloaded years ago are inadvertently misrepresenting compensation structures.

Third, agents fail to specify a definite termination date. ORC 4735.681 requires a definite duration. An agreement that states "until buyer purchases a home" without a calendar end date does not meet the statutory standard. Best practice in 2026 is limiting initial agreements to 90 days or tying them to a specific property tour.

Fourth, agents neglect to explain the compensation section clearly. The buyer must understand whether they might owe compensation directly if the seller refuses to pay, how much the agent's fee is, and whether it is a flat fee or percentage. Ambiguity here invites disputes and Division complaints.

Fifth, agents sign on behalf of their brokerage without proper authority. In Ohio, the agency relationship technically exists between the buyer and the brokerage, not the individual agent. The form must reflect this, and the agent must be authorized by their broker to enter the agreement. Agents who switch brokerages mid-relationship without executing a new agreement create an enforceability gap.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokerage responsibility under ORC 4735.18(A)(6) includes supervising affiliated agents' compliance with agency law. A broker who ignores systemic failures in buyer agreement execution is personally at risk.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Agreement execution date vs. first showing date | Agreement must predate any touring activity | Every transaction |
| Form version | Must be post-August 2024 revision or brokerage-approved equivalent | Quarterly |
| Termination date present | Calendar date required; no open-ended language | Every transaction |
| Compensation clarity | Specific amount or percentage; disclosure that it is negotiable | Every transaction |
| Broker signature/authorization | Agent authorized in writing to bind brokerage | Onboarding and annually |

**Brokers should implement a pre-showing checkpoint in their transaction management systems.** Before any showing is scheduled, the system should flag whether a signed buyer agreement exists in the file. Many [agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) not because they are careless but because no system prompts them at the right moment.

Training is the other half of enforcement. A quarterly compliance review that walks agents through the updated forms, explains recent Division enforcement actions, and tests their understanding of the compensation disclosure requirements will reduce errors dramatically. Brokers who build this into their [new agent training timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) protect themselves from vicarious liability.

## Ohio Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026: Duration and Scope Limitations

The post-settlement emphasis on limiting agreement scope deserves its own discussion. Ohio law requires a definite termination date, and the NAR settlement practice changes strongly encourage agreements that are "reasonably limited" in duration and scope.

What does "reasonably limited" mean in Ohio practice? The Division has not published a bright-line rule, but enforcement trends suggest that agreements lasting more than six months without renewal will draw scrutiny. Agreements that cover "all property types in all of Ohio" are harder to defend than those specifying a geographic area and property type relevant to the buyer's actual search.

**Scope limitation also means compensation terms cannot exceed what is agreed upon.** If your agreement states a 2.5% fee and the seller offers 3% through negotiation, the excess does not automatically flow to you—it must be addressed in the agreement or a separate disclosure. Agents who pocket undisclosed excess compensation face disciplinary action under ORC 4735.18(A)(9) for failing to account for funds.

## Handling Modifications, Cancellations, and Disputes

Buyers have the right to terminate a non-exclusive agreement (Form 102) at any time, but exclusive agreements (Form 101) bind both parties through the termination date unless modified in writing. Ohio REALTORS provides Form 107 (Termination of Agency Agreement) for this purpose.

If a dispute arises over whether the agreement was properly executed, the burden of proof falls on the agent claiming the agency relationship existed. This is why document management matters—keeping timestamped, signed copies in your transaction file is not just good practice, it is your defense. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) often starts with missing or improperly executed paperwork.

Arbitration clauses in buyer agency agreements are enforceable in Ohio under the Federal Arbitration Act and Ohio Revised Code Chapter 2711, but they must be conspicuous and separately initialed to withstand a challenge. Burying an arbitration clause in page-four fine print invites a court to find it unconscionable.
