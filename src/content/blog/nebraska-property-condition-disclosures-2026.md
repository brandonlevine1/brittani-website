# Nebraska Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026: What Every Agent Must Get Right

Failing to deliver a disclosure form on time might seem like a minor paperwork hiccup—until a buyer rescinds, a broker faces a commission clawback, or an errors-and-omissions claim lands on someone's desk. Nebraska property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 requirements remain one of the most mishandled compliance areas across the state's residential real estate market. This post breaks down the statutes, the specific forms, the penalties for noncompliance, and what brokers should be auditing in every file.

## The Statutory Framework Agents Must Know

Nebraska's seller disclosure obligations are codified in Neb. Rev. Stat. sections 76-2,117 through 76-2,120, commonly called the Nebraska Seller Property Condition Disclosure Act. The Act mandates that sellers of residential real property (one to four dwelling units) provide a written disclosure statement to prospective buyers before or at the time of a purchase agreement. If the disclosure arrives after contract formation, the buyer gets a three-business-day rescission window under 76-2,118.

The Nebraska Real Estate Commission (NREC) administers agent licensing and enforces conduct standards through Title 299 of the Nebraska Administrative Code. Agents who facilitate a transaction without ensuring proper disclosure delivery risk disciplinary action under 299 NAC Chapter 4, which covers unprofessional conduct. The Commission can impose license suspension, probation, or revocation—on top of any civil liability the agent personally incurs.

Environmental disclosures layer additional federal requirements on top of Nebraska state law. The Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act (42 U.S.C. 4852d) and its implementing regulation at 40 C.F.R. Part 745 require disclosure of known lead-based paint hazards in any residential property built before 1978. Both sellers and their agents bear independent enforcement exposure under this federal rule, with fines up to $19,507 per violation as of the 2025 HUD adjustment.

## Nebraska Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026: Key Forms and Documents

**The primary form agents encounter is the Seller Property Condition Disclosure Statement, which the Nebraska Association of Realtors publishes as form NE-PCD.** This form tracks the statutory requirements in 76-2,117 and covers structural systems, mechanical systems, water and sewer, environmental hazards, and known material defects. Sellers must complete it in good faith—meaning they disclose what they actually know, not what a home inspector might later find.

For pre-1978 homes, agents must use the federally prescribed "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form (commonly referenced as the EPA lead paint disclosure form). Nebraska does not have a separate state-level lead paint disclosure requirement; the federal form satisfies the obligation. The listing agent must retain a signed copy for at least three years after closing.

Additional environmental disclosures may arise when a property sits within a known contamination area. The Nebraska Department of Environment and Energy (NDEE) maintains a public registry of sites with verified contamination under the Remedial Action Plan Monitoring Act. While the state does not impose a standalone disclosure form for these sites, known contamination is a material fact that must be disclosed under 76-2,117(3).

| Document | Governs | Who Must Provide | Retention Period |
|----------|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| NE-PCD (Seller Property Condition Disclosure) | Neb. Rev. Stat. 76-2,117 | Seller (agent must ensure delivery) | Duration of listing + 5 years |
| EPA Lead Paint Disclosure | 42 U.S.C. 4852d / 40 C.F.R. 745 | Seller and listing agent jointly | 3 years post-closing |
| NDEE Contamination Notice (if applicable) | Material fact under 76-2,117(3) | Seller; agent if agent has knowledge | Duration of listing + 5 years |
| Radon Disclosure (voluntary but recommended) | EPA guidance / NAR best practice | Seller | No statutory mandate in NE |

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Civil liability is the most direct consequence. Under 76-2,119, a buyer who does not receive the required disclosure may bring a civil action for actual damages resulting from undisclosed defects. Nebraska courts have held that agents who knowingly participated in—or negligently facilitated—a nondisclosure can be named as defendants alongside the seller.

The NREC can open a complaint investigation if a buyer, cooperating agent, or any member of the public files a grievance. Potential outcomes range from a letter of caution for minor administrative lapses to formal disciplinary proceedings for willful omissions. A 2024 NREC enforcement bulletin reminded licensees that failure to ensure disclosure delivery constitutes a violation of Neb. Rev. Stat. 81-885.24(29), which prohibits conduct that demonstrates incompetence or untrustworthiness.

Deal cancellation is also on the table. If the seller delivers the disclosure late (after contract execution), the buyer's three-day rescission right under 76-2,118 resets the deal timeline. Agents who let this window lapse without proper documentation often find themselves explaining to their broker why a commission evaporated at the eleventh hour. For lead paint violations specifically, the EPA can impose fines independently of any state action—meaning an agent could face both NREC discipline and a five-figure federal penalty from a single transaction.

## Common Mistakes Nebraska Agents Make

**Mistake one: treating the disclosure as a closing task rather than a pre-contract requirement.** The statute is explicit—delivery must occur before or at the time of the purchase agreement. Agents who stuff the form into a closing package create rescission risk and expose their brokerage to liability. The three-day rescission clock only applies when delivery happens after contract, and it is not a grace period you should plan around.

Mistake two: listing agents accepting incomplete forms from sellers. When a seller leaves multiple sections blank or writes "unknown" across the board without explanation, the form may not satisfy the statutory "good faith" standard. The listing agent's job is not to fill in answers, but it is to send the form back and ask the seller to complete it properly before marketing begins.

Mistake three: failing to provide the lead paint disclosure for properties where the build date is ambiguous. If county records are unclear or the property has had multiple additions, the safe practice is to disclose. Agents who assume a property is post-1978 without verification put themselves on the wrong side of 40 C.F.R. 745.107, which places the burden on the seller's agent to ensure compliance.

Mistake four: not documenting the date and method of delivery. Nebraska's rescission clock runs from delivery, not from signing. If an agent cannot prove when the buyer received the disclosure—email timestamp, signed receipt, transaction management log—the buyer's rescission right remains ambiguous. This is the kind of gap that [makes agents miss critical deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) and turns a straightforward closing into a dispute.

Mistake five: ignoring environmental red flags that fall outside the standard form. A property adjacent to a former dry cleaner, within a wellhead protection area, or near a NDEE-listed site triggers a duty to disclose material facts the agent actually knows. The NE-PCD form alone may not capture these issues, and agents who rely solely on the form's checkboxes leave exposure on the table.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

**Brokers carry supervisory liability under Neb. Rev. Stat. 81-885.24 and NREC Rule 299 NAC 3-004, which require designated brokers to maintain reasonable oversight of their agents' transaction files.** A broker who fails to establish disclosure compliance protocols is not insulated by an agent's negligence—the Commission can sanction both independently.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Frequency |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| Disclosure delivery timing | Date on NE-PCD vs. contract execution date | Every file at contract stage |
| Lead paint form completeness | All fields signed, 10-day inspection acknowledgment initialed | Every pre-1978 property |
| Seller completeness | No unexplained blanks on NE-PCD | Before listing goes active |
| Delivery documentation | Email receipt, e-signature log, or physical signed acknowledgment | Every file at contract stage |
| Environmental flag review | Cross-reference property address with NDEE public registry | Properties in industrial or mixed-use zones |

Brokers should build a file review checkpoint at the executed-contract stage, not at closing. Waiting until closing to discover a missing disclosure creates the exact rescission exposure the statute was designed to prevent. A [transaction coordinator or compliance workflow](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) can catch these gaps before they metastasize into formal complaints.

Quarterly file audits are a best practice the NREC has publicly encouraged through continuing education bulletins. Brokers who document their audit process—dates reviewed, deficiencies noted, corrective actions taken—create a defensible record if a complaint later surfaces. The standard is "reasonable supervision," not perfection, but "we don't have a process" is not a defense the Commission accepts kindly.

## Environmental Disclosures Beyond Lead Paint

Radon remains a gray area in Nebraska. The state has some of the highest average indoor radon levels in the country, yet there is no statutory disclosure mandate specific to radon. The [NREC](https://nrec.nebraska.gov) has recommended—but not required—that agents encourage sellers to disclose known radon test results. Agents operating in high-radon counties like Douglas, Sarpy, or Lancaster should treat known radon levels as material facts regardless of whether a checkbox exists on the form.

Underground storage tanks, former agricultural chemical use, and proximity to Superfund sites listed on the EPA's National Priorities List also trigger material-fact disclosure duties. Nebraska's agricultural heritage means many rural and suburban lots were previously farmed with chemicals that may leave residual contamination. When an agent has actual knowledge—from the seller, from public records, or from their own observation—the duty to disclose attaches.

The practical takeaway: the NE-PCD form is a floor, not a ceiling. Any material fact an agent knows about environmental conditions must be disclosed even if the form does not include a specific line item for it.
