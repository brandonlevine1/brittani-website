## Michigan 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: The State-Level Rules Agents Overlook

**Most Michigan agents understand IRC Section 1031 in broad strokes but have never examined how the state's own tax code interacts with a deferred exchange.** Michigan 1031 exchange state tax implications 2026 carry real consequences for your clients and your license. A blown deadline, a misunderstood filing obligation, or casual tax advice from an unlicensed mouth can collapse a deal and trigger liability that lands squarely on your E&O policy.

Michigan's individual income tax—codified under the Michigan Income Tax Act of 1967, MCL 206.1 et seq.—generally conforms to federal treatment of like-kind exchanges. That conformity sounds simple until you dig into transfer tax obligations, withholding triggers for out-of-state sellers, and the specific forms that must accompany a closing. This post unpacks exactly what licensed agents and brokers need to know to keep transactions clean and compliant through the end of 2026.

## How Michigan Conforms to Federal 1031 Treatment

Michigan does not have a separate state statute authorizing like-kind exchanges. Instead, the state's income tax calculation begins with federal adjusted gross income (AGI) on Form MI-1040, Line 10. Because a properly executed 1031 exchange removes gain from federal AGI entirely, it automatically passes through as deferred at the Michigan level.

This conformity holds true for both full deferrals (where the replacement property is of equal or greater value) and partial deferrals (where boot is received). Any boot recognized on the federal return flows through to Michigan taxable income and is subject to the state's flat 4.25% individual income tax rate. Corporate entities filing on MI-1120 follow the same logic—gain excluded federally stays excluded at the state level.

**The risk surfaces when conformity is assumed but not verified.** Michigan has decoupled from certain federal provisions in past legislative sessions, and agents should confirm annually that no new decoupling language has been enacted in the Michigan Income Tax Act. As of July 2026, full conformity with IRC 1031 remains intact.

## Transfer Tax Obligations That Survive a 1031 Exchange

One of the most misunderstood points among Michigan agents is the relationship between 1031 exchanges and real estate transfer tax. The Michigan Real Estate Transfer Tax Act, MCL 207.521 et seq., imposes a state transfer tax of $3.75 per $500 of value and a county transfer tax of $0.55 per $500. These taxes apply at the time of conveyance regardless of whether the sale is part of a deferred exchange.

| Tax Type | Rate | Exemption for 1031? | Filing Form |
|----------|------|---------------------|-------------|
| State Transfer Tax | $3.75 per $500 of value | No | Form 2766 |
| County Transfer Tax | $0.55 per $500 of value | No | Form 2766 |
| Michigan Income Tax on Gain | 4.25% flat | Yes, if exchange qualifies | MI-1040 / MI-1120 |
| Federal Capital Gains Tax | 15-20% + 3.8% NIIT | Yes, if exchange qualifies | Form 8824 |

The seller must still complete the Michigan Real Estate Transfer Tax Valuation Affidavit (Form 2766) at closing. Agents who tell clients that a 1031 exchange exempts them from transfer tax are giving incorrect advice—and exposing themselves to liability. The exemptions listed in MCL 207.526 cover specific situations (transfers between spouses, transfers to trusts, transfers by government entities), but a like-kind exchange is not among them.

## Withholding Rules for Out-of-State Sellers

Michigan does not impose a blanket withholding requirement on real estate sales by nonresidents in the same way states like California or Maryland do. However, agents handling [transactions that cross state lines](/blog/where-deals-break-down) should understand that a nonresident seller completing a 1031 exchange in Michigan may still owe Michigan income tax if the exchange ultimately fails or boot is received.

The Michigan Department of Treasury does not require closing agents to withhold a percentage of gross proceeds for nonresident sellers. Instead, nonresident sellers are expected to file MI-1040 and report any Michigan-sourced income—including recognized gain on a failed or partial exchange—on Schedule 1, Line 9. If your client resides in Ohio but sells Michigan rental property and the 1031 identification period lapses without a valid replacement property, the entire gain becomes Michigan-sourced taxable income.

**Agents representing out-of-state investors must ensure the client understands this filing obligation before closing.** Verbal reassurance that "your accountant will handle it" does not insulate you from a complaint if the client later claims they were not informed of the potential Michigan tax liability.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make With 1031 Exchanges

The errors below are not hypothetical. They surface in LARA complaint files, E&O claims, and post-closing disputes with alarming regularity.

**First, agents provide specific tax advice.** Telling a client "you won't owe Michigan tax if we structure this as a 1031" crosses the line from factual disclosure into tax advisory territory. Michigan's Occupational Code, MCL 339.2512, defines the scope of a real estate licensee's authority—and interpreting tax law is not within it. The correct practice is to refer clients to a CPA or tax attorney and document that referral in writing.

Second, agents fail to coordinate [timeline management across multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) when one leg of the exchange involves a tight closing window. The 45-day identification period and 180-day exchange period are federal deadlines codified in IRC 1031(a)(3), but missing them triggers both federal and Michigan tax consequences simultaneously. An agent who allows a replacement property closing to drift past day 180 without flagging the issue has contributed to a tax event that could cost the client tens of thousands of dollars.

Third, agents confuse the transfer tax affidavit with income tax deferral. Filing Form 2766 and paying transfer tax at closing does not affect whether the income tax deferral is valid. These are independent obligations under separate Michigan statutes.

Fourth, agents assume all property types qualify. Under IRC 1031 as amended by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, only real property qualifies for like-kind exchange treatment. Personal property, including furniture packages in short-term rental units, does not qualify—and Michigan's conformity means the same exclusion applies at the state level.

Fifth, agents neglect to confirm that the qualified intermediary (QI) is properly structured. Michigan does not license or regulate QIs at the state level, which means there is no state safety net if the QI mishandles funds. Agents who recommend a specific QI without verifying their bonding, insurance, and segregated account practices carry referral risk.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers operating under Michigan's Occupational Code have supervisory responsibilities outlined in MCL 339.2512a. When agents participate in 1031 exchange transactions, brokers should enforce specific compliance checkpoints.

| Broker Audit Item | Why It Matters | Documentation Required |
|-------------------|----------------|----------------------|
| Written tax referral in file | Proves agent did not provide unauthorized tax advice | Email or signed referral letter to CPA/attorney |
| Form 2766 completed correctly | Transfer tax errors create title cloud and LARA complaints | Copy of recorded affidavit |
| Timeline tracking for 45/180-day deadlines | Missed deadlines equal failed exchange and potential E&O claim | Calendar entries with client acknowledgment |
| QI identity and contact documented | Protects brokerage if QI defaults or commingles funds | QI engagement letter in transaction file |
| Scope-of-service disclosure | Clarifies agent is not acting as tax or legal advisor | Signed disclosure or listing/buyer agreement language |

Brokers who fail to audit these items expose the entire office to vicarious liability. LARA's Licensing Division investigates complaints against individual licensees, but the Bureau of Professional Licensing can also open a parallel inquiry against the supervising broker under Administrative Rule 339.22301 if supervisory failures are evident.

The cost of a single [blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) is already steep. When that blown closing also triggers an unintended six-figure tax event for the client, the reputational and financial damage compounds exponentially.

## Penalties and Liability When Compliance Fails

If a Michigan taxpayer fails to report gain from a collapsed 1031 exchange, the Michigan Department of Treasury can assess the 4.25% tax plus a 5% per month late-filing penalty (capped at 25%) and interest at the rate published annually in Revenue Administrative Bulletin. For a $400,000 gain, that translates to $17,000 in base state tax, potentially $4,250 in penalties, and accumulating interest.

Agents do not pay this tax themselves. But agents who provided affirmative guidance—"don't worry, it's tax-free"—may face civil suits for negligent misrepresentation, LARA complaints resulting in license suspension or revocation, and E&O claims that increase future premiums across the brokerage. Michigan courts have held real estate licensees to a [standard of care that includes timely, accurate disclosures](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) about material transaction risks.

> MCL 339.2512(1)(a) provides that a licensee shall not make "any material misrepresentation or untruthful advertising" concerning a real estate transaction. Courts have interpreted this to include affirmative statements about tax consequences that induce a party to proceed with a sale.

The safest posture is factual neutrality: inform the client that 1031 exchanges exist, that Michigan generally conforms to federal treatment, and that they must engage qualified tax counsel for specific guidance. Document everything.

## Filing Requirements Your Clients Must Meet

Even when a 1031 exchange is fully deferred, Michigan taxpayers must file. The gain does not appear on MI-1040 because it never entered federal AGI, but the IRS requires Form 8824 (Like-Kind Exchanges) to be attached to the federal return. Michigan accepts the federal return as the starting point, so no separate Michigan form beyond MI-1040 is required for a fully deferred exchange.

For partial exchanges where boot is recognized, the boot amount flows through the federal return into Michigan taxable income automatically. No special Michigan schedule exists for like-kind exchange reporting. The simplicity of this system is both its strength and its trap—agents and clients often assume "no special form" means "no obligation," when in reality the obligation is simply embedded in the standard filing.

Clients who are nonresidents and have no other Michigan-sourced income may still need to file MI-1040 with a Schedule NR (Nonresident Schedule) if any portion of the exchange results in recognized gain. The filing threshold applies; as of 2026, nonresidents with Michigan-sourced income exceeding the personal exemption amount must file.
