## Virginia Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026: The New Reality

The phrase "Virginia commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026" is no longer a hypothetical topic at continuing education seminars. It describes the daily operating environment for every licensee in the Commonwealth. Nearly two years after the settlement's practice changes took effect in August 2024, Virginia agents are still making avoidable mistakes that put their licenses—and their clients' transactions—at risk.

This post breaks down the specific statutes, forms, compliance obligations, and enforcement risks that Virginia agents and brokers face right now. If you have not audited your practices since the initial rule changes, you are already behind.

## The Legal Framework Governing Commission in Virginia

Virginia's real estate licensing authority is the Virginia Real Estate Board (VREB), which operates under the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR). The Board enforces the Virginia Real Estate Transaction Recovery Act (Title 54.1, Chapter 21 of the Code of Virginia) and its administrative regulations at 18 VAC 135-20.

**The specific regulation most relevant to post-settlement commission practices is 18 VAC 135-20-280, which governs disclosure obligations and prohibits agents from receiving compensation that has not been properly disclosed to all parties.** Virginia law already required written brokerage agreements before the NAR settlement, but the settlement layered additional specificity requirements on top of existing state mandates.

The NAR settlement, finalized in 2024, prohibited MLS-based offers of buyer-agent compensation and required written buyer representation agreements specifying the exact compensation a buyer's agent would receive. Virginia's existing statutory framework under Va. Code Section 54.1-2138 already mandated that brokerage relationships be disclosed in writing—making the Commonwealth slightly ahead of some states—but the form and substance of those disclosures have changed materially.

## Required Forms and When They Must Be Signed

Virginia Association of Realtors (VAR) updated its standard forms in late 2024 and again in early 2025 to comply with settlement terms. The forms agents must know include the following:

| Form Number | Form Name | When Required | Key Change Post-Settlement |
|---|---|---|---|
| VAR Form 460-A | Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement | Before first showing | Must state specific dollar amount or percentage of compensation |
| VAR Form 460-B | Non-Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement | Before first showing | Same compensation specificity requirement |
| VAR Form 600 | Residential Purchase Agreement | At offer submission | Cannot include blanket cooperating compensation fields |
| VAR Form 710 | Agency Disclosure | First substantive contact | Must clarify that compensation is negotiable and not set by law |

**Form 460-A is the document that generates the most compliance risk because it requires a definite compensation figure—not a range, not "to be determined," and not language referencing whatever the listing broker offers.** The settlement explicitly bars buyer-agent compensation from being open-ended or contingent on MLS data that no longer exists.

Agents working with buyers who tour properties from multiple listings must ensure the agreement covers all showings or execute property-specific addenda. The Virginia Real Estate Board has indicated through informal guidance letters that a single signed 460-A covering a defined period satisfies the pre-showing requirement, provided the compensation terms remain consistent.

For more detail on how Virginia handles the broader agency disclosure framework, see our breakdown of [Virginia agency disclosure requirements](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026).

## How Compensation Actually Flows Now

Before the settlement, the compensation path was simple: listing broker offered a split in the MLS, buyer's agent accepted it by showing the property, and the listing broker paid from proceeds at closing. That pipeline is gone.

Now compensation to a buyer's agent in Virginia can come from three sources: the buyer directly, the seller through a negotiated concession written into the purchase contract, or the listing broker through a separate off-MLS agreement. Each path requires distinct documentation. The purchase agreement (VAR Form 600) must reflect the source clearly, and the settlement statement must match.

| Compensation Source | Required Documentation | Risk if Undocumented |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer pays directly | Form 460-A with specific amount; reflected on buyer's closing disclosure | VREB disciplinary action; potential RESPA concerns |
| Seller concession | Written into Form 600 offer; seller acceptance required | Deal could unravel if seller rejects; cannot be assumed |
| Listing broker agreement | Separate written agreement between cooperating brokers | Violation of 18 VAC 135-20-280 if not disclosed to buyer |

This three-track system means that at any point during negotiations, the compensation arrangement could shift. Agents must update their buyer clients in writing whenever the source or amount of compensation changes. Failing to do so is not merely a best-practice gap—it creates regulatory exposure under Virginia's disclosure statutes.

## What Happens If an Agent Fails to Comply

The consequences for non-compliance in Virginia are layered across regulatory, contractual, and civil liability frameworks.

**Regulatory penalties from VREB can include fines up to $2,500 per violation, mandatory additional education, license suspension, or revocation under Va. Code Section 54.1-2109.** The Board's enforcement process begins with a complaint (often from a consumer or another agent), moves to investigation, and can result in a formal hearing before the Board.

On the contractual side, a buyer who was never presented with a proper written agreement before showings may argue that the agent has no enforceable claim to compensation at all. Virginia courts have historically upheld the requirement that real estate compensation agreements be in writing under the Statute of Frauds (Va. Code Section 11-2). Without a signed 460-A or 460-B, the buyer's agent may have no legal basis to collect a fee—even if the transaction closes.

Civil liability extends further. If a buyer later claims they overpaid because their agent's undisclosed compensation was baked into the purchase price through a seller concession they did not understand, the agent and brokerage face potential damages claims. The NAR settlement itself was born from exactly this type of consumer harm allegation.

## Common Mistakes Virginia Agents Are Making Right Now

After monitoring compliance patterns over the past eighteen months, five errors keep recurring across Virginia brokerages.

First, agents are showing properties before executing a buyer representation agreement. Some treat the first showing as a "casual" introduction and delay paperwork until the buyer gets "serious." Virginia law and the settlement terms do not recognize this distinction. The agreement must be signed before any touring activity begins.

Second, agents are using vague language in the compensation field of Form 460-A. Writing "up to 3%" or "as negotiated" does not satisfy the specificity requirement. The amount must be a defined number—either a flat dollar figure or a specific percentage—that the buyer can evaluate before agreeing.

Third, agents fail to update buyers when the compensation source changes mid-transaction. A buyer might sign the 460-A expecting to pay their agent directly, then the seller agrees to cover the fee as a concession. That shift must be documented in writing and acknowledged by the buyer. Silence here creates a disclosure violation.

Fourth, some listing agents are still communicating compensation offers through back channels that mimic the old MLS cooperative compensation field. Emails to buyer's agents saying "we're offering 2.5% to cooperating brokers" may technically comply with the MLS prohibition, but they create documentation problems if not properly linked to a formal written agreement between brokerages.

Fifth, agents neglect to confirm that the settlement statement at closing accurately reflects the agreed compensation structure. Discrepancies between the buyer agreement, the purchase contract, and the closing disclosure are audit red flags that [can derail closings at the last moment](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Virginia bear supervisory liability under 18 VAC 135-20-300, which requires them to maintain adequate oversight of their agents' practices. The post-settlement environment demands specific audit protocols.

**Every principal broker should verify that a signed buyer representation agreement exists in the transaction file before the first showing is logged.** This is not a closing checklist item—it is a pre-activity requirement. Brokerages that rely on agents to self-report compliance are accepting unnecessary risk.

Brokers should also audit compensation language for specificity. A quarterly file review should flag any buyer agreement that contains conditional, vague, or open-ended compensation terms. The fix is simple: reject the form and require a compliant version before work continues.

Transaction management systems should include a mandatory checkbox or gate that prevents a showing from being scheduled without an uploaded, signed 460-A or 460-B. If your current [transaction coordination workflow](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) does not include this gate, it needs one immediately.

Additionally, brokers must maintain records of any inter-brokerage compensation agreements executed off-MLS. These documents should be filed alongside the listing agreement and purchase contract. VREB auditors will expect to see a clear paper trail connecting the compensation terms in the buyer agreement to the actual disbursement at closing.

The Virginia Real Estate Board provides its [full regulatory text and disciplinary procedures online](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov/Boards/Real-Estate) for brokers who want to review the specific standards against which they will be measured.

## Practical Steps for Compliance in 2026

Virginia agents operating under the post-settlement rules should adopt three non-negotiable habits. Present the buyer representation agreement at the first meeting—not the first showing, the first meeting. Use the exact dollar or percentage field on VAR Form 460-A without qualifiers. Document every compensation discussion in writing, even if it happens verbally first.
