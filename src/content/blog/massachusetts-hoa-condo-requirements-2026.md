## The Statute That Governs Every Condo Resale in Massachusetts

Understanding **Massachusetts HOA & condo disclosure rules 2026** starts with one statute: Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 183A, Section 6(d). This section mandates that a resale certificate be produced by the condominium organization and delivered to the buyer before any unit changes hands. It is the single most important disclosure document in a Massachusetts condominium transaction, and agents who overlook it expose their clients—and themselves—to serious liability.

The law applies to every condominium created under Chapter 183A, which covers the vast majority of condo developments across the Commonwealth. Standalone homeowners associations that are not condominiums under 183A may operate under different governing documents, but the 6(d) certificate requirement captures a huge share of the Massachusetts market. Agents working in Boston, Cambridge, Worcester, and the surrounding suburbs will encounter this requirement on nearly half their transactions.

Unlike some states where HOA disclosures are optional add-ons, Massachusetts treats the resale certificate as a statutory obligation tied to the organization's fiduciary duties. The certificate must be signed by an authorized trustee or managing agent. Failure to produce it does not simply create an inconvenience—it creates legal exposure for the organization and can derail a closing.

## What the 6(d) Certificate Must Contain

The Section 6(d) certificate is not a generic form letter. The statute prescribes specific categories of information that must appear, and management companies are expected to compile this data accurately within 10 business days of receiving a written request.

**Required contents include the unit's monthly common expense assessment, any special assessments that have been voted but not yet collected, and any outstanding amounts owed by the selling unit owner.** The certificate must also state whether the organization has a right of first refusal, identify any pending litigation involving the association, and disclose the amount held in capital reserve funds.

Beyond the statutory minimums, many management companies in Massachusetts now include additional exhibits—insurance certificates, budgets, meeting minutes, and rules and regulations packages. While not all of these are required under 6(d), agents representing buyers should insist on receiving them because they directly affect the buyer's financing and decision-making. Lenders such as Fannie Mae and FHA routinely require condominium questionnaires that overlap with but are distinct from the 6(d) certificate.

| Required 6(d) Content | Purpose for Buyer |
|---|---|
| Monthly common expense amount | Budget planning and loan qualification |
| Special assessments (voted or pending) | Identifies unexpected costs at or after closing |
| Unit owner arrears | Confirms seller's account is current |
| Right of first refusal | May delay or block the transaction |
| Pending litigation | Affects insurability and lender approval |
| Capital reserve fund balance | Indicates financial health of the association |

## Timing, Delivery, and the 10-Day Rule

The organization has 10 business days from receipt of a written request to deliver the completed 6(d) certificate. Agents must understand that this clock does not start from a phone call or a verbal ask—the request must be in writing. Most management companies accept email requests, but some still require a specific form submission through their portal.

**If the association or management company fails to deliver within the 10-day window, the trustee or manager who is responsible for producing the document may be held personally liable for damages resulting from the delay.** This is a rarely enforced provision, but it exists in the statute and gives agents a tool to escalate when a management company drags its feet.

Agents should build the 6(d) request into their transaction timeline immediately after an accepted offer—not after the home inspection, not after the mortgage commitment, and certainly not the week before closing. Experienced transaction coordinators in Massachusetts typically send the request within 48 hours of a signed purchase and sale agreement. If you are still managing these timelines manually, you are inviting missed deadlines. ## Consequences of Non-Compliance

The liability picture for agents who fail to deliver proper condominium disclosures is more severe than many assume. Massachusetts courts have recognized that a buyer's right to receive accurate condominium information prior to closing is fundamental to informed consent. When that right is frustrated, the consequences cascade.

A buyer who closes without receiving a 6(d) certificate—or who receives one containing material inaccuracies—may have grounds to rescind the transaction or pursue damages against the seller, the management company, and potentially the listing agent who failed to procure or transmit the document. Under M.G.L. Chapter 93A (the Massachusetts Consumer Protection Act), a pattern of failing to provide required disclosures could expose a brokerage to treble damages and attorneys' fees.

The Massachusetts Division of Professional Licensure, which oversees real estate licensing through the Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons, can also take disciplinary action against agents who demonstrate a pattern of negligence in disclosure compliance. Sanctions range from fines to license suspension. The [Massachusetts Board of Registration](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/board-of-registration-of-real-estate-brokers-and-salespersons) publishes enforcement actions, and disclosure failures appear regularly in their records.

## Common Mistakes Massachusetts Agents Make With Condo Disclosures

**Mistake one: Requesting the 6(d) certificate too late in the transaction.** Many agents wait until the buyer's mortgage commitment is in hand before sending the request, leaving fewer than 10 business days before the scheduled closing. When the management company takes the full 10 days—as they are entitled to—the agent is left scrambling for an extension or, worse, asking the buyer to close without the document.

Mistake two: Confusing the 6(d) certificate with the lender's condominium questionnaire. These are separate documents. The lender questionnaire (often a Fannie Mae Form 1076 or equivalent) addresses project-wide eligibility questions such as owner-occupancy ratios, delinquency percentages, and commercial space thresholds. The 6(d) certificate is a unit-specific statutory disclosure. Agents who assume one covers the other leave gaps in both compliance and lending.

Mistake three: Failing to review the certificate before passing it to the buyer. An agent who blindly forwards a 6(d) certificate without reading it may miss red flags—an undisclosed special assessment, pending litigation that could affect insurance, or an error in the reported monthly fee. The standard of care in Massachusetts expects agents to perform a basic review of documents they transmit to clients.

Mistake four: Not confirming that the seller's account is current before closing. The 6(d) certificate will state whether the seller owes money to the association, but agents sometimes receive a certificate that is weeks old by closing day. Balances can change. A follow-up confirmation or estoppel letter close to closing ensures no surprises appear on the settlement statement.

Mistake five: Ignoring the right of first refusal provision. Some Massachusetts condominiums retain a right of first refusal that allows the association to match a buyer's offer. If the 6(d) certificate indicates this right exists and the agent does not trigger the waiver process, the closing may be legally ineffective.

| Mistake | Risk Level | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Late 6(d) request | High — closing delay or cancellation | Request within 48 hours of P&S execution |
| Confusing 6(d) with lender questionnaire | Medium — loan denial or compliance gap | Order both separately and track each |
| Not reviewing certificate contents | High — undisclosed liabilities | Read every certificate before forwarding |
| Stale account balance at closing | Medium — post-closing dispute | Request updated estoppel within 5 days of closing |
| Ignoring right of first refusal | High — transaction may be voidable | Trigger waiver notice immediately upon identifying the provision |

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Broker oversight in Massachusetts extends beyond simply reminding agents to request the 6(d) certificate. Under 254 CMR 2.00, the regulations governing real estate brokers, a broker is responsible for supervising agents in their office and ensuring compliance with all applicable laws. Disclosure failures by a salesperson reflect directly on the supervising broker's license.

**Brokers should build a condo disclosure checklist into their transaction management system that flags three triggers: the date the 6(d) was requested, the date it was received, and the date it was delivered to the buyer.** Without these audit points, a brokerage has no way to prove compliance if a complaint is filed. Documentation is the broker's best defense against Board of Registration inquiries. Firms that rely on agents' memories rather than [structured checklist systems](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) are accepting unnecessary risk.

Quarterly file audits should specifically examine condo transactions for the presence of the 6(d) certificate, evidence of timely delivery to the buyer, and confirmation that any right of first refusal was properly addressed. Brokers who discover compliance gaps should document remedial training and update their policies. The Board looks favorably on brokerages that self-correct before a complaint forces their hand.

Beyond the legal minimum, forward-thinking brokerages are also auditing whether agents provide buyers with the condominium's master deed, declaration of trust, rules and regulations, and most recent budget—even though these are not all required by Section 6(d). Buyer's agents who fail to request these supporting documents put their clients at a disadvantage. Many [transaction breakdowns occur](/blog/where-deals-break-down) precisely because critical association information surfaces too late.
