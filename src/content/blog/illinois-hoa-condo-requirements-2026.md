## Why Illinois HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention

If you list or sell properties governed by a homeowners association or condominium board in Illinois, the **Illinois HOA & condo disclosure rules 2026** carry real teeth. Two separate statutes—the Condominium Property Act and the Common Interest Community Association Act—impose overlapping but distinct obligations on sellers, associations, and the agents who facilitate the transaction. Getting it wrong can kill a deal, expose you to liability claims, and trigger regulatory scrutiny from the Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation (IDFPR).

Illinois stands apart from many states because it grants buyers a statutory rescission period tied directly to the delivery of association documents. That means missing a deadline does not just create friction; it hands the buyer a legal exit ramp your seller never anticipated.

## The Two Statutes Every Illinois Agent Must Know

**The Illinois Condominium Property Act (765 ILCS 605) governs traditional condominiums**, while the Common Interest Community Association Act (765 ILCS 160) covers townhome associations, planned developments, and other non-condo HOAs. Agents frequently confuse the two, and that confusion creates gaps in disclosure compliance.

Under 765 ILCS 605/22.1, the seller of a condominium unit must provide the buyer with a copy of the declaration, bylaws, rules, most recent financial statement, and a paid assessment letter confirming no outstanding balances. The paid assessment letter must come directly from the association or its management company. Under 765 ILCS 160/1-35, the seller of a unit in a common interest community must deliver a disclosure packet that includes similar governing documents, the association's current budget, reserve study (if one exists), and any pending special assessments.

The practical difference matters for timing. Section 22.1 does not specify a pre-closing delivery window in the same granular way, but Section 1-35 of the CICA Act explicitly grants a 15-day cancellation window measured from the date the buyer receives the packet. Miss the delivery and the clock never starts—meaning the buyer can cancel even minutes before closing.

## Key Forms and Documents Agents Must Track

Illinois does not mandate a single state-published form for HOA or condo disclosures. Instead, agents rely on the [Illinois REALTORS multi-board forms](https://www.illinoisrealtors.org) and local MLS-specific addenda. The most commonly used forms include:

| Form / Document | Source | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Condominium Rider (Form 7.0) | Illinois REALTORS | Attaches condo-specific terms to purchase contract |
| HOA Rider / Common Interest Community Addendum | Illinois REALTORS / Local boards | Adds CICA Act obligations to purchase contract |
| Paid Assessment Letter (765 ILCS 605/22.1) | Condo association or management co. | Confirms no unpaid assessments at closing |
| Resale Package / Disclosure Packet | Association or management company | Full governing-document bundle required by statute |
| Financial Disclosure Summary | Association | Budget, reserves, pending special assessments |

Ordering the resale package early is non-negotiable. Most management companies need 10 to 14 business days to compile the documents. If your listing goes under contract and you wait until attorney review is complete to order the package, you are already behind the statutory clock under CICA.

**Agents who handle this proactively—ordering documents at or before listing—protect their sellers from cancellation risk and keep the transaction moving.** If you are [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), a system for flagging condo and HOA document orders at the listing stage prevents last-minute chaos.

## Liability When Disclosure Fails

The consequences of non-compliance divide into three categories: buyer rescission, civil liability, and regulatory action.

Buyer rescission is the most immediate risk. Under 765 ILCS 160/1-35, the buyer can cancel and recover all earnest money if the disclosure packet was never delivered or was materially incomplete. Courts have interpreted "materially incomplete" broadly—omitting the reserve study or delivering an outdated budget can trigger the right. Under 765 ILCS 605/22.1, failure to provide the paid assessment letter means the buyer takes the unit free of unpaid assessment liens, shifting financial exposure back to the association and potentially to the seller.

Civil liability hits agents when buyers or sellers claim they relied on an agent's assurance that documents were delivered or were unnecessary. Illinois courts apply the standard of care outlined in the Real Estate License Act of 2000 (225 ILCS 454), which requires licensees to exercise reasonable skill and care. An agent who fails to advise a seller about statutory disclosure obligations—or who tells a buyer they can waive the 15-day cancellation period without informed consent—faces malpractice exposure.

The IDFPR can also investigate and discipline licensees for violations of 225 ILCS 454/15-5(a), which prohibits incompetence and negligence in performing licensed activities. Sanctions range from fines up to $25,000 per violation to license suspension or revocation.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Illinois HOA & Condo Disclosures

**Five recurring errors show up in disciplinary files and transaction disputes across Illinois.** Recognizing them now prevents expensive lessons later.

First, agents assume the attorney review period covers disclosure delivery. Attorney review under the standard multi-board contract is a separate mechanism. It does not pause or replace the 15-day CICA cancellation window. The two timelines run independently, and confusing them leaves sellers exposed.

Second, listing agents fail to distinguish between a condo governed by 765 ILCS 605 and a townhome community governed by 765 ILCS 160. A townhome with shared walls is not automatically a condominium. The type of declaration recorded against the property determines which statute applies, and the wrong rider attached to the contract creates a disclosure gap that neither party may notice until closing week.

Third, agents accept an informal email from a board member as a substitute for the paid assessment letter. The statute requires the letter to come from the association's authorized representative, typically the management company. An email from a neighbor who serves as treasurer—without formal board authorization—may not satisfy the statutory requirement and will not be accepted by most title companies.

Fourth, buyer's agents waive the 15-day review period at their client's request without documenting informed consent. While a buyer can agree to shorten or waive the window, doing so without a signed acknowledgment creates a he-said-she-said scenario if the buyer later discovers undisclosed special assessments. Protect yourself with a written waiver that explicitly references 765 ILCS 160/1-35.

Fifth, agents neglect to verify that the resale package is current. Management companies sometimes recycle packages prepared for a prior listing period. If the association approved a special assessment between the package date and the contract date, the disclosure is stale. Always confirm the package date and cross-reference it against the latest board meeting minutes. When deals fall apart over stale documents, the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) hits everyone at the table.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers carry supervisory liability under 225 ILCS 454/15-15. If an agent in your office repeatedly mishandles condo or HOA disclosures, the IDFPR can hold the broker accountable for inadequate supervision. Audit protocols should address the following areas.

| Audit Item | Frequency | What to Check |
|---|---|---|
| Correct rider attached to contract | Every condo/HOA transaction | Match property type to 765 ILCS 605 or 765 ILCS 160 |
| Resale package order confirmation | Within 48 hours of executed contract | Date-stamped order receipt from management company |
| Paid assessment letter in file | Before closing clearance | Letter on association letterhead, dated within 30 days |
| 15-day delivery log (CICA transactions) | Before buyer contingency expiration | Proof of delivery date to buyer or buyer's agent |
| Waiver documentation (if applicable) | Before closing | Signed buyer acknowledgment referencing statute |

Brokers should also confirm that agents are not inadvertently practicing law by interpreting governing documents for clients. Advising a buyer on what a condo declaration "means" crosses the line into unauthorized practice. The agent's role is to deliver the documents and recommend the buyer consult an attorney during the review period.

Running a [weekly pipeline review](/blog/weekly-pipeline-review-system) that includes a compliance checkpoint for HOA and condo transactions catches problems before they metastasize. Build the audit into your transaction management workflow rather than relying on end-of-quarter file reviews.

## Deadlines That Trip Up Even Experienced Agents

The 15-day cancellation period under CICA starts when the buyer actually receives the packet—not when it was mailed, not when the management company says it shipped. Proof of delivery matters. Use email with read receipts, a signed acknowledgment form, or certified mail with return receipt. Without evidence of the delivery date, the cancellation window arguably never begins, and the buyer retains the right to walk at any point.

For condominiums under 765 ILCS 605/22.1, the paid assessment letter should be dated as close to closing as practical. Title companies routinely reject letters older than 30 days because assessments can accrue monthly. Ordering the letter too early means ordering it twice—an avoidable cost and an avoidable delay.

Special assessments approved after the resale package date but before closing create a gray zone. Best practice: include a contract provision requiring the seller to supplement the disclosure packet if new assessments are approved between the effective date and closing. This is not a statutory mandate, but it protects both parties and reduces post-closing disputes.
