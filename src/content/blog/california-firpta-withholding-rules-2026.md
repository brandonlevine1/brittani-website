## Understanding California FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

The intersection of federal tax law and California state withholding requirements creates a two-layer compliance obligation that trips up even experienced agents. **California firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 require agents, escrow officers, and buyers to coordinate on both a 15% federal withholding and a separate 3 1/3% state withholding—each with its own forms, exemptions, and deadlines.** Getting either layer wrong exposes buyers to direct liability and can collapse a deal days before recording.

FIRPTA—the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act—was enacted in 1980 under 26 U.S.C. Sections 1445 through 1445(e). California layered on its own withholding requirement under Revenue and Taxation Code Section 18662, administered by the Franchise Tax Board (FTB). These are not redundant. They operate independently, and satisfying one does not excuse the other.

This post breaks down the specific forms, withholding percentages, exemption pathways, liability exposure, and the most common agent errors that lead to post-closing disasters.

## Federal FIRPTA: The 15% Withholding Agents Must Enforce

Under Section 1445 of the Internal Revenue Code, any buyer acquiring U.S. real property from a "foreign person" must withhold 15% of the gross sales price and remit it to the IRS within 20 days of closing using Form 8288 and Form 8288-A. The term "foreign person" includes nonresident aliens, foreign corporations, foreign partnerships, foreign trusts, and foreign estates.

The withholding rate dropped from 15% to 10% for properties sold between $300,001 and $1,000,000 when the buyer intends to use the property as a residence—but only in that narrow band. For sales above $1,000,000, the full 15% applies regardless of buyer intent. Properties at or below $300,000 where the buyer will reside in the home are fully exempt from federal withholding.

Agents do not file Form 8288 themselves, but they are the professionals most likely to identify foreign seller status early in the transaction. If you fail to flag the issue and the buyer later faces IRS collection, your errors-and-omissions carrier will hear about it.

## California's Separate 3 1/3% State Withholding Under Section 18662

**California imposes its own withholding obligation entirely independent of FIRPTA.** Under Revenue and Taxation Code Section 18662, the buyer (or more practically, the escrow company acting as the buyer's agent for withholding purposes) must withhold 3 1/3% of the total sales price when the seller is a nonresident of California or a foreign person. This applies to all California real property sales by non-residents unless a specific exemption is claimed.

The FTB's [Real Estate Withholding guidelines](https://www.ftb.ca.gov/pay/withholding/real-estate-withholding.html) spell out the exemption categories. The seller may claim exemption using FTB Form 593, which replaced the older 593-C and 593-E forms after the FTB consolidated them. A seller qualifies for exemption if, among other reasons, the property was their principal residence, the sale results in a loss, or the transaction is tax-deferred under IRC Section 1031.

| Withholding Layer | Rate | Authority | Form Filed | Filing Deadline |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Federal FIRPTA | 15% (or 10% for residence $300K-$1M) | IRS, 26 U.S.C. 1445 | Form 8288 + 8288-A | 20 days after closing |
| California State | 3 1/3% of total sale price | FTB, Rev. & Tax Code 18662 | Form 593 | 20th day of month following close |
| Reduced Federal | Variable (seller applies) | IRS | Form 8288-B (seller files) | Before or at closing |
| Reduced California | Variable (seller applies) | FTB | Form 589 or written request | Before closing |

This dual-layer structure means a foreign seller on a $2,000,000 sale could see $300,000 (federal) plus $66,667 (state) withheld at closing—a combined $366,667 held before any actual tax liability is calculated.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The liability chain is straightforward and unforgiving. At the federal level, if the required withholding is not collected and remitted, the buyer is personally liable for the tax, plus interest and penalties under IRC Section 1461. The IRS does not chase the seller first. It pursues the buyer, who then turns to their agent and escrow officer for contribution.

At the state level, Revenue and Taxation Code Section 18668 makes the buyer liable for any amount not withheld. The FTB can assess a penalty equal to the greater of $500 or 10% of the amount required to be withheld. Your client—the buyer—ends up holding the bag for a five- or six-figure liability because their agent never asked the seller's agent whether the seller was a foreign national.

**Deal cancellations are common when FIRPTA issues surface late.** Buyers who learn at the eleventh hour that $300,000-plus will be withheld from proceeds often see sellers walk away rather than accept a dramatically reduced net. Transactions that could have closed smoothly with early planning fall apart because no one raised the issue during the listing phase.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with FIRPTA Transactions

The first and most frequent error is assuming escrow will handle everything. Escrow officers manage paperwork execution, but they rely on agents to identify foreign seller status. If the listing agent never asks and the seller never volunteers the information, escrow may process the transaction without withholding—leaving the buyer exposed.

The second mistake is confusing residency with citizenship. A Canadian citizen who has lived in California for 20 years and holds a green card is not a "foreign person" under FIRPTA. Conversely, a U.S. citizen who moved abroad five years ago is not a foreign person for FIRPTA purposes but may be a nonresident for California FTB purposes. Agents conflate these categories constantly, and the result is either unnecessary withholding or dangerous under-withholding.

The third error is failing to advise the seller early about reduced withholding options. A foreign seller can file IRS Form 8288-B to request a withholding certificate showing their actual expected tax liability, which is often far lower than 15%. This process takes 90 days or more. If you bring it up two weeks before closing, the seller has no viable path to reduce withholding, and they blame you for the surprise.

The fourth mistake is treating California Form 593 as optional. Some agents believe that if FIRPTA is satisfied, the state withholding is automatically covered. It is not. Form 593 must be completed and filed separately with the FTB, and the withholding must be remitted by the 20th day of the month following the month in which the transaction closed. Missing this deadline triggers penalties against the buyer.

The fifth error involves improperly claiming exemptions. Agents sometimes advise sellers to check the "principal residence" box on Form 593 when the seller actually lived in the property for fewer than two of the last five years, or when the property was primarily a rental. An inaccurate Form 593 does not shield the buyer from liability if the FTB later audits and determines the exemption was invalid.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory responsibility under California Business and Professions Code Section 10159.2, which requires brokers to exercise reasonable supervision over agents' activities. A pattern of FIRPTA noncompliance at a brokerage invites DRE inquiry and civil liability. Here is what managing brokers should build into their compliance reviews.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Foreign seller identification | Listing file contains FIRPTA affidavit or notation of seller status | Every transaction with a non-U.S.-born seller name or entity |
| Form 593 completion | Signed Form 593 in escrow file; correct box checked | Every sale involving out-of-state or foreign seller |
| Withholding certificate timeline | If Form 8288-B filed, confirm filing date vs. expected close | At contract ratification for foreign sellers |
| Agent training records | Annual FIRPTA/withholding training documented | Annually, at minimum |
| Escrow instruction review | Instructions reference both federal and state withholding | Before escrow opens on flagged transactions |

**Brokers who rely on escrow companies to catch FIRPTA issues are delegating a duty they cannot legally outsource.** The California Department of Real Estate expects brokers to maintain transaction oversight. A broker who cannot demonstrate that their agents followed a FIRPTA identification protocol faces potential disciplinary action under 10 CCR 2725, which governs supervision standards.

Regular training sessions—documented in writing—give brokers a defensible record. If a transaction goes sideways, the broker can show that the agent received FIRPTA training, reducing the firm's vicarious liability exposure. Without that documentation, the brokerage absorbs the full weight of any claim.

## Reduced Withholding and Exemption Strategies for Foreign Sellers

Foreign sellers are not without options, but those options require advance planning. The IRS withholding certificate process via [Form 8288-B](https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-8288-b) allows sellers to demonstrate that their actual tax liability on the sale will be less than 15% of the gross proceeds. The IRS then issues a certificate authorizing reduced or zero withholding. Processing typically takes 90 days, though delays are common.

On the California side, sellers can request reduced withholding from the FTB by filing Form 589 (Nonresident Reduced Withholding Request) or by submitting a written request demonstrating that the 3 1/3% withholding exceeds the seller's actual California tax liability on the gain. Installment sales under IRC Section 453 also trigger special withholding calculations—agents must confirm escrow handles the proportional withholding correctly on each installment payment.

## Connecting FIRPTA Compliance to Broader Transaction Management

FIRPTA obligations do not exist in a vacuum. They interact with disclosure duties, agency obligations, and closing timelines in ways that demand coordination. An agent who surfaces a FIRPTA issue at contract ratification gives all parties time to plan. An agent who surfaces it at final walkthrough creates chaos.

The same discipline that prevents [missed deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) in standard transactions applies with even greater force here. A forgotten Form 8288 filing deadline means the IRS assesses penalties against your buyer. A missed FTB Form 593 submission means your buyer gets a collections notice months after they moved in. These are not hypothetical—FTB audits of real estate withholding compliance have increased steadily since 2023.

Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) often reveals that compliance failures cluster around the same structural weakness: agents who treat tax withholding as someone else's job. It is not. California firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 make the buyer the responsible party by statute, but the agent is the professional who controls information flow and timing. That makes you the last line of defense before a deal collapses or a client faces six-figure IRS liability—and the first person they call when something goes wrong.
