## Arizona Building Code Updates 2026: The Regulatory Shift Agents Cannot Ignore

The Arizona building code updates 2026 cycle represents the most significant shift in residential construction standards the state has seen since it adopted the 2018 International Residential Code. For licensed agents and their brokers, these changes affect everything from disclosure obligations to inspection timelines and buyer negotiation leverage. Ignoring them creates real liability exposure—the kind that ends careers and triggers E&O claims.

Arizona's adoption process flows through the Arizona Department of Housing (ADOH) and individual municipalities, which means enforcement varies by jurisdiction. The state legislature, through A.R.S. Title 41, Chapter 37, grants ADOH authority to establish minimum standards, but cities like Phoenix, Tucson, and Scottsdale often adopt more restrictive local amendments. Agents working across multiple jurisdictions face a patchwork of requirements that demand more than surface-level awareness.

## What Actually Changed in the 2026 Code Cycle

**Arizona formally adopted the 2021 International Residential Code (IRC) and the 2021 International Energy Conservation Code (IECC) as its baseline, effective January 1, 2026.** This replaced the prior 2018 IRC baseline that had been in effect since 2020. The lag between ICC publication and state adoption is typical in Arizona, where the legislative and municipal review process adds years to implementation.

The most transaction-relevant changes fall into three categories: energy efficiency mandates, structural requirements for extreme heat resilience, and updated electrical standards for EV-ready infrastructure. For agents, the energy provisions matter most because they directly impact appraisal values, buyer expectations, and seller disclosure obligations.

Under the updated IECC provisions, new construction must meet higher insulation R-values, tighter building envelope requirements, and mandatory solar-ready provisions in Climate Zones 2B and 3B (which cover the vast majority of Arizona's population centers). Existing homes are grandfathered, but any renovation requiring a permit that exceeds 50% of the home's assessed value may trigger compliance with current code under most municipal interpretations.

## How This Affects Disclosure and the SPDS

The Seller's Property Disclosure Statement (SPDS)—specifically the Arizona Association of Realtors form—remains the primary vehicle through which code issues surface in transactions. Section 4 of the SPDS asks sellers about permits, renovations, and known defects. When a seller has completed unpermitted work that does not meet the current building code, that fact must be disclosed.

A.R.S. 33-422 governs seller disclosure obligations and establishes that sellers must provide written disclosure of all known material facts about the property's physical condition. Building code violations are material facts. Period. The statute does not require the seller to go searching for violations, but if they know about them—or should reasonably know based on work they commissioned—the duty attaches.

**Agents have an independent duty under A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(16) to disclose material facts actually known to them.** This means if you walk a listing and notice unpermitted additions, exposed wiring that clearly violates NEC standards, or a converted garage without proper egress, you cannot simply rely on the seller checking "no" on the SPDS. Your knowledge creates your own disclosure obligation, separate from your client's.

| Scenario | Seller Obligation | Agent Obligation | Form/Document |
|----------|------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Known unpermitted addition | Disclose on SPDS Section 4 | Disclose if actually known | SPDS, MLS remarks |
| Failed inspection for code issue | Disclose on SPDS | Advise client to disclose; note in BINSR response | SPDS, BINSR |
| Pre-2026 home with no updates | No affirmative duty to upgrade | No duty to flag grandfathered items | None required |
| Post-2026 renovation without permits | Disclose on SPDS Section 4 | Disclose if actually known; recommend permit verification | SPDS, permit records |

## The BINSR Process and Code-Related Inspection Findings

The Buyer's Inspection Notice and Seller's Response (BINSR)—AAR Form BINSR—is where code issues most often derail transactions. Under the 2026 code updates, home inspectors are increasingly flagging items that were previously acceptable but now fall below current standards. This creates confusion because existing homes are not retroactively required to meet new code, yet buyers use these findings as negotiation leverage.

Agents on both sides need to understand the distinction between a code violation (unpermitted work that never met any applicable code) and a code deficiency (work that met code when performed but does not meet current standards). Only the former is a genuine material defect requiring disclosure. The latter is a preference issue that belongs in negotiations, not in the disclosure framework.

The BINSR timeline remains 10 days from contract acceptance under the standard AAR Residential Resale Purchase Contract (Section 6a), unless otherwise negotiated. Buyers who discover code violations during inspection can request repairs, a credit, or cancellation. Sellers can agree, counter, or refuse—but refusing on a legitimate code violation invites the buyer to cancel and creates potential disclosure problems for future buyers if the seller relists without addressing the issue.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Building Code Issues

**Mistake one: Assuming all code issues are the seller's problem.** Arizona case law, particularly the holding in Hill v. Jones (1986), established that agents can be independently liable for failing to disclose material facts they knew or should have known. If you list a property with an obvious unpermitted addition—no permit on file with the municipality, visible construction deficiencies—and a buyer later discovers it, your E&O carrier will not be pleased.

Mistake two: Confusing municipal code adoption timelines. Phoenix adopted the 2021 IRC with local amendments effective March 2026, while some smaller municipalities like Maricopa and Casa Grande are still operating under the 2018 IRC with transitional provisions. Agents who [assume deadlines are uniform across jurisdictions](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) end up giving incorrect advice about what a property needs to pass inspection. Always verify with the local building department, not the state baseline.

Mistake three: Failing to recommend a permit search during due diligence. The standard of care in Arizona increasingly expects buyer's agents to suggest that clients verify permits for any visible additions or renovations. Municipal permit records are public and often searchable online. An agent who never mentions this step—when obvious additions are visible—exposes themselves to a negligence claim if unpermitted work surfaces post-closing.

Mistake four: Misusing the "as-is" clause to avoid code disclosure. Selling a property "as-is" under the AAR Purchase Contract does not eliminate disclosure obligations. A.R.S. 33-422 applies regardless of the contract's condition language. Agents who advise sellers that an as-is sale means they can skip disclosing known code violations are giving dangerously incorrect legal guidance.

Mistake five: Not updating MLS remarks when code issues are discovered mid-listing. If an inspection reveals a code violation during a failed escrow and the property returns to market, the listing agent must update disclosures. MLS rules from the Arizona Regional MLS (ARMLS) require that material facts known to the listing agent be reflected in the listing. Omitting this information is a violation of ARMLS rules and ADRE regulations simultaneously.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carry supervisory responsibility under A.R.S. 32-2153 and Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE) Commissioner's Rules (A.A.C. R4-28-301 through R4-28-303). When agents under their supervision mishandle code-related disclosures, the broker's license is also at risk. ADRE investigations routinely name both the agent and the supervising broker in complaints.

**Brokers should audit three specific areas related to the 2026 code updates.** First, verify that all SPDS forms returned by sellers are complete in Sections 4 and 5, with no blanked-out or "unknown" responses on items where the seller clearly has knowledge. Second, review BINSR responses on transactions involving properties built before 2010 to ensure code-related inspection findings are being addressed or properly documented as declined. Third, confirm that listing agents are pulling permit histories for properties with visible additions before going to market.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag | Action Required |
|-----------|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| SPDS Section 4 completeness | Every transaction | Blank fields on renovated properties | Return to agent for seller follow-up |
| BINSR code findings documentation | Every transaction with inspection objections | Code violations dismissed without seller acknowledgment | Require written seller acknowledgment |
| Permit history verification | All listings with visible additions | No permit record for obvious structural work | Require agent disclosure in MLS |
| MLS remarks after failed escrow | All re-listed properties | No update after inspection-discovered issues | Require immediate remarks update |
| Agent CE compliance on code topics | Annual | No code-related CE completed | Recommend or require elective CE |

The ADRE requires 24 hours of continuing education every two years for license renewal under A.R.S. 32-2130. While no specific code-related CE is mandated, brokers who [build systematic review processes](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) into their transaction oversight reduce risk substantially. Consider requiring agents to complete at least one elective course on construction or building code awareness per renewal cycle.

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When Agents Get This Wrong

The consequences of mishandling building code disclosures in Arizona range from uncomfortable to career-ending. At the lowest level, a buyer who discovers an undisclosed code violation post-closing can demand rescission under A.R.S. 33-422. Rescission means unwinding the entire transaction—the buyer returns the property, the seller returns the purchase price, and everyone's commission gets clawed back.

Beyond rescission, negligent agents face ADRE disciplinary action. Commissioner's Rule A.A.C. R4-28-1101 lists grounds for discipline that include "failing to disclose material facts" and "conducting business in a manner that endangers the public." Penalties range from a letter of concern to license revocation, with fines up to $1,000 per violation under A.R.S. 32-2153.

Civil liability adds another layer. Arizona courts have awarded damages to buyers who purchased properties with undisclosed code violations, including the cost of bringing the property into compliance, diminution in value, and in egregious cases, punitive damages. The 2019 Maricopa County Superior Court case involving a Chandler listing with an undisclosed unpermitted second story resulted in a six-figure judgment against both the seller and listing agent. E&O insurance provides some protection, but policies typically exclude intentional nondisclosure.

## Practical Steps for Agents Handling Code-Affected Transactions

Start every listing appointment by asking the seller directly whether any work has been done without permits. Document the answer in writing—an email confirming the conversation works. If the answer is yes, discuss options: retroactive permitting, disclosure with pricing adjustment, or pre-listing remediation.

For buyer's agents, always recommend a permit search as part of due diligence. Most Arizona municipalities offer online permit portals. Maricopa County's portal covers unincorporated areas, while [Phoenix](https://www.phoenix.gov/pdd/permits), Scottsdale, and Tempe each maintain separate systems. Make permit verification as routine as ordering a title report—it takes five minutes and can save months of post-closing litigation.

When inspection reports reference current code standards as the basis for flagging issues on older homes, educate your client on the difference between a true violation and a grandfathered condition. This distinction shapes negotiation strategy and prevents unreasonable BINSR demands that kill deals unnecessarily. An agent who [understands where deals commonly break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) can guide clients through code findings without panic.
