## Understanding Idaho Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

If you hold a license in Idaho and list or sell homes built before 1978, you are bound by federal lead paint disclosure rules that carry real financial teeth. The Idaho lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 have not fundamentally changed from prior years at the state level, but updated federal penalty amounts and increased enforcement scrutiny from HUD and the EPA make compliance failures more expensive than ever. Getting this wrong costs agents money, deals, and potentially their license.

Idaho does not layer additional state-specific lead paint statutes on top of the federal framework the way some states do. Instead, agents operate under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulations at 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F. The Idaho Real Estate Commission (IREC) expects agents to comply with these federal mandates as part of their professional obligations under Idaho Code Title 54, Chapter 20.

The practical effect is straightforward: if you represent a seller or landlord of pre-1978 housing, you must ensure that the required disclosure is delivered, signed, and retained. No exceptions for "as-is" sales, estate transactions, or foreclosures handled by individuals rather than banks.

## Which Properties Trigger the Requirement

**Every residential property built before 1978 triggers the federal lead paint disclosure obligation, regardless of renovation history or current condition.** This includes single-family homes, condominiums, townhomes, duplexes, and multi-unit buildings of four or fewer units sold without a property management intermediary.

Certain exemptions exist. Housing designated exclusively for elderly persons, zero-bedroom units such as lofts or studios without a separate sleeping area, and short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or fewer are exempt. Properties sold at foreclosure are also exempt, though this only applies to the foreclosure sale itself—not to the subsequent resale by the buyer who acquired the property at auction.

Idaho's housing stock makes this disclosure particularly relevant. According to U.S. Census data, roughly 28 percent of Idaho's residential units were built before 1978, with higher concentrations in Boise's North End, older neighborhoods in Pocatello and Idaho Falls, and rural communities across the state where housing turnover is slower.

## The Forms Idaho Agents Must Use

Idaho does not mandate a state-created lead paint disclosure form. Instead, agents typically use the EPA's standard "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form, which satisfies the requirements of 40 CFR 745.113. Most Idaho brokerages provide this through their transaction management software or use the form embedded within the Idaho Association of Realtors (IAR) transaction packet.

| Requirement | Form or Document | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Lead paint disclosure | EPA "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" | 40 CFR 745.113 |
| Pamphlet delivery | "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" (EPA pamphlet) | EPA / HUD |
| Buyer acknowledgment | Signed confirmation on the disclosure form | Included in standard EPA form |
| 10-day inspection waiver or election | Documented on the disclosure form or separate addendum | Agent-prepared or brokerage template |

The IAR purchase and sale agreement references the lead paint disclosure requirement but does not replace the standalone disclosure form. Agents must include both the signed disclosure form and confirmation that the EPA pamphlet was delivered. The pamphlet must be the current version—the EPA updated it in 2021, and using an outdated version does not satisfy the regulatory requirement.

If your brokerage uses [transaction coordination tools to track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), ensure the lead paint disclosure is built into your pre-listing checklist as a mandatory document for any property with a build date before 1978.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Non-compliance with lead paint disclosure requirements triggers three distinct categories of consequences: federal penalties, civil liability, and potential state licensing action.

**Federal penalties under 40 CFR 745.118 now reach $19,507 per violation following the 2024 inflation adjustment, and HUD can assess penalties of up to $21,410 per violation.** These are not theoretical numbers. The EPA has pursued enforcement actions against individual agents—not just brokerages or sellers—when evidence shows the agent knew the property was pre-1978 and failed to ensure disclosure.

Civil liability exposes agents to treble damages. Under Section 1018(b)(2) of Title X, a buyer who was not provided the disclosure may sue the seller and the seller's agent for up to three times the amount of actual damages. In Idaho, where home prices in the Boise metro have pushed past $450,000 for many pre-1978 homes, treble damages on even modest remediation costs create six-figure exposure.

At the state level, IREC treats failure to comply with mandatory disclosure laws as a violation of Idaho Code 54-2060, which covers unprofessional conduct. Sanctions range from a letter of reprimand to license suspension or revocation. IREC's enforcement division reviews complaints and can initiate investigations based on buyer grievances or audit findings.

| Consequence | Authority | Maximum Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| EPA civil penalty | 40 CFR 745.118 | $19,507 per violation |
| HUD civil penalty | 24 CFR 35.88 | $21,410 per violation |
| Private lawsuit (treble damages) | Title X, Section 1018(b)(2) | 3x actual damages |
| State license action | Idaho Code 54-2060 | Suspension or revocation |
| Deal cancellation | Contract law / buyer rescission | Return of earnest money + damages |

Buyers can also rescind the contract entirely if they discover the disclosure was never provided. Idaho courts have upheld rescission even after closing in cases where the omission was willful or where the agent had actual knowledge of lead hazards and failed to disclose.

## Common Mistakes Idaho Agents Make

Five specific errors appear repeatedly in IREC complaints and legal disputes involving lead paint disclosure in Idaho transactions.

**First, agents assume that renovated homes are exempt.** A pre-1978 home that received new drywall, fresh paint, or even full interior remodeling still requires the disclosure. The exemption applies only based on construction date, not current condition. Renovation may reduce lead hazards, but it does not eliminate the legal obligation to disclose.

Second, agents deliver the EPA pamphlet but fail to get the buyer's signed acknowledgment of receipt. The form requires three signatures from the buyer: acknowledgment of the disclosure itself, acknowledgment of pamphlet receipt, and election regarding the 10-day inspection period. Missing any one of these renders the form incomplete.

Third, listing agents delegate the disclosure entirely to the seller without reviewing it for completeness. If a seller checks "unknown" for every field and the agent has actual knowledge of lead paint—perhaps from a prior inspection report in MLS history—the agent may face separate liability for failing to disclose material facts under Idaho Code 54-2087.

Fourth, agents in dual-agency transactions forget that both sides of the obligation must be satisfied. The disclosure must be provided to the buyer, and the buyer must have the opportunity to conduct a lead inspection. In a dual-agency scenario, the single agent bears responsibility for ensuring both the seller's disclosure obligation and the buyer's receipt are properly documented.

Fifth, agents fail to retain the signed form for the required three-year period. Agents who switch brokerages, migrate to new transaction management platforms, or simply lose track of old files put themselves at risk if a complaint surfaces two years post-closing. Understanding [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) often starts with inadequate document retention systems.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers designated under IREC bear direct supervisory responsibility for their agents' compliance with federal disclosure requirements. Idaho Code 54-2038 establishes that the designated broker is accountable for the acts of licensees operating under their supervision.

**A broker's audit checklist for lead paint disclosure should verify five items for every pre-1978 transaction.** First, confirm the disclosure form is present in the file with all required signatures—seller, buyer, and agents for each side. Second, verify that the EPA pamphlet delivery is documented. Third, check that the buyer's election regarding the 10-day inspection period is clearly marked (accepted, waived, or completed with results). Fourth, confirm the disclosure was delivered before the buyer became obligated under the purchase agreement—meaning before the offer was accepted, not at closing. Fifth, confirm the property's build year against county assessor records if there is any ambiguity.

Brokers should conduct these audits quarterly at minimum. IREC's [compliance guidelines](https://irec.idaho.gov/) reference the designated broker's duty to maintain adequate supervision systems, and a pattern of missing lead paint disclosures across multiple files suggests a systemic failure that invites regulatory scrutiny.

For brokerages handling high volume in markets like Boise, Meridian, or Nampa where pre-1978 inventory still trades regularly, building automated flags into your transaction workflow eliminates the most common audit failures. This is particularly relevant for teams [scaling from five to fifteen deals per month](/blog/scaling-from-5-to-15-deals), where manual oversight breaks down quickly.

## The 10-Day Inspection Period: Details That Matter

Federal regulations grant buyers a 10-day window to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense. This period is a default—parties may agree in writing to a different timeframe, longer or shorter, but the buyer cannot be denied the opportunity altogether.

In practice, most Idaho buyers waive this inspection. The waiver must be explicit and documented on the disclosure form. An agent who simply never mentions the inspection option has not obtained a valid waiver. Silence is not consent under 40 CFR 745.110(a).

If the buyer elects to conduct an inspection, the seller must permit reasonable access to the property. An inspection that reveals lead hazards does not automatically void the contract, but it gives the buyer grounds to negotiate remediation, request a price reduction, or withdraw within the inspection contingency period if the purchase agreement provides for it.
