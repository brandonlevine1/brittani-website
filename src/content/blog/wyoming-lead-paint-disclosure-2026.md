## Why Wyoming Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026 Still Trip Up Experienced Agents

Federal lead paint disclosure rules have been on the books since 1996, yet enforcement actions and deal blow-ups keep happening in Wyoming. Understanding the Wyoming lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 is not optional—it is a condition of lawfully marketing and selling any pre-1978 residential property in the state. The stakes are higher this year because HUD raised its maximum per-violation civil penalty to $19,507, and private plaintiffs can seek treble damages under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act (Title X of the Housing and Community Development Act of 1992, codified at 42 U.S.C. 4851–4856).

Wyoming does not layer additional state-specific lead paint disclosure statutes on top of the federal mandate. That distinction matters because it means agents rely entirely on 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart F, and EPA's Section 1018 rule for compliance guidance. There is no Wyoming Real Estate Commission form that supersedes the federal disclosure—but there are still procedural details that agents in Cheyenne, Casper, and Jackson routinely mishandle.

## The Legal Foundation: Federal Law as Wyoming's Standard

**The controlling statute is 42 U.S.C. 4852d, implemented through 24 CFR 35.80–35.98 (HUD) and 40 CFR 745.100–745.119 (EPA).** These regulations require sellers and landlords of pre-1978 housing to disclose known lead-based paint hazards, provide the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home," and give buyers a 10-day opportunity to conduct a lead inspection before becoming obligated under the contract.

The Wyoming Real Estate Commission (WREC) enforces its own licensing standards under W.S. 33-28-301 through 33-28-303, which broadly require licensees to act with competence and honesty. A failure to comply with federal disclosure law can therefore trigger a separate WREC disciplinary action for incompetence or violation of law, even though no Wyoming-specific statute mirrors 42 U.S.C. 4852d. The dual exposure—federal civil penalties plus state licensing consequences—makes this an area where sloppy paperwork can end a career.

If you handle transactions in neighboring states, note that some jurisdictions impose additional state-layer requirements. ## Forms and Documents Wyoming Agents Actually Use

Because Wyoming defers to federal law without creating a state-specific form, agents typically use one of two document sets. The first is the EPA's standard disclosure form titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" (commonly called the "EPA Lead Paint Disclosure Form" or referenced by its inclusion in 24 CFR Part 35, Appendix A). The second is any substantially equivalent form embedded in Wyoming Association of REALTORS (WAR) transaction packages.

| Document | Source | Required For | Retention Period |
|----------|--------|--------------|-----------------|
| EPA Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Form | 24 CFR 35, Appendix A | All sales of pre-1978 housing | 3 years from closing |
| EPA Pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" | EPA Section 1018 | Must be delivered to buyer before obligation | No retention rule, but proof of delivery recommended |
| Lead Inspection Contingency Clause | Contract addendum or built into purchase agreement | Buyer must receive 10-day inspection window (can be waived in writing) | 3 years as part of contract file |

**The 10-day inspection period is not automatically embedded in the WAR purchase agreement.** Agents must add the contingency language or use a separate addendum. If the buyer chooses to waive the inspection period, that waiver must be documented in writing—verbal waivers do not satisfy 24 CFR 35.90(a).

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Non-compliance triggers three distinct liability channels. First, HUD and the EPA can assess civil penalties up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted annually for inflation under 28 CFR 85.5). Second, buyers can file a private civil action under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3) seeking treble damages—meaning the court can award three times the actual damages suffered. Third, the Wyoming Real Estate Commission can initiate disciplinary proceedings under W.S. 33-28-303(a)(ii) for violations of law in the course of practice.

A buyer who discovers undisclosed lead hazards after closing does not need to prove the agent personally knew about the paint. The federal rule imposes an affirmative obligation on the seller's agent to ensure the disclosure form is completed and the pamphlet is delivered. Ignorance of the seller's knowledge is not a defense if the agent failed to facilitate the disclosure process itself.

Deal cancellation is also on the table. Under 24 CFR 35.90(a), if the buyer was never afforded the 10-day inspection opportunity, the contract may be voidable at the buyer's election. That means a deal can unravel weeks or months after mutual acceptance if the paperwork was never properly executed.

## Common Mistakes Wyoming Agents Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in WREC complaint files and in post-closing disputes across the state.

**First, agents assume new paint or renovation eliminates the disclosure obligation.** It does not. The trigger is construction date, not current condition. If the home was built before 1978, the disclosure applies regardless of subsequent remodeling, encapsulation, or abatement—unless a certified inspector has confirmed complete abatement and that documentation is provided to the buyer.

Second, listing agents hand the EPA pamphlet to the buyer's agent and consider the job done. The regulation requires that the buyer personally receive the pamphlet. Delivery to the buyer's agent does not satisfy the rule unless the buyer's agent actually transmits it to the client and that transmission is documented.

Third, agents allow the 10-day inspection window to run concurrently with due diligence without clearly noting it in writing. The 10-day period can be modified by mutual written agreement—shortened, lengthened, or waived entirely—but it must be explicitly addressed. Silence in the contract is not a waiver.

Fourth, agents fail to retain signed disclosure forms for the full three-year statutory period. Electronic storage counts, but the file must be accessible and legible. Agents who switch brokerages mid-cycle sometimes lose track of these documents during the transfer.

Fifth, agents incorrectly exempt certain property types. The federal rule excludes housing for the elderly (where no children under six reside), zero-bedroom units, and properties sold at foreclosure. However, a standard single-family home being sold by a bank after foreclosure IS exempt only at the foreclosure sale—not at the subsequent retail resale. Agents misread this exemption frequently.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Broker-level compliance starts with file review. Under W.S. 33-28-302, Wyoming brokers bear supervisory responsibility for the conduct of their affiliated licensees. That means a broker whose agent repeatedly skips lead paint disclosures faces personal regulatory exposure—not just vicarious liability.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|------------|---------------|-----------|
| Signed EPA disclosure form in file | Signatures of seller, buyer, and both agents | Every pre-1978 transaction at closing |
| EPA pamphlet delivery confirmation | Buyer's initials or separate acknowledgment | Every pre-1978 transaction at contract |
| 10-day inspection clause or written waiver | Explicit language in contract or addendum | Every pre-1978 transaction at contract |
| Three-year retention compliance | Files accessible and complete | Annual audit of closed files |
| Agent training documentation | Lead paint module completion logged | Annually or at onboarding |

Brokers should build a checklist specific to pre-1978 properties that fires at listing intake—not at closing. By the time a transaction reaches the closing table, the disclosure window has long passed. If the form was never signed at contract stage, retroactive execution does not cure the violation; it only creates evidence of late compliance.

A broker who discovers a gap mid-transaction should immediately deliver the pamphlet, obtain the signed disclosure, and offer the buyer their full 10-day inspection window—even if it delays closing. The alternative is a voidable contract and potential federal penalty exposure for the entire brokerage.

## Wyoming-Specific Considerations: Rural Properties and Age Uncertainty

Wyoming's housing stock presents a unique challenge. Many rural properties—particularly ranch houses, homestead structures, and small-town homes—lack clear construction records. When the build date is uncertain, the safe practice is to disclose. HUD guidance states that if a property's age cannot be confirmed as 1978 or later, agents should treat it as pre-1978 and complete the disclosure.

County assessor records in Wyoming are the primary resource for confirming construction dates, but assessor data in less populated counties (think Niobrara, Hot Springs, or Washakie) can be incomplete or based on estimates. Agents should document the source of their construction-date determination in the transaction file. A note stating "per Natrona County Assessor records, year built: 1965" provides a defensible paper trail if questions arise later.
