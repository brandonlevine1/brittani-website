## The Regulatory Framework Behind Texas Commission & Licensing Rules 2026

The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) governs over 200,000 active licensees, and 2026 brings continued enforcement of rules that shifted significantly after the NAR settlement took effect in August 2024. Understanding the current Texas commission & licensing rules 2026 requirements is not optional — it is the difference between getting paid and getting sanctioned. Agents who assume the rules stayed the same after their last renewal cycle are the ones most likely to face complaints.

TREC operates under the authority of the Texas Occupations Code, Chapter 1101, and its administrative rules in Title 22, Part 23 of the Texas Administrative Code. These statutes define who can earn a commission, under what conditions, and what documentation must exist before a single dollar changes hands. The commission itself has broad enforcement powers including suspension, revocation, and administrative penalties up to $5,000 per violation per day.

Brokers bear the ultimate responsibility for supervising their agents' compliance. If an agent under your sponsorship violates commission or licensing rules, TREC can — and routinely does — hold the broker jointly liable under Section 1101.803 of the Occupations Code.

## Written Agreements: The Non-Negotiable Starting Point

**Every commission arrangement in Texas must be memorialized in writing before the agent performs substantive brokerage services.** This has been TREC's position for years under Section 1101.652(b)(22), but the post-NAR-settlement environment makes enforcement far more aggressive. The days of relying on MLS cooperation offers as your de facto compensation agreement are gone.

For buyer representation, the standard form is TREC's promulgated Buyer/Tenant Representation Agreement or the Texas Association of Realtors' TAR-2501 (Residential Buyer/Tenant Representation Agreement). This form must specify the amount or method of calculating the agent's compensation. Vague references to "customary commission" or "as offered by listing broker" no longer satisfy the specificity requirement.

On the listing side, the TAR-1101 (Residential Real Estate Listing Agreement — Exclusive Right to Sell) remains the standard. Paragraph 5 addresses the broker's compensation and must now explicitly state whether the seller authorizes any portion of that compensation to be offered to a buyer's broker — and if so, how that offer will be communicated outside of the MLS.

## How Commission Flows Under Texas Law

Texas law is unambiguous: only a licensed broker can receive commission directly from a principal in a transaction. An individual sales agent or associate broker cannot be paid directly by a buyer, seller, or another brokerage. All compensation must flow through the agent's sponsoring broker under Section 1101.651(a)(2).

This matters more than many agents realize. If you are a solo agent operating under a virtual brokerage, your broker still must be the entity that invoices the title company or receives wired funds at closing. Agents who direct title companies to cut a check in their personal name are committing a licensing violation, regardless of intent.

| Compensation Scenario | Compliant? | Relevant Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Seller pays listing broker, who splits with buyer's broker per written agreement | Yes | Occupations Code 1101.652 |
| Buyer pays buyer's broker directly per buyer rep agreement | Yes, if paid through broker entity | TREC Rule 535.2 |
| Agent receives check from title company in personal name | No | Occupations Code 1101.651(a)(2) |
| Agent earns referral fee without active license | No | Occupations Code 1101.351 |
| Broker pays unlicensed assistant a transaction bonus | No | Occupations Code 1101.651(a)(1) |

The penalties for improper commission receipt are severe. TREC can revoke or suspend a license, and the agent may also face civil liability if a party to the transaction sues for damages resulting from unlicensed activity.

## Common Mistakes That Get Texas Agents in Trouble

Mistake one: failing to disclose intermediary status. Texas is an intermediary state, not a dual agency state, and the distinction matters. Under Section 1101.559, a broker acting as an intermediary must obtain written consent from both parties using TREC's [Information About Brokerage Services form (IABS)](https://www.trec.texas.gov/forms/information-about-brokerage-services) before the transaction reaches a substantive stage. Agents who skip this step or deliver it at the wrong time expose their broker to liability.

Mistake two: advertising commission rates as if they are standardized. TREC Rule 535.155 prohibits any representation that commission rates are fixed or standard in the industry. Even casual language on a website like "the standard 3% buyer agent commission" can trigger a TREC complaint. Every reference to compensation must make clear it is negotiable.

**Mistake three: continuing to practice on an expired or inactive license.** Texas licenses expire on a two-year cycle, and the grace period for late renewal is six months with a penalty. An agent who closes a transaction during a lapse — even by a single day — has committed a violation under Section 1101.651(a)(1) and cannot legally receive compensation for that deal.

Mistake four: failing to update your license information within the required timeframe. Under TREC Rule 535.7, agents must notify TREC of any change in contact information, including business address or phone number, within 30 days. This includes changes to your sponsoring broker. Agents who change brokerages without filing the proper transfer paperwork on TREC's online portal risk a gap in their licensing record that invalidates transactions.

Mistake five: accepting compensation from a party who is not your broker. This includes accepting gifts valued over $50 from service providers involved in a transaction, which TREC treats as an undisclosed incentive under its advertising rules. Title company gift cards, lender kickbacks, or builder bonuses paid directly to agents without broker knowledge all fall into this category.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

**Brokers who treat compliance as a set-it-and-forget-it exercise are the ones who end up before TREC's enforcement committee.** Active supervision is not a suggestion in Texas — it is a legal requirement under Section 1101.803, which holds brokers responsible for the conduct of their sponsored agents.

Every broker should maintain a documented system for verifying that buyer representation agreements exist before their agents submit offers. TREC has signaled through recent enforcement actions that they will hold brokers accountable when agents repeatedly fail to secure written agreements. If your brokerage lacks a tracking system for this, you are exposed.

Commission disbursement records must be retained for four years under TREC Rule 535.5. Brokers should audit these records quarterly to confirm that no payments were routed improperly. The audit should verify that every closed transaction has a matching written agreement specifying the compensation amount, and that all funds flowed through the brokerage account before reaching the agent.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Risk if Skipped |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer rep agreements on file for every active transaction | Per transaction | TREC complaint, commission clawback |
| IABS delivery confirmation in transaction file | Per transaction | Intermediary violation, civil liability |
| License status verification for all sponsored agents | Monthly | Vicarious liability for unlicensed activity |
| Advertising review for commission-related language | Quarterly | TREC fine, public cease-and-desist |
| Commission disbursement ledger reconciliation | Quarterly | Trust account violation, license suspension |

Brokers should also audit their advertising policies. Under TREC Rule 535.154, every advertisement by a sponsored agent must include the broker's name. Social media posts, email signatures, and even Zillow profiles count. If an agent's Instagram bio references commission rates without the broker's name, the broker bears enforcement risk.

## Continuing Education and Renewal Compliance

Texas requires 18 hours of continuing education per renewal cycle, including the mandatory four-hour Legal Update I (Course 33501) and four-hour Legal Update II (Course 33502) courses. These courses are updated annually by TREC and cover statutory changes directly relevant to commission and licensing rules. Skipping them is not just a renewal problem — it means you are operating without knowledge of current law.

The remaining 10 elective hours must come from TREC-approved providers. Hours completed more than two years before your renewal date do not count. Agents who stack all their CE into the final month before expiration frequently encounter provider delays or reporting lags that push their renewal past the deadline.

**First-time renewals have an additional requirement: 90 hours of qualifying courses must be completed within the first two years of licensure under the SAE (Sales Agent Apprentice Education) program.** Failure to complete SAE requirements results in automatic license expiration — not suspension, expiration — which requires starting the application process over.

## Enforcement Actions and Real Consequences

TREC publishes its enforcement actions monthly, and the patterns are instructive. In fiscal year 2025, the commission issued over 400 agreed orders, with the most common violations being failure to disclose agency relationships, advertising violations, and practicing on an expired license. Fines ranged from $500 to $5,000 per violation, with some brokers paying upwards of $15,000 for systemic supervisory failures.

Beyond fines, TREC can require additional education hours, mandate a period of supervised practice, or issue a formal reprimand that becomes part of the licensee's permanent public record. For agents involved in commission disputes, TREC does not adjudicate the money itself — but it will discipline agents whose commission practices violate the Occupations Code. Civil courts handle the actual money fights.

> Section 1101.654 of the Texas Occupations Code authorizes TREC to suspend or revoke a license if the license holder "fails to properly account for or remit money that belongs to another person and that is received by the license holder acting as a broker or salesperson."

Deal cancellation is another practical consequence. If a buyer or seller discovers that an agent lacked proper authority (no written agreement, expired license, or undisclosed intermediary status), rescission of the contract becomes a viable legal argument. This puts not just the commission at risk but the entire transaction — a reality explored in detail in our analysis of [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down).
