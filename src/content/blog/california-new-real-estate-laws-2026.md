The legislative session that wrapped in late 2025 produced a wave of statutory changes that took effect on January 1, 2026, with several more provisions phasing in by July 1. If you hold a California real estate license, the phrase **California new real estate laws 2026** should be prompting you to audit your transaction files, update your form packets, and brief your team. The California Department of Real Estate (DRE) and the California Association of Realtors (C.A.R.) have both issued advisories, yet compliance gaps are already surfacing in transactions statewide.

This post breaks down the statutes that matter most to working agents and managing brokers, identifies the specific forms affected, and flags the liability triggers you cannot afford to miss.

## California New Real Estate Laws 2026: The Major Statutory Changes

**Three bills signed into law during 2025 carry the biggest operational impact for residential practitioners.** Assembly Bill 1289 amended Civil Code Sections 1102.6 through 1102.6d to expand seller disclosure obligations around climate-related hazards. Senate Bill 874 revised Business and Professions Code Section 10147.5 to codify written buyer representation agreements before touring activity. And Assembly Bill 602 updated Government Code Section 66016.5 with new fee transparency requirements that affect agents advising buyers on closing costs.

Each of these laws carries distinct compliance timelines. AB 1289's climate disclosure provisions took full effect January 1, 2026. SB 874's buyer agreement mandate allows a grace period for open house contexts until July 1, 2026. AB 602's fee transparency rules apply to any subdivision map filed after March 1, 2026.

The California Bureau of Real Estate issued [Regulatory Alert 2026-03](https://www.dre.ca.gov/) in February confirming enforcement would begin immediately upon effective dates, with no informal warning period.

## Updated Disclosure Forms You Need in Your Packet

C.A.R. revised several standard forms to reflect the 2026 changes. The Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS, C.A.R. Form TDS-Revised 01/26) now includes Section IV-C, requiring sellers to disclose known wildfire rebuilding restrictions, flood zone reclassifications from FEMA's 2025 map updates, and any pending special assessments related to climate adaptation infrastructure.

The Buyer Representation and Broker Compensation Agreement (BRBC, C.A.R. Form BRBC-Revised 01/26) replaces the prior buyer-broker agreement and adds mandatory fields for anticipated compensation sources, maximum amounts, and a conspicuous notice that compensation is negotiable. Agents must present and execute this form before conducting any touring activity under SB 874.

| Form | C.A.R. Number | Key Change | Effective Date |
|------|---------------|------------|----------------|
| Transfer Disclosure Statement | TDS (Rev. 01/26) | Climate hazard section IV-C added | January 1, 2026 |
| Buyer Representation Agreement | BRBC (Rev. 01/26) | Pre-touring execution required | July 1, 2026 |
| Seller Property Questionnaire | SPQ (Rev. 01/26) | Insurance claim history expanded to 10 years | January 1, 2026 |
| Statewide Buyer and Seller Advisory | SBSA (Rev. 04/26) | AB 602 fee transparency language added | April 1, 2026 |
| Agent Visual Inspection Disclosure | AVID (Rev. 01/26) | Climate-visible damage checklist added | January 1, 2026 |

Agents still using prior-year forms expose themselves and their brokers to rescission claims and DRE disciplinary action. The SPQ (Seller Property Questionnaire, Rev. 01/26) now asks for a full ten-year insurance claim history rather than five, which directly impacts buyer insurability assessments.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: What Agents Risk

**Failing to deliver the revised TDS triggers a statutory rescission right under Civil Code Section 1102.3.** The buyer can cancel up to five days after delivery of a late TDS in a standard transaction, or three days if delivered in person. If the TDS is never delivered, the rescission window stays open indefinitely until close of escrow.

For the BRBC requirement under SB 874, the DRE has indicated it will treat violations as a breach of Business and Professions Code Section 10176(i), which covers conduct that constitutes a basis for license discipline. First violations may result in a restricted license or monetary fine up to $10,000 per transaction. Repeat violations could trigger suspension or revocation proceedings.

Beyond regulatory penalties, agents face civil liability. A buyer who was never presented a BRBC yet was charged a commission through the transaction has standing to file a claim for disgorgement of that commission. The brokerage's E&O carrier may deny coverage if the agent operated outside the statutory framework, leaving the agent personally exposed.

Tracking these deadlines across multiple active deals requires systematic processes. Many agents managing high volumes find that [keeping multiple transactions organized](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) is the difference between compliance and a missed disclosure window.

## Common Mistakes Agents Are Already Making

Five months into the 2026 statutory year, DRE complaints and C.A.R. legal hotline data reveal a pattern of recurring errors.

**First, agents are using the old BRBC form or no written agreement at open houses.** SB 874's grace period for open house contexts expires July 1, 2026, but the statute already requires a written agreement before any private showing. Agents who tour a buyer through a property without an executed BRBC are in violation right now.

Second, listing agents are leaving Section IV-C of the revised TDS blank rather than marking "unknown." Civil Code Section 1102.7 requires that every section be completed. A blank field is not the same as "Seller is not aware," and courts have held that incomplete forms constitute non-delivery, reopening the buyer's rescission rights.

Third, agents are failing to update their AVID (Agent Visual Inspection Disclosure) to include the new climate-damage checklist items. The revised form requires agents to note visible evidence of prior fire damage, drainage issues consistent with flood exposure, and soil movement. Skipping these fields creates liability under the agent's independent duty of visual inspection per Civil Code Section 2079.

Fourth, buyer's agents are neglecting to provide the updated SBSA (Statewide Buyer and Seller Advisory, Rev. 04/26) that includes AB 602 fee language. This form is the primary vehicle for advising buyers about potential impact fees on new construction. Without it, agents may face claims of inadequate buyer counseling.

Fifth, agents are not retaining digital copies of e-signed documents separately from their transaction management platform exports. The 2026 amendment to Business and Professions Code Section 10148 requires that if e-signatures were used, the licensee must maintain an independently accessible digital copy, not just a link to a cloud platform that could become inaccessible.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce Immediately

Managing brokers carry supervisory liability under Business and Professions Code Section 10159.2. The 2026 changes intensify the audit requirements because the DRE has signaled it will hold brokers accountable for systemic form failures across their offices.

**Brokers should immediately verify that every active agent has access to and is using the January 2026 and April 2026 form revisions.** A single transaction closed with outdated forms exposes the brokerage to rescission liability and provides the DRE with grounds for a broker audit.

Conversely, a brokerage with no documented training or policy update may face enhanced penalties.

## The Climate Disclosure Expansion Under AB 1289

AB 1289 represents the most substantive addition to California's transfer disclosure framework since the Natural Hazard Disclosure Statement (NHD) was mandated in 1998. The new Section IV-C of the TDS goes beyond what the NHD report covers by requiring sellers to disclose subjective knowledge about climate-related conditions affecting the property.

This includes whether the property has ever been subject to a mandatory evacuation order, whether the seller has been denied homeowner's insurance renewal due to wildfire or flood risk, and whether any climate adaptation assessments have been levied by the local jurisdiction. These questions require seller knowledge rather than third-party reporting, which creates a distinct liability layer.

Listing agents have an independent duty under Civil Code Section 1102.6c to disclose facts known to them, even if the seller fails to disclose. If an agent knows the property was in a mandatory evacuation zone during the 2025 fire season but the seller leaves that field blank, the agent must note the discrepancy and cannot rely on the seller's omission as a defense.

## SB 874 and the Buyer Representation Mandate

California's codification of the written buyer representation requirement mirrors the NAR settlement framework but adds state-specific enforcement teeth. Unlike the NAR guidelines, which rely on MLS compliance mechanisms, SB 874 makes operating without a written buyer agreement a licensable offense.

The statute requires that the BRBC specify the services the agent will provide, the duration of the agreement, and the compensation terms including all potential sources. Agents cannot use vague language like "customary commission" or reference MLS cooperative compensation fields that no longer exist in most California MLS systems.

**One nuance catching agents off guard: the BRBC must be property-specific or include an explicit geographic and property-type scope.** A blanket agreement covering "all properties in California" without further specificity does not satisfy the statute's informed consent requirement. The DRE's FAQ bulletin clarifies that overly broad agreements may be treated as unenforceable, leaving the agent without a valid compensation mechanism.

For agents [who already struggle with deadline management](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), adding another pre-showing requirement creates operational friction. Building the BRBC execution into a standard pre-touring workflow prevents last-minute scrambles that lead to violations.

## Preparing Your Practice for the July 1 Provisions

The remaining SB 874 provisions taking effect July 1 eliminate the open house grace period entirely. After that date, an agent who engages in any discussion of buyer representation, provides property-specific advice, or arranges further showings based on an open house interaction must have an executed BRBC in place before that next step.

This does not mean you need a BRBC to greet someone at an open house. The trigger is the transition from general information to directed buyer services. Agents hosting open houses for their own listings are acting as seller's agents and do not need a BRBC with visitors. But the moment you offer to show a visitor other properties or discuss their purchasing criteria in a representational capacity, the statute applies.
