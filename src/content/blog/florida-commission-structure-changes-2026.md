## Florida Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026: The Ground-Level Reality

The phrase "Florida commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026" is now part of every listing presentation and buyer consultation in the state. Since the practice changes took effect in August 2024, Florida agents have had nearly two years to adjust, yet enforcement from the Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) is tightening as violations accumulate. This post breaks down the specific forms, statutes, and liability exposures that Florida licensees face right now.

The NAR settlement eliminated blanket cooperative compensation offers through MLS systems and required written buyer representation agreements before property tours. Florida Realtors responded with updated contract forms, and the FREC issued interpretive guidance under Chapter 475, Florida Statutes. Agents who treat this as a minor paperwork change are already facing complaints.

## How the Settlement Changed Florida Commission Mechanics

**Before August 2024, a listing broker could place a cooperative compensation offer in the MLS, and a buyer's agent could rely on that offer without a separate written agreement.** That mechanism is gone. The settlement requires that buyer agent compensation be negotiated independently of MLS data fields.

In Florida, this means the compensation discussion now happens in three distinct documents. First, the buyer representation agreement establishes what the buyer owes the agent. Second, the purchase contract may include a request for the seller to cover part or all of that amount. Third, if the seller agrees, a separate addendum or contract term memorializes that concession.

The Florida Realtors/Florida Bar Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (FR/Bar Contract, revised 2025) includes updated language in Section 10 addressing broker compensation sourcing. Agents must ensure the version date on their contract matches the most recent revision, because older versions lack the required disclosure fields.

## Required Forms and Where Agents Go Wrong

Florida Realtors released several updated forms specifically to address the settlement's requirements. The most critical ones are listed below alongside their functions.

| Form Number | Form Name | Purpose | When It Must Be Signed |
|---|---|---|---|
| BRA-19 | Buyer Representation Agreement | Establishes buyer-agent compensation terms | Before first property tour |
| FR/Bar Contract (Rev. 2025) | Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase | Contains compensation concession language in Section 10 | At offer submission |
| BCDA-2 | Buyer Compensation Disclosure Addendum | Discloses source of buyer agent payment when seller concession is involved | Attached to signed contract |
| TC-1 | Transaction Broker Notice | Clarifies limited representation and compensation structure | At first substantive contact |

The BRA-19 requires a specific dollar amount or percentage. FREC has clarified in its 2025 guidance bulletin that "negotiable" or blank fields do not satisfy the written agreement requirement under Florida Statute 475.278. An agent who tours a home without a completed BRA-19 is operating outside the settlement's mandated practices and risks disciplinary action.

Florida Statute 475.25(1)(b) prohibits conduct that constitutes fraud, misrepresentation, concealment, dishonest dealing, or a failure to disclose material information. Commission sourcing qualifies as material information. The [Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR)](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/intentions2.asp?chession=&catid=&typeid=) oversees FREC enforcement and publishes disciplinary actions publicly.

## Common Mistakes Florida Agents Make Under the New Rules

Five errors appear repeatedly in FREC complaints and brokerage audits since the settlement took effect.

**First, agents use outdated contract forms.** The 2023 and early 2024 versions of the FR/Bar Contract lack the compensation disclosure provisions added in the 2025 revision. Using the wrong version creates ambiguity about who pays what, and ambiguity invites rescission claims. Brokers should purge outdated PDFs from shared drives and transaction management systems immediately.

Second, agents show property before obtaining a signed BRA-19. This happens most often at open houses where a prospective buyer requests a private showing the same day. The settlement does not include an "open house exception" for subsequent tours. If you move from the open house to a private showing, the agreement must be signed first.

Third, agents leave the compensation field in the BRA-19 as a range rather than a fixed amount or percentage. FREC's interpretive guidance states that the amount must be "objectively ascertainable." A range like "2-3%" does not meet that standard because the buyer cannot determine their financial obligation at the time of signing.

Fourth, buyer agents fail to attach the BCDA-2 addendum when the seller agrees to fund the buyer agent's commission. Without this form, the buyer's file lacks documentation showing where the money came from, creating audit problems for the brokerage and potential RESPA-adjacent concerns.

Fifth, listing agents verbally communicate commission availability to buyer agents without putting it in writing. Verbal offers of compensation are unenforceable and create he-said-she-said disputes at closing. Any seller-funded buyer agent compensation must be memorialized in the purchase contract or a separate written agreement between the listing broker and cooperating broker.

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of noncompliance fall into three categories: regulatory discipline, civil liability, and transactional fallout.

**FREC can impose administrative fines ranging from $1,000 to $5,000 per violation under Florida Administrative Code Rule 61J2-24.001.** Repeated violations trigger license suspension or revocation proceedings. The DBPR has publicly stated that settlement-related compliance is an enforcement priority through 2026.

Civil liability presents a larger financial risk. A buyer who discovers that commission terms were not properly disclosed may pursue a claim under Florida's Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act (FDUTPA, Chapter 501, Florida Statutes). FDUTPA allows recovery of actual damages, attorney fees, and court costs. A single successful claim can cost an agent tens of thousands of dollars.

Transactional fallout is the most immediate consequence. Title companies and closing attorneys in Florida are increasingly flagging files where the BRA-19 is missing or where the contract's compensation terms conflict with the buyer representation agreement. When a title company refuses to disburse buyer agent compensation because the paperwork is inconsistent, the deal stalls at the closing table. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) in the transaction timeline helps agents identify these documentation gaps before they become emergencies.

| Violation Type | Potential Penalty | Enforcing Body |
|---|---|---|

| Outdated contract form used | Deal rescission, civil damages | Courts, title companies |
| Undisclosed commission source | FDUTPA claim, FREC complaint | Buyer's attorney, FREC |
| Verbal-only compensation offer | Unenforceability at closing | Title company, courts |

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under Florida Statute 475.25(1)(u) for failing to maintain proper oversight of their agents' transactions. The post-settlement environment demands updated audit protocols.

**Every brokerage in Florida should be auditing three elements in every transaction file: the presence of a signed BRA-19 dated before the first showing, the correct version of the FR/Bar Contract, and consistency between the BRA-19 compensation terms and the contract's payment provisions.** Discrepancies between these documents are the single most common audit failure.

Brokers should also implement a policy requiring agents to submit the BRA-19 to the transaction coordinator or file system within 24 hours of signing. Delays in filing create gaps that are difficult to reconstruct during a FREC audit. The burden falls on the broker to prove compliance, not on the regulator to prove noncompliance.

Training is not optional. Florida Statute 475.17 requires brokers to supervise all licensees operating under their license. If an agent uses an outdated form or skips the BRA-19, the broker faces co-liability. Structured onboarding that covers the new commission workflow should be part of every brokerage's operations, and agents joining mid-year need immediate training before they touch a transaction. Quarterly file reviews should specifically check for the BCDA-2 addendum in any transaction where the seller contributed to the buyer agent's commission. If the form is missing, the broker should require a post-closing correction affidavit and document the remediation in the agent's file.

## Seller-Side Obligations and Listing Agreement Updates

The settlement did not eliminate the possibility of sellers paying buyer agent compensation. It eliminated the MLS as the vehicle for offering it. Florida listing agents must now address compensation strategy during the listing consultation rather than defaulting to a standard percentage in the MLS.

The Florida Realtors Exclusive Right of Sale Listing Agreement (ERS-18, revised 2025) includes an updated section where sellers authorize or decline to offer buyer agent compensation. If the seller agrees to offer compensation, the listing agent must document the specific amount and the mechanism for communicating it. That communication cannot occur through MLS data fields.

Sellers who refuse to offer buyer agent compensation need to understand that buyers will either pay their own agent or negotiate a concession into the purchase price. Listing agents have a disclosure obligation under Florida Statute 475.278(3) to inform sellers how the commission structure may affect showing activity and offer volume. Failing to have this conversation opens the listing agent to claims of inadequate representation.

## The 2026 Enforcement Trajectory

FREC's 2025-2026 enforcement plan, outlined in its annual report to the legislature, identifies commission disclosure violations as a top-three priority alongside trust account deficiencies and unauthorized practice. The commission has added two additional investigators to its South Florida division specifically to handle the increased complaint volume related to buyer representation agreements.

Agents should expect random audit requests, particularly in high-volume markets like Miami-Dade, Broward, and Orange County. FREC's audit authority under Florida Statute 475.5015 allows it to demand transaction records within 10 business days of a written request. Agents who cannot produce a signed BRA-19 for every buyer transaction face immediate probable cause proceedings.
