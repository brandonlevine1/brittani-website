## Understanding New York Teams & Supervision Rules 2026

The New York Department of State (DOS) has never been casual about how real estate teams operate, and the enforcement posture heading into 2026 leaves zero room for ambiguity. If you are forming, joining, or managing a team in New York, the **New York teams & supervision rules 2026** framework demands your attention now—not after a complaint hits your license file. The regulatory structure sits primarily within Article 12-A of the Real Property Law (RPL) and Title 19 of the New York Codes, Rules and Regulations (NYCRR), specifically Parts 175 and 177.

New York does not license "teams" as independent entities. Every salesperson and associate broker must operate under a single sponsoring broker, and teams function as marketing arrangements rather than separate legal structures. This distinction matters because it places the full weight of supervisory responsibility on the broker of record, regardless of how the team brands itself or splits commissions internally.

## The Legal Framework: RPL Article 12-A and 19 NYCRR Part 175

**Section 441 of the Real Property Law establishes the broker's non-delegable duty to supervise all licensees operating under their license.** This is not a suggestion or best practice—it is a statutory obligation that survives any internal team agreement. A broker cannot contractually shift supervisory liability to a team leader who holds only a salesperson license.

19 NYCRR Section 175.21 further specifies that the broker must maintain a definite place of business and retain records for at least three years. For teams operating out of satellite offices or co-working spaces, this creates a compliance trap. Every transaction file, every disclosure form, every piece of correspondence must be accessible to the supervising broker within that record-keeping framework.

The DOS also enforces 19 NYCRR Section 175.25, which governs advertising. Team names are permitted, but only when the brokerage name appears with equal or greater prominence. The regulation uses the phrase "as licensed" repeatedly—meaning the public must be able to identify the responsible brokerage from any advertisement, business card, or digital listing.

## Team Advertising and Naming Compliance

Most DOS complaints against teams originate from advertising violations. The rule is blunt: a consumer must never be confused about which brokerage holds responsibility for the transaction. Under [19 NYCRR 175.25](https://www.dos.ny.gov/licensing/re_salesperson/re_salesperson_law.html), every advertisement—including social media profiles, yard signs, and email signatures—must display the brokerage name as licensed with DOS.

A team calling itself "The Smith Group" cannot place that name on a yard sign without the brokerage name appearing in a manner that is at least as prominent. Font size, placement, and visibility all factor into DOS enforcement decisions. The DOS has issued guidance letters confirming that a team name in 48-point font with the brokerage in 12-point font at the bottom does not satisfy the regulation.

**Agents who fail to include proper brokerage identification face individual fines of up to $1,000 and potential license suspension under RPL Section 441-c.** The broker also faces discipline for permitting the violation, which is why proactive advertising audits are non-negotiable.

## Supervision Standards: What "Reasonable and Adequate" Actually Means

RPL Section 441(1) requires brokers to provide "reasonable and adequate supervision" of all sponsored licensees. The statute does not define a specific cadence of file reviews or meeting schedules, which has led to inconsistent practices across the state. However, DOS disciplinary decisions provide interpretive guidance.

In multiple adjudicated cases, DOS has found brokers deficient where they could not produce evidence of regular file reviews, had no written supervisory policies, or relied entirely on team leaders to self-police. The standard effectively requires documented oversight—meaning if you cannot prove you supervised, you did not supervise in the eyes of the regulator.

| Supervision Element | Minimum Standard | Best Practice |
|---|---|---|
| File review frequency | At least quarterly | Monthly with written notes |
| Written supervision plan | Required by DOS guidance | Updated annually, signed by all team members |
| Advertising audit | Upon complaint | Quarterly sweep of all platforms |
| Trust account oversight | Broker-only access | Weekly reconciliation with dual signatures |
| Continuing education tracking | At license renewal | Semi-annual verification |

For agents working within teams, this means your broker has both the right and obligation to review every file you touch. Resistance to oversight is not just unprofessional—it can form the basis of a DOS complaint against you for obstructing supervision.

## Common Mistakes That Trigger DOS Enforcement

Three to five errors appear repeatedly in DOS disciplinary records involving teams. Understanding these patterns can prevent a complaint from ever reaching your license file.

**First, team leaders acting as de facto brokers without a broker's license.** This happens when a team leader independently approves advertising, holds earnest money, or directs newer agents without the sponsoring broker's documented delegation. RPL Section 440 is explicit: only a licensed broker may perform brokerage functions. A salesperson or associate broker performing these acts without proper authority commits an unlicensed activity violation.

Second, failing to disclose the agency relationship using the correct DOS form. New York requires delivery of the [Disclosure Form for Buyer and Seller](https://www.dos.ny.gov/forms/licensing/1735-a.pdf) (DOS-1735-a) at first substantive contact. Teams often fumble this when multiple members interact with a client before anyone formally presents the disclosure. The form must be signed and retained—a verbal explanation does not satisfy the statute.

Third, commingling team marketing expenses with escrow or trust funds. Under 19 NYCRR 175.23, brokers must maintain separate escrow accounts. When teams create informal "team accounts" for shared expenses and inadvertently route client funds through them, the violation is immediate and severe. License revocation is on the table for commingling, even in small dollar amounts.

Fourth, inconsistent commission structures that lack written documentation. While commission splits are generally a private matter between broker and agent, disputes within teams frequently expose the absence of written independent contractor agreements. DOS Form 1736 (the salesperson application) requires disclosure of the sponsoring broker, but the internal team agreement needs to be equally formalized to avoid disputes that escalate to regulatory complaints.

Fifth, allowing unlicensed assistants to perform licensed activities. Team assistants who show properties, negotiate terms, or discuss pricing without a license violate RPL Section 440-a. The fine structure applies to both the unlicensed individual and the broker who permitted the activity. If your [transaction coordination workflow](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) includes unlicensed staff, their role must be strictly administrative.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers who sponsor teams carry a heightened audit obligation compared to those managing only individual agents. The team structure introduces additional failure points that require systematic review.

Start with advertising. Every quarter, a broker should conduct a full sweep of team websites, social media accounts, listing portals, print materials, and signage. Document each review with screenshots and dates. When you find a violation, issue a written correction notice and retain the agent's acknowledgment. This paper trail is your defense if DOS opens an investigation.

**Trust account reconciliation cannot be delegated to team leaders under any circumstances.** The broker must personally verify or directly supervise the reconciliation process monthly. 19 NYCRR 175.23 places this responsibility squarely on the broker's license. A team leader who handles deposits without the broker's documented authorization creates joint liability.

Personnel files for every team member should include current license status verification, signed supervisory agreements, copies of completed continuing education, and any disciplinary correspondence. When agents [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) or make errors, the broker's documented response to those errors becomes part of the regulatory record.

| Audit Category | Documentation Required | Retention Period |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising compliance | Screenshots, correction notices | 3 years minimum |
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly statements, broker sign-off | 3 years per 19 NYCRR 175.23 |
| Agency disclosure delivery | Signed DOS-1735-a forms | 3 years from transaction close |
| License status verification | DOS confirmation printouts | Duration of sponsorship + 3 years |
| Written team agreements | Signed originals or certified copies | Duration of relationship + 3 years |

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, Liability, and Deal Collapse

The penalty structure under New York law is not theoretical. RPL Section 441-c authorizes fines up to $1,000 per violation, license suspension, and license revocation. For brokers, a pattern of supervisory failure can result in permanent revocation—ending their ability to sponsor any licensee in New York.

Civil liability extends beyond regulatory penalties. When a team member commits fraud, misrepresentation, or negligence during a transaction, the supervising broker faces vicarious liability under respondeat superior principles. The injured party can sue both the individual agent and the broker. Errors and omissions insurance may not cover intentional supervisory failures, leaving the broker personally exposed.

Deals can also unwind entirely when compliance failures surface during the transaction. A buyer who discovers they never received DOS-1735-a has grounds to rescind. A seller who learns the listing agent operated without proper brokerage authority may void the listing agreement. These scenarios create [blown closings](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) that cost everyone time, money, and reputation.

> "The broker of record is responsible for all acts of licensed salespersons and associate brokers performed in the regular course of business."  
> — RPL Section 441(1), New York Real Property Law

## Building a Compliant Team Structure in 2026

The path forward requires written policies, regular audits, and technology that keeps supervisory records organized without creating administrative gridlock. Every team should maintain a written operating agreement that specifies who performs what function, how supervision occurs, and what the escalation path looks like when problems arise.

Brokers should require all team members to use standardized [checklists](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) for disclosure delivery, file documentation, and advertising submissions. Standardization reduces the variability that creates compliance gaps. When every agent follows the same process, auditing becomes systematic rather than investigative.
