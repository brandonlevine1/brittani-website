## Understanding Indiana Real Estate License Reciprocity & Portability 2026

The phrase "Indiana real estate license reciprocity & portability 2026" generates confusion for agents licensed in neighboring states who want to close deals across the border. Indiana sits at the crossroads of the Midwest, sharing boundaries with Ohio, Michigan, Illinois, Kentucky, and beyond—making cross-state practice a daily reality for agents in metros like Cincinnati-area, Louisville-area, and Chicago-area markets. Yet the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC), operating under the Indiana Professional Licensing Agency (IPLA), does not offer the kind of automatic reciprocity many agents assume exists.

Indiana's licensing framework is governed by Indiana Code Title 25, Article 34.1, and administered through rules codified in 876 IAC (Indiana Administrative Code). The distinction between "reciprocity" and "portability" matters here more than in most states, because Indiana recognizes neither concept in the way that, say, a multistate compact would. Agents who misunderstand this distinction put transactions—and their own careers—at serious financial risk.

## How Indiana Actually Handles Out-of-State Licensees

**Indiana does not maintain formal reciprocity agreements with any state as of 2026.** What it does offer is a streamlined application process for experienced agents under IC 25-34.1-3-4.5, which allows the IREC to waive pre-license education requirements for applicants who hold an active license in another state. This is not reciprocity; it is an education waiver at the Commission's discretion.

To apply, out-of-state agents must submit Form 47709 (Application for Real Estate Broker or Salesperson License) to the IPLA along with a certified license history from their home state, proof of errors and omissions insurance, and the $60 application fee. The Indiana state-specific exam portion is never waived. Every applicant must pass the Indiana law section administered through PSI Exams regardless of experience or home-state credentials.

The practical effect is that an Ohio agent cannot simply drive across the state line into Lawrenceburg and represent a buyer without first obtaining an Indiana license. The same applies to Illinois agents working the Hammond or Crown Point markets. No cooperative agreement eliminates the testing requirement.

## Portability Versus Reciprocity: The Indiana Framework

Portability, as defined by the Association of Real Estate License Law Officials (ARELLO), refers to the ability of a licensee to conduct limited brokerage activities in another state without obtaining a second license. Indiana's version of portability is narrow and situation-specific. Under 876 IAC 5-1-14, a non-resident licensee may engage in [referral-only activity](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) if they hold a valid license in their home state and do not perform acts that require physical presence in Indiana.

| Feature | Reciprocity (Not Available in IN) | Indiana's Education Waiver | Portability (Limited) |
|---------|----------------------------------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| Full license transfer | Would allow direct transfer | Not a transfer—new license required | No license issued |
| Exam requirement | Would waive all exams | Waives education only, state exam required | Not applicable |
| Broker affiliation | Would honor home-state broker | Must affiliate with IN broker | Home-state broker sufficient |
| Scope of practice | Full practice rights | Full practice after licensure | Referral activity only |
| Governing statute | N/A | IC 25-34.1-3-4.5 | 876 IAC 5-1-14 |

This table clarifies why so many agents get tripped up. The education waiver feels like reciprocity but is not. The referral exception feels like portability but does not allow showing property, writing offers, or negotiating on behalf of clients within Indiana's borders.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of practicing without proper Indiana licensure are not hypothetical. IC 25-34.1-6-6 establishes that unlicensed practice constitutes a Class A misdemeanor, punishable by up to one year in jail and fines up to $5,000. Civil penalties assessed by IREC can reach $1,000 per violation under IC 25-1-11-12.

**Beyond criminal and administrative penalties, the transaction itself becomes legally vulnerable.** A buyer or seller who discovers their agent lacked proper Indiana licensure may seek rescission of the purchase agreement, return of earnest money, and damages for any losses incurred. The agent's E&O carrier in their home state will almost certainly deny coverage for acts performed outside the scope of their license.

Brokerages face exposure too. Under IC 25-34.1-4-10, the managing broker is responsible for supervising all licensees affiliated with their office. If a broker knowingly permits an out-of-state agent to practice without Indiana credentials, the broker's own license is subject to suspension or revocation. Commission-sharing arrangements with unlicensed individuals violate IC 25-34.1-5-7, which prohibits paying compensation to any person not properly licensed in Indiana.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Indiana Portability

Three to five errors surface repeatedly in IREC disciplinary actions and compliance audits. These are not edge cases—they represent patterns that trap experienced agents who should know better.

**First, agents assume that holding a license in a bordering state grants automatic practice rights in Indiana.** This is the single most common violation flagged in IREC's annual enforcement reports. An Illinois agent listing a property in Lake County, Indiana without an Indiana license is performing unlicensed activity, period.

Second, agents confuse referral fees with active representation. You may receive a referral fee from an Indiana-licensed agent for sending a client their way. You may not attend showings, negotiate terms, or advise the client on Indiana-specific contract provisions—even if you frame it as "just helping out." The moment you perform a licensable act under IC 25-34.1-2-4, you need an Indiana license.

Third, agents who do obtain an Indiana license through the education waiver forget that Indiana requires separate continuing education. Indiana mandates 12 hours of CE every renewal cycle under IC 25-34.1-3-10, including specific required topics set by IREC. Completing CE in your home state does not satisfy Indiana's requirement unless the courses are separately approved by IREC.

Fourth, agents fail to affiliate with an Indiana-licensed broker before conducting business. Form 47709 requires identification of your sponsoring Indiana broker. You cannot be a free-floating licensee in Indiana—the license is not active until broker affiliation is confirmed in the IPLA system. Agents who pass the exam but skip this step are still technically unlicensed for practice purposes.

Fifth, agents operating across state lines neglect to disclose their licensure status on Indiana's standard forms. The Indiana Association of Realtors purchase agreement and agency disclosure forms require disclosure of the agent's Indiana license number. Entering an out-of-state license number does not satisfy this requirement.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in Indiana border markets carry a heavier compliance burden than their counterparts in interior markets. The duty of supervision under [876 IAC 5-1-12](https://www.in.gov/pla/professions/real-estate-commission/) extends to verifying that every agent conducting licensed activity holds a current, active Indiana license.

## The 2026 Regulatory Landscape for Multi-State Agents in Indiana

IREC has not signaled any movement toward a multistate licensing compact as of mid-2026. Unlike nursing or physical therapy, real estate licensing remains a state-by-state patchwork. The NAR has periodically advocated for greater portability, but legislative progress remains slow at the state level.

What has changed in 2026 is enforcement intensity. IPLA's budget allocation for real estate compliance investigations increased following a legislative appropriation in HEA 1234 (2025 session), which expanded the agency's capacity to pursue unlicensed practice complaints. Border-market brokerages in Clark County, Floyd County, Lake County, and Dearborn County report increased scrutiny of cooperative transactions involving out-of-state agents.

Agents licensed in states that do offer some form of cooperative agreement—such as the [portability provisions in Washington State](/blog/washington-license-reciprocity-portability-2026)—sometimes assume Indiana operates similarly. It does not. Each state must be evaluated independently, and Indiana's position remains firmly in the "apply, test, affiliate" camp.

## Practical Steps for Agents Seeking Indiana Licensure in 2026

The pathway for an out-of-state agent to obtain an Indiana license is straightforward but not instant. Start by requesting a certified license history from your home state—this must come directly from the issuing commission, not from you. Submit Form 47709 to IPLA with the $60 fee, your license history, proof of E&O coverage that includes Indiana, and identification of your sponsoring Indiana broker.

Once IPLA confirms your education waiver eligibility, schedule the Indiana state exam through PSI. The exam covers Indiana-specific law including the Indiana Real Estate License Act, IREC administrative rules, Indiana landlord-tenant law, and Indiana-specific contract provisions. Study materials should focus on IC 25-34.1 and 876 IAC in their entirety.

After passing the exam, your license activates upon broker affiliation confirmation. From application to active license, expect 4-6 weeks if your paperwork is clean. Incomplete applications—especially those missing the certified license history or lacking broker identification—are returned without processing, adding weeks of delay that can [kill pending deals](/blog/where-deals-break-down).
