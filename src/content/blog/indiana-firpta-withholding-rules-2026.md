## Indiana FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026: The Agent's Compliance Playbook

Handling a transaction involving a non-U.S. seller in Indiana means complying with both federal FIRPTA requirements and Indiana's own withholding statute—and the stakes for getting it wrong land squarely on the agents involved. The Indiana firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 have not loosened; if anything, increased IRS enforcement activity and Indiana Department of Revenue audits make this the year to sharpen your process. This post breaks down what licensed agents and their brokers must know to keep deals intact and avoid personal liability.

FIRPTA—the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act, codified at 26 U.S.C. Sections 1445 through 1445(e)—requires that when a foreign person sells U.S. real property, the buyer must withhold a percentage of the gross sales price and remit it to the IRS. The federal withholding rate sits at 15% of the amount realized for most residential transactions above $1,000,000, with reduced rates for lower-priced residences. Indiana layers its own requirement on top through Indiana Code 6-3-4-8.1, which mandates state-level withholding on gains realized by nonresidents, including foreign nationals.

## How Federal FIRPTA Applies in Indiana Transactions

**The buyer—not the seller—bears primary responsibility for withholding under FIRPTA.** If the buyer fails to withhold and the IRS later determines the seller was a foreign person, the buyer becomes liable for the tax, plus interest from the date of closing. Agents should understand that the closing agent (typically an Indiana title company or real estate attorney) handles the mechanical filing, but the legal obligation rests with the buyer.

The key federal forms are IRS Form 8288 (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Certain Dispositions by Foreign Persons) and IRS Form 8288-A (Statement of Withholding on Dispositions by Foreign Persons). Both must be filed within 20 days of closing. If a seller claims an exemption—say, through a FIRPTA affidavit certifying U.S. person status—the buyer's agent should confirm that affidavit is retained in the transaction file.

A withholding certificate application on IRS Form 8288-B allows the seller to request a reduced withholding amount or exemption before closing. This form must be filed with the IRS at least on the closing date, and many practitioners file well in advance because processing can take 90 days or longer. If the application is pending at closing, the closing agent typically holds the withheld funds in escrow until the IRS issues a determination.

## Indiana's State-Level Withholding Under IC 6-3-4-8.1

Indiana does not simply defer to the federal process. Under [Indiana Code 6-3-4-8.1](https://iga.in.gov/laws/2024/ic/titles/6#6-3-4-8.1), a transferee (buyer) or the buyer's agent must withhold Indiana adjusted gross income tax on the gain recognized by a nonresident seller. The current individual income tax rate in Indiana is 3.05%, applied to the estimated gain rather than the gross sales price.

The withholding is reported and remitted using Indiana Department of Revenue Form WH-18 (Indiana Nonresident Withholding Exemption/Certificate) in conjunction with the appropriate income tax withholding coupon. The seller can file a nonresident return at year-end to claim a refund if the actual tax liability is lower than the withholding amount. Sellers may also file Form WH-18 to claim an exemption if they qualify—such as selling at a loss or exchanging into another Indiana property under IRC Section 1031.

| Requirement | Federal FIRPTA | Indiana IC 6-3-4-8.1 |
|---|---|---|
| Withholding base | Gross sales price (amount realized) | Estimated gain on sale |
| Standard rate | 15% (10% if residence, price $300K–$1M) | 3.05% of gain |
| Filing forms | IRS 8288, 8288-A | IN DOR Form WH-18, withholding coupon |
| Filing deadline | 20 days after closing | By the 20th of the month following closing |
| Exemption mechanism | Seller's non-foreign affidavit; Form 8288-B | Form WH-18 exemption claim |
| Liable party if not withheld | Buyer | Transferee/buyer or closing agent |

Both layers apply simultaneously. A foreign seller in Indianapolis selling a rental property for $800,000 with $200,000 in gain would face 15% federal withholding on $800,000 ($120,000) plus 3.05% state withholding on $200,000 ($6,100), absent any exemption.

## What Happens If an Agent Fails to Comply

**Noncompliance exposes the buyer to dollar-for-dollar liability for the amount that should have been withheld, plus penalties and interest.** The IRS treats this as a strict liability issue—lack of knowledge is not a defense once the transferee should have known the seller was foreign. For agents, the downstream risks include malpractice claims from their own clients, errors and omissions insurance claims, and disciplinary proceedings before the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC) under Indiana Administrative Code Title 876.

The Indiana Real Estate Commission, housed within the Indiana Professional Licensing Agency, can impose sanctions ranging from letters of reprimand to license suspension or revocation under 876 IAC 6-1. An agent who advises a client to skip withholding, or who fails to flag a foreign seller's status during the transaction, may face allegations of incompetent or dishonest dealing under Indiana Code 25-34.1-11-5(a)(2).

Title companies and closing attorneys also carry exposure. If the closing agent handled remittance and failed, they share liability. But here is the agent's trap: if you knew or should have known the seller was foreign and did nothing to ensure compliance, the buyer's attorney will name you in any subsequent claim. Your brokerage's E&O policy will be tested, and many policies contain exclusions for regulatory violations the agent was specifically trained to avoid.

## Common Mistakes Indiana Agents Make With FIRPTA Transactions

The first error is assuming the title company handles everything and there is nothing for the agent to do. Title companies process the forms, but the listing agent often has the earliest indication that a seller is a foreign national—through the listing agreement, communication history, or address discrepancies. Agents who ignore red flags expose their clients and themselves.

The second mistake is confusing residency with citizenship. A green card holder is a U.S. person for FIRPTA purposes and is exempt from withholding. An agent who treats a permanent resident alien as a foreign person creates unnecessary escrow holds and delays. Conversely, a Canadian citizen who winters in Carmel but holds no green card is a foreign person under FIRPTA regardless of how long they have owned the property.

**The third error is failing to account for Indiana's separate state withholding.** Agents sometimes believe that federal FIRPTA compliance covers everything. It does not. If the state withholding under IC 6-3-4-8.1 is missed, the Indiana Department of Revenue can assess penalties against the buyer and the closing agent, and the seller's proceeds may be insufficient to cover the liability after the fact.

The fourth mistake involves timing on IRS Form 8288-B applications. Agents who wait until the week before closing to suggest the seller apply for a withholding certificate effectively guarantee the certificate will not arrive before closing. The 90-day IRS processing window means this form should be filed the moment a listing goes under contract—or earlier if feasible.

The fifth error is accepting a non-foreign affidavit without verifying the taxpayer identification number. Under Treasury Regulation Section 1.1445-2(b)(2), the affidavit must include the transferor's name, TIN, and address, along with a statement that the transferor is not a foreign person. An affidavit missing the TIN does not satisfy the regulation, and the buyer remains obligated to withhold.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers supervising Indiana agents have an affirmative obligation under 876 IAC 6-3-1 to maintain oversight of transaction files. For FIRPTA compliance, that means building a repeatable audit checkpoint into the office's transaction management workflow. If your brokerage does not already flag foreign-seller status at the listing intake stage, you are behind.

## Seller's Options to Reduce or Eliminate Withholding

Foreign sellers are not without recourse. The most common path is filing IRS Form 8288-B to apply for a withholding certificate based on the seller's actual tax liability. If the seller's gain is small relative to the 15% withholding on gross proceeds—common in low-margin flips or properties held for decades with high basis—the IRS may issue a certificate authorizing reduced withholding or no withholding at all.

A 1031 exchange can also affect withholding obligations. Under Treasury Regulation Section 1.1445-5(b)(2), a qualified intermediary in a like-kind exchange may apply for a withholding certificate showing zero tax due if the exchange is fully tax-deferred. Indiana agents working with foreign sellers pursuing 1031 exchanges should involve a tax advisor before the exchange agreement is executed.

Installment sales create additional complexity. When a foreign seller finances part of the purchase price, FIRPTA withholding applies to each installment payment—not just the closing disbursement. The buyer must withhold 15% of each principal payment and remit it via Form 8288 for each installment. Agents who structure creative financing without flagging this obligation leave their clients exposed.

## Coordinating With Title Companies and Tax Counsel

The practical reality in Indiana is that most FIRPTA withholding mechanics are handled at the closing table by the title company or the closing attorney. Marion County, Hamilton County, and the surrounding metro area title operations are generally well-versed in FIRPTA. But agents working with smaller title operations in rural Indiana counties—where foreign-seller transactions are rare—should not assume the closing agent has deep FIRPTA experience.

**The listing agent's job is to identify the issue early, the buyer's agent's job is to protect their client from withholding liability, and both should confirm in writing that the closing agent is handling remittance.** A brief email to the closing agent confirming FIRPTA applicability and requesting confirmation of Form 8288 filing creates a paper trail that protects everyone involved.
