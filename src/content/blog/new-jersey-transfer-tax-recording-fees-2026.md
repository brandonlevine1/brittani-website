## Understanding New Jersey Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Getting the numbers wrong on a New Jersey closing statement is not an abstract risk—it is a tangible one that stalls recordings, exposes agents to E&O claims, and occasionally kills deals outright. The New Jersey transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 carry specific dollar amounts, graduated rate thresholds, and form requirements that differ meaningfully from neighboring states. Agents who treat these figures as "the title company's problem" often discover too late that the responsibility chain extends to anyone who prepared or approved the settlement statement.

New Jersey imposes its Realty Transfer Fee (RTF) under N.J.S.A. 46:15-5 through 46:15-10, administered by the Division of Taxation within the Department of the Treasury. The fee is collected at the county level when the deed is presented for recording. Unlike a flat-rate stamp tax, New Jersey's RTF operates on a graduated schedule that shifts based on the total consideration paid—and it includes a separate "mansion tax" layer for high-value transactions.

## The 2026 RTF Rate Schedule: Graduated Tiers Explained

**The RTF is not a single percentage; it is a tiered schedule that increases at specific consideration thresholds.** Under N.J.A.C. 18:16-2.5, the fee per $500 of consideration (or fractional part thereof) rises as the transaction value climbs. For transactions involving property that is not a new construction sold by the builder, the 2026 schedule remains as follows.

| Consideration Range | Fee per $500 of Consideration | Paid By |
|---|---|---|
| $0 – $150,000 | $2.00 | Seller |
| $150,001 – $200,000 | $3.35 | Seller |
| $200,001 – $350,000 | $3.90 | Seller |
| $350,001 – $550,000 | $4.25 | Seller |
| $550,001 – $850,000 | $4.80 | Seller |
| $850,001 – $1,000,000 | $5.30 | Seller |
| Over $1,000,000 | $5.30 + 1% of full price | Seller + Buyer (1% mansion tax) |

The 1% General Purpose Fee—commonly called the "mansion tax"—is codified under N.J.S.A. 46:15-7.2 and applies to the buyer on any residential transaction where total consideration exceeds one million dollars. This is a separate obligation from the seller's RTF, and agents representing buyers in that price range must disclose it early. Failing to account for the mansion tax in a buyer's estimated closing costs is one of the fastest ways to erode client trust at the table.

## Recording Fees and County Clerk Requirements

Recording fees in New Jersey are governed by N.J.S.A. 22A:4-4.1. The base fee structure charges $30 for the first page of any document submitted for recording and $10 for each additional page. These fees apply to deeds, mortgages, assignments, and any other instrument the county clerk processes. Several counties layer additional surcharges—often labeled "document storage" or "records preservation"—that can add $10-$25 per document.

**Agents should never estimate recording fees without confirming the actual page count of the deed and mortgage documents.** A standard warranty deed runs two to three pages, but deeds with lengthy legal descriptions, easement language, or rider attachments can balloon to six or more. Mortgage documents from national lenders routinely exceed 30 pages, pushing recording costs past $300 on a single instrument.

Beyond page fees, New Jersey requires the [RTF-1 (Affidavit of Consideration)](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/pdf/other_forms/lpt/rtf1.pdf) to accompany every deed. The RTF-1 documents the sale price, identifies exemptions if applicable, and calculates the fee due. Without a properly executed RTF-1, the county clerk will reject the deed. Period.

## Required Forms and Statutory Filing Obligations

New Jersey's closing paperwork stack is thicker than most states. Agents and their transaction coordinators must verify the presence and accuracy of several critical forms before the recording package leaves the closing table. The primary documents include:

The RTF-1 (Affidavit of Consideration for Realty Transfer Fee) declares the consideration and computes the fee. Both the grantor and grantee must sign it. Form RTF-1EE applies when the seller qualifies for a senior citizen, blind, or disabled exemption under N.J.S.A. 46:15-10, reducing the fee by $50 on the first $150,000 of consideration. Form GIT/REP-1 (Seller's Residency Certification) or GIT/REP-3 (Non-Resident Seller's Tax Declaration) addresses the state's Gross Income Tax requirement. If the seller is a non-resident, 2% of the consideration must be withheld and remitted to the Division of Taxation—or the deed will not record.

These forms interact directly with recording acceptance. A missing GIT/REP form does not merely trigger a fine; it halts the entire recording. County clerks in Bergen, Essex, and Middlesex have become particularly strict about rejecting packages with incomplete or unsigned affidavits, which means [deals can break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) at the last possible moment.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With NJ Transfer Taxes and Recording Fees

Five errors appear repeatedly in transaction audits across New Jersey brokerages.

**First, agents miscalculate the RTF by applying a flat rate instead of the graduated schedule.** A sale at $375,000 does not generate the same per-$500 rate across the full amount; it steps through the $150,000, $200,000, and $350,000 tiers sequentially. Software handles this correctly, but agents who hand-calculate estimates on listing presentations often quote the wrong number and then face uncomfortable corrections at closing.

Second, listing agents forget to disclose the RTF-1EE exemption opportunity to qualifying sellers. Seniors over 62 and individuals who are blind or disabled can save hundreds of dollars, yet the exemption only applies if the correct form is filed. Agents who never mention it miss a legitimate service opportunity—and occasionally face complaints.

Third, agents representing buyers above $1,000,000 fail to include the mansion tax in early net sheet estimates. When a buyer discovers an unexpected $12,000-$15,000 charge days before closing, the fallout is predictable. The obligation exists by statute, and no amount of "my agent didn't tell me" changes the dollar amount due.

Fourth, transaction files arrive at the closing table with the wrong GIT/REP form variant. A New Jersey-resident seller needs GIT/REP-1; a non-resident seller needs GIT/REP-3 with the 2% estimated tax payment. Mixing these up—or leaving the form blank—triggers a recording rejection that can delay title transfer by weeks. Tracking [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without a system virtually guarantees this error eventually.

Fifth, agents underestimate recording fees by assuming a fixed per-document cost rather than a per-page cost. A mortgage with 35 pages costs $370 to record ($30 + $340), not the $30 flat amount some agents pencil onto their worksheets.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.1 and the New Jersey Real Estate Commission's oversight framework. When transfer tax or recording fee errors cause financial harm to a client, the Commission can discipline both the agent and the supervising broker. Auditing for compliance is not optional.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| RTF-1 accuracy | Graduated calculation matches HUD/CD line items | Every transaction |
| GIT/REP form selection | Correct variant for seller's residency status | Every transaction |
| Mansion tax disclosure | Buyer acknowledgment documented above $1M | Every applicable deal |
| Recording fee estimates | Page-count-based calculation, not flat guess | Every transaction |
| RTF-1EE eligibility screening | Seller age/disability status flagged at listing | Every listing intake |

Brokers should require agents to submit completed fee worksheets for review at least 72 hours before scheduled closings. This buffer allows time to correct errors before they reach the county clerk's office. Firms that rely on [memory rather than checklists](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) expose themselves to repeated audit failures.

Additionally, brokers must verify that their agents are not inadvertently practicing law by advising clients on exemption eligibility without proper qualification. The line between "informing a client that an exemption exists" and "advising them on whether they qualify" is thin in New Jersey, and the Real Estate Commission has issued guidance cautioning agents to defer eligibility determinations to attorneys or CPAs.

## Liability Consequences for Non-Compliance

**A recording rejection is not just an inconvenience—it can unwind financing, trigger per-diem penalties, and expose agents to malpractice claims.** When a deed fails to record because of an incomplete RTF-1 or missing GIT/REP form, the buyer does not hold legal title. If the buyer's lender discovers the gap, it may freeze the mortgage disbursement or demand additional documentation, delaying possession.

Under N.J.S.A. 46:15-9, failure to pay the correct RTF subjects the grantor to a penalty of 10% of the deficient amount plus interest. If the deficiency results from information an agent supplied on the RTF-1, the seller may pursue the agent for indemnification. E&O carriers in New Jersey have flagged RTF miscalculations as an emerging claim category, particularly on transactions in the $800,000-$1,200,000 range where the mansion tax threshold creates confusion.

The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends beyond the immediate transaction. Reputation damage, referral loss, and potential Commission complaints compound quickly. The New Jersey Real Estate Commission can impose fines up to $10,000 per violation under N.J.S.A. 45:15-17 and may suspend or revoke a license for patterns of negligence.

## Staying Current as Rates and Forms Evolve

New Jersey's Division of Taxation periodically updates RTF thresholds and form versions. The most reliable primary source for current schedules is the [Division of Taxation's Realty Transfer Fee page](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/lpt/rtffaq.shtml), which publishes rate tables, form downloads, and administrative bulletins. Agents should bookmark this page and check it quarterly—not annually.

Form version changes are particularly dangerous because county clerks will reject outdated versions without explanation. The RTF-1 was last revised in format during 2023, and another revision cycle is anticipated. Agents who photocopy old forms from their desk drawer rather than downloading current versions from the Division's website risk silent rejections that cascade into closing delays.
