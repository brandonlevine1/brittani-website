## Understanding Arizona Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026

The Arizona buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 represent the most significant shift in buyer-side practice since the state adopted mandatory agency disclosure decades ago. Following the NAR settlement implementation and the Arizona Department of Real Estate's (ADRE) updated compliance guidance issued in late 2024, every licensee representing buyers must secure a written agreement before substantive services begin. Failing to do so is no longer just a best-practice gap — it is a path to lost commissions, regulatory sanctions, and potential civil liability.

Arizona operates under A.R.S. Title 32, Chapter 20, which governs real estate licensee conduct. ADRE Commissioner's Rule R4-28-1101 through R4-28-1103 outlines the duties owed to clients and customers, and the written buyer agency agreement is the mechanism that triggers client-level fiduciary obligations. Without it, agents cannot claim the buyer was their client, which undercuts any commission dispute or liability defense.

This post breaks down exactly what the forms require, where agents are getting tripped up, and what managing brokers must enforce at the office level.

## Which Forms Satisfy Arizona's Written Agreement Mandate

The [Arizona Association of REALTORS](https://www.aaronline.com/manage-risk/legal-tools/forms/) (AAR) publishes the two primary forms used statewide. The Buyer Broker Exclusive Employment Agreement (BBEE) grants one brokerage the exclusive right to represent the buyer for a stated period. The Buyer Broker Non-Exclusive Employment Agreement (BBNEE) permits the buyer to work with multiple brokerages simultaneously.

Both forms require agents to specify compensation terms in a clear dollar amount or percentage, a definite termination date, the scope of services provided, and the geographic or property-type parameters of the engagement. The AAR updated these forms in January 2025 to align with the NAR settlement's prohibition on offers of compensation through the MLS, adding explicit language about how the buyer's agent will be paid.

**Agents who use outdated versions of these forms — pre-2025 revisions — are operating with language that does not reflect current compensation disclosure rules.** The form revision date appears in the lower-left footer of every AAR document. If your footer reads anything earlier than 01/2025, replace it immediately.

| Form Number | Form Name | Use Case | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| BBEE | Buyer Broker Exclusive Employment Agreement | Single-brokerage representation | Exclusive right; buyer cannot hire another agent during term |
| BBNEE | Buyer Broker Non-Exclusive Employment Agreement | Multiple-brokerage or limited engagement | Non-exclusive; buyer retains flexibility |
| LBBA | Limited Buyer Broker Agreement | Property-specific showing or open house | Covers a single property or single day of touring |

The Limited Buyer Broker Agreement (LBBA) emerged as a practical solution for open house scenarios. It satisfies the written-agreement-before-touring requirement without locking a casual visitor into a multi-month exclusive relationship. ADRE has not mandated a specific form, but the AAR versions carry widely accepted legal language vetted by Arizona real estate attorneys.

## Statutory Authority and Regulatory Enforcement

Arizona's statutory framework does not contain a single sentence that says "you must use a buyer agency agreement." Instead, enforcement flows from overlapping obligations. A.R.S. 32-2153(A) lists grounds for disciplinary action, including failing to disclose agency relationships, failing to exercise reasonable skill and care, and conducting business in a manner that endangers the public interest.

> A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(19): "Acting in a manner that does not conform to the duties set forth in section 32-2156 or rules adopted pursuant to this chapter."

A.R.S. 32-2156 defines the duties an agent owes to a client — loyalty, obedience, disclosure, confidentiality, accounting, and reasonable skill and care. Those duties only attach when a brokerage relationship exists. A brokerage relationship, under ADRE Commissioner's Rules, requires a written employment agreement. The logic chain is clear: no written agreement means no client relationship, which means the agent has no standing to claim fiduciary protections or compensation.

**ADRE has signaled through informal guidance bulletins and CE course updates that it considers failure to obtain a timely buyer agency agreement a potential violation of A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(3), which covers "substantial misrepresentation" of the agent's role.** An agent who acts as a buyer's representative without documentation could be characterized as misrepresenting the nature of the relationship to the buyer, the seller, and cooperating licensees.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of operating without a signed buyer agency agreement stack quickly. Commission disputes top the list. Without a written agreement specifying compensation, agents have no contractual basis to demand payment from the buyer or to enforce any seller-offered concession earmarked for buyer-agent compensation.

Arizona courts have consistently held that real estate commission claims require written authorization under the Statute of Frauds (A.R.S. 44-101(7)). If a deal closes and the buyer refuses to pay, or the listing side disputes the commission allocation, an agent without a signed BBEE or BBNEE has no enforceable claim. Period.

On the regulatory side, ADRE can impose discipline ranging from a letter of concern to license revocation. The more common outcomes for a first offense include a citation with a civil penalty (fines typically range from $1,000 to $5,000 per violation), mandatory additional continuing education hours, or a consent order requiring practice changes. Repeated violations escalate to suspension.

Civil liability adds a third layer. A buyer who later claims they were misled about the agent's role — or who argues they would have negotiated differently had they understood compensation obligations — can file a complaint with ADRE and pursue damages in court. Errors and omissions carriers have been flagging missing buyer agency agreements as a coverage concern, and some policies now explicitly require timely execution as a condition of defense.

## Common Mistakes Arizona Agents Make With Buyer Agreements

**The first and most frequent error is timing — agents present the agreement after they have already begun showing property.** Under the post-settlement standard, the agreement must be signed before touring. Not in the car on the way to the first showing. Not at the kitchen counter of the second home. Before.

The second mistake involves leaving compensation fields blank or entering vague language like "per MLS" or "standard commission." The AAR forms require a specific amount or percentage. Referencing an MLS offer that may or may not exist at any given property creates ambiguity that exposes the agent in a dispute. Each agreement must clearly state what the buyer will owe if no seller concession covers the cost.

Third, agents frequently neglect to match the termination date to a realistic timeline. Setting a 12-month exclusive term for a buyer who needs to close in 90 days invites complaints about overreach. Setting a 30-day term and then continuing to work with the buyer after expiration — without renewing — creates a gap where no agreement is in force.

Fourth, some agents use the BBEE form when the buyer explicitly requests non-exclusive representation. This mismatch can be construed as a failure to honor the buyer's instructions and, in extreme cases, a violation of A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(22) regarding conduct that demonstrates incompetence.

Fifth, agents fail to provide the buyer with a signed copy. Arizona's Commissioner's Rules require that all parties receive copies of executed documents. An unsigned or unreturned agreement is functionally the same as no agreement at all if a dispute reaches ADRE's desk.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers carry direct responsibility for supervisory compliance under A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(21) and ADRE Rule R4-28-502. If an agent under your license operates without buyer agency agreements, the broker faces potential discipline alongside the agent. This is not a theoretical risk — ADRE has issued consent orders against designated brokers for systemic supervision failures.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Signed buyer agreement on file before first showing date | Every transaction | Showing date precedes agreement date |
| Compensation field completed with specific amount | Every transaction | Blank, "TBD," or "per MLS" language |
| Termination date present and not expired | Monthly review | Agreement expired but activity continued |
| Correct form version (01/2025 or later) | Quarterly form audit | Footer showing pre-2025 revision date |
| Copy provided to buyer (confirmed in file) | Every transaction | No buyer-signed copy in transaction folder |

**Brokers should implement a policy requiring the buyer agency agreement to be uploaded to the transaction management system before any showing confirmation is sent.** This creates a hard gate that prevents agents from skipping the step under time pressure. The similar approach [used in Washington State](/blog/washington-buyer-agency-agreement-requirements-2026) has proven effective for brokerages operating in multiple jurisdictions.

If your office uses transaction coordinators, ensure they are trained to flag missing agreements at intake rather than after an accepted offer. The compliance gap between showing and contract is where most violations occur. ## Compensation Disclosure and the NAR Settlement's Arizona Impact

The NAR settlement eliminated offers of buyer-agent compensation through MLS systems nationwide, and Arizona's two major MLSs — ARMLS and TARMLS — removed cooperative compensation fields in mid-2024. This means buyer-agent pay must now be addressed in one of three ways: a seller concession negotiated in the purchase contract, a direct payment from the buyer per the buyer agency agreement, or a combination of both.

AAR updated the Residential Resale Real Estate Purchase Contract (form RRPC) to include Section 8f, which addresses buyer-broker compensation as a potential seller concession. If the seller agrees, compensation flows through escrow at closing. If not, the buyer agency agreement controls, and the buyer is personally responsible for the agreed amount.

This structural change makes the buyer agency agreement far more than a disclosure document — it is now the primary instrument guaranteeing an agent gets paid. Agents who treat it as a formality rather than a financial contract are setting themselves up for unpaid commissions. ## Practical Implementation for Arizona Teams and Solo Agents

Solo agents should build the buyer agency presentation into their initial consultation workflow — not as an afterthought, but as the centerpiece of the first meeting. Explain what the agreement does, how compensation works post-settlement, and what the buyer's options are (exclusive vs. non-exclusive, duration, termination provisions). Treat it like a listing presentation for the buy side.

Teams face additional complexity. If multiple team members may show property to a single buyer, the agreement must name the brokerage (not the individual agent) as the employing broker. ADRE's licensing structure ties the agreement to the brokerage, not the individual licensee, so a team member substitution does not invalidate the contract — but the buyer should be informed in writing of any change in their primary point of contact.
