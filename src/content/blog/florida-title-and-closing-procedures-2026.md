## Understanding Florida Title & Closing Procedures 2026

Florida remains one of the few states where both attorneys and licensed title agents can conduct real estate closings, and that dual-track system creates specific obligations agents must understand. The rules governing **Florida title & closing procedures 2026** have not undergone radical statutory changes from the prior year, but enforcement actions from the Florida Department of Financial Services and the Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) continue to tighten expectations around agent conduct at or near the settlement table. Knowing what you can and cannot do during this process is not optional knowledge — it is license-preservation territory.

The statutory backbone for title and closing work in Florida sits primarily in Chapter 627, Part XI (Title Insurance) and Chapter 475 (Real Estate Brokers and Sales Associates) of the Florida Statutes. Agents who blur the line between facilitating the transaction and performing closing functions risk violating the unauthorized practice of law standards set by the Florida Supreme Court in *The Florida Bar re Advisory Opinion — Nonlawyer Preparation of Residential Leases* and subsequent opinions. Your job is to get the parties to the table with clean contracts and met contingencies — not to prepare deeds, draft affidavits, or interpret title exceptions.

## The Title Commitment: What Agents Must Review

**Every agent should read the title commitment before the buyer's objection deadline expires.** The title commitment is issued by the title insurer (or its agent) and contains three schedules: Schedule A identifies the parties and the property, Schedule B-I lists requirements that must be satisfied before the policy will issue, and Schedule B-II lists exceptions from coverage that will survive into the final policy.

Under the FAR/BAR Contract for Sale and Purchase (Form FAR/BAR-6, revised 2024), the seller must deliver marketable title, and the buyer has a specific number of days (typically five) after receiving the title commitment to object to defects. If the buyer's agent fails to calendar this deadline or neglects to review Schedule B-II exceptions — like an unrecorded easement or an outstanding municipal lien — the buyer loses the right to object and accepts title as-is. This is one of the most frequent points where deals unravel or agents face post-closing complaints.

Agents should also confirm that the legal description in Schedule A matches the legal description in the executed contract. Discrepancies between survey, tax records, and the title commitment create closeable problems that become uncloseable if discovered at the table. Order title work within 48 hours of a fully executed contract whenever possible, and verify that the title agent has received the survey if one exists.

## Who Does What: Buyer vs. Seller Responsibilities at Closing

Florida's closing customs vary by county, and the FAR/BAR contract (Paragraph 9) defaults to "county custom" unless the parties specify otherwise in a rider or addendum. The table below summarizes the standard allocation in most Florida counties versus the South Florida exceptions.

| Responsibility | Most FL Counties (Seller Pays) | Miami-Dade/Broward/Sarasota/Collier (Buyer Pays) |
|---|---|---|
| Owner's Title Insurance Premium | Seller | Buyer |
| Title Search and Exam | Seller | Buyer |
| Closing Agent Selection | Seller (typically) | Buyer (typically) |
| Lender's Title Insurance | Buyer | Buyer |
| Documentary Stamp Tax on Deed | Seller | Seller |
| Recording of Deed | Buyer | Buyer |

Documentary stamp taxes are governed by Florida Statute Section 201.02 and are calculated at $0.70 per $100 of consideration statewide, except in Miami-Dade County where an additional $0.45 per $100 surtax applies to non-homestead transactions. Agents who miscalculate doc stamps during net sheet preparation create liability for themselves and confusion for clients. Always use the correct county rate on your preliminary estimates and disclose that estimates are not guarantees.

## The Role of the Closing Agent and TRID Compliance

Florida closings are conducted by either a board-certified real estate attorney or a licensed title agent operating under Florida Statutes Section 627.7711. The closing agent prepares the Closing Disclosure (CD), coordinates lien payoffs, records documents, and disburses funds. Agents do not perform these functions, but they absolutely must understand the timeline constraints.

The TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure (TRID) rules require that the buyer receive the Closing Disclosure at least three business days before consummation. If the closing agent delivers a revised CD that triggers a new three-day waiting period — due to an APR increase exceeding tolerances, a change in loan product, or the addition of a prepayment penalty — closing must be postponed. Agents who schedule movers, utility transfers, or walkthrough inspections before confirming the CD delivery date are setting up their clients for chaos.

**Agents should request confirmation from the lender and title company that the initial CD was delivered and acknowledged before treating a closing date as firm.** This single habit prevents more blown closings than any other procedural safeguard. ## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Florida Closings

Five specific errors show up repeatedly in FREC disciplinary actions and title company complaint logs. Each one is preventable.

**First, agents prepare net sheets with incorrect documentary stamp or intangible tax calculations.** The intangible tax on new mortgages is $0.002 per $1.00 of the obligation (Florida Statute Section 199.133), and it is paid by the borrower at closing. Agents who omit it from buyer estimates create trust issues and last-minute scrambles. Use the county comptroller's published rates, not a generic calculator built for another state.

Second, agents give legal opinions about title exceptions. When a buyer asks, "Is this easement a problem?" the correct answer is to refer them to a real estate attorney — not to offer reassurance based on your personal experience with similar language. Florida Bar Advisory Opinion 96-4 specifically addresses the line between real estate brokerage services and the practice of law. Crossing it exposes you to FREC discipline and potential Bar complaints.

Third, agents fail to verify wire instructions through a secondary channel before closing. Wire fraud remains the number one closing-related crime in Florida, and the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center reported over $145 million in losses from real estate wire fraud nationally in 2024. If your office does not have a [wire instructions verification process](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process), you are operating without a safety net.

Fourth, agents do not confirm that the seller has paid outstanding HOA/COA assessments or obtained an estoppel letter before the closing date. Under Florida Statute Section 720.30851 (HOA) and Section 718.116 (condominiums), the association can require an estoppel certificate, and the requesting party typically has 10 business days to receive it. Missing this step can delay disbursement or leave the buyer responsible for unpaid assessments.

Fifth, agents treat the closing date as sacred even when contract contingencies remain open. If the inspection period has not expired, the appraisal has not been received, or a loan commitment letter is outstanding, pushing toward closing without written extensions exposes the agent to complaints of misrepresentation or negligence.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under Florida Statute Section 475.25(1)(u) for the actions of their sales associates. That means if an agent in your office is preparing closing documents, offering legal advice about title exceptions, or failing to verify wire instructions, you share in the liability. FREC has consistently held that "lack of knowledge" of an associate's misconduct is not a defense if the broker failed to maintain reasonable supervisory procedures.

| Audit Point | Frequency | What to Check |
|---|---|---|
| Net sheet accuracy | Every transaction | Correct doc stamps, intangible tax, prorations |
| Title commitment review confirmation | Every transaction | Agent documented review and noted deadlines |
| Wire fraud protocol compliance | Quarterly + random | Callback verification logs, client education emails |
| Escrow deposit delivery | Every transaction | Delivered within timeline specified in contract (typically 3 days) |
| Closing Disclosure reconciliation | Every transaction | Agent verified CD matches contract terms before closing |

**Brokers should also audit whether agents are properly handling earnest money deposits under the timelines specified in the FAR/BAR contract.** Florida Statute Section 475.25(1)(d) makes failure to deliver escrow funds within the agreed timeframe a disciplinable offense. The standard FAR/BAR contract requires delivery within three business days after the effective date. Late deposits can void the contract and trigger FREC complaints.

## Florida Title & Closing Procedures 2026: Protecting Your License at the Table

The Florida Department of Financial Services maintains a [searchable database of title agent disciplinary actions](https://www.myfloridacfo.com/division/agents/), and real estate agents appear in adjacent complaints when their conduct overlaps with title and closing functions. The consequences range from formal reprimands and fines (typically $1,000 to $5,000 per violation) to license suspension or revocation under Section 475.25.

Deal cancellation is the more immediate risk. When an agent's error causes a missed title objection deadline, an incorrect CD, or a wiring mistake, the transaction collapses — and the agent's E&O carrier gets a call. Even if the deal eventually closes, the reputational damage and the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) ripple forward into lost referrals and reduced production for months.

Florida title & closing procedures 2026 demand that agents maintain procedural discipline from contract execution through recording. The agents who thrive in this environment are the ones who treat every closing file like an audit file — because eventually, one will be.
