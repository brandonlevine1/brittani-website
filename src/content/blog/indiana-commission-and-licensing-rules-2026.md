## Indiana Commission & Licensing Rules 2026: The Statutory Framework Agents Cannot Ignore

The Indiana commission & licensing rules 2026 represent a tightening regulatory environment that catches underprepared agents off guard every quarter. Between the post-settlement commission disclosure requirements and Indiana-specific licensing statutes under Indiana Code Title 25, Article 34.1, the margin for error has narrowed considerably. Agents who operated on handshake deals or vague commission expectations in prior years now face real financial and legal exposure.

Indiana's real estate licensing authority — the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC), housed within the Indiana Professional Licensing Agency (IPLA) — enforces these rules with increasing rigor. Disciplinary actions published by IREC in 2025 showed a 22% increase in formal complaints related to commission disputes and unlicensed activity. The message is clear: know the rules or risk your license.

## Who Regulates Commission and Licensing in Indiana

The [Indiana Real Estate Commission](https://www.in.gov/pla/professions/indiana-real-estate-commission/) operates under the authority granted by IC 25-34.1 and the administrative rules codified in 876 IAC. IREC has the power to investigate complaints, issue fines up to $10,000 per violation, suspend or revoke licenses, and require restitution. The Commission meets monthly and publishes enforcement actions in its public meeting minutes.

**All commission payments in Indiana must flow through a licensed broker.** IC 25-34.1-10-10 makes it explicitly illegal for a salesperson to receive compensation from anyone other than their managing broker. This means no direct payments from clients, no side arrangements with cooperating agents, and no referral fees paid to anyone without an active Indiana license or proper exemption.

The distinction matters because Indiana does not issue "agent" licenses — it issues salesperson licenses and broker licenses. A salesperson operates under the supervision of a managing broker, and every commission dollar must pass through that broker's trust or operating account first.

## Commission Structures and Written Agreement Requirements

Since the implementation of NAR settlement terms that took effect nationally in August 2024, Indiana agents must secure written agreements before showing property or performing compensable services. Indiana Association of REALTORS (IAR) Form 43 — the Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement — and IAR Form 46 — the Non-Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement — are the standard instruments for buyer-side relationships.

On the listing side, IAR Form 17 (Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement) remains the dominant form. The commission rate, any offered cooperating compensation, and the specific services to be performed must all be detailed in writing. Vague or blank commission fields on these forms create enforcement problems and potential deal cancellations.

| Requirement | Buyer-Side (IAR Form 43/46) | Listing-Side (IAR Form 17) |
|---|---|---|
| Written agreement required before services | Yes | Yes |
| Commission rate must be specific and objective | Yes — cannot be open-ended | Yes |
| Broker signature required | Yes | Yes |
| Cancellation clause mandatory | Yes | Yes |
| MLS offer of compensation allowed | Separate from buyer agreement | Optional — seller decides |

The key shift for 2026 is that buyer agents can no longer rely on MLS-published cooperating compensation as their sole source of payment assurance. If the listing offers no compensation, the buyer agent must look to their written buyer representation agreement for payment — and that agreement must have been executed before any showing took place.

## Licensing Requirements and Renewal Obligations

Indiana operates on a three-year license renewal cycle managed by IPLA. Salespersons and brokers must complete 36 hours of continuing education (CE) during each cycle, broken into 12-hour annual segments. The required curriculum includes core law courses that cover commission disclosure rules, trust account management, and agency relationships.

**Failure to renew on time triggers automatic inactive status.** An agent whose license lapses cannot legally participate in any real estate transaction — including receiving compensation for deals that close during the inactive period. IREC treats this as practicing without a license under IC 25-34.1-10-3, which carries both civil penalties and potential criminal misdemeanor charges.

To reinstate, agents must complete all delinquent CE hours, pay reinstatement fees, and — if more than three years have passed — retake the state licensing examination. The practical cost extends beyond fees: any pending transactions must be reassigned to another licensed agent within the brokerage, creating client confusion and potential lost revenue.

For agents tracking CE deadlines and compliance documentation, this is exactly the kind of recurring administrative burden that [falls through the cracks without a system](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines). A missed renewal date does not pause your pipeline — it just makes every pending deal legally vulnerable.

## Common Mistakes That Trigger Disciplinary Action

Five specific errors appear repeatedly in IREC disciplinary filings and should be on every Indiana agent's radar for 2026.

**First: Accepting compensation from someone other than your managing broker.** This violation of IC 25-34.1-10-10 surfaces most often in referral fee arrangements where an agent receives a check directly from an out-of-state brokerage. Even if the amount is correct and the referral was legitimate, the payment routing makes it illegal. Every dollar must flow through your managing broker.

Second: Performing compensable services before executing a written buyer representation agreement. Post-settlement rules require specificity and timing. Showing a property, providing a market analysis, or submitting an offer without a signed IAR Form 43 or 46 in place exposes the agent to forfeiture of commission and potential IREC discipline.

Third: Failing to disclose who is paying the agent's commission to all parties in the transaction. Indiana's agency disclosure requirements under 876 IAC 1-1-1 through 1-1-14 mandate that all parties understand the source and amount of compensation. The State Form 55919 (Agency Disclosure) must be presented at first substantive contact, and commission terms must be transparent before an offer is written.

Fourth: Letting a license lapse mid-transaction and continuing to work the deal. IREC sees this quarterly. The agent assumes their renewal went through, continues performing services, and only discovers the lapse weeks later. By then, they have potentially committed unlicensed practice.

Fifth: Operating a team without proper broker oversight of commission splits and advertising. Indiana requires that all advertising include the brokerage name, and team names cannot imply a separate corporate entity. Commission split arrangements within teams must be documented through the managing broker — not through informal team leader agreements.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in Indiana bear personal regulatory liability for their agents' compliance failures. Under IC 25-34.1-10-7.5, the managing broker is responsible for supervising all licensed activities conducted by salespersons affiliated with the brokerage. This is not a passive obligation — IREC expects affirmative oversight.

**Brokers should conduct quarterly audits of three specific areas.** First, verify that every active transaction file contains a properly executed written agreement (IAR Form 43, 46, or 17) dated before the first substantive service was performed. Second, confirm that all commission payments were processed through the brokerage trust or operating account with proper documentation. Third, review agent license status through the IPLA online verification portal to catch approaching expirations before they lapse.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Frequency | Liability if Missed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Written agreements | Executed before services; specific commission terms | Every transaction | Broker discipline under IC 25-34.1-10-7.5 |
| Commission routing | All payments through broker accounts | Monthly reconciliation | Fines up to $10,000; potential license suspension |
| License status | Active status for all affiliated agents | Quarterly minimum | Vicarious liability for unlicensed practice |
| Advertising compliance | Brokerage name on all materials; no misleading team names | Monthly spot-checks | IREC complaint and fine |
| Agency disclosure | State Form 55919 in every file | Every transaction | Transaction voidability; client restitution |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance issues will eventually face an IREC investigation they could have prevented. The [paperwork burden is real](/blog/why-agents-hate-paperwork), but the cost of non-compliance — measured in suspended licenses, voided transactions, and personal liability — makes systematic auditing non-negotiable.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: What Is Actually at Stake

Indiana does not treat commission and licensing violations as minor paperwork infractions. The consequences operate on three levels: regulatory, civil, and transactional.

At the regulatory level, IREC can impose fines up to $10,000 per violation, require completion of additional education, place licenses on probation, suspend licenses for defined periods, or permanently revoke them. Multiple violations compound — a second offense within five years typically results in suspension rather than a fine alone.

At the civil level, a commission earned without proper written authorization is not merely at risk — it may be ordered returned with interest. Indiana courts have consistently held that real estate commission agreements must satisfy the Statute of Frauds (IC 32-21-1-1), meaning an oral promise to pay a commission is unenforceable regardless of the work performed. Agents who close a deal without a signed agreement have no legal standing to collect.

At the transactional level, agency disclosure failures can give buyers or sellers grounds to rescind a purchase agreement. If a party can demonstrate they were not informed of material compensation arrangements before entering a contract, they may argue the contract was induced without informed consent. This theory has succeeded in Indiana courts, resulting in deal cancellations weeks or months after closing.

## How Indiana Compares to Neighboring States

Agents who hold licenses in multiple states — or who refer business across state lines — need to understand where Indiana's rules diverge from its neighbors. [License reciprocity and portability](/blog/washington-license-reciprocity-portability-2026) issues arise frequently in border markets like the Chicago-NW Indiana corridor and the Louisville-Southern Indiana market.

Indiana does not offer full reciprocity with any state. It does allow experience-based examination waivers for agents licensed in other jurisdictions who meet specific hour and education requirements. The application process requires submission through IPLA with verified license history from the originating state.

For commission purposes, an agent licensed only in Illinois cannot earn a commission on an Indiana transaction — even if the referring agent is properly licensed in their home state. Referral fees paid to out-of-state licensees must still comply with IC 25-34.1-10-10 and flow through a licensed Indiana broker who has a cooperation agreement in place.
