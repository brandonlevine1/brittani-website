## Understanding Virginia Continuing Education Requirements 2026

Every two years, thousands of Virginia real estate licensees scramble to meet their continuing education obligations before renewal deadlines hit. The Virginia continuing education requirements 2026 cycle is no different, and agents who wait until the last month often find themselves locked out of approved courses or, worse, practicing on a lapsed license. The Virginia Real Estate Board (VREB), operating under the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), sets and enforces these standards under the authority of Title 54.1, Chapter 21 of the Code of Virginia.

DPOR does not send multiple reminders. Your license renewal date is based on the last day of the month in which your license was originally issued, and it falls on a two-year cycle. If you miss it, you cannot write offers, show property, or collect compensation until reinstatement is complete.

The stakes extend beyond your own income. Brokers carrying agents with lapsed licenses face their own regulatory exposure, including potential violations of Virginia Administrative Code 18 VAC 135-20-10 through 18 VAC 135-20-370. The compliance chain starts with individual agents but the liability rolls uphill fast.

## Hour Breakdowns: Salespersons vs. Brokers

**Virginia separates its CE requirements based on license type, and the distinction matters more than most agents realize.** Salespersons holding an active license must complete 16 total clock hours of approved continuing education during each two-year renewal period. Brokers must complete 24 total hours, reflecting their supervisory responsibilities.

The composition of those hours is not optional. Both salespersons and brokers must complete 8 hours of required courses designated by the Virginia Real Estate Board, covering topics like fair housing, ethics, agency law updates, and changes to Virginia statutes. The remaining hours differ by license class.

| Requirement | Salesperson (Active) | Broker (Active) |
|---|---|---|
| Total CE Hours | 16 | 24 |
| Required/Mandatory Topics | 8 hours | 8 hours |
| Broker Management Courses | Not applicable | 8 hours |
| Elective Hours | 8 hours | 8 hours |
| Renewal Cycle | Every 2 years | Every 2 years |
| Inactive License CE | 0 hours (but must complete CE to reactivate) | 0 hours (but must complete CE to reactivate) |

Agents on inactive status do not need to complete CE for renewal, but they cannot perform any licensable activity. Reactivation requires completing all CE that would have been required during the inactive period, which can stack up quickly if you let it go more than one cycle.

## Required Course Topics for 2026

The Virginia Real Estate Board updates its mandatory course curriculum periodically, and the 2026 cycle includes modules reflecting recent legislative changes. Among the required 8 hours, expect coverage of fair housing enforcement updates, [agency disclosure obligations](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026), and changes to the Virginia Residential Property Disclosure Act (Code of Virginia Section 55.1-700 et seq.).

VREB also mandates instruction on supervision standards, which ties directly into how brokers must oversee their agents' transaction files. If you handle [property condition disclosures](/blog/virginia-property-condition-disclosures-2026), the required coursework will touch on recent updates to what sellers must reveal and how agents must handle incomplete or missing forms.

Elective hours give agents more flexibility. You can choose approved courses in commercial real estate, property management, real estate finance, technology, or specialized topics like green building certifications. However, every elective course must come from a DPOR-approved provider, and completion must be reported electronically through the provider's system to DPOR.

## What Happens When You Fail to Comply

**The consequences of missing your CE deadline are immediate and mechanical — there is no grace period built into Virginia law.** On the day after your renewal deadline, if DPOR has not received verification of your completed hours and your renewal application (along with the renewal fee), your license status changes to lapsed. A lapsed license means you are unlicensed, period.

Any real estate activity you conduct while lapsed constitutes unlicensed practice under Code of Virginia Section 54.1-2106.1. Penalties include civil fines up to $1,000 per violation, and each transaction or act of solicitation can be counted as a separate violation. Criminal penalties for willful unlicensed practice are also possible under Virginia law.

Deals in progress can be severely disrupted. If a listing agent's license lapses mid-transaction, the listing agreement itself may become voidable. Buyers represented by a lapsed agent have grounds to question whether proper agency existed, potentially unwinding contracts. The Virginia Real Estate Board can also impose additional sanctions including license revocation for repeated violations or practicing while lapsed.

Reinstatement requires completing all outstanding CE, submitting a reinstatement application, and paying the reinstatement fee (currently set by DPOR regulation). If your license has been lapsed for more than one renewal cycle, you may need to retake the Virginia licensing examination entirely.

## Common Mistakes Virginia Agents Make

Experienced agents fall into these traps just as often as newer licensees. The patterns are predictable but the consequences remain serious every time.

**First: confusing course approval across states.** Agents holding licenses in both Virginia and neighboring jurisdictions sometimes assume that CE completed for Maryland or DC will automatically count toward Virginia requirements. It does not. Virginia only accepts courses from DPOR-approved providers specifically approved for Virginia credit. You must verify approval status on the [DPOR license lookup system](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov/) before enrolling.

Second: completing hours but failing to verify provider reporting. Even if you sit through every required hour, your renewal will be denied if the provider did not transmit your completion data to DPOR. Agents should confirm within 10 business days of completing any course that the hours appear on their DPOR license record. Relying on a certificate of completion alone is not sufficient — DPOR needs the electronic reporting.

Third: ignoring the broker management requirement. Brokers who complete 24 generic hours without ensuring 8 of those hours qualify as broker management courses will be short on compliance. DPOR rejects renewals that do not meet the categorical breakdown, regardless of total hours completed.

Fourth: waiting until the final week of the renewal period. Popular approved providers frequently sell out their final sessions before deadline months. Online courses may have technical issues or processing delays that prevent timely reporting to DPOR. Building a 30-day buffer is not conservative — it is necessary.

Fifth: assuming inactive status requires no action. While inactive licensees do not need CE for renewal, they still must submit the renewal application and fee. Failing to renew even an inactive license causes it to lapse, which means future reactivation becomes significantly more expensive and time-consuming.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Virginia Code Section 54.1-2108 places direct supervisory responsibility on brokers for the activities of their affiliated licensees. This extends beyond transaction oversight into ensuring that every agent under a broker's umbrella maintains proper licensure.

| Broker Audit Item | How to Verify | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Agent license status | DPOR online license lookup | Monthly minimum |
| CE completion progress | Request proof from agents 60 days before renewal | Per agent cycle |
| Broker management hours (for associate brokers) | Confirm course category with provider | Each renewal cycle |
| New licensee post-license requirements | Track 30-hour post-license deadline | Within first year |
| Provider approval status | Cross-reference with DPOR approved list | Before recommending courses |

**Brokers who discover an affiliated agent has lapsed should immediately suspend that agent's authority to act on behalf of the brokerage.** Allowing a lapsed agent to continue showing homes, writing offers, or attending closings exposes the brokerage to regulatory action and civil liability. Document the suspension in writing and notify all parties to active transactions.

Smart brokerages build tracking systems that flag upcoming renewal dates at the 90-day, 60-day, and 30-day marks. This is not hand-holding — it is risk management. A single [missed deadline](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) from one agent can create liability across an entire office's transaction pipeline.

Brokers should also verify that new agents complete Virginia's 30-hour post-license education requirement within 12 months of initial licensure. This is separate from continuing education and is governed by 18 VAC 135-20-60. Failure to complete post-license education results in the license being placed on inactive status automatically.

## How CE Connects to Transaction Compliance

Continuing education is not just a checkbox — the topics covered in mandatory courses directly affect how agents handle [disclosure obligations](/blog/virginia-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) and transaction documentation in practice. Virginia's required curriculum addresses changes to the Residential Property Disclosure Statement and the disclaimers agents must include in listing and buyer agreements.

Agents who skip or rush through CE often miss updates to forms and procedures that VREB has implemented. The Virginia REALTORS association updates its standard forms periodically, and CE courses are designed to walk licensees through changes to documents like the Regional Sales Contract (Form 600), the Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement, and the Buyer Representation Agreement.

Understanding these updates matters because using outdated forms or failing to disclose required information creates liability that extends well beyond the renewal cycle. An agent who missed the 2026 CE module on updated buyer agency requirements, for example, might use language that does not comply with recent [commission structure changes](/blog/virginia-commission-structure-changes-2026) in Virginia — creating exposure for both the agent and their broker.
