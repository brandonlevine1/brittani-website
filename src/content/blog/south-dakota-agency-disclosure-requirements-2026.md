## Understanding South Dakota Agency Disclosure Requirements 2026

The South Dakota agency disclosure requirements 2026 remain one of the most frequent compliance triggers flagged during commission audits. Every licensed agent in the state must disclose their agency relationship before engaging in substantive discussion with a consumer, and the timing of that disclosure trips up even experienced practitioners. Missing or late disclosures account for a disproportionate share of complaints filed with the South Dakota Real Estate Commission (SDREC).

South Dakota governs agency relationships through SDCL Chapter 36-21A, the Brokerage Disclosure Act. This statute defines the types of agency relationships permitted, the duties owed to each party, and the exact moment disclosure must happen. The law was not overhauled for 2026, but SDREC enforcement guidance issued in late 2025 clarified several ambiguities around electronic delivery and team-based disclosures that agents need to internalize now.

**If you are operating under the assumption that your brokerage's onboarding paperwork covers agency disclosure automatically, stop.** The obligation belongs to the individual licensee at the point of first substantive contact, regardless of what the office intake process looks like. The form must be agent-specific and transaction-specific.

## The Statutory Framework: SDCL 36-21A

South Dakota's Brokerage Disclosure Act (SDCL 36-21A) establishes four recognized agency relationships: seller's agent, buyer's agent, disclosed dual agent, and transaction broker. Each relationship carries specific statutory duties enumerated in the code. The differences between them matter for disclosure purposes because the form must identify which relationship the agent is entering.

Under SDCL 36-21A-4, a licensee must provide a written disclosure of agency relationships to all prospective buyers and sellers at the first substantive contact. "Substantive contact" is defined as any conversation that goes beyond general factual information about the market and moves into discussing the consumer's specific property needs, financial situation, or motivation. A casual open house greeting does not trigger the requirement, but asking a visitor whether they are pre-approved does.

The statute also requires that the consumer sign the disclosure form acknowledging receipt. An unsigned form is not compliant. If the consumer refuses to sign, the agent must note the refusal on the form, date it, and retain the document in the transaction file.

> "A licensee shall provide a written disclosure of the brokerage relationships offered by the licensee to all parties to a contemplated real estate transaction at the first substantive contact." — SDCL 36-21A-4

For agents who also handle [seller disclosure compliance](/blog/south-dakota-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026), the agency disclosure is a separate obligation with a separate timing requirement. Conflating the two creates audit exposure.

## Forms and Documentation: What You Actually Hand Over

The SDREC does not mandate a single numbered state form in the same way some other commissions do. Instead, it requires that any disclosure form used must contain the elements specified in SDCL 36-21A-5, including a description of each agency relationship type, the duties associated with each, and a space for the consumer's acknowledgment signature. Most agents in the state rely on the South Dakota Association of REALTORS form titled "Disclosure of Brokerage Relationships," which satisfies all statutory requirements.

| Element | Required by Statute | Common Location on Form |
|---------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Description of seller agency | Yes — SDCL 36-21A-5 | Section 1 |
| Description of buyer agency | Yes — SDCL 36-21A-5 | Section 2 |
| Disclosed dual agency explanation | Yes — SDCL 36-21A-9 | Section 3 |
| Transaction broker duties | Yes — SDCL 36-21A-10 | Section 4 |
| Consumer acknowledgment signature | Yes — SDCL 36-21A-4 | Bottom of form |
| Date of disclosure | Yes — SDREC enforcement guidance | Adjacent to signature |

Some brokerages have developed proprietary forms. If your brokerage uses a custom version, confirm with your broker that it was reviewed against the 2025 SDREC enforcement bulletin. The bulletin specifically addressed electronic signatures and clarified that e-signed disclosures are acceptable under SDCL 53-12-12 (the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act) provided the consumer affirmatively consents to electronic delivery.

## Dual Agency and Transaction Brokerage: The Consent Trap

**Disclosed dual agency is where most compliance failures concentrate.** Under SDCL 36-21A-9, a licensee may act as a dual agent only if both the buyer and seller provide written informed consent before the dual agency relationship commences. The initial disclosure form alone does not satisfy this requirement. A separate written consent to dual agency must be obtained.

The distinction matters: the initial disclosure tells the consumer that dual agency exists as a possibility. The consent form authorizes the agent to actually enter that relationship in a specific transaction. Agents who treat the initial disclosure as blanket permission to practice dual agency are violating the statute.

Transaction brokerage under SDCL 36-21A-10 offers an alternative that avoids the dual agency consent requirement, but it carries its own disclosure obligation. The agent must clearly communicate that they are not advocating for either party and that confidential information will not be shared. Many agents in the Rapid City and Sioux Falls markets have shifted toward transaction brokerage precisely because it simplifies disclosure compliance, but the written form still must be signed.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, Rescission, and Liability

Failing to meet South Dakota agency disclosure requirements carries real consequences across three dimensions: regulatory discipline, transaction validity, and civil liability.

**The SDREC has authority under SDCL 36-21A-23 to impose disciplinary action** including formal reprimand, fines up to $2,000 per violation, license suspension, and license revocation. The commission's 2024-2025 enforcement report showed 14 formal actions involving disclosure failures, with the average fine landing at $1,200. License suspensions ranged from 30 days to six months depending on whether the violation was a first offense.

On the transaction side, a consumer who was not properly disclosed to may have grounds to rescind the transaction. South Dakota courts have recognized that failure to disclose agency creates a material defect in the consent underlying the contract. While rescission is not automatic, it becomes a viable argument in any post-closing dispute.

| Consequence | Authority | Typical Outcome |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Fine | SDCL 36-21A-23 | $500–$2,000 per violation |
| License suspension | SDREC disciplinary board | 30 days to 6 months |
| License revocation | SDREC disciplinary board | Repeat or egregious offenses |
| Transaction rescission | Civil court action | Case-by-case, fact-dependent |

Civil lawsuits arising from undisclosed dual agency have produced six-figure settlements in South Dakota within the last three years. Your errors and omissions insurance may cover defense costs, but carriers are increasingly scrutinizing whether disclosure protocols were followed before extending coverage.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Agency Disclosure

Five specific errors surface repeatedly in SDREC complaint files and brokerage audits. Recognizing them is the first step toward preventing them.

**First: Disclosing too late.** Agents routinely wait until the purchase agreement stage to present the disclosure form. By that point, multiple substantive conversations have already occurred. The statute requires disclosure at the first such contact, not the first written offer. If you discussed a buyer's price range at an open house two weeks ago, disclosure was already overdue.

Second: Using the wrong relationship designation. Agents sometimes check "buyer's agent" on the disclosure form while actually functioning as a transaction broker because they showed the buyer a property listed by their own brokerage. The form must reflect the actual relationship, not the aspirational one.

Third: Failing to obtain a separate dual agency consent. As discussed above, the initial disclosure form is not a dual agency consent form. Agents who proceed with dual agency based solely on the initial disclosure are out of compliance with SDCL 36-21A-9. The consent must be transaction-specific and signed by both parties.

Fourth: Not retaining copies of unsigned or refused forms. When a consumer refuses to sign, the agent's obligation does not evaporate. The agent must document the refusal, note the date, and retain the form. Agents who simply discard unsigned forms have no evidence of attempted compliance during an audit.

Fifth: Assuming team members are covered by the team lead's disclosure. Each licensee who has substantive contact with a consumer must provide their own disclosure. If a showing agent on your team meets the buyer first and then you take over, your disclosure is still required. The [tracking of multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) becomes especially important when team members rotate through client contacts.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under SDCL 36-21-97 for the compliance of their affiliated licensees. A broker who fails to implement reasonable systems to ensure disclosure compliance faces their own disciplinary exposure independent of the individual agent's violation.

**Start with a file review protocol that checks for disclosure timing, not just disclosure presence.** A signed form in the file is insufficient if the date on that form post-dates the first substantive contact documented elsewhere in the transaction record. Cross-reference the disclosure date against the first showing confirmation, the first email discussing property criteria, or the first text exchange about financing.

Audit dual agency consent separately from the initial disclosure. Your compliance checklist should have two distinct line items: one for the general disclosure form and one for the dual agency consent (if applicable). Brokerages that collapse these into a single checklist item consistently miss the consent gap.

Implement a quarterly sample audit of at least 10% of closed files. The SDREC does not mandate a specific audit frequency, but commission investigators have stated publicly that brokerages with documented audit programs receive more favorable treatment during investigations. Document your findings and any corrective actions taken.

## Staying Compliant With South Dakota Agency Disclosure Requirements 2026

The rules have not changed dramatically, but enforcement attention has intensified. The SDREC's 2025 enforcement bulletin signals a continued focus on disclosure timing and dual agency consent through 2026. Agents who build disclosure delivery into their first-contact workflow rather than their offer-preparation workflow will stay ahead of audit risk.

South Dakota agency disclosure requirements 2026 demand precision at a specific moment in the client relationship, and the margin for error remains razor-thin. Confirm your forms match current statutory language, train every team member on their individual disclosure obligation, and build a file review system that catches gaps before they become complaints. The agents and brokers who treat disclosure as a mechanical habit rather than an afterthought are the ones who keep their licenses clean and their transactions intact.
