## Pennsylvania Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026: The Rules You Cannot Ignore

Wire fraud losses in residential real estate topped $446 million nationally in 2024, and Pennsylvania ranked among the top ten states for reported incidents. Understanding Pennsylvania wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 is no longer optional knowledge for licensed agents—it is a compliance obligation enforced by the Pennsylvania State Real Estate Commission (SREC) and embedded in the standard forms published by the Pennsylvania Association of Realtors (PAR). If you close transactions in the Commonwealth without following these protocols, you are one spoofed email away from a blown deal, a devastated client, and a disciplinary hearing.

The SREC's enforcement posture shifted meaningfully in late 2025 when it issued Guidance Bulletin 2025-03, which tied wire fraud prevention directly to the duties of licensees under 63 Pa.C.S. Section 455.604—the statute governing standards of conduct for real estate professionals. That bulletin made clear that failure to warn consumers about wire fraud risks constitutes a violation of the licensee's duty to act with reasonable skill and care. Agents who previously treated wire fraud notices as "nice to have" paperwork now face the same disciplinary exposure as those who mishandle escrow funds.

## What the Law Actually Requires of Pennsylvania Agents

The statutory foundation sits in the Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), codified at 63 Pa.C.S. Chapter 45. Section 455.604(a)(12) prohibits conduct that demonstrates "incompetency to act as a real estate licensee in a manner that safeguards the interests of the public." The SREC interprets this to include the failure to warn clients about wire fraud schemes that routinely target real estate closings.

**PAR responded by creating two dedicated forms: Form WFN (Wire Fraud Notice) and Form WFW (Wire Fraud Warning).** Form WFN is a standalone consumer disclosure that explains how business email compromise scams work, instructs buyers to verify wiring details by phone using a known number, and includes an acknowledgment signature line. Form WFW is a shorter addendum designed to attach to the Agreement of Sale, creating a contractual record that both parties received the warning.

Both forms were updated in January 2026 to reflect new language recommended by the SREC's Guidance Bulletin. The key addition: a clause stating that the agent and brokerage are not responsible for independently verifying wire instructions issued by the settlement agent, but that they will not transmit wiring details via email, text, or other unsecured electronic communication. This language protects agents who follow protocol while exposing those who cut corners.

## When and How to Deliver the Required Disclosures

Timing matters. PAR's 2026 guidelines recommend delivering Form WFN at the time of the initial buyer consultation or, at the latest, when the Agreement of Sale is executed. Form WFW should be attached as an addendum to the Agreement of Sale itself. Waiting until a week before closing—when wiring instructions are already in play—defeats the purpose and may not satisfy the SREC's standard of care.

The delivery method also carries compliance implications. Pennsylvania regulation 49 Pa. Code Section 35.336 governs how licensees transmit documents to consumers. Electronic delivery is permitted if the consumer has consented to electronic communications under the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (73 Pa.C.S. Chapter 22). But here is the catch: if you email wire fraud warnings, you must use encrypted or secured transmission—sending a wire fraud notice in an unencrypted email is almost comedically self-defeating.

For agents handling [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), tracking which buyers have signed WFN and which Agreements of Sale include WFW can slip through the cracks fast. The penalty for that slip is not hypothetical.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, Liability, and Deal Collapse

The SREC has statutory authority under 63 Pa.C.S. Section 455.501 to impose civil penalties of up to $10,000 per violation, suspend or revoke licenses, and require corrective education. A single missing wire fraud notice probably will not trigger revocation on its own, but it becomes devastating evidence if a client actually loses money. At that point, the missing form transforms from a paperwork gap into proof of negligence.

**Civil liability exposure is where agents face the real financial danger.** Pennsylvania courts apply a negligence standard: did the agent owe a duty, did they breach it, and did that breach cause damage? After Guidance Bulletin 2025-03, the duty is clearly established. A buyer who loses $300,000 to a spoofed wiring scheme will have a straightforward negligence claim against any agent who failed to deliver the required disclosures or who transmitted wire instructions through unsecured channels.

| Scenario | Likely Outcome for Agent |
|----------|------------------------|
| WFN delivered, buyer still defrauded | Agent likely protected; duty satisfied |
| WFN never delivered, buyer defrauded | Civil liability + SREC discipline probable |
| Agent emailed wire instructions directly | Shared liability with fraudster possible |
| Broker had no audit system in place | Broker faces vicarious liability + SREC action |
| Agent used encrypted portal for all docs | Strongest defensive position |

Deals can also collapse entirely when title companies discover mid-transaction that required disclosures are missing. Some Pennsylvania title underwriters now require proof of WFN delivery before issuing closing protection letters. No letter, no closing.

## Common Mistakes Pennsylvania Agents Make

**First, agents assume the title company handles everything.** While the settlement agent manages the actual wire transfer, the disclosure obligation under RELRA falls on the licensee who is guiding the consumer through the transaction. Delegating the warning to the title company does not satisfy your independent duty.

Second, agents deliver Form WFN but skip Form WFW. The standalone notice creates a record, but attaching WFW to the Agreement of Sale builds contractual protection. Without it, a buyer's attorney can argue the warning was informal and insufficient. Using both forms together is the standard that PAR—and increasingly, the SREC—expects.

Third, agents transmit wire instructions on behalf of the settlement agent "as a convenience." This is the single most dangerous thing you can do. Every time you copy-paste wiring details into an email to your buyer, you insert yourself into the chain of liability. If that email is intercepted and altered, the buyer's loss is partially your problem. PAR's guidelines explicitly state: never relay wire instructions. Direct the client to call the title company using a phone number obtained independently.

Fourth, agents forget to update forms mid-year. PAR published a revised WFN in January 2026 with additional language about AI-generated voice scams. Agents still using the 2024 or 2025 version are delivering an outdated disclosure that may not cover emerging fraud vectors. Check the [PAR forms library](https://www.parealtor.org/standard-forms/) quarterly.

Fifth, agents do not document delivery. A signed WFN sitting in your transaction file is gold. A verbal "I told them about wire fraud" is worthless in a disciplinary hearing. If you work without a system that [verifies wire instruction protocols](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) and logs delivery timestamps, you are building your compliance on sand.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Pennsylvania carry heightened responsibility under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.242, which requires direct supervision of all licensees' activities. The SREC does not accept "I didn't know my agent skipped the form" as a defense. Brokers must establish written policies, train agents on those policies, and audit for compliance.

At minimum, a broker's wire fraud compliance program should verify three things for every closed transaction: that Form WFN was signed before or at the time of contract execution, that Form WFW was attached to the Agreement of Sale, and that no agent in the office transmitted wiring details via unsecured communication. These three checkpoints are auditable and defensible.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Evidence Required |
|-------------------|-----------|-------------------|
| WFN signed and dated | Every transaction | Signed form in file |
| WFW attached to Agreement of Sale | Every transaction | Executed addendum |
| No wire instructions sent by agents | Quarterly email audit | Email search log |
| Agent training completion | Annual | Training attendance record |
| Form version current with PAR updates | Quarterly | Form header date check |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-police are the ones who end up before the SREC explaining why their office had no controls in place. The Commission has signaled through multiple enforcement actions in 2025 that supervisory failures carry independent penalties separate from the individual agent's violations.

For offices managing high transaction volumes, tracking form completion across dozens of agents manually is [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down). Automated compliance tracking is not a luxury—it is the only reliable way to prove your brokerage met its supervisory obligations when the SREC comes asking.

## Building a Defensible Wire Fraud Prevention Protocol

A defensible protocol has three layers: education, documentation, and verification. Education means every agent in your office can explain what a business email compromise attack looks like and knows they must never send wire instructions. Documentation means signed forms in every file, timestamped and version-controlled. Verification means a callback procedure—confirming wiring details by phone before funds move—is standard practice your clients understand before they ever sit at a closing table.

> "A licensee shall not engage in conduct that demonstrates incompetency to act as a real estate licensee in such manner as to safeguard the interests of the public." — 63 Pa.C.S. Section 455.604(a)(12)

That statutory language is broad enough to cover any failure to protect a client from a foreseeable harm. Wire fraud in real estate is no longer unforeseeable—it is statistically predictable. The SREC will hold you to that standard.
