## Pennsylvania Commission & Licensing Rules 2026: The Statute-Level Details That Matter

The Pennsylvania commission & licensing rules 2026 cycle brings continued enforcement from the Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission (PREC) under the Department of State, and agents who treat compliance casually are the ones getting fined. The Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), codified at 63 P.S. Section 455.101 et seq., governs every transaction you touch. Its implementing regulations at 49 Pa. Code Chapter 35 spell out how commissions are earned, paid, and documented.

**If you hold a Pennsylvania license, commission is not yours until your broker says it is.** That single principle drives nearly every compliance question agents ask about compensation. The state treats any deviation from this chain of payment as a violation, and enforcement actions have ticked upward in recent renewal cycles.

This post breaks down the specific statutes, the forms you need to file, the penalties for non-compliance, and the audit responsibilities that fall on brokers. If you manage a team or run an office, the broker enforcement section at the end is written directly for you.

## How Commission Payment Works Under RELRA

Pennsylvania law is explicit: all compensation flows through the broker of record. Section 455.604 of RELRA prohibits any licensee from receiving compensation for real estate services from anyone other than their employing broker. This means a seller, buyer, cooperating brokerage, or title company cannot cut a check to a salesperson directly.

The broker must deposit commission funds into either a business operating account or an escrow/trust account as outlined in 49 Pa. Code Section 35.327. The broker then disburses the agent's share according to their independent contractor agreement or employment contract. There is no statutory requirement that the split be any particular percentage — that remains a private business arrangement.

Where agents get tripped up is on bonuses, referral fees, and incentives paid by builders or developers. Under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.325(b), even these must route through the broker. An agent who accepts a gift card, bonus check, or travel incentive directly from a third party without broker involvement is technically in violation.

## Licensing Requirements and Renewal Deadlines

Pennsylvania operates on a biennial renewal cycle. All salesperson and broker licenses expire on May 31 of even-numbered years, with the next renewal deadline falling on May 31, 2026. The renewal fee is currently $96 for salespersons and $163 for brokers, paid through the [Pennsylvania Licensing System (PALS)](https://www.pals.pa.gov/).

| Requirement | Salesperson | Broker |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-license education | 75 hours | 240 hours (plus experience) |
| Continuing education per cycle | 14 hours | 14 hours |
| Renewal fee | $96 | $163 |
| Renewal cycle | Biennial (even years) | Biennial (even years) |
| Deadline | May 31, 2026 | May 31, 2026 |

Of those 14 CE hours, the PREC mandates specific topics. You must complete at least 3.5 hours in modules approved by the Commission that cover fair housing, agency law, or ethics. The remaining hours can be elective, but all courses must be taken through PREC-approved providers.

**A license that lapses for more than five years requires you to retake the state exam.** If your lapse is under five years, you can reinstate by completing all outstanding CE, paying a late fee, and submitting a reinstatement application. During any period of lapse, you cannot practice, and any commission earned on transactions closed during that window is forfeitable.

## The Consumer Notice and Required Disclosure Forms

Pennsylvania does not use a traditional agency disclosure form in the way that some neighboring states do. Instead, the PREC requires delivery of the Consumer Notice, designated as form REL-001. This form must be presented to every consumer at the first substantive contact — before any confidential information is exchanged.

The Consumer Notice explains the types of agency relationships available, the duties owed under each, and how compensation may be structured. It does not create an agency relationship on its own. That is established through a separate written agreement, either a listing agreement (such as the PAR Exclusive Right to Sell form, PAR Form ERS) or a buyer representation agreement (PAR Form ABR).

Since the 2024 practice changes following the NAR settlement, Pennsylvania brokerages have adapted their buyer agreements to include explicit compensation terms. The PAR Form ABR now requires a specific dollar amount or percentage for buyer-agent compensation, and this must be agreed upon before touring properties. Failure to secure a signed buyer agreement before showing homes is a compliance gap that the PREC has flagged in recent bulletins.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: What Actually Happens

The consequences for violating commission and licensing rules in Pennsylvania range from corrective education to criminal charges. The PREC has broad enforcement authority under 63 P.S. Section 455.604 and 63 P.S. Section 455.606.

| Violation | Potential Consequence |
|---|---|
| Practicing on expired license | Up to $10,000 fine; criminal misdemeanor |
| Receiving commission directly from consumer | License suspension or revocation |
| Failing to deliver Consumer Notice (REL-001) | Formal reprimand; fine up to $5,000 per occurrence |
| Commingling escrow funds | Automatic license revocation proceedings |
| Advertising without broker approval | Fine; possible suspension |
| Failing to complete CE by deadline | License lapse; cannot practice until reinstated |

Deal cancellation is also on the table. If a buyer or seller discovers that their agent was unlicensed at the time of contract execution, the transaction is voidable. Pennsylvania courts have upheld rescission claims in cases where the agent's license was expired — even by a single day — at the time they performed a licensed activity.

Beyond state action, agents face civil liability. A consumer who suffers damages due to unlicensed activity or undisclosed conflicts can file suit under RELRA's private right of action provision. Attorneys' fees are recoverable in some circumstances, making these cases expensive to defend even when the underlying amount in controversy is modest.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Pennsylvania

**Mistake one: presenting a property without a signed buyer representation agreement.** Since the 2024-2025 practice changes took hold, this is the most frequently cited infraction in PREC complaint filings. Agents assume a verbal understanding suffices. It does not. The PAR Form ABR must be executed before substantive brokerage services begin, and that includes touring homes.

Mistake two: failing to deliver the Consumer Notice at first substantive contact. Many agents hand over REL-001 at the listing appointment or buyer consultation, but miss the obligation when a cold call or open house visitor starts asking about specific properties. The regulation at 49 Pa. Code Section 35.336 does not carve out exceptions for casual conversations that become substantive discussions.

Mistake three: accepting referral fees from out-of-state agents without broker routing. Interstate referral arrangements are common, particularly along the Pennsylvania-New Jersey and Pennsylvania-Delaware borders. But if a New Jersey brokerage sends a referral fee directly to a PA salesperson rather than to the PA broker, both parties may be in violation. The PA agent must ensure the check is made payable to their brokerage. Tracking these handoffs is exactly the kind of detail that falls through the cracks when you are [managing multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without a system.

Mistake four: advertising properties or services on social media without including the broker's name. Under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.305, all advertising by a licensee must include the name of the broker or the registered trade name of the brokerage. An Instagram post promoting a listing that only identifies the individual agent — without the brokerage name — is a technical violation.

Mistake five: letting CE lapse and continuing to show property or negotiate contracts during the grace period. Pennsylvania does not offer a grace period. If your license expired on May 31 and you did not renew, you are unlicensed on June 1. Any activity you perform that day exposes you to the penalties outlined above.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers of record carry personal liability for the compliance failures of their licensees under 63 P.S. Section 455.604a. The PREC does not accept ignorance as a defense. If an agent in your office is operating on an expired license, accepting direct compensation, or failing to deliver REL-001, the broker faces discipline alongside the agent.

**Every Pennsylvania broker should maintain a quarterly audit cadence covering three areas: license status verification, compensation routing, and form compliance.** For license status, pull PALS records for every affiliated licensee at least once per quarter to confirm active status. For compensation, reconcile every commission disbursement to confirm that no payments bypassed the brokerage operating account. For form compliance, spot-check transaction files to verify that REL-001 was delivered and that signed buyer agreements are in place.

Many brokers delegate this audit function to a transaction coordinator or compliance manager. If your brokerage handles volume, this is where a [dedicated TC role](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) becomes a risk-management tool rather than just an administrative convenience. The cost of a single PREC enforcement action — legal fees, potential suspension, reputational damage — far exceeds the cost of building compliance checks into your workflow.

Brokers should also maintain written policies on advertising approval, referral fee processing, and CE tracking. These policies do not need to be complex, but they need to exist in writing so that the brokerage can demonstrate a compliance program if the PREC opens an investigation. An agent who violates a clearly documented policy shifts more of the disciplinary weight onto themselves rather than the broker, though the broker is never fully insulated.
