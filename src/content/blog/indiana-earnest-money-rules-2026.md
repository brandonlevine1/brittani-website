## Understanding Indiana Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

Handling earnest money incorrectly in Indiana can end a career faster than a failed inspection kills a deal. The rules governing Indiana earnest money & escrow laws 2026 are codified in Indiana Code Title 25, Article 34.1 and enforced by the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC), and every licensed agent and managing broker needs to know them cold. Ignorance of deposit timing, trust account rules, or disbursement protocols is not a defense when a complaint lands on the Commission's desk.

Indiana does not treat earnest money as a mere formality. It is a legally binding good-faith deposit that carries fiduciary obligations the moment a check or wire hits the brokerage. The statutory framework has not changed dramatically in recent years, but enforcement trends and form revisions for 2026 demand renewed attention.

## Statutory Framework: IC 25-34.1 and 876 IAC 8

The Indiana Real Estate License Act, found at [IC 25-34.1](https://iga.in.gov/laws/2024/ic/titles/25#25-34.1), establishes the licensing requirements and standards of conduct for all agents and brokers. Section 25-34.1-3-9 specifically lists grounds for disciplinary action, including commingling funds, failing to deposit earnest money within the required timeframe, and disbursing trust funds without proper authorization.

The implementing regulations live in 876 IAC 8 (Indiana Administrative Code, Title 876, Article 8). Rule 876 IAC 8-2-3 mandates that brokers maintain complete records of all trust account transactions, including the source, date, and amount of every deposit. Rule 876 IAC 8-2-4 sets the deposit deadline: earnest money must reach the trust account by the close of the next banking day following acceptance of the purchase agreement, unless the contract specifies a different timeline.

**Brokers who allow funds to sit in a desk drawer or agent's personal account—even for 48 hours—are in violation.** The Commission does not need a harmed party to initiate action; routine audits catch these failures regularly.

## Forms Indiana Agents Must Use Correctly

The Indiana Association of Realtors (IAR) publishes the standard forms most agents rely on. The IAR Purchase Agreement (Form 46) is the workhorse contract for residential transactions, and its earnest money provisions dictate who holds funds, how much is deposited, and when. Form 46's Section 2 addresses earnest money amount, holder designation, and the timeline for deposit.

When disputes arise over earnest money disbursement, agents turn to the IAR Earnest Money Dispute and Interpleader form. This form provides the mechanism for a broker to petition a court to hold the funds when buyer and seller cannot agree on release. Indiana does not have a statutory automatic-release provision—meaning the money stays frozen until both parties consent or a court orders distribution.

| Form | Purpose | Key Section |
|------|---------|-------------|
| IAR Form 46 | Purchase Agreement | Section 2 – Earnest Money |
| IAR Earnest Money Dispute | Interpleader filing | Entire form |
| IAR Form 50 | Buyer's Response/Counteroffer | Amends deposit terms |
| Broker Trust Account Ledger (876 IAC 8-2-3) | Internal recordkeeping | All entries |

Agents should also be aware that Form 46 includes language permitting the parties to designate a title company or attorney as escrow holder instead of the listing broker. When this box is checked, the deposit timeline obligation shifts, but the listing agent still bears the responsibility of transmitting the funds promptly.

## Deposit Timing and Trust Account Requirements

**The single most litigated issue in Indiana earnest money disputes is late deposit.** Under 876 IAC 8-2-4, the clock starts ticking the moment both parties have signed the purchase agreement—not when the agent physically receives the check. If a buyer hands an agent a check on Monday evening and the seller accepts the offer Tuesday morning, that check must be in the trust account by the close of business Wednesday.

Indiana permits brokers to hold a single pooled trust account rather than opening individual escrow accounts per transaction. However, the pooled account must be maintained at an Indiana-licensed financial institution, clearly labeled as a trust or escrow account, and never contain personal or operating funds. The only exception is a nominal amount (typically under $500) to cover bank fees—often called a "cushion"—but even that must be documented.

Wire transfers and electronic deposits satisfy the banking-day requirement as long as the funds are credited by end of business. Agents accepting cashier's checks or personal checks must physically deliver them to the office or deposit them directly; mailing a check does not meet the next-banking-day standard if delivery takes longer.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The Indiana Real Estate Commission has broad authority to discipline licensees under IC 25-34.1-3-9. Penalties for earnest money violations include license suspension, license revocation, fines up to $10,000 per violation, and mandatory completion of additional education. The Commission can also issue cease-and-desist orders and require restitution to harmed parties.

Beyond regulatory penalties, agents face civil liability. A buyer whose earnest money is lost due to commingling or mishandling can sue the agent and the brokerage for breach of fiduciary duty. Indiana courts have consistently held that the broker bears vicarious liability for agent misconduct involving trust funds, regardless of whether the broker had actual knowledge of the violation.

| Violation | Potential Consequence |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Late deposit (876 IAC 8-2-4) | Fine, suspension, or revocation |
| Commingling funds (IC 25-34.1-3-9) | Revocation, civil suit, restitution |
| Unauthorized disbursement | Fine up to $10,000, license action |
| Failure to maintain records (876 IAC 8-2-3) | Audit failure, fine, probation |
| Conversion of trust funds | Criminal theft charges, permanent revocation |

Criminal exposure is real. Converting escrow funds to personal use constitutes theft under Indiana criminal law (IC 35-43-4-2), and prosecutors have charged agents in cases involving amounts as low as $2,000. The reputational damage alone—public records, local media, MLS gossip—effectively ends an agent's market presence.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Earnest Money in Indiana

**Mistake one: holding the check "until inspection."** Some buyer's agents tell their clients they will not deposit the earnest money check until after the inspection contingency clears. This directly violates 876 IAC 8-2-4 unless the purchase agreement explicitly states a delayed deposit. The standard IAR Form 46 does not include delayed-deposit language by default, so agents who hold checks are operating outside the contract and the law simultaneously.

Mistake two: depositing into the wrong account. Agents who work across multiple brokerages or who recently switched firms occasionally deposit earnest money into a former broker's trust account or, worse, a personal business account. Even an honest mistake triggers a commingling violation because the funds were never under the supervision of the correct principal broker.

Mistake three: releasing funds on verbal authorization. Indiana requires written mutual consent for earnest money disbursement. An agent who releases funds based on a phone call from the other party's agent—without a signed release or court order—exposes the brokerage to liability even if the verbal instruction was legitimate. The written-release requirement protects everyone in the chain.

Mistake four: failing to document the deposit date. Agents who deposit earnest money on time but fail to record the deposit date in the transaction file create an audit vulnerability. When IREC audits the brokerage, the burden falls on the broker to prove compliance. No timestamp means no proof, and the Commission presumes noncompliance in ambiguous cases.

Mistake five: ignoring wire fraud protocols when receiving electronic earnest money. Indiana has seen a rise in business email compromise schemes targeting escrow funds. Agents who do not independently verify wire instructions—using a known phone number, not one provided in the suspect email—risk facilitating fraud and face negligence claims from buyers who lose their deposits. ## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in Indiana carry the ultimate liability for trust account compliance. IREC holds the principal broker responsible under IC 25-34.1-3-5 for supervising all licensees affiliated with the brokerage, including their handling of client funds. A broker who delegates deposit responsibility to agents without verification systems is setting up a future enforcement action.

**Brokers should audit trust accounts monthly at minimum, reconciling bank statements against the transaction ledger.** Indiana does not mandate a specific audit frequency in statute, but the Commission's published guidance recommends monthly reconciliation. Any discrepancy—even $50—must be investigated and resolved before the next reconciliation period.

Key enforcement items for brokers include confirming that every accepted offer has a corresponding deposit entry within the required timeframe, verifying that disbursement occurs only upon written authorization from all parties, and ensuring that inactive funds (deals that fell through months ago) are not languishing in the account. Stale funds invite complaints and signal poor internal controls.

Brokers should also maintain a written office policy specifying who is authorized to make deposits, who has check-signing authority, and how agents must report received earnest money. This policy should be signed annually by every affiliated agent. When disputes arise, brokers who can produce a signed policy demonstrate good-faith compliance efforts to the Commission—often the difference between a warning letter and formal charges.

## Earnest Money Disputes: The Indiana Interpleader Process

When a transaction collapses and both parties claim the earnest money, Indiana law does not give the broker discretion to decide who gets the funds. The broker must either obtain mutual written release or file an interpleader action in court. IC 34-57-2 governs interpleader proceedings in Indiana, allowing the broker to deposit the disputed funds with the court and request discharge from further liability.

The interpleader process costs money—filing fees, attorney fees, and time. Many brokers build this risk into their office policies by requiring agents to use the IAR Earnest Money Dispute form immediately upon receiving conflicting demands. Delay in filing interpleader can itself create liability if one party argues the broker should have acted sooner.

Agents sometimes try to avoid disputes by releasing funds to whichever party "clearly" deserves them based on contract language. This shortcut is dangerous. Even when the contract seems unambiguous, the other party may disagree, and an unauthorized disbursement exposes the brokerage to a claim regardless of the broker's good intentions. The safe path is always written mutual consent or court interpleader.

## Staying Compliant Under Indiana Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

Indiana's regulatory environment rewards documentation and punishes assumptions. Every deposit, every authorization, and every disbursement must be provable in writing. Agents who treat earnest money handling as a clerical afterthought will eventually face a complaint that could have been prevented with basic discipline.

The 2026 landscape for Indiana earnest money & escrow laws 2026 remains grounded in the same statutory framework that has governed the state for years, but enforcement attention is increasing as consumer complaints rise and [deadline-tracking failures](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) become more visible. Agents and brokers who build compliance into their daily workflow—rather than scrambling after a problem surfaces—protect both their licenses and their clients.
