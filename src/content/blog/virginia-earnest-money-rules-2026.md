## Understanding Virginia Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

Mishandling earnest money in Virginia does not simply upset a client—it can end a career. The Virginia Real Estate Board (VREB) treats escrow violations as among the most serious infractions a licensee can commit, and the consequences have only tightened heading into 2026. Every agent and broker operating under the Virginia Real Estate Board's jurisdiction needs a working command of **Virginia earnest money & escrow laws 2026** to protect their license and their clients' funds.

Virginia's statutory framework for escrow sits primarily in the Code of Virginia, Title 54.1, Chapter 21, with the operative sections being 54.1-2108 and the regulations found in 18 VAC 135-20-180 through 135-20-210. These are not suggestions. They carry the force of law, and VREB investigators audit escrow accounts without warning.

The Virginia Association of Realtors (VAR) contract suite—especially Form 600 (the Residential Contract of Purchase) and Form 601 (the standard addenda)—builds the statutory requirements into fill-in-the-blank fields that, if completed incorrectly, expose agents to both regulatory and civil liability. Knowing the statutes is non-negotiable; knowing how they map onto the forms you use daily is what separates compliant agents from those answering Board complaints.

## Deposit Deadlines and Delivery Requirements

**Virginia Code Section 54.1-2108 mandates that earnest money deposits be placed in an escrow account by the end of the fifth business banking day following ratification, unless the contract states otherwise.** That "unless" clause matters. If you write a different deadline into VAR Form 600's Paragraph 4, that contractual term controls—but it cannot conflict with the five-day statutory maximum for delivering funds to the escrow holder.

The deposit must land in a federally insured depository institution. Personal holding, desk-drawer storage, or any delay beyond the deadline triggers an automatic violation. The clock starts on the date the last party signs and the contract becomes binding—not the date the agent receives the check.

Wire transfers count as received on the date the funds clear. Personal checks count as deposited when the escrow holder physically deposits them, not when they clear. This distinction trips up agents who assume a bounced check creates no violation if it was deposited on time—it does create liability, just a different kind.

## Who May Hold Escrow Funds in Virginia

Virginia permits three categories of escrow holders: the listing broker, the selling (buyer's) broker, or a licensed settlement agent such as a title company or attorney. The choice is documented in VAR Form 600, Paragraph 4(a). Whichever entity is named bears full fiduciary responsibility for those funds.

| Escrow Holder | Common Scenario | Key Obligation |
|---|---|---|
| Listing Broker | Broker's own listings, smaller firms | Maintain separate escrow account per 18 VAC 135-20-185 |
| Buyer's Broker | When buyer's side negotiates control | Same account requirements; must notify listing side of receipt |
| Settlement Agent | Title company or attorney | Governed by Virginia Consumer Real Estate Settlement Protection Act (CRESPA) |
| Third-Party Escrow | Court-ordered or agreed neutral | Must still meet VREB account standards if a licensee is involved |

A broker cannot commingle escrow funds with operating funds. Period. The escrow account must be labeled as such, and the broker must be able to produce a reconciliation report at any time. VREB conducts random and complaint-triggered audits under 18 VAC 135-20-190, and a single commingling finding can result in license revocation.

## Virginia Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026: Dispute Resolution and Release

When a transaction falls apart, the earnest money becomes the most contested asset in the deal. Virginia's approach is codified in 54.1-2108(D): when both parties agree in writing to the disbursement, the escrow holder releases the funds. When they do not agree, the holder must retain the deposit and may not unilaterally release it to either side.

**If no written agreement is reached within 180 days of the demand for release, the escrow holder may petition the circuit court for an interpleader action to deposit the funds with the court.** This is not optional generosity—it is the statutory escape valve. Agents who release funds based on verbal instructions, a single party's demand, or their own interpretation of who "deserves" the money are committing a VREB violation.

VAR Form 600, Paragraph 21, addresses default. It specifies that if the buyer defaults, the deposit may be retained by the seller as liquidated damages—but only if both parties initial that provision at contract formation. Without those initials, the default clause does not activate automatically, and the dispute resolution process above kicks in.

The practical takeaway: document every communication about deposit release in writing. Use VAR Form 905 (Release of Deposit) to memorialize any agreed disbursement. Never rely on text messages or phone conversations alone.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Earnest Money

Knowing the law in theory is different from applying it under the pressure of a live transaction. These are the errors VREB investigators see most frequently in disciplinary files.

**First, agents routinely miscalculate the five-business-day deposit window.** They count calendar days instead of banking days, or they start the clock from contract execution rather than ratification. Ratification occurs when the last party signs and that acceptance is communicated—not when the agent picks up the signed copy. Missing this distinction by even one day exposes the broker to a Board complaint.

Second, agents accept earnest money checks made out to themselves or their team name rather than the escrow holder named in the contract. A check payable to "Jane Smith Real Estate Group" when the contract names "ABC Title Company" as escrow holder creates an endorsement problem and delays deposit. Some agents attempt to cure this by depositing into their broker's operating account and then cutting a new check—which constitutes commingling.

Third, agents fail to confirm receipt with the other side. Virginia's good-faith dealing standards and the VAR contract both assume that the listing agent will be notified when the deposit arrives. Silence breeds suspicion, and suspicion breeds Board complaints. A simple written confirmation—email is sufficient—on the day of deposit eliminates this entirely.

Fourth, agents do not calendar the deposit deadline as a hard task. They treat it as administrative noise rather than a compliance trigger. One overlooked deadline on a Friday before a Monday holiday can turn a five-day window into a violation. This is exactly the kind of date-sensitive compliance step that automated [transaction coordination tools](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) were designed to catch before it becomes a problem.

Fifth, agents misunderstand their role during disputes. When a buyer and seller disagree about the deposit, agents sometimes advocate for their client's "right" to the funds and pressure the escrow holder to release. This exposes both the agent and the broker to interference claims and potential civil liability.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Virginia carry personal liability for escrow violations committed by their agents. Under 18 VAC 135-20-180, the principal broker is the named custodian of the escrow account and must perform monthly reconciliations. This is not delegable in the regulatory sense—even if a bookkeeper performs the reconciliation, the broker signs off and bears responsibility for errors.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Regulatory Source |
|---|---|---|
| Bank reconciliation of escrow account | Monthly, within 30 days of statement | 18 VAC 135-20-190 |
| Trial balance of individual deposits | Monthly | 18 VAC 135-20-190 |
| Confirmation that deposits were timely | Per transaction | 54.1-2108 |
| Review of deposit receipts and form accuracy | Per transaction | Broker supervisory duty, 54.1-2100 |
| Verification of proper payee on checks | Per transaction | 18 VAC 135-20-185 |

**Brokers who fail to reconcile monthly face a presumption of negligence in any VREB proceeding.** The Board's investigative staff has stated publicly that an unreconciled account is treated as a red flag warranting deeper audit, regardless of whether actual mishandling occurred.

Beyond the numbers, brokers should enforce a written office policy specifying which agents may accept deposits, how deposits must be transmitted to the escrow holder, and what documentation must be retained. A signed acknowledgment from each agent that they have read and understood this policy creates an affirmative defense if one agent goes rogue.

Brokers should also track whether agents are using current VAR forms. Outdated versions of Form 600 may reference superseded statutory language or omit updated disclosure paragraphs, which can create ambiguity in the escrow terms. Form version control is a supervisory duty that VREB expects brokers to exercise. For agents who manage high transaction volumes, the risk multiplies—our analysis of [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) shows that deposit-related errors cluster in the first 72 hours after ratification, precisely when agents are juggling the most tasks.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

VREB's disciplinary authority comes from the Code of Virginia Section 54.1-2105, which permits fines, mandatory education, license suspension, and license revocation. For escrow-specific violations, the Board's sanction schedule (published in its Disciplinary Proceedings Guidelines) typically starts at a $1,000 fine for a first-offense late deposit and escalates to $2,500 or suspension for repeat violations or commingling.

Civil liability runs parallel. A buyer whose deposit was mishandled can sue the agent, the broker, and the escrow holder for breach of fiduciary duty. Virginia courts have awarded both actual damages and attorney's fees in cases where the escrow holder's negligence caused a deal to collapse and the buyer lost a favorable interest rate lock or the property itself.

Criminal exposure is rare but not impossible. Conversion of escrow funds—using client money for personal expenses—constitutes embezzlement under Virginia Code Section 18.2-111. Felony charges carry up to 20 years of imprisonment for amounts exceeding $1,000.

The [Virginia Real Estate Board's website](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov/Boards/Real-Estate) publishes recent disciplinary orders, and agents should review them quarterly to understand current enforcement trends.

## Practical Compliance Workflow for 2026

A defensible escrow workflow in Virginia requires three non-negotiable elements: a calendared deposit deadline tied to the ratification date, a written confirmation loop between both sides and the escrow holder, and immediate documentation filed to the transaction record.

Start with ratification. The moment you have a binding contract, calculate five business banking days forward, excluding federal holidays and weekends. Enter that date as a hard deadline in whatever system you use—your CRM, your TC's task board, or a platform like [Brittani AI](https://brittani.ai/pricing) that flags compliance deadlines automatically based on your state's rules.

Next, confirm deposit. When the escrow holder receives and deposits the funds, obtain written confirmation (an email from the settlement agent or broker's bookkeeper is standard) and forward it to all parties. Attach the confirmation to the transaction file.

Finally, retain the paper trail. Keep copies of the check (front and back), the deposit slip or wire confirmation, the escrow holder's acknowledgment, and any VAR Form 905 if the deposit is later released or disputed. Virginia's record retention requirement under 18 VAC 135-20-170 mandates that transaction records be kept for a minimum of three years after closing or termination.

The agents who treat **Virginia earnest money & escrow laws 2026** as a background concern are the same agents who appear in VREB disciplinary orders. Build escrow compliance into your transaction launch sequence, audit it monthly, and you remove one of the most preventable risks in residential real estate.
