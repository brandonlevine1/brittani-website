## Why Michigan Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026 Demand Your Attention

The Michigan lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 affect a massive portion of the state's housing stock. According to Census data, roughly 65 percent of Michigan's residential properties were built before 1978 — the year the federal government banned lead-based paint for residential use. That means nearly two-thirds of every listing appointment you walk into triggers a federal disclosure obligation that carries real legal teeth.

This is not a state-specific statute you can blame on Lansing. The requirement originates from federal law: the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018), codified at [42 U.S.C. 4852d](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/4852d) and enforced by EPA regulations at 40 CFR Part 745. Michigan does not layer additional state-level lead paint disclosure mandates on top of the federal framework, but the Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA) considers federal compliance a condition of professional licensure conduct under the Occupational Code (MCL 339.2512).

Agents who treat this as a formality — just another box to check — are the ones most likely to face enforcement actions, deal cancellations, or six-figure lawsuits from buyers whose children test positive for elevated blood lead levels.

## The Federal Framework That Governs Michigan Transactions

**Every agent acting as a seller's representative or buyer's representative in a pre-1978 property transaction has independent legal obligations under 40 CFR 745.107.** You are not merely a witness to the seller's disclosure. You are a regulated party with your own compliance duties.

The law requires three things before a buyer is obligated under a purchase agreement. First, the seller must disclose any known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards in the property. Second, the seller must provide the buyer with any available reports or records regarding lead in the property. Third, the buyer must receive a copy of the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home."

Agents on both sides must ensure that these steps occur, that the proper form is signed by all parties, and that the buyer receives the mandatory 10-day inspection opportunity. The regulation explicitly states that the agent's duty exists independently — meaning "my seller refused" is not a defense that protects your license.

## The Specific Forms Michigan Agents Must Use

Michigan does not have a state-promulgated lead paint disclosure form with a unique state form number. Instead, agents in Michigan overwhelmingly use one of two standard forms for compliance.

The first is the Michigan Realtors form titled "Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (Sale)" — cataloged in the Michigan Realtors forms library and included in standard transaction packages through TransactionDesk and other platforms used by Michigan MLS boards. The second option is the generic EPA/HUD "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form, which the federal government provides as a sample in 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F, Appendix A.

| Form Element | Required Content |
|---|---|
| Seller's Disclosure Statement | Known presence of lead paint, known hazards, or statement of no knowledge |
| Records and Reports | List of all available records provided to buyer, or statement that none exist |
| Buyer's Acknowledgment | Confirmation of receipt of EPA pamphlet and any records |
| Inspection Opportunity | Buyer's election to conduct or waive the 10-day inspection period |
| Agent Certification | Both agents confirm compliance with 42 U.S.C. 4852d requirements |
| Signatures and Date | All parties and agents must sign and date |

The form must be attached to or incorporated into the purchase agreement. It is not optional addenda — it is a condition precedent to an enforceable contract for pre-1978 housing under federal law.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, Liability, and License Risk

**The penalty structure for lead paint disclosure violations operates at three levels, and agents are individually liable at each one.** This is where Michigan agents frequently underestimate their exposure.

At the federal level, EPA can impose civil penalties up to $27,018 per violation (adjusted for inflation as of 2026). Criminal penalties under 42 U.S.C. 4852d can reach $27,018 per violation per day plus potential imprisonment. HUD and EPA have conducted enforcement sweeps targeting real estate professionals in high-lead states, and Michigan's housing stock profile makes it a priority jurisdiction.

At the state level, LARA can pursue license discipline under MCL 339.2512 for conduct that violates applicable law in the course of real estate practice. A documented failure to ensure lead paint disclosure compliance is grounds for reprimand, fine, license suspension, or revocation. Brokers face additional exposure under MCL 339.2512e for failing to supervise licensees who violate disclosure requirements.

At the civil level, a buyer can rescind the contract, recover treble damages (three times actual damages), court costs, and attorney fees under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3). A single failure to disclose in a property where a child later develops lead poisoning can produce a judgment well into six figures. Michigan courts have allowed these claims to proceed even where the agent claims ignorance of the seller's knowledge.

## Common Mistakes Michigan Agents Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in Michigan enforcement actions, lawsuits, and broker audits. Each one is avoidable with proper systems.

**First, agents collect the disclosure after mutual acceptance instead of at or before the time the buyer signs the purchase agreement.** The regulation at 40 CFR 745.113 requires disclosure before the purchaser is obligated under the contract. A form signed at closing is untimely and does not satisfy the statute. The buyer retains all remedies as if no disclosure occurred.

Second, agents allow sellers to check "unknown" on every field without any follow-up conversation. While sellers have no legal duty to conduct testing, the agent has a duty not to assist in concealment. If you know the seller scraped and repainted last summer to cover peeling paint, checking "unknown" creates fraud exposure for everyone involved.

Third, buyer's agents waive the 10-day inspection period on behalf of their clients without a written, signed waiver from the buyer specifically referencing lead paint. A general inspection contingency waiver does not substitute. The lead paint inspection opportunity is a separate, federally mandated right that requires its own documented election or waiver.

Fourth, agents fail to provide the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" in the buyer's preferred language. The pamphlet is available in multiple languages, and failure to provide it in a language the buyer understands has been cited in enforcement actions.

Fifth, agents do not retain the signed disclosure form for the required three-year period following completion of the transaction. Digital storage counts, but the file must be retrievable. Agents who switch brokerages and lose access to old transaction files create a compliance gap that surfaces during audits. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Michigan carry supervisory liability under both the Occupational Code and federal regulation. A broker who fails to implement reasonable policies ensuring lead paint disclosure compliance cannot hide behind agent independence.

| Broker Responsibility | Audit Frequency | Risk if Missed |
|---|---|---|
| Verify disclosure form in every pre-1978 transaction file | Every transaction at file review | LARA license action, vicarious liability |
| Confirm EPA pamphlet delivery documented | Every transaction at file review | Federal fine exposure |
| Check that buyer inspection election/waiver is signed | Every transaction at file review | Contract rescission risk |
| Retain completed forms for minimum 3 years | Annual records audit | Inability to defend against future claims |
| Train agents on disclosure timing requirements | At onboarding and annually | Pattern-of-negligence claims |

Brokers should build the lead paint disclosure check into their first-touch file review, not the final compliance audit. By the time a file reaches closing, an untimely disclosure cannot be fixed retroactively. The damage is done.

## Rental Transactions and the Overlooked Lease Requirement

Most agents think of lead paint disclosure only in the context of sales. However, 40 CFR 745, Subpart F applies equally to lessors and their agents for pre-1978 rental housing. Michigan agents who handle property management or assist landlord clients with lease-up of older properties carry the same disclosure obligations.

The lessor must disclose known lead paint or hazards, provide available records, and deliver the EPA pamphlet before the lessee is obligated under the lease. The form is substantially similar to the sales version, with language adjusted for the lease context. Michigan Realtors provides a lease-specific version in its forms library.

Agents who treat their rental transactions as lower-stakes paperwork exercises are exposing themselves to the same federal penalty structure that applies to sales. EPA does not distinguish between a $150,000 home sale and a $1,200-per-month lease when calculating per-violation fines.

## Exemptions: When the Disclosure Does Not Apply

Not every transaction involving a pre-1978 property triggers the disclosure. The regulation at 40 CFR 745.101 carves out specific exemptions that Michigan agents should know.

Properties confirmed to be lead-free by a certified inspector are exempt. Foreclosure sales are exempt. Housing for the elderly or persons with disabilities is exempt if no child under six resides or is expected to reside there. Zero-bedroom units (studios, lofts, dormitories) are exempt. Short-term leases of 100 days or fewer are exempt.

The exemption must be documented. If you are relying on a certified inspection to claim exemption, that inspection report should be in your transaction file. A verbal assurance from the seller that "an inspector cleared it years ago" without documentation does not satisfy the regulation.
