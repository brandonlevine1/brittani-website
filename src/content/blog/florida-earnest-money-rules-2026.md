## Understanding Florida Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

Florida earnest money & escrow laws 2026 carry real teeth, and mishandling a deposit remains one of the fastest ways for an agent—or an entire brokerage—to face disciplinary action from the Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR). The governing framework sits primarily in Chapter 475, Florida Statutes, supplemented by Rule 61J2 of the Florida Administrative Code. Every licensee operating in the state must know these rules cold, because the consequences extend well beyond losing a commission.

The Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) treats escrow violations as a matter of public trust. A single late deposit or an unreported dispute can trigger an investigation that results in fines up to $5,000 per violation, license suspension, or outright revocation. Agents who assume their brokerage "handles all that" are gambling with their careers.

## Statutory Framework: The Rules That Govern Every Deposit

**Florida Statute 475.25(1)(d) is the statute every licensee should bookmark.** It defines the duties surrounding escrow funds and outlines the four dispute-resolution options a broker must follow when a transaction collapses and both parties claim the deposit. Those options are mediation, binding arbitration, interpleader action in circuit court, or requesting a FREC escrow disbursement order (EDO).

Rule 61J2-14.008, F.A.C., establishes the deposit timeline. Once the brokerage receives earnest money, it must be placed into the escrow account by the end of the third business day following receipt—unless the purchase contract specifies otherwise. Missing this window is treated the same as conversion of funds, regardless of intent.

Rule 61J2-14.010 requires brokers to reconcile escrow accounts monthly and to retain those reconciliation records for at least five years. FREC auditors pull these records routinely, and gaps in documentation alone can lead to a formal complaint.

## Key Deadlines and Deposit Amounts in Practice

Florida's Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (FAR/BAR "As-Is" Contract, Form ASIS-6, and the standard FAR/BAR Contract, Form CR-6) both contain specific fields for earnest money amounts and deposit deadlines. Agents fill in the amount, the escrow agent's name, and the deadline by which additional deposits—if any—are due.

| Requirement | Standard Timeline | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Initial deposit to escrow | 3 business days after brokerage receipt | Rule 61J2-14.008 |
| Additional deposit (if contracted) | Per contract terms (commonly 10-14 days) | FAR/BAR CR-6 / ASIS-6 |
| Broker dispute notification to FREC | 15 business days after last demand | F.S. 475.25(1)(d) |
| Monthly escrow reconciliation | By end of following month | Rule 61J2-14.010 |
| Record retention for escrow docs | 5 years | Rule 61J2-14.012 |

Agents often confuse "receipt by the brokerage" with "receipt by the agent personally." Under FREC interpretation, the clock starts when the brokerage—meaning any authorized representative, including the sales associate—takes physical or constructive possession. Waiting to hand it to the office manager on Monday does not pause the deadline.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Earnest Money

Errors in escrow handling are rarely malicious, but FREC does not require intent to find a violation. The following five mistakes surface repeatedly in disciplinary cases published in the DBPR's enforcement records.

**Holding the check "until inspection" without contract authorization.** Some agents tell buyers they will hold the deposit until the inspection period passes. Unless the contract explicitly authorizes delayed delivery to escrow, this violates Rule 61J2-14.008. The deposit must go into escrow within three business days regardless of contingency status.

Writing the deposit check to the agent personally instead of to the escrow holder named in the contract is another frequent issue. Even when the agent intends to transfer it immediately, a check made payable to an individual licensee rather than the brokerage or title company creates an audit trail that suggests mishandling. FREC has cited licensees for this even when funds were ultimately deposited correctly.

Failing to document a buyer's request for return of deposit after a contingency failure creates a he-said-she-said situation that can escalate into a formal dispute. Agents should always obtain written release instructions and use the FAR/BAR Release and Cancellation of Contract form to memorialize the agreement. Without it, the broker faces the 15-business-day dispute-notification clock the moment either party makes a conflicting demand.

A fourth mistake is depositing funds into the wrong account—an operating account rather than the designated trust or escrow account. Even a same-day correction can trigger a complaint if discovered during audit. The fifth: failing to notify the broker of record promptly when a dispute arises, which delays the broker's ability to meet the 15-business-day FREC notification requirement.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

FREC's disciplinary guidelines, outlined in Rule 61J2-24.001, assign penalty ranges based on the severity of the violation and prior disciplinary history. A first-time failure to deposit earnest money within the required timeframe typically results in a fine between $250 and $1,000, plus an administrative reprimand on the licensee's record.

Repeated offenses or evidence of conversion—using deposit funds for personal purposes—escalate to suspension (up to 10 years) or permanent revocation. The brokerage's license is equally at risk if the broker knew or should have known about the violation. FREC has historically held brokers to a higher standard of oversight, meaning ignorance is not a defense.

Beyond FREC penalties, agents face civil liability. A buyer whose deposit is lost or improperly withheld can sue for breach of fiduciary duty and seek damages that exceed the deposit amount itself. Deal cancellation also becomes far more likely when the opposing party discovers escrow mishandling, because trust—the currency that holds transactions together—evaporates instantly. Understanding [where deals typically break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) helps agents recognize that escrow errors are preventable, not inevitable.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

**Broker responsibility under Florida law is non-delegable.** A broker cannot assign escrow compliance to an office manager and then claim ignorance when FREC comes knocking. Chapter 475.25(1)(k) specifically holds the broker responsible for failure to maintain trust funds properly, even if the error originated with a sales associate.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Regulatory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Escrow account reconciliation | Monthly | Rule 61J2-14.010 |
| Verification of 3-day deposit compliance | Per transaction | Rule 61J2-14.008 |
| Written escrow dispute log | Ongoing | F.S. 475.25(1)(d) |
| Review of payee names on deposit instruments | Per transaction | Best practice / FREC guidance |
| Retention of release and cancellation forms | 5 years minimum | Rule 61J2-14.012 |

Brokers should implement a system that flags deposits not confirmed within 48 hours of contract execution. Waiting until the third business day to even check introduces unnecessary risk. A digital tracking system—or a transaction coordinator tasked specifically with deposit verification—reduces exposure. For agents managing [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), the risk of missing a deposit deadline increases with every new contract signed in the same week.

Quarterly internal audits that mirror FREC's own examination procedures will catch discrepancies before they become complaints. FREC's [official escrow account requirements](https://www.myfloridalicense.com/intentions2.asp?chession=&catid=7&divid=2) page details what examiners look for, and brokers who replicate that checklist internally stay ahead of enforcement actions.

## Dispute Resolution: The Four Paths Under Florida Law

When a transaction fails and both buyer and seller claim the earnest money deposit, the broker cannot simply decide who gets it. Florida Statute 475.25(1)(d) requires the broker to notify FREC within 15 business days of receiving the last conflicting demand and then pursue one of four resolution methods.

Mediation is the least adversarial path and keeps costs low for both parties. Arbitration produces a binding result but requires prior written agreement from both parties—typically included in the FAR/BAR contract's dispute resolution clause. Interpleader means the broker files a court action asking a judge to determine who receives the funds; the broker deposits the money with the court and is released from further liability. The FREC EDO process allows the commission itself to order disbursement after reviewing evidence from both sides.

**Agents who try to resolve escrow disputes informally—without notifying the broker or triggering the formal process—expose themselves to separate disciplinary action for failing to follow statutory procedure.** The intent may be good, but the law does not reward improvisation here. ## The Role of Title Companies and Attorney Escrow

Florida is an attorney-closing state in many counties, and title companies frequently serve as the escrow agent named in the contract rather than the listing brokerage. When a third-party escrow agent holds the deposit, the brokerage's liability shifts—but it does not disappear entirely.

The listing agent still has a duty to confirm that the deposit was delivered to the named escrow agent within the contract's stated timeline. If the buyer's agent holds the check for four days before delivering it to the title company, and the listing agent fails to follow up, both agents may share culpability in a complaint. Passive reliance on the other side of the transaction is a pattern FREC sees frequently in enforcement files.

When the brokerage itself holds escrow, the broker must maintain a separate trust account—or a clearly designated portion of a trust account—that is never commingled with operating funds. Even interest earned on escrow deposits must be handled according to the contract terms; Florida allows interest-bearing escrow accounts, but the contract must specify who receives the interest.
