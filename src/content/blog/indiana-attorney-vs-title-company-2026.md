## Indiana Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Definitive Answer

The question of Indiana attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 comes up in nearly every transaction cycle, and for good reason. Indiana sits in a distinct category among U.S. states: it does not require attorney involvement at closing, yet attorneys routinely participate, particularly in commercial deals and complex residential sales. Understanding the legal framework is not optional for agents who want to avoid liability exposure and keep deals on schedule.

Indiana is what practitioners call a "title state," meaning title insurance companies and their authorized closing agents handle the bulk of residential closings. Unlike states such as Georgia, South Carolina, or New York, Indiana imposes no statutory mandate that a licensed attorney conduct settlement, review documents, or supervise the closing table. The Indiana Professional Licensing Agency (IPLA) and the Indiana Department of Insurance share regulatory oversight depending on whether the question involves agent conduct or title company operations.

## The Statutory Framework Governing Closings

**Indiana Code Title 27, Article 7 (IC 27-7-3) establishes the authority of title insurance companies to issue policies, conduct searches, and prepare certain closing documents.** This statute grants title insurers and their authorized agents the ability to prepare deeds, mortgages, and settlement statements in connection with issuing a title insurance policy without running afoul of unauthorized practice of law (UPL) prohibitions.

The Indiana Supreme Court retains inherent authority over UPL matters. In the landmark case of *In re Decker*, the Court clarified that document preparation incidental to title insurance issuance does not constitute the practice of law. However, independent legal advice — such as counseling a party on contract terms, interpreting contingency clauses, or advising on tax consequences — remains squarely within attorney territory.

IC 25-34.1, the Indiana Real Estate License Act, defines the boundaries of what licensed real estate agents and brokers may do. Agents may fill in blanks on pre-approved IAR (Indiana Association of Realtors) forms but may not draft custom legal provisions or advise on legal rights. The relevant forms include the IAR Purchase Agreement (Form 100), the Seller's Residential Real Estate Sales Disclosure (State Form 46234), and the various addenda numbered in the IAR library.

## Who Actually Sits at the Table: A Comparison

The following table breaks down who performs what role at a typical Indiana residential closing:

| Function | Title Company/Closing Agent | Attorney | Real Estate Agent |
|----------|----------------------------|----------|-------------------|
| Conduct title search | Yes (IC 27-7-3) | Yes | No |
| Prepare deed | Yes (incidental to policy) | Yes | No |
| Prepare settlement statement | Yes | Yes | No |
| Provide legal advice to parties | No | Yes | No |
| Witness/notarize signatures | Yes (if commissioned) | Yes (if commissioned) | Yes (if commissioned) |
| Disburse funds from escrow | Yes | Yes (if holding escrow) | No |

This division matters because agents who blur these lines face disciplinary complaints before the IPLA and potential civil liability. The Indiana Real Estate Commission, operating under the IPLA, investigates complaints under 876 IAC 1-1 and can suspend or revoke licenses for conduct that constitutes UPL or misrepresentation of authority.

## When Attorneys Are Practically Necessary (Even If Not Legally Required)

While no statute mandates attorney presence, several transaction types in Indiana practically demand one. Estate sales where the personal representative holds limited authority, short sales requiring lender negotiation, and transactions involving boundary disputes or title defects all benefit from attorney involvement. Agents who attempt to "handle it themselves" in these situations expose their brokerage to errors and omissions claims.

**The Indiana Supreme Court's [Rules of Professional Conduct](https://www.in.gov/courts/rules/prof-conduct/) define what constitutes legal practice, and agents must understand that even well-intentioned guidance can cross the line.** Explaining the legal effect of a contingency removal, for instance, is legal advice. Telling a client when a deadline occurs on a form is factual communication. The distinction matters enormously when a deal goes sideways and someone files a complaint.

Brokerages in northern Indiana counties — Lake, Porter, LaPorte — historically see higher attorney involvement at closings due to local custom inherited from Illinois practice patterns. Southern and central Indiana closings overwhelmingly use title companies exclusively. Neither approach is wrong; both comply with state law.

## Common Mistakes Indiana Agents Make at Closing

**Mistake one: drafting custom language in the "Additional Provisions" section of IAR Form 100 that constitutes legal drafting.** Agents frequently insert sentences about property condition warranties, liability releases, or repair obligations that go beyond filling in blanks. The IPLA has issued guidance letters confirming that creating new contractual obligations — rather than selecting from pre-approved options — risks a UPL finding.

Mistake two: telling clients they do not need an attorney when the situation clearly warrants one. This advice itself can constitute a legal opinion. The safer approach is to inform clients of their right to retain counsel without expressing a position on whether they should.

Mistake three: directing clients to a specific title company in exchange for marketing support, office space, or referral fees. This violates RESPA Section 8 (12 U.S.C. 2607), which carries penalties of up to $10,000 and one year imprisonment per violation. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau enforces RESPA, and [affiliated business arrangement disclosures](/blog/where-deals-break-down) must be provided whenever a referral relationship exists.

Mistake four: assuming the title company will catch all contract deadline issues. Title companies prepare documents and facilitate closing; they do not monitor contingency deadlines, financing commitments, or inspection periods on behalf of agents. When a deadline passes unnoticed and a deal collapses, the listing or buyer's agent typically bears responsibility. Building systematic [deadline tracking processes](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) is not optional.

Mistake five: attending closing and offering opinions on document content. Agents present at closing sometimes explain deed language, loan terms, or proration calculations to clients. Unless the agent is also a licensed attorney, this conduct risks a UPL complaint from the Indiana Supreme Court's Disciplinary Commission.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability under IC 25-34.1-4-10 for the acts of their sponsored agents. This means a single agent's UPL violation or RESPA infraction can generate brokerage-level consequences including fines, license suspension, and civil judgments.

Every Indiana brokerage should maintain a written policy specifying which closing tasks agents may and may not perform. The policy should explicitly address: who may recommend closing providers, what disclosures must accompany any affiliated business arrangement, and what language agents are permitted to insert into transaction documents. Annual audits of closed transaction files should check for custom language in addenda, missing affiliated business disclosures, and evidence of agents providing legal opinions in writing.

| Audit Item | What to Look For | Consequence of Failure |
|------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Affiliated Business Disclosure | Signed ABA form in every file where agent referred to affiliated title company | RESPA Section 8 violation; CFPB enforcement action |
| Custom contract language | Non-IAR language in Form 100 addenda | UPL complaint; E&O claim |
| Attorney recommendation documentation | Written disclosure that attorney consultation is the client's right | IPLA disciplinary action |
| Closing attendance notes | Agent comments on legal document content | UPL finding; license discipline |
| Deadline compliance records | Evidence of systematic tracking for all contingency periods | Breach of fiduciary duty claim |

Brokers should also verify that their E&O insurance policies cover UPL allegations, as many standard policies exclude claims arising from activities outside the scope of a real estate license. A coverage gap here can turn a minor complaint into a catastrophic financial event for the brokerage.

## The Role of the Indiana Department of Insurance

Title companies operating in Indiana must be licensed by the Indiana Department of Insurance under IC 27-7. The Department regulates title insurance rates, agent licensing, and escrow account management. When a closing agent mishandles funds or fails to record documents properly, the Department has authority to revoke the agent's title insurance license and impose civil penalties.

For real estate agents, this regulatory structure means that verifying your closing partner's active licensure is a baseline duty. The Department maintains a public search tool for licensed title insurance agents and companies. Sending a client to an unlicensed entity — even unknowingly — creates liability exposure that no E&O policy will cover.

**Agents should also understand that Indiana's title insurance rates are not regulated as strictly as in some states, meaning closing costs can vary significantly between providers.** Providing clients with multiple options rather than a single recommendation protects both the client's interests and the agent's RESPA compliance posture.
