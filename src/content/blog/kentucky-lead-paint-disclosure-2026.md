## Understanding Kentucky Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

Every year, agents in Kentucky lose time, money, and occasionally their license status over a two-page form that takes five minutes to complete correctly. The Kentucky lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain rooted in the same federal mandate that has applied since 1996, but enforcement actions, penalty amounts, and buyer expectations have all shifted. If you are listing or selling residential property built before 1978, this obligation is non-negotiable.

The federal authority comes from Section 1018 of the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992, commonly called Title X. The implementing regulation is 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F, administered jointly by the EPA and HUD. Kentucky does not have a separate state-level lead paint disclosure statute that overrides or supplements the federal rule, but the Kentucky Real Estate Commission (KREC) holds licensees accountable through its own disciplinary framework under KRS Chapter 324.

**Agents often assume that because this is a "federal" rule, it falls outside the scope of KREC enforcement.** That assumption is wrong. KREC treats a failure to provide required disclosures as a violation of KRS 324.160, which covers dishonest dealing, and it can result in license suspension, probation, or revocation independent of any EPA penalty.

## Which Properties Trigger the Disclosure Obligation

Not every transaction requires a lead paint disclosure. The rule applies exclusively to "target housing," defined as residential dwellings constructed before January 1, 1978. This includes single-family homes, duplexes, condominiums, townhomes, and apartments being sold or leased.

Several categories are exempt. Zero-bedroom dwellings such as studio apartments, lofts, and dormitories fall outside the requirement. Housing exclusively designated for elderly or disabled persons — where children under six are not expected to reside — is also exempt. Foreclosure sales and short sales are not exempt; the disclosure still applies if the property was built before 1978.

Kentucky has a significant inventory of pre-1978 housing. According to U.S. Census data, approximately 38 percent of Kentucky's housing stock was built before 1978, with the highest concentrations in Louisville, Lexington, Covington, and Frankfort. Agents working those markets will encounter this requirement on a near-daily basis, unlike peers in newer suburban developments where pre-1978 homes are rare.

## The Exact Forms and Documents Kentucky Agents Must Use

The primary document is the EPA's "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form. This is the federally prescribed disclosure that sellers and landlords must sign. The Kentucky Association of Realtors (KAR) incorporates this into its transaction packet as Form LBP, which mirrors the EPA language but includes Kentucky-specific signature blocks and agent acknowledgment lines.

| Document | Source | Purpose | Required Signature(s) |
|----------|--------|---------|----------------------|
| EPA Disclosure Form (Lead-Based Paint) | EPA / HUD | Disclose known lead hazards and reports | Seller, Buyer, Both Agents |
| KAR Form LBP | Kentucky Association of Realtors | Kentucky-formatted version of EPA disclosure | Seller, Buyer, Both Agents |
| "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" pamphlet | EPA | Inform buyer of lead risks | Buyer acknowledgment of receipt |
| Lead inspection contingency waiver/election | Included in Form LBP | Document buyer's 10-day inspection election | Buyer |

In addition to the disclosure form, sellers must provide buyers with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." The most current version is dated December 2012 and updated with minor corrections through 2024. Agents should confirm they are distributing the current edition, not an outdated photocopy from 2005 that has been circulating in some offices.

**The 10-day inspection opportunity is a federal right that cannot be waived by the seller or the listing agent.** Buyers may choose to waive it themselves, but the option must be presented. This election is documented directly on Form LBP, where the buyer initials either acceptance or waiver of the inspection period.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: What Agents and Brokers Actually Face

Federal penalties under 40 CFR 745.118 can reach $19,507 per violation as of 2025 inflation-adjusted amounts, with that figure expected to hold or increase slightly into 2026. The EPA can pursue civil penalties against sellers, landlords, and their agents individually. Criminal penalties, while rare, can reach $19,507 per day of violation plus imprisonment.

At the state level, KREC treats disclosure failures as potential violations of KRS 324.160(4), which addresses conduct that demonstrates "incompetency or untrustworthiness." Disciplinary outcomes range from a formal reprimand and mandatory continuing education to license suspension or revocation. Agents who have faced KREC complaints over missing lead paint disclosures in the past five years have generally received probationary terms with added CE requirements, though repeat offenders face suspension.

Beyond regulatory penalties, civil liability is the most common real-world consequence. Under federal law, buyers who were not provided the required disclosure can pursue treble damages (three times the amount of actual damages) in court. This means if a buyer spends $15,000 on lead abatement they were never warned about, a court can award $45,000 plus attorney fees.

| Consequence | Authority | Typical Outcome |
|-------------|-----------|----------------|
| Federal civil penalty | EPA, 40 CFR 745.118 | Up to $19,507 per violation |
| Federal criminal penalty | EPA / DOJ | Up to $19,507/day + imprisonment |
| KREC disciplinary action | KRS 324.160 | Reprimand, probation, suspension, revocation |
| Civil lawsuit (buyer vs. seller/agent) | Federal and state courts | Treble damages + attorney fees |
| Contract rescission | Contract law | Buyer may void purchase agreement |

A buyer can also seek contract rescission — meaning the entire deal unwinds. The buyer returns the property, and the seller returns the purchase price. In practice, this scenario occurs most often when significant lead contamination is discovered post-closing and the buyer can demonstrate they would not have proceeded had disclosure been made. Similar liability structures apply in neighboring states, as outlined in resources about [Tennessee lead paint disclosure](/blog/tennessee-lead-paint-disclosure-2026) requirements.

## Common Mistakes Kentucky Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosures

**The first and most frequent error is simply forgetting the form entirely.** This happens most often in FSBO-to-agent conversions, estate sales where family members are unfamiliar with requirements, and investor flip transactions where the seller assumes a renovation eliminates the obligation. A renovated pre-1978 home still requires the disclosure unless every painted surface has been tested and certified lead-free by a licensed inspector.

The second mistake is failing to get all required signatures. Form LBP requires signatures from the seller, the buyer, the listing agent, and the buyer's agent. A missing agent signature does not void the disclosure, but it creates a compliance gap that KREC auditors and E&O carriers will flag. Agents sometimes sign their own line but forget to confirm their cooperating agent has signed the other.

Third, agents frequently mishandle the 10-day inspection period. The federal rule grants buyers 10 calendar days to conduct a lead inspection at their own expense, unless the parties mutually agree to a different timeframe. Some listing agents pressure buyers to waive this period or fail to document the buyer's election. If the buyer's waiver line is blank or unsigned, the inspection period remains open — creating ambiguity about when contingencies actually expire. For agents juggling multiple closings, tracking these overlapping deadlines is where deals fall apart, a problem discussed in detail in our piece on [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

Fourth, agents provide outdated pamphlets. The EPA has revised "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" multiple times since its original 1995 publication. Distributing a superseded version technically fails to satisfy the regulatory requirement, and buyers' attorneys have raised this point in litigation.

Fifth, some agents assume the disclosure obligation transfers entirely to the seller and take no personal responsibility for ensuring compliance. Under 40 CFR 745.115(a)(2), the agent representing the seller is independently required to "ensure compliance" with the disclosure rule. This is not a passive duty — it requires affirmative action to confirm the seller has disclosed all known information and that the buyer received the pamphlet and opportunity to inspect.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under KRS 324.160 and 201 KAR 11:121 for ensuring their agents comply with disclosure requirements. A broker who fails to implement reasonable systems for compliance shares exposure to both KREC discipline and civil liability.

**At minimum, brokers should audit every closed file involving a pre-1978 property for the presence of a fully executed Form LBP with all four signatures.** This audit should happen before the file is archived, not six months later when a complaint arrives. Kentucky's record retention requirement under 201 KAR 11:121 mandates that brokers maintain transaction documents for five years, which means an incomplete disclosure form can surface in an audit years after closing.

Brokers should also verify that their office maintains a current supply of the EPA pamphlet and that agents know where to access it digitally. A quarterly compliance check — reviewing a random sample of pre-1978 transaction files — can catch systemic issues before they become KREC complaints. The EPA's enforcement database shows that multi-agent offices are more likely to face pattern-and-practice investigations when multiple violations are discovered simultaneously.

Training is the other critical element. KREC requires 12 hours of continuing education per renewal cycle for salespersons and 12 hours for brokers, but lead paint disclosure is not a mandatory topic. Brokers who add a 30-minute module on disclosure compliance during office meetings dramatically reduce their exposure. ## The Seller's Duty Versus the Agent's Duty

A common source of confusion is where the seller's obligation ends and the agent's begins. Under the federal rule, the seller must disclose any known lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards, provide available records or reports, and sign the disclosure form. The seller is not required to conduct testing — only to disclose what they already know.

> "Each agent shall ensure compliance with all requirements of this subpart with respect to the seller or lessor represented by that agent." — 40 CFR 745.115(a)(2)

The agent's duty is procedural and supervisory. You must ensure the form is presented, explain its purpose if asked, confirm all parties sign, deliver the pamphlet, and preserve the 10-day inspection opportunity. You are not responsible for the accuracy of the seller's representations about known hazards — but you are responsible for making sure the seller actually makes those representations on the form.

If you know or have reason to know that the seller is providing false information on the disclosure, your duty shifts. Kentucky licensees are bound by KRS 324.160's prohibition against knowingly making false statements in connection with a real estate transaction. Ignoring a seller's obvious misrepresentation on the lead disclosure form could implicate both the seller and the listing agent.
