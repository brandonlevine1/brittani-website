## Understanding Massachusetts Escrow Regulations 2026

Massachusetts treats escrow obligations more like trust law than simple contract mechanics, and the consequences for getting it wrong can end a career. The rules governing earnest money deposits, escrow account maintenance, and disbursement timelines sit primarily within 254 CMR 7.00, the regulation issued by the Massachusetts Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons. If you hold a license in the Commonwealth, Massachusetts escrow regulations 2026 are not optional reading—they are your fiduciary baseline.

Unlike states that rely heavily on title companies or independent escrow officers, Massachusetts places significant escrow responsibility directly on brokers and the attorneys involved in the transaction. The Board enforces these rules through audits, complaint investigations, and disciplinary hearings that can result in fines, suspension, or revocation. Understanding exactly what the regulations demand—and where agents routinely slip—separates a smooth closing from a Board complaint.

## The Statutory Framework: 254 CMR 7.00 and M.G.L. Chapter 112

**The primary authority governing escrow accounts for Massachusetts real estate licensees is [254 CMR 7.00](https://www.mass.gov/doc/254-cmr-7-standards-of-practice), issued under the enabling statute M.G.L. Chapter 112, Sections 87PP through 87DDD.** These sections define what constitutes a "client trust account," dictate deposit timelines, and spell out record-keeping minimums. The Board of Registration sits within the Division of Professional Licensure and has direct enforcement power.

Under 254 CMR 7.00(3), every broker who receives funds on behalf of a buyer or seller must deposit those funds into a separate, interest-bearing or non-interest-bearing client trust account at a Massachusetts-based, FDIC-insured bank. The regulation explicitly prohibits placing escrow funds in out-of-state accounts, money market funds, or investment vehicles unless all parties consent in writing. This is a departure from some neighboring states that allow broader deposit options.

The deposit deadline is strict: funds must reach the trust account by the close of the next business day after the broker receives the signed purchase and sale agreement. If the preliminary deposit accompanies an offer rather than the P&S, the timeline triggers from the date the offer is accepted and the deposit becomes contractually binding.

## Forms and Documentation Agents Must Know

Massachusetts does not use a single state-mandated escrow receipt form the way some states do. Instead, agents rely on the standard forms published by the Greater Boston Real Estate Board (GBREB) and the Massachusetts Association of Realtors (MAR). The most relevant forms for escrow purposes include:

| Form | Purpose | Issued By |
|------|---------|-----------|
| MAR Standard Purchase and Sale Agreement | Defines deposit amount, holder, and deadline | Massachusetts Association of Realtors |
| GBREB Standard Form P&S | Alternative P&S for eastern Massachusetts markets | Greater Boston Real Estate Board |
| Escrow Receipt (broker-created) | Acknowledges receipt of deposit funds | Individual brokerage |
| 254 CMR 7.00 Trust Account Record | Ledger format for individual transaction tracking | Required by regulation |

There is no single "Form 12" or numbered state receipt. Brokers are required to issue a written acknowledgment of deposit receipt and maintain ledger records showing the date received, amount, source, property address, and eventual disbursement. Many brokerages create their own branded receipt forms, but they must contain—at minimum—the information mandated by 254 CMR 7.00(4).

**The absence of a mandated state form does not reduce liability.** Agents who fail to issue a written receipt or who rely solely on email confirmations without a formal ledger entry expose their broker to Board sanctions. This is one of the most frequent audit findings the Division of Professional Licensure reports in its annual disciplinary summaries.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The consequences for escrow violations in Massachusetts cascade quickly from administrative to financial to criminal. The Board of Registration can initiate disciplinary proceedings on its own or in response to a consumer complaint. Here is how the penalty structure breaks down:

A first-time violation of the deposit timeline—missing the next-business-day deadline—typically results in a letter of reprimand and mandatory continuing education. Repeated violations or documented patterns trigger formal hearings where fines of up to $1,000 per violation can be assessed. License suspension ranges from 30 days to indefinite, depending on severity.

Co-mingling funds—placing escrow deposits into an operating account even temporarily—is treated as a presumptive breach of fiduciary duty. Under M.G.L. Chapter 112, Section 87AAA, the Board may summarily suspend a license pending a hearing when co-mingling is established by documentary evidence. In practice, this means the agent cannot practice from the day the suspension order is issued until the hearing resolves, which can take 60 to 90 days.

Beyond Board discipline, agents face civil liability. If a buyer's deposit is lost due to mishandling—whether through co-mingling, unauthorized disbursement, or broker insolvency—the agent and broker are jointly and severally liable under common-law agency principles. The Massachusetts Real Estate Guaranty Fund (M.G.L. Chapter 112, Section 87AAA1/2) provides a recovery mechanism for consumers, but claims against the fund trigger automatic license revocation for the responsible licensee.

| Violation | Typical Penalty | Statutory Basis |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| Late deposit (first offense) | Reprimand + CE requirement | 254 CMR 7.00(3) |
| Late deposit (repeat) | Fine up to $1,000 + possible suspension | 254 CMR 7.00(3) |
| Co-mingling of funds | Summary suspension pending hearing | M.G.L. c.112 §87AAA |
| Unauthorized disbursement | License revocation + civil liability | 254 CMR 7.00(5) |
| Failure to maintain records | Fine + mandatory audit | 254 CMR 7.00(4) |

Deal cancellation is also a real risk. If a seller discovers that their agent's broker failed to properly escrow the deposit, the seller may claim material breach and terminate the purchase and sale agreement. Courts have upheld this remedy in several Massachusetts appellate decisions, most notably in cases where the deposit was never actually segregated from operating funds.

## Common Mistakes Massachusetts Agents Make

Five errors appear again and again in Board disciplinary records and brokerage audit findings. None of them are exotic. All of them are preventable.

**First, agents deposit checks before the purchase and sale agreement is fully executed.** A preliminary deposit accompanying an offer is not yet escrow—it becomes escrow only when the binding contract is signed. Depositing prematurely creates accounting confusion and potential liability if the offer is rejected and the refund process is delayed. Agents should hold unsigned-offer deposits in the office safe and log them separately until the P&S is executed.

Second, agents rely on their personal bank accounts to "temporarily" hold funds when the office is closed or when they receive a deposit on a weekend. This is textbook co-mingling, regardless of intent. The regulation makes no exception for weekends, holidays, or convenience. The next-business-day rule exists precisely to account for non-banking days.

Third, agents fail to communicate the escrow holder's identity to the buyer in writing. The purchase and sale agreement should name the escrow holder, but in transactions where the holder changes—due to attorney involvement or a switch between the listing broker and the buyer's attorney—agents sometimes neglect to document the change. This creates disputes at closing and can trigger Board complaints.

Fourth, agents neglect to confirm that wire transfers actually arrived. A wire instruction sent is not a deposit completed. Agents who check the box without verifying receipt from the escrow holder expose the transaction to [wire fraud risks](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) and leave the deposit timeline technically unmet.

Fifth, agents assume the attorney "handles everything" and stop tracking the deposit. Even when a transaction attorney holds escrow, the listing broker retains a supervisory obligation under 254 CMR 2.00 to verify that the deposit was received and properly held. Agents who abdicate this responsibility put their broker's license at risk.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Broker liability in Massachusetts is not derivative—it is direct. Under 254 CMR 2.00(7), the broker of record is personally responsible for the maintenance and accuracy of all escrow records, regardless of which agent in the office handled the transaction. This means brokers cannot delegate escrow compliance to office managers or transaction coordinators without maintaining an active oversight system.

**Quarterly trust account reconciliations are the minimum standard the Board expects during an audit.** The reconciliation must compare the bank statement balance against the sum of all individual transaction ledgers, with any discrepancy documented and resolved within 5 business days. Brokers who wait until year-end to reconcile are gambling with their license.

Brokers should also audit deposit timing on a rolling basis. Every escrow receipt should be date-stamped with both the date funds were received and the date they were deposited. A gap exceeding one business day requires an explanation on file—and if the explanation is "the agent forgot," the broker has a supervision problem that the Board will assign to them personally.

The supervisory requirements extend to teams. If a team leader within the brokerage accepts deposits on behalf of team members, the broker must verify that the team leader's practices meet 254 CMR 7.00 independently. The proliferation of teams in Massachusetts metro markets has made this a growing audit focus for the Division of Professional Licensure. Agents operating on teams should review how [team supervision rules](/blog/washington-teams-supervision-rules-2026) function in other states to understand the broader regulatory direction.

Record retention is another audit trigger. Massachusetts requires brokers to retain escrow records for a minimum of three years following the close of a transaction or the termination of the listing agreement—whichever comes later. Records must include copies of deposit checks, wire confirmations, ledger entries, and disbursement authorizations. Digital storage is permitted, but the records must be producible within 48 hours of a Board request.

## Interest-Bearing Accounts and IOLTA Rules

Massachusetts allows but does not require brokers to hold escrow deposits in interest-bearing accounts. When the deposit is nominal or the holding period is short, most brokers use non-interest-bearing trust accounts to avoid the administrative overhead of interest allocation. However, when deposits are substantial—common in the Boston metro market where deposits routinely exceed $50,000—interest-bearing accounts become a fiduciary best practice.

If the broker elects an interest-bearing account and the interest is not designated for a specific party in the purchase and sale agreement, the funds must be deposited into an IOLTA (Interest on Lawyers' Trust Accounts) account administered under Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court Rule 3:07. The interest accrues to the Massachusetts IOLTA Committee, which funds legal aid programs. Brokers who pocket escrow interest without written authorization from all parties violate both 254 CMR 7.00 and potentially M.G.L. Chapter 266 (larceny statutes).

## Disbursement Rules and Dispute Resolution

Disbursement of escrow funds requires written authorization from all parties to the transaction or a court order. The broker cannot release funds based solely on one party's demand, even if that party claims entitlement under the contract. When buyer and seller dispute ownership of the deposit, the broker's obligation is to hold the funds and either obtain mutual written release instructions or interplead the funds with the appropriate Massachusetts court.

Interpleader actions are filed in the Superior Court or District Court depending on the amount in controversy. The broker who interpleads is generally entitled to recover reasonable attorney's fees from the deposited funds, though this must be disclosed in advance. Agents should know that [understanding where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) often means understanding exactly when escrow disputes arise—and having documentation ready before they escalate.

The timeline for disbursement after closing is not explicitly set by regulation, but industry practice and title insurance requirements generally demand same-day or next-day disbursement once the deed is recorded. Holding funds beyond this window without cause exposes the broker to complaints from both the receiving party and their attorney.
