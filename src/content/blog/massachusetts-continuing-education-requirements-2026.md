## Massachusetts Continuing Education Requirements 2026: The Full Breakdown

Failing to meet Massachusetts continuing education requirements 2026 is not a hypothetical problem—it is a tangible risk that results in license suspension, lost commissions, and potential liability in active transactions. The Massachusetts Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons, operating under the Division of Professional Licensure, enforces these rules with little tolerance for agents who miss deadlines or submit incomplete coursework. Every licensed salesperson and broker in the Commonwealth needs to understand exactly what is required, when it is due, and what goes wrong when the requirement is ignored.

Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 112, Section 87XX, along with 254 CMR 7.00, governs the continuing education mandate for real estate licensees. The Board requires 12 hours of approved CE every two-year renewal cycle. This is not a suggestion—it is a condition of active licensure.

## Who Must Comply and What the 12 Hours Must Include

Every individual holding an active salesperson or broker license issued by the Board of Registration must complete CE before renewal. There is no exemption for agents who closed zero transactions during the cycle, nor for those nearing retirement. The only exception applies to licensees in their first renewal period, who may have reduced requirements depending on when their initial license was issued.

**The 12 hours break down into mandated topics and elective hours.** At minimum, 1 hour must cover Massachusetts fair housing law under M.G.L. Chapter 151B, and 1 hour must address agency disclosure obligations under 254 CMR 3.00. The remaining 10 hours can be filled with board-approved elective courses covering topics like contract law, ethics, property management, or new legislation affecting practice.

| Requirement | Hours | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Fair Housing (M.G.L. Ch. 151B) | 1 | Mandatory every cycle |
| Agency Law (254 CMR 3.00) | 1 | Mandatory every cycle |
| Elective Topics | 10 | Must be board-approved |
| Total | 12 | Due before license expiration |

Course providers must be approved by the Board. Taking a course from a non-approved provider—even one approved in another state—does not satisfy the requirement. Agents can verify approved providers through the Division of Professional Licensure's [online licensing portal](https://www.mass.gov/orgs/division-of-professional-licensure).

## Renewal Deadlines and the Biennial Cycle

Massachusetts real estate licenses expire on a rolling biennial schedule, not on a single statewide date. Your specific expiration date was assigned when you first received your license, and it renews every two years from that date. This means two agents in the same brokerage can have entirely different deadlines.

**The Board does not send reminder notices as a legal obligation.** While courtesy emails may arrive, relying on them is a documented cause of missed renewals. Agents must track their own expiration date and submit their renewal application—along with proof of CE completion—before that date passes.

Renewal is handled through the ePLACE portal operated by the Division of Professional Licensure. The renewal fee for salespersons is currently $100, and for brokers it is $150. Late renewals within the grace period carry additional fees and potential restrictions on practice.

## What Happens When You Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance are not abstract. If your license lapses, you are legally prohibited from conducting any real estate brokerage activity. Any transaction you participate in while unlicensed can be voided, and you may face personal liability to clients who suffer damages as a result.

Under 254 CMR 2.00, the Board has authority to impose fines, issue cease-and-desist orders, and refer matters to the Attorney General's office. Agents who practice on an expired license face potential disciplinary action even after they eventually renew. The Board treats unlicensed activity as a serious violation, not an administrative oversight.

If your license has been lapsed for more than two years, you cannot simply complete CE and renew. You must retake the Massachusetts licensing examination and file a new application as if you were obtaining your license for the first time. The cost in time, exam fees, and lost business during this period is substantial—[missing deadlines is a pattern](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) that compounds quickly in real estate.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With CE Compliance

**First, agents frequently confuse Massachusetts-approved courses with courses approved in other states.** A course that satisfies New Hampshire or Connecticut requirements has no bearing on your Massachusetts license unless it has been independently approved by the Massachusetts Board. Even reciprocity agreements between states do not extend to CE course acceptance.

Second, agents wait until the final weeks before expiration to begin their coursework. Twelve hours of education cannot always be completed in a single weekend, particularly if you need specific mandatory topics that are offered on limited schedules. Procrastination leads to panic enrollment in courses that may not count.

Third, some agents fail to retain completion certificates. The Board may audit your CE records at any time, and you are responsible for proving compliance. Providers are required to report completion, but system errors happen. Keep your own copies for a minimum of four years.

Fourth, agents assume that attending a conference or industry event counts toward their CE hours. Unless the specific session is offered by a board-approved provider and carries a course approval number, it does not count regardless of how educational it felt. Always confirm the approval status in writing before registering.

Fifth, brokers operating under a corporation or LLC sometimes forget that their individual license still requires personal CE completion. The entity license does not substitute for or override the individual broker's education obligation under M.G.L. Chapter 112.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory responsibility under 254 CMR 2.01 that extends to verifying their affiliated agents maintain active, compliant licenses. This is not optional oversight—it is a regulatory duty. A broker who permits an unlicensed agent to transact business faces their own disciplinary exposure.

| Broker Duty | Frequency | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Verify agent license status | Before each renewal cycle | 254 CMR 2.01 |
| Confirm CE completion records | Annually recommended | Board best practices |
| Remove lapsed agents from active roster | Immediately upon expiration | 254 CMR 2.00 |
| Maintain agent supervision records | Ongoing | M.G.L. Ch. 112 |

**Brokers should build a tracking calendar that flags each agent's renewal date at least 90 days in advance.** This gives agents adequate warning and gives the brokerage time to suspend an agent's transaction authority if compliance is not confirmed. Waiting until an agent's license has already lapsed creates retroactive liability for deals that closed during the gap.

The Board expects brokers to act as the first line of enforcement. During audits, the Board reviews brokerage records to determine whether supervision was adequate. If an agent practiced on an expired license and the broker did not catch it, the broker faces potential fines and license suspension of their own. ## Massachusetts CE Versus Neighboring States

Agents licensed in multiple New England states sometimes assume that requirements are interchangeable. They are not. Massachusetts has one of the lower hour counts in the region but stricter topic mandates.

| State | CE Hours Per Cycle | Cycle Length | Mandatory Topics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Massachusetts | 12 | 2 years | Fair housing, agency |
| Connecticut | 12 | 2 years | Fair housing, legal updates |
| New Hampshire | 15 | 2 years | Core + electives |
| Rhode Island | 24 | 2 years | Multiple mandated |

The comparatively low hour count in Massachusetts does not mean the state is lenient on enforcement. The Board's smaller volume of required hours makes it easier to audit compliance closely. Do not mistake fewer hours for a relaxed regulatory posture.

For agents considering [license portability](/blog/washington-license-reciprocity-portability-2026) across state lines, understanding each jurisdiction's distinct requirements is non-negotiable. Completing 12 hours in Massachusetts and assuming it covers your other state obligations is a recipe for dual-state compliance failure.
