# Oregon Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026: What Every Agent Must Get Right

Oregon property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 remain one of the most frequent sources of post-closing disputes in the state. A single missed checkbox or an incomplete environmental acknowledgment can expose agents, sellers, and brokerages to liability that dwarfs any commission check. This guide breaks down the exact statutory obligations, the forms you should be using, and the enforcement consequences that follow non-compliance.

## The Statutory Framework Governing Oregon Disclosures

Oregon's disclosure obligations trace back to ORS 105.462 through ORS 105.490. These statutes require sellers of residential property to deliver a written property condition disclosure statement to prospective buyers before or at the time mutual acceptance occurs. The law applies to transactions involving one-to-four residential dwelling units, including condominiums.

**ORS 105.464 specifically enumerates the categories of disclosure required.** These include structural defects, water and sewer systems, insulation and ventilation, hazardous materials, environmental hazards, and land use restrictions. The seller must disclose known material defects — Oregon does not impose a duty to investigate, but the "known or should have known" standard has been interpreted broadly by Oregon courts.

The Oregon Real Estate Agency (OREA) oversees licensee conduct and can impose disciplinary action for disclosure failures under OAR 863-015-0125. Agents should also be familiar with the federal Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act (42 U.S.C. 4852d), which adds a separate layer for homes built before 1978.

## Forms Oregon Agents Must Use in 2026

The Oregon Real Estate Forms (OREF) system provides the standardized documents most brokerages require. For property condition disclosures, agents should know three primary forms inside and out.

| Form | Purpose | When Delivered |
|------|---------|----------------|
| OREF 001 — Seller's Property Disclosure Statement | Discloses known material defects per ORS 105.464 | Before or at mutual acceptance |
| OREF 070 — Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | Federal compliance for pre-1978 homes | Before buyer signs purchase agreement |
| OREF 033 — Property Disclosure Addendum | Supplements or amends the original disclosure | When new information arises |

The Seller's Property Disclosure Statement tracks ORS 105.464 categories closely and asks yes/no/unknown questions about defects, repairs, and environmental conditions. It includes sections on mold, radon, underground storage tanks, and proximity to agricultural or forestry operations — conditions that frequently trigger environmental concerns in rural Oregon markets.

For agents working in neighborhoods near Superfund sites or DEQ-listed contaminated properties, the disclosure statement alone may not suffice. Best practice is to attach relevant DEQ Environmental Cleanup Site Information (ECSI) database records as supplemental documentation, even though the statute does not explicitly mandate this step.

## Environmental Disclosures: Where Oregon Gets Specific

Oregon stands apart from many states because ORS 105.464(1)(h) explicitly requires disclosure of environmental hazards including, but not limited to, lead-based paint, asbestos, radon, formaldehyde, contaminated soil or water, and fuel storage tanks. Many agents treat this section as a simple yes/no exercise and move on. That's a mistake.

**The Oregon Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) maintains the ECSI database, which lists over 5,000 properties with known or suspected contamination.** If a seller's property appears in that database, failure to disclose can be catastrophic. Buyers who discover post-closing that they purchased a property adjacent to — or directly impacted by — a listed contamination site have successfully pursued rescission claims.

Radon is another area where Oregon agents need heightened awareness. The Oregon Health Authority reports that roughly one in three Oregon homes tests above the EPA action level of 4 pCi/L. While sellers are not required to test, they must disclose known test results. Agents who advise sellers to withhold radon test data are exposing themselves to personal liability and potential license revocation.

For agents who also handle [Washington property condition disclosures](/blog/washington-property-condition-disclosures-2026), note that Oregon's environmental categories are more granular and the seller's duty extends to conditions that are "known or reasonably should have been known," a slightly different standard than Washington's actual knowledge requirement.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of disclosure failures in Oregon fall into three categories: civil liability, regulatory discipline, and deal collapse.

Under ORS 105.475, a buyer who does not receive the required disclosure statement — or who receives one containing material misrepresentations — may revoke the offer within five business days of receipt. If the sale has already closed, the buyer can pursue damages including repair costs, diminished property value, and incidental expenses. Oregon courts have awarded attorney fees in cases where sellers or their agents knowingly concealed defects.

On the regulatory side, OREA can suspend or revoke a license under ORS 696.301 if an agent is found to have "knowingly made a material misrepresentation" or failed to exercise reasonable care in a disclosure transaction. Fines can reach $5,000 per violation under OAR 863-015-0005. Repeated offenses or egregious concealment can result in permanent revocation.

**Deal cancellation is often the most immediate consequence.** When a buyer discovers mid-escrow that disclosures were incomplete, the transaction frequently dies. The listing agent loses the commission, the buyer's agent loses time, and both brokerages absorb wasted overhead. This scenario is [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) most painfully — not at inspection, but at the disclosure review stage.

## Common Mistakes Oregon Agents Make With Disclosures

Five errors appear with alarming regularity in OREA complaint files and civil litigation records.

**First, agents fill out the disclosure form on behalf of the seller.** ORS 105.465 places the duty on the seller, not the agent. When an agent checks boxes or writes descriptions, they become personally liable for any inaccuracy. The correct practice is to provide the blank form, explain each section verbally, and let the seller complete it in their own hand or electronically with their own attestation.

Second, agents accept "unknown" answers without follow-up. Oregon law permits a seller to mark "unknown," but a pattern of unknowns — particularly on systems the seller has used daily for years — can indicate willful avoidance. Agents have a duty under OAR 863-015-0125 to advise sellers that blanket "unknown" responses may not insulate them from liability if a court later determines the defect was obvious.

Third, agents deliver disclosures late. The statute contemplates delivery before or at mutual acceptance. Delivering the disclosure days after the buyer signs the purchase agreement restarts the buyer's revocation window and creates unnecessary risk of cancellation.

Fourth, agents fail to update disclosures when new information surfaces. If a seller discovers a leak, receives a code violation notice, or learns about a neighboring contamination issue after signing the initial disclosure, ORS 105.475(2) requires an amended disclosure. Agents who ignore mid-transaction changes are inviting claims.

Fifth, agents neglect the lead-based paint disclosure for pre-1978 properties entirely. This is a [federal requirement with significant penalties](/blog/washington-lead-paint-disclosure-2026) — up to $19,507 per violation under HUD enforcement guidelines updated in 2024. Oregon agents sometimes assume the state disclosure form covers lead paint. It does not satisfy the federal mandate; OREF 070 must be completed separately.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Principal brokers carry supervisory liability under ORS 696.805. If an affiliated agent mishandles disclosures, the brokerage is exposed. Effective audit protocols focus on three areas.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Red Flag |
|-------------|---------------|----------|
| Form completeness | Every section of OREF 001 answered; no blank fields | Multiple "N/A" entries without explanation |
| Delivery timing | Disclosure delivered before or at mutual acceptance | Disclosure date later than agreement date |
| Lead paint compliance | OREF 070 completed for all pre-1978 homes | Missing form in transaction file |
| Amendment protocol | Updated disclosure if seller reports new defect | No addendum after inspection repair request |
| Agent vs. seller authorship | Seller's signature, handwriting, or verified electronic completion | Agent's handwriting on seller's form |

Brokers should conduct quarterly file audits and require agents to submit disclosure documents to the transaction management system within 24 hours of execution. Waiting until closing to collect paperwork guarantees that gaps go undetected until a complaint arrives.

Training is equally important. OREA's 2025 continuing education requirements already touch on disclosure obligations, but brokers should supplement with brokerage-specific sessions that walk through actual OREF form scenarios. Role-playing a difficult seller conversation — where the seller wants to mark "unknown" on a known defect — is more effective than any slide deck.
