## Why Texas Agents Still Get Tripped Up on Recording Fees

Texas does not impose a state-level transfer tax on real property conveyances, which makes it an outlier among the majority of U.S. states. That single fact creates a dangerous assumption: agents who think "no transfer tax" means "nothing to worry about" routinely botch closing cost estimates and misquote fees to clients. Understanding Texas transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 is not optional knowledge — it is a baseline competency that protects your license, your brokerage, and your client's closing timeline.

The absence of a transfer tax does not eliminate the financial obligations that attach to recording deeds, deeds of trust, liens, and other instruments at the county clerk's office. Texas counties charge per-page recording fees, and the amounts are set by statute under Texas Local Government Code Chapter 118. Agents who fail to account for these costs accurately on the Seller's Net Sheet or in Paragraph 12 of the TREC One to Four Family Residential Contract (TREC Form 20-17) risk triggering last-minute disputes that [blow closings entirely](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

## The Statutory Framework: Texas Local Government Code Chapter 118

**Recording fees in Texas are governed exclusively by state statute, not by county discretion.** Texas Local Government Code Section 118.011 establishes the fee schedule for the county clerk's recording services. Counties cannot unilaterally raise these fees beyond what the legislature authorizes, though certain supplemental charges — like courthouse security fees under Section 118.011(e) — do apply.

Here is the current fee structure agents should know for 2026 closings:

| Document/Service | Base Fee | Per Additional Page | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deed (warranty, special warranty, quitclaim) | $26 | $4 | First page includes indexing |
| Deed of Trust | $26 | $4 | Lender typically pays |
| Release of Lien | $26 | $4 | Seller's responsibility |
| Lis Pendens | $26 | $4 | Filed by attorney |
| Plat or Map | $46 | $4 | Larger format fee |

Additional county-level charges include a $10 courthouse security fund fee (authorized under Local Government Code Section 291.008) and, in some counties, a records management fee up to $10 per instrument (Section 118.0216). The Texas Legislature has not passed any amendments to Chapter 118 effective for fiscal year 2026, so these amounts remain unchanged from 2025.

## What "No Transfer Tax" Actually Means in Texas

Texas Property Tax Code and the Texas Tax Code do not contain any provision imposing an excise tax, documentary stamp tax, or conveyance tax on the transfer of real property. This is codified by omission — there is simply no enabling statute. Compare this to states like New York, Pennsylvania, or California, where transfer taxes can range from 0.1% to over 2% of the sale price.

However, agents must not confuse the absence of a transfer tax with the absence of all government-imposed closing costs. Property taxes are prorated at closing and often represent the single largest line item on a Texas settlement statement. Additionally, some municipalities charge impact fees or development fees that appear on new construction closings. These are distinct from transfer taxes but frequently confused by newer agents who [miss critical deadlines and cost breakdowns](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

## Texas Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026: Buyer vs. Seller Responsibilities

**Paragraph 12 of TREC Form 20-17 governs how closing costs, including recording fees, are allocated between buyer and seller.** The standard contract language assigns the cost of recording the deed to the buyer and the cost of releasing existing liens to the seller. But these allocations are negotiable, and agents who treat them as fixed rules misrepresent the contract to their clients.

Here is the typical allocation:

| Fee Type | Default Payer (TREC 20-17) | Negotiable? | Typical Amount |
|---|---|---|---|
| Recording fee for deed | Buyer | Yes | $26-$50 |
| Recording fee for deed of trust | Buyer/Lender | Yes | $50-$150 |
| Release of existing liens | Seller | Yes | $26-$50 per lien |
| Tax certificates | Seller | Yes | $10-$50 |
| Title insurance premium | Per contract negotiation | Yes | Varies by sale price |

Agents should note that when lenders are involved, the deed of trust recording fee frequently appears on the buyer's Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure under Section H (Other Costs). Misquoting this figure early in the transaction causes TRID tolerance violations for the lender, which can delay closing by days.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make on Texas Recording Fees

The Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) does not directly regulate recording fee calculations, but TREC's [Standards of Practice](https://www.trec.texas.gov/) hold agents accountable for providing accurate cost information to clients. Here are the five errors that show up most often in Texas transactions:

**First, agents quote flat recording fees without accounting for page count.** A deed of trust for a conventional loan with riders can easily run 30-40 pages. At $26 for the first page and $4 per additional page, that is $142-$182 — not the "$50 recording fee" many agents casually estimate. When the settlement statement arrives and the number is triple what the agent quoted, trust erodes instantly.

Second, agents forget supplemental county fees. The courthouse security fee and records management fee add $10-$20 per instrument. On a transaction with four recorded documents, that is an extra $40-$80 nobody budgeted for. Small amounts, but they create line-item disputes at the closing table.

Third, agents in counties with electronic recording (e-recording) assume the fees are identical to paper recording. While the per-page statutory fees remain the same, some e-recording vendors charge a technology or convenience fee ranging from $3 to $10 per document. These vendor fees are not statutorily mandated, but they still appear on the settlement statement.

Fourth, agents misattribute lien release costs. When a seller has multiple existing liens — a first mortgage, a HELOC, and a mechanics lien — each release is a separate recorded instrument with its own fee. Agents who list a single "recording fee" line item on the seller's net sheet understate the seller's actual costs.

Fifth, agents fail to verify that documents were actually recorded post-closing. The title company handles this operationally, but the agent's fiduciary duty includes confirming that the client's deed appears in the county records. A [solid transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) should include a post-closing verification step.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers operating under TREC Rule 535.2 carry supervisory responsibility for their agents' transaction conduct. When recording fee errors cascade into client complaints or TREC complaints, the broker's license is at risk alongside the agent's. Here is what designated brokers should be reviewing.

**Audit every Seller's Net Sheet and Buyer's Estimated Closing Costs worksheet your agents produce.** These documents are not regulated forms, but they create reliance — courts and TREC panels have treated materially inaccurate estimates as evidence of misrepresentation under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.652(b). If your agent quotes $50 in recording fees and the actual figure is $250, that is a material misstatement.

Brokers should also verify that agents are using the correct version of TREC promulgated forms. TREC updates its forms periodically, and using an outdated version of Form 20-17 can introduce contract language that conflicts with current fee allocation standards. TREC's enforcement division has issued informal guidance that continued use of superseded forms may constitute a violation of Section 1101.652(b)(22) of the Texas Occupations Code.

Finally, brokers need a system for tracking post-closing recording confirmations. If a deed goes unrecorded because of a title company error, the buyer is exposed to priority disputes under Texas Property Code Section 13.001. The brokerage that facilitated the transaction may face liability claims even though recording is technically the title company's duty — because the agent recommended that title company.

## Liability Risks When Compliance Fails

The consequences of recording fee mismanagement are not hypothetical. Texas courts have entertained claims against agents and brokerages where inaccurate closing cost disclosures caused financial harm to buyers or sellers. Under DTPA (Deceptive Trade Practices Act) Section 17.46, a materially misleading representation about transaction costs can trigger treble damages if the court finds the conduct was committed knowingly.

TREC's disciplinary actions for fee-related misrepresentations typically result in agreed orders ranging from reprimands to license suspensions. The most common enforcement pathway is a client complaint filed under TREC Rule 535.155, which triggers an investigation. Even if the complaint does not result in formal discipline, the investigation itself costs time, legal fees, and reputational damage.

Recording failures create a different risk category. An unrecorded deed leaves the buyer vulnerable under Texas's race-notice recording statute (Property Code Section 13.001). If a subsequent purchaser records first without notice of the prior conveyance, the subsequent purchaser prevails. The original buyer's recourse is then limited to breach of warranty claims against the seller — and potentially negligence claims against the professionals who handled the closing.
