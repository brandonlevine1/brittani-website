## Why Pennsylvania Teams & Supervision Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

The Pennsylvania State Real Estate Commission has made its enforcement priorities clear: teams operating outside the supervision framework risk fines, license actions, and deal-killing liability. Understanding Pennsylvania teams & supervision rules 2026 is no longer optional—it is the baseline for staying in business. The Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (63 P.S. Sections 455.101–455.902) and its implementing regulations at 49 Pa. Code Chapter 35 set the ground rules, but the Commission's recent enforcement actions show that agents and brokers are still getting caught off guard.

Pennsylvania does not formally define "team" in statute the way some other states do. Instead, the Commission treats teams as marketing arrangements operating under a single broker, which means every supervisory obligation that applies to individual licensees applies equally—and sometimes more aggressively—to team structures. If your brokerage runs two or three teams, the broker of record is personally on the hook for every compliance failure across all of them.

This post breaks down the specific statutes, forms, advertising rules, and enforcement consequences that Pennsylvania teams face right now—and what brokers need to audit before the Commission does it for them.

## The Legal Framework: Statutes and Regulations That Govern Teams

**Pennsylvania's Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA) at 63 P.S. Section 455.604 places all supervisory responsibility squarely on the broker of record.** There is no delegation loophole. A team leader who holds a salesperson license cannot supervise other salespersons, period. Only an associate broker who has received explicit written authorization from the broker of record may assist with supervisory functions—and even then, ultimate liability remains with the broker.

The implementing regulations at [49 Pa. Code Section 35.242](https://www.pacodeandbulletin.gov/) spell out what "supervision" actually means: reviewing contracts, ensuring proper disclosure delivery, monitoring advertising, overseeing escrow deposits, and maintaining records. The Commission has stated in multiple adjudications that "passive supervision"—simply being available by phone—does not satisfy the standard. Active, documented oversight is required.

Section 35.305 of the same code addresses advertising directly. Every advertisement by a team must include the employing broker's legal or registered trade name in a "clear and conspicuous" manner. The team name alone is not sufficient. Agents who run social media ads, yard signs, or direct mail using only the team name without broker identification violate this section every time the ad runs.

## Advertising Rules: Where Most Teams Get It Wrong

The advertising requirements trip up more Pennsylvania teams than any other single rule. Section 35.305 does not distinguish between a full-page newspaper ad and an Instagram story. Every piece of marketing that identifies a team must also identify the brokerage. The Commission has issued formal citations for yard signs, vehicle wraps, and Facebook carousel ads that displayed a team name without the broker's name.

| Advertising Element | Requirement | Common Violation |
|---|---|---|
| Yard signs | Broker name visible, same prominence standard | Team name in large font, broker in tiny print below |
| Social media profiles | Broker name on profile or in each ad | Team branding only, no brokerage mention |
| Business cards | Must include broker's legal or trade name | Team logo dominates, broker name absent |
| Email signatures | Broker identification required | Only team name and personal title listed |
| Direct mail | Broker name clear and conspicuous | Postcard with team name, broker in 6pt footer |

The "clear and conspicuous" standard means the broker's name cannot be buried in fine print while the team name dominates the layout. The Commission applies a reasonable-consumer test: would a member of the public understand who the responsible broker is? If the answer is no, the ad violates 49 Pa. Code Section 35.305.

For agents who also handle disclosure timing and delivery across multiple deals, mismatched advertising can create downstream confusion about who represents whom. Understanding [how deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) often starts with exactly this type of identity confusion at the front end of a transaction.

## What Happens When You Fail to Comply

**Non-compliance with team supervision and advertising rules carries real financial and professional consequences.** The Commission can impose civil penalties of up to $10,000 per violation under 63 P.S. Section 455.801. Each advertisement that runs without proper broker identification counts as a separate violation—meaning a 30-day social media campaign with daily posts could theoretically generate 30 distinct citations.

License suspension and revocation remain on the table for repeat offenders or egregious violations. The Commission regularly publishes enforcement actions in its quarterly bulletin, and those actions become public record searchable by consumers and opposing counsel. Beyond Commission penalties, a broker whose team operated without proper supervision faces civil liability in private lawsuits—particularly if a consumer alleges they were confused about representation.

Deal cancellations are a less obvious but equally damaging consequence. When a buyer's attorney discovers that the listing agent's team advertising never properly identified the broker, they may raise agency disclosure challenges. Pennsylvania's Consumer Notice form (REL-1) requires delivery at the first substantive contact. If the team's branding misled the consumer about who held the license, the entire agency relationship can be called into question—potentially unwinding a deal days before closing.

The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends beyond any single fine. Lost commission, damaged reputation, and potential E&O claims compound the financial hit.

## Common Mistakes Pennsylvania Teams Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in Commission enforcement actions and broker audit findings. Each one is preventable, but each one persists because agents assume someone else is handling compliance.

**First, team leaders acting as de facto supervisors without broker authorization.** A top-producing salesperson who recruits three buyers' agents and starts assigning them leads, reviewing their contracts, and approving their marketing has crossed into supervisory territory. Unless that person holds an associate broker license and has written authorization from the broker of record, this arrangement violates RELRA Section 455.604.

Second, failing to deliver the Consumer Notice (Form REL-1) at first substantive contact when a team member—not the team leader—initiates the relationship. Every licensee on the team has an independent obligation to ensure the consumer receives this form. The team leader's prior delivery does not satisfy the requirement for a different agent's contact.

Third, maintaining separate team bank accounts for earnest money deposits. Pennsylvania law requires that all escrow funds flow through the broker's designated trust account. Teams that collect deposits into a "team operating account" violate 49 Pa. Code Section 35.327, and the broker faces automatic disciplinary action.

Fourth, using team names that sound like independent brokerage names. If "The Keystone Home Group" operates as a team under ABC Realty, the Commission may find that the name misleads consumers into believing they are dealing with a standalone brokerage. The Commission has flagged names containing words like "Realty," "Real Estate," or "Properties" when used by teams rather than licensed brokerages.

Fifth, inconsistent record retention across team members. Pennsylvania requires brokers to maintain transaction records for three years under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.286. When team members store documents in personal cloud accounts, on personal devices, or in systems the broker cannot access, the broker cannot satisfy audit requests—and the Commission holds the broker responsible for the gap.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

The broker of record cannot outsource supervisory duties to a team leader and call it a day. Here is what a defensible compliance audit looks like in Pennsylvania.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Advertising compliance | Every active ad, sign, social profile, and printed material | Monthly |
| Consumer Notice delivery | REL-1 forms signed and dated in every active file | Per transaction |
| Escrow account deposits | All team deposits routed through brokerage trust account | Weekly reconciliation |
| Supervisory authority documentation | Written authorization letters for any associate broker assisting with oversight | Annually, or upon team roster change |
| Record retention access | Broker has direct access to all team transaction files | Quarterly spot check |

**Brokers who rely on verbal assurances from team leaders are building their defense on sand.** The Commission expects written policies, documented training, and audit trails. When a complaint arrives, the broker's first line of defense is the paper trail showing active supervision—not a team leader's testimony that "we talked about it."

## The Role of the Consumer Notice and Disclosure Timing

Pennsylvania's Consumer Notice (Form REL-1) must be provided at the initial interview—defined as the first contact where substantive real estate information is discussed. For teams, this creates a wrinkle: if a consumer first contacts the team leader, receives the REL-1, and is then handed off to a team member who begins working with them independently, that team member must confirm the consumer's understanding of the agency relationship. The form itself does not need to be re-delivered, but the Commission expects documentation that the new agent clarified their role.

> "The duty to provide the Consumer Notice rests with each licensee who engages in substantive discussion with a consumer, regardless of whether another licensee previously provided the form." — Pennsylvania State Real Estate Commission Guidance, 49 Pa. Code Section 35.336

This means teams need internal protocols for handoffs. A simple signed acknowledgment noting the date a new team member took over active communication with the consumer satisfies the documentation standard. Without it, the broker has no defense if the consumer later claims confusion about representation.
