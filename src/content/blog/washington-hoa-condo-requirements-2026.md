## Understanding Washington HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026

Washington state imposes some of the most prescriptive resale disclosure requirements in the Pacific Northwest for properties governed by a homeowners association or condominium regime. If you represent buyers or sellers in these transactions and you are not intimately familiar with **Washington HOA & condo disclosure rules 2026**, you are carrying liability that could cost your client—and your license—far more than a missed commission check. The statutes are unforgiving, the rescission timelines are rigid, and the consequences of sloppy compliance land squarely on the licensee who failed to manage the process.

Two separate statutory frameworks apply depending on whether the property is a condominium or a non-condo HOA community. Confusing the two is the first mistake agents make, and it cascades into the wrong forms, wrong timelines, and wrong expectations set with clients.

## The Two Statutes You Must Know

Washington's condominium disclosure obligations live in **RCW 64.34** (the Washington Condominium Act), specifically sections 64.34.410 through 64.34.425. Non-condominium homeowners associations—think single-family planned communities, townhome developments without a condominium declaration—fall under **RCW 64.38** (the Homeowners' Associations Act), particularly section 64.38.035.

Both statutes require the seller to furnish a **resale certificate** to the buyer before or shortly after mutual acceptance. The content requirements differ slightly between the two, but the rescission mechanics are nearly identical: the buyer gets five calendar days from receipt to cancel the contract without penalty. That five-day clock is statutory and cannot be shortened by agreement between the parties.

**The distinction between RCW 64.34 and RCW 64.38 is not academic—it determines which resale certificate form the association must produce.** Ordering the wrong one, or assuming your townhome listing is a condo when it is actually a planned community under a separate HOA declaration, will trigger downstream problems with title, with the buyer's review period, and potentially with the enforceability of the transaction itself.

| Feature | Condominium (RCW 64.34) | Non-Condo HOA (RCW 64.38) |
|---------|------------------------|---------------------------|
| Governing statute | RCW 64.34.425 | RCW 64.38.035 |
| Document name | Resale certificate | Resale certificate |
| Buyer rescission period | 5 days from receipt | 5 days from receipt |
| Required financial disclosures | Budget, reserves, assessments, litigation | Budget, reserves, assessments |
| Typical HOA production time | 10–14 business days | 7–14 business days |
| Fee range (2026) | $200–$375 | $150–$300 |

Agents working both sides of a transaction should also cross-reference the requirements in [Washington's seller disclosure framework](/blog/washington-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) because the Form 17 does not substitute for the resale certificate. They are separate obligations with separate liability tracks.

## What the Resale Certificate Must Contain

For condominiums under RCW 64.34.425, the resale certificate package is extensive. The association must include the declaration, bylaws, and rules; the most recent financial statement and budget; a statement of monthly assessments; any pending special assessments or capital improvements; a description of insurance coverage; the number of units whose owners are delinquent; and disclosure of any pending litigation involving the association.

**Missing even one of these components means the resale certificate is arguably incomplete, and an incomplete certificate may not trigger the buyer's five-day rescission clock.** The Washington Court of Appeals addressed this issue in *Burien Place Condo Ass'n v. Seymour*, reinforcing that substantial compliance is the minimum threshold. Agents should review the package before forwarding it to the buyer and flag gaps to the association's management company immediately.

For HOA communities under RCW 64.38.035, the required content is similar but slightly narrower. The association must disclose the governing documents, current assessments, any pending increases, the reserve balance, and any known violations or legal actions. The five-day rescission right applies identically.

## Forms and Delivery Mechanics

The Northwest Multiple Listing Service (NWMLS) forms most commonly used in these transactions include **Form 22D (Resale Certificate, Condominium)** and **Form 22D (HOA)**. These addenda are attached to the purchase and sale agreement and establish the contractual framework for who orders the certificate, who pays for it, and the timeline for delivery. As of early 2026, NWMLS also provides Form 22DC for condominium contingencies that wrap the resale certificate review into the inspection timeline.

Delivery method matters. RCW 64.34.425(4) specifies that the resale certificate must be delivered to the purchaser—meaning the buyer or the buyer's agent. Electronic delivery is acceptable under Washington's UETA statute (RCW 19.360), but the delivering party should retain proof of transmission and receipt. A listing agent who emails the 80-page PDF to the buyer's agent but gets no confirmation has not closed the loop.

You should also understand how [buyer contingency deadlines](/blog/washington-buyer-contingency-deadlines-2026) interact with the resale certificate review window. The five-day rescission right runs independently of the inspection contingency, which means a buyer can rescind under the resale certificate even after waiving their inspection objections.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

The liability exposure here operates on multiple levels. First, if the resale certificate is never delivered, the buyer's rescission right never expires. This means a buyer could theoretically cancel weeks after closing if the certificate was omitted—though as a practical matter, courts examine whether the buyer had actual knowledge of the HOA terms through other means.

Second, the Department of Licensing can pursue disciplinary action against a licensee under **RCW 18.85.361** for failure to exercise reasonable skill and care. Omitting or mishandling the resale certificate in a condo transaction is exactly the type of conduct that triggers a complaint to the Washington Real Estate Commission.

Third, the seller may pursue a claim against the listing agent for negligence if the transaction falls apart because the certificate was never ordered or was delivered late. Conversely, a buyer's agent who fails to advise the client of their rescission rights could face a malpractice claim if the buyer later discovers material defects in the association's finances that were disclosed in the certificate they never reviewed.

> "The purchaser's right to cancel under RCW 64.34.425 is not contingent upon the purchaser's actual review of the resale certificate—only upon delivery. The clock starts when the document reaches the purchaser or the purchaser's agent." — Washington Real Estate Commission advisory, 2024.

For a deeper understanding of how [agency disclosure obligations](/blog/washington-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) layer on top of these requirements, note that Form 22D does not relieve you of your duty to explain the significance of the resale certificate contents to your client.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With HOA and Condo Disclosures

**First: ordering the resale certificate too late.** Many listing agents wait until mutual acceptance to order the certificate, then discover the management company needs 10–14 business days to produce it. Meanwhile, the buyer's contingency period is running. Best practice is to order the certificate during the pre-listing phase so it is ready to deliver at mutual acceptance.

Second: confusing the resale certificate with the HOA disclosure addendum on Form 17. The Form 17 Seller Disclosure Statement includes a section about HOA membership, but this does not satisfy the statutory requirement under either RCW 64.34 or 64.38. They are legally distinct documents with different delivery requirements.

Third: failing to confirm receipt with the buyer's side. An email attachment sent to a buyer's agent's spam folder does not constitute delivery. Agents should request a written acknowledgment and calendar the five-day window from the confirmed receipt date.

Fourth: assuming the five-day rescission period can be shortened or waived. It cannot. Any contractual provision purporting to waive the buyer's rescission right under RCW 64.34.425 or RCW 64.38.035 is void as a matter of law. Agents who tell their clients otherwise are practicing law—badly.

Fifth: ignoring updates that arrive after the initial certificate delivery. If the association issues a special assessment or files a lawsuit between delivery of the resale certificate and closing, the seller has an obligation to supplement the disclosure. Agents who stop monitoring the association's communications after the review period expires are leaving their clients exposed.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carry supervisory liability under RCW 18.85.275 for the transactions their affiliated licensees handle. A broker's compliance audit for HOA and condo transactions should cover several specific checkpoints.

| Audit Checkpoint | What to Verify | Risk If Missed |
|------------------|---------------|----------------|
| Certificate ordered | Dated order confirmation in file | Delayed delivery, extended rescission risk |
| Correct statute applied | RCW 64.34 vs. 64.38 matched to property type | Incomplete certificate, void rescission clock |
| Delivery confirmed | Written acknowledgment from buyer or buyer's agent | Indefinite rescission exposure |
| Rescission deadline calendared | Five-day window noted with specific expiration date | Missed objection deadline, client harm |
| Supplements delivered | Any post-delivery updates from HOA forwarded | Material omission liability |

**Brokers should build these checkpoints into their transaction management workflows rather than relying on individual agents to self-police.** A single missed resale certificate in a 200-unit condo building can cascade into an E&O claim that affects the entire brokerage.

The Washington Department of Licensing's [real estate division](https://www.dol.wa.gov/professional-licenses/real-estate-professionals) publishes updated guidance periodically on supervisory obligations, and brokers should review those bulletins at least quarterly.
