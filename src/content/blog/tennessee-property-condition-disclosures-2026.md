## Why Tennessee Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026 Rules Demand Your Attention

Tennessee's disclosure framework is deceptively simple—until something goes wrong. The state relies on a single primary statute, Tennessee Code Annotated Section 66-5-201 through 66-5-210, to govern residential property condition disclosures, but the consequences of sloppy compliance land squarely on the agent and the brokerage. Understanding the full scope of Tennessee property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 requirements is not optional homework; it is the bare minimum professional standard that the Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) expects every licensee to meet.

Agents in neighboring states like Virginia face their own variations, and comparing frameworks can sharpen your understanding of [how property condition disclosures differ state to state](/blog/virginia-property-condition-disclosures-2026). Tennessee's approach is narrower in form count but broader in potential liability exposure when sellers omit known defects and agents look the other way.

## The Statutory Foundation: T.C.A. Section 66-5-201 et seq.

Tennessee law requires sellers of residential real property (one to four units) to provide a written disclosure to the buyer before the buyer becomes obligated under a contract. The specific form mandated is the **Tennessee Residential Property Condition Disclosure** form, sometimes referenced as the **TF-340** in Tennessee REALTORS association materials. The statute spells out categories of disclosure that include structural systems, water and sewer, environmental hazards, and known material defects.

**Sellers must answer each question on the form based on their actual knowledge—not speculation, not inspection results they never read.** The form asks about roof condition, HVAC age, basement flooding history, presence of lead-based paint, radon, underground storage tanks, and proximity to landfills or hazardous waste sites. A blank answer is not a safe answer; TREC has historically treated incomplete forms as a compliance failure attributable to the listing agent who accepted them.

The [Tennessee Real Estate Commission](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regboards/trec.html) has enforcement authority over licensees who fail to ensure proper disclosure delivery. Civil courts handle disputes between buyers and sellers, but TREC can independently open investigations and impose discipline.

## Exemptions: Who Does Not Have to Disclose

Not every transaction triggers the disclosure requirement. T.C.A. Section 66-5-202 lists specific exemptions that agents must memorize rather than guess at. Exempt transfers include sales by court-appointed fiduciaries (executors, administrators, guardians), transfers pursuant to foreclosure or deed in lieu, transfers between co-owners or spouses, and new construction sold by a builder that has never been occupied.

| Transaction Type | Disclosure Required? | Statutory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| Standard resale (1-4 units) | Yes | T.C.A. 66-5-201 |
| Estate or probate sale | No | T.C.A. 66-5-202(1) |
| Foreclosure / deed in lieu | No | T.C.A. 66-5-202(3) |
| New construction, never occupied | No | T.C.A. 66-5-202(7) |
| Transfer between co-owners | No | T.C.A. 66-5-202(5) |
| Court-ordered sale (divorce) | No | T.C.A. 66-5-202(2) |

The exemption does not mean the property has no issues—it means the statutory form is not legally required. Agents representing buyers in exempt transactions should counsel clients to rely heavily on inspections because the disclosure safety net has been removed.

## Environmental Disclosures: Lead Paint, Radon, and Beyond

Environmental disclosures in Tennessee operate on two tracks. The first is federal: the EPA's Lead-Based Paint Disclosure rule (42 U.S.C. Section 4852d) applies to any home built before 1978. Agents must provide buyers with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home," obtain signatures on the **Lead-Based Paint Disclosure form**, and allow a 10-day inspection opportunity unless the buyer waives it in writing.

**Failure to deliver the lead paint disclosure can result in penalties up to $19,507 per violation under HUD enforcement, plus treble damages in private litigation.** Tennessee does not layer additional state-level lead paint requirements on top of the federal mandate, but that federal floor is already substantial.

The second track is state-specific and embedded within the TF-340 form itself. Questions about radon gas levels, underground storage tanks, and proximity to environmental hazards are part of the Tennessee disclosure form. Tennessee is classified by the EPA as Zone 1 for radon risk across many counties, meaning elevated radon levels are common. Sellers who have tested and received high results must disclose them. Agents should remind sellers that burying a radon test report does not eliminate the disclosure obligation—it creates one.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance split into three channels: civil liability, regulatory discipline, and transactional fallout.

On the civil side, buyers who discover undisclosed material defects can pursue damages under T.C.A. Section 66-5-208. The statute provides that a seller who fails to disclose a known defect may be liable for actual damages suffered by the buyer. Agents who knowingly participated in the omission—or who should have caught it—can be dragged into that lawsuit as co-defendants.

TREC discipline operates independently. Under Tennessee Rule 1260-02-.12, the Commission can sanction licensees for failing to exercise reasonable skill and care. Sanctions range from a letter of warning to license suspension or revocation, with civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation under T.C.A. Section 62-13-313. Repeated violations or those involving intent will trigger harsher outcomes.

Transactionally, a missing or defective disclosure gives the buyer a potential exit ramp. While Tennessee does not codify a standalone rescission period tied to the disclosure form, a buyer's attorney can argue that the contract was formed without legally required material information, undermining the meeting of the minds. Deals collapse, earnest money fights ensue, and everyone's time evaporates.

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Agents Make

**Mistake 1: Accepting a partially completed disclosure form.** Sellers leave blanks because they are unsure. Agents accept it to avoid confrontation. TREC views blanks as a red flag that the listing agent did not fulfill their advisory role. The correct response is to instruct the seller that "unknown" is an acceptable answer—but a blank is not.

**Mistake 2: Confusing exemption from disclosure with exemption from honesty.** A foreclosure sale is exempt from the TF-340 form, but if the listing agent personally knows the basement floods every spring, they still cannot conceal that fact. T.C.A. Section 62-13-403 imposes a duty on licensees to disclose material adverse facts they know about, independent of the statutory disclosure form.

Mistake 3: Failing to re-disclose after material changes. If a pipe bursts between the disclosure date and closing, the seller's original disclosure is now inaccurate. Tennessee law requires updated disclosure when the seller becomes aware of a change. Agents who ignore mid-contract developments are exposing themselves to post-closing claims.

Mistake 4: Omitting the lead paint disclosure on pre-1978 properties listed as exempt from state disclosure. The federal lead paint rule has no exemptions that mirror Tennessee's state exemptions. An estate sale of a 1955 ranch home still requires the EPA lead paint form even though the TF-340 is not required.

Mistake 5: Treating the disclosure as a closing document rather than a pre-contract document. The statute says disclosure must occur before the buyer becomes obligated. Handing it over at the closing table is too late and may give the buyer grounds to void the transaction.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability under T.C.A. Section 62-13-404 for the acts of their affiliated licensees performed within the scope of the agency relationship. A compliance audit is not a luxury—it is self-preservation.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure form completeness | No blank answers; all pages signed and dated | Every listing file at contract |
| Lead paint form presence | EPA form in file for all pre-1978 properties | Every applicable listing |
| Timing verification | Disclosure delivered before executed contract | Spot-check monthly |
| Exemption documentation | Written note in file explaining why exemption applies | Every exempt listing |
| Mid-transaction updates | Amended disclosure if material change occurs | As-needed basis |

Brokers should build a checklist into their file review process that catches these items before closing rather than after a complaint. Training sessions on disclosure obligations belong in quarterly meetings, not just onboarding week. If you are managing a team with [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), systematizing disclosure review prevents one agent's oversight from becoming the entire brokerage's problem.
