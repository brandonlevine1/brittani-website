## Why Pennsylvania's Tax Treatment Differs From Federal Rules

Real estate agents across the commonwealth routinely advise clients on like-kind exchanges without fully understanding Pennsylvania 1031 exchange state tax implications 2026. The disconnect between federal and state treatment catches sellers—and their agents—off guard at the worst possible moment: closing. Pennsylvania does conform to IRC Section 1031, but the mechanics of how gain is calculated, reported, and taxed at the state level diverge enough to create liability exposure for everyone at the table.

Under 72 P.S. Section 7303, Pennsylvania taxes all classes of income at a flat 3.07% rate with no distinction between short-term and long-term gains. That means even when a seller properly defers federal capital gains tax through a qualified intermediary structure, the state filing obligations remain independent. Agents who assume "federal deferral equals state deferral" without confirming the documentation chain put their clients—and their licenses—at risk.

The Pennsylvania Department of Revenue (DOR) treats like-kind exchange reporting as a separate compliance event. Sellers must demonstrate that the replacement property meets like-kind standards under both federal and Pennsylvania law, and the burden of proof falls on the taxpayer during any audit.

## The Forms That Matter: PA Schedule D, REV-227, and REV-1840

**Pennsylvania requires specific form filings that do not mirror the federal Form 8824 process.** Individual sellers report exchange transactions on PA Schedule D (PA-40 D), which is filed alongside the annual PA-40 personal income tax return. Pass-through entities—LLCs and partnerships—must use the PA-20S/PA-65 Schedule D instead.

When a nonresident seller conducts an exchange involving Pennsylvania real property, the withholding rules under 72 P.S. Section 7316.1 apply. The buyer or settlement agent must withhold at the prescribed rate and submit payment to the DOR using Form REV-227 (Estimated Withholding Tax for Nonresidents) within 30 days of closing. This withholding obligation exists regardless of whether the seller intends to complete a 1031 exchange.

The seller can request exemption from withholding by submitting REV-1840 (Application for Exemption from Withholding) prior to settlement. Agents should confirm with the settlement attorney that this form is in hand well before the closing date—not the morning of. If the exchange later fails (missed identification deadline, receipt of boot, disqualified property), the deferred gain snaps back and the full 3.07% becomes due with penalties and interest.

| Form | Purpose | Who Files | Deadline |
|------|---------|-----------|----------|
| PA-40 D (Schedule D) | Report exchange gain/deferral for individuals | Seller | Annual PA-40 due date |
| PA-20S/PA-65 Schedule D | Report exchange gain/deferral for pass-throughs | Entity | Annual entity return due date |
| REV-227 | Nonresident withholding remittance | Buyer or settlement agent | 30 days post-closing |
| REV-1840 | Exemption from nonresident withholding | Seller (nonresident) | Before settlement |
| REV-1689 | Installment sale reporting (boot received over time) | Seller | Annual filing year of receipt |

## Pennsylvania 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: The Nonresident Trap

Nonresident sellers represent the highest-risk scenario for agents involved in 1031 exchanges in Pennsylvania. The state's withholding statute does not automatically carve out an exception for like-kind exchanges. That means unless the seller files REV-1840 and receives approval, funds will be withheld at closing—potentially breaking the exchange by diverting proceeds away from the qualified intermediary.

**This is the single most common way a 1031 exchange fails in Pennsylvania without anyone intending it to fail.** The withholding amount gets pulled from settlement proceeds, the qualified intermediary receives less than the full sale price, and the exchange is partially taxable because the seller is deemed to have received boot. Agents who do not flag this issue early in the listing process share responsibility for the outcome.

The [Pennsylvania Department of Revenue's withholding guidance](https://www.revenue.pa.gov/FormsandPublications/PAPersonalIncomeTaxGuide/Pages/Nonresidents-and-Part-Year-Residents.aspx) makes clear that the exemption request must be substantiated with documentation of the exchange structure. Settlement agents who process closings without resolving the withholding question expose themselves—and cooperating agents—to potential malpractice claims.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Pennsylvania 1031 Exchanges

The errors below repeat across brokerages of every size. Most stem from agents conflating federal and state rules or assuming that tax compliance belongs exclusively to the CPA.

**First, agents fail to flag the 45-day identification deadline relative to local government transfer tax filings.** Pennsylvania's Realty Transfer Tax (RTT), administered under 72 P.S. Section 8101-C, applies at the point of initial sale. Agents sometimes tell sellers that the RTT will be deferred along with the income tax—this is incorrect. The 1% state RTT and any local RTT are due at closing regardless of exchange status. Misstating this creates a trust issue and potential deal collapse when the seller sees unexpected costs on the settlement statement.

Second, agents neglect to confirm whether the replacement property is located inside or outside Pennsylvania. If a seller exchanges Pennsylvania property for replacement property in another state, the Pennsylvania gain is still reportable on PA Schedule D. The deferral applies, but the basis tracking and future disposition reporting remain a Pennsylvania obligation. Agents who say "you're selling in PA and buying in Florida, so Pennsylvania is out of the picture" are dead wrong.

Third, agents ignore the installment sale interaction. When boot is received and paid over multiple years—or when seller financing bridges part of the transaction—Form REV-1689 governs the Pennsylvania reporting. Agents who structure creative terms without alerting the tax advisor to state-specific installment rules create downstream audit exposure.

Fourth, agents assume all like-kind property qualifies identically under state and federal law. While Pennsylvania generally conforms to the federal definition, mixed-use properties with personal property components may trigger different treatment at the state level. A vacation rental with significant furniture and equipment allocations may not fully qualify.

Fifth, agents wait until the transaction is under contract to raise exchange planning. By then, the seller may have already selected a settlement company that lacks qualified intermediary relationships, or the timeline may be too compressed to secure REV-1840 approval for nonresidents.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under the Pennsylvania Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. Section 455.101 et seq. When an agent provides guidance—even casually—about 1031 exchange tax treatment, the broker's compliance obligation activates. The State Real Estate Commission can discipline both the agent and the broker if material misrepresentations harm a consumer.

**Brokers should audit three areas in every transaction file where a 1031 exchange is mentioned.** First, confirm that the file contains a written referral to a qualified tax professional and that no agent communication makes specific tax promises. Second, verify that nonresident withholding has been addressed in writing before the settlement date—either through REV-1840 approval or acknowledgment that withholding will occur. Third, check that the settlement statement properly reflects Realty Transfer Tax as a closing cost and does not conflate it with deferred income tax.

| Audit Checkpoint | What to Look For | Risk If Missing |
|------------------|------------------|-----------------|
| Written tax referral | Letter or email referring seller to CPA/attorney | Unauthorized practice of law claim |
| Nonresident withholding resolution | REV-1840 filed or withholding acknowledged | Broken exchange, seller liability |
| RTT disclosure on settlement statement | 1% state + local RTT shown as due at closing | Consumer complaint, license discipline |
| Qualified intermediary documentation | QI agreement in file before closing | No proof exchange was properly structured |
| Timeline tracking (45/180 days) | Calendar entries or transaction coordinator notes | Missed deadlines, taxable event |

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: What Is Actually at Stake

The Pennsylvania DOR imposes a 5% per month late-filing penalty on unpaid tax, capped at 25% of the amount due. Underpayment interest accrues at 0.75% per month. For a seller deferring $200,000 in gain, the state tax due upon a failed exchange is $6,140—and if unreported for a year, penalties and interest push that above $8,000.

Agents do not directly pay these penalties, but their exposure is real. A seller who loses deferral because of agent misguidance—say, the withholding issue described above—has grounds for a professional liability claim. Pennsylvania courts have recognized that real estate agents owe a duty of competence that extends to identifying tax-adjacent compliance steps, even if the agent is not providing tax advice.

The State Real Estate Commission can also take independent action. Under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.292, conduct that demonstrates incompetence or untrustworthiness in a transaction is grounds for license suspension or revocation. Telling a seller "don't worry, the 1031 handles everything" without specifying that Pennsylvania has its own reporting requirements qualifies as a material omission.
