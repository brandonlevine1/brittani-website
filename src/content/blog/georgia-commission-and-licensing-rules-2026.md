## Georgia Commission & Licensing Rules 2026: The Statute-Level Details That Matter

The Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) does not leave much room for ambiguity when it comes to how agents earn, split, and protect their commissions. Understanding Georgia commission & licensing rules 2026 at the statutory level separates agents who close cleanly from those who face disciplinary hearings. The regulatory framework sits primarily in Title 43, Chapter 40 of the Official Code of Georgia Annotated (O.C.G.A.), supplemented by GREC's administrative rules under Chapter 520-1.

Every dollar of commission an agent earns in this state flows through a licensing and brokerage structure that GREC actively audits. If you have not reviewed your compliance posture since your last renewal cycle, now is the time. This post breaks down the specific statutes, forms, penalties, and operational mistakes that trip up even experienced licensees.

## Licensing Structure and Renewal Requirements

Georgia issues three license types under O.C.G.A. 43-40-8 through 43-40-12: Community Association Manager, Salesperson, and Broker. Salespersons must complete 75 hours of pre-license education, pass the state and national exam portions, and affiliate with a licensed broker before conducting any brokerage activity. Broker applicants need three years of active licensee experience within the five years preceding application, plus 60 additional hours of broker-level coursework.

**Renewal cycles in Georgia run on a four-year schedule, with 36 hours of continuing education required per cycle.** At least 3 of those hours must cover license law, and another 3 must address a GREC-approved topic such as fair housing or ethics. The renewal fee is $125 for salespersons and $170 for brokers, due by the licensee's assigned expiration date.

Failing to renew before expiration triggers "expired" status. GREC allows reinstatement within one year at double the fee, but during that window you cannot practice. After 12 months, your license is gone — full stop — and you start over with pre-license education and the exam.

## How Commission Agreements Work Under Georgia Law

O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(a)(21) makes it a violation for any licensee to accept compensation from anyone other than their affiliated broker. This is not a suggestion; it is the single most important structural rule governing commissions in Georgia. An agent cannot receive a referral fee, bonus, or split directly from a cooperating brokerage, a title company, or a client without that payment routing through their broker.

The [Brokerage Relationships in Real Estate Transactions Act (BRRETA)](https://grec.state.ga.us/information-research/license-law/), codified at O.C.G.A. 10-6A-1 et seq., governs how agency relationships and compensation disclosures operate. BRRETA requires that agents present the "Brokerage Relationships in Real Estate Transactions" disclosure (GREC Form 700) at the first substantive contact with a prospective client. This form is not optional and must be signed before discussing property details.

| Form | Purpose | When Required |
|------|---------|---------------|
| GREC Form 700 | Brokerage relationship disclosure | First substantive contact |
| GAR Form F201 | Exclusive Seller Listing Agreement | At listing appointment |
| GAR Form F301 | Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement | Before showing properties |
| GAR Form F501 | Seller's Property Disclosure | At or before offer acceptance |
| GREC Form CE-1 | Continuing education reporting | Each renewal cycle |

Georgia Association of Realtors (GAR) forms F201 and F301 contain the commission terms, including the percentage or flat fee, duration, and conditions under which the commission is earned. These contracts bind the client to the broker, not to the individual agent.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Georgia

**Mistake one: Collecting a commission check directly from a cooperating broker.** This seems obvious, but GREC disciplinary records show it happens annually — particularly with agents who change brokerages mid-transaction. The statute is unforgiving: O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(a)(21) treats direct payment as a sanctionable offense regardless of intent.

Mistake two: Allowing a listing agreement to expire without a written extension, then continuing to market the property. An agent who procures a buyer after the listing has lapsed has no enforceable right to a commission unless the seller signed a protection clause covering that specific buyer. Agents frequently skip this step and then find themselves in a dispute they cannot win.

Mistake three: Failing to present GREC Form 700 before the first substantive discussion. GREC defines "substantive" broadly — it includes discussing specific property features, price ranges, or motivations. Agents who wait until the offer stage have already violated BRRETA, and the resulting agency relationship may be legally void.

Mistake four: Not documenting dual agency consent in writing. O.C.G.A. 10-6A-12 requires informed written consent from both parties before a broker may act as a dual agent. Verbal agreements do not satisfy the statute. If a dispute arises and you lack signed consent, you face license discipline and potential civil liability for the full commission amount.

Mistake five: Mishandling the timing of earnest money deposits. [GREC Rule 520-1-.10 requires](https://grec.state.ga.us/) that trust funds be deposited within five business days of a binding agreement, unless the contract specifies otherwise. Late deposits trigger trust account violations, which GREC investigates aggressively because they implicate fiduciary duty.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Georgia brokers carry statutory responsibility for their affiliated agents under O.C.G.A. 43-40-18. This is not passive oversight — GREC expects brokers to maintain systems that catch violations before they become enforcement actions. The code holds brokers liable for failing to supervise, even if they had no actual knowledge of the agent's misconduct.

**Brokers should audit three areas quarterly at minimum: trust account reconciliations, disclosure form compliance, and commission payment routing.** Trust accounts require monthly reconciliation under GREC Rule 520-1-.10, with records retained for three years. A single unreconciled month can trigger a formal investigation if a complaint surfaces.

Commission payment routing audits should verify that no agent received direct payment from any party other than the brokerage. Brokers can run this check through their accounting software by flagging any commission disbursement that did not originate from the broker's operating account. The penalty for a broker who knowingly allows improper disbursement is license revocation under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(b).

| Audit Area | Frequency | GREC Authority | Penalty for Non-Compliance |
|------------|-----------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | Rule 520-1-.10 | Fines up to $1,000; license suspension |
| GREC Form 700 in every file | Per transaction | O.C.G.A. 10-6A-5 | Void agency; civil liability |
| Commission routing documentation | Quarterly | O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(a)(21) | License revocation |
| Dual agency consent forms | Per transaction | O.C.G.A. 10-6A-12 | Commission disgorgement; sanctions |
| CE compliance of agents | Annually | O.C.G.A. 43-40-20 | Broker liable for supervision failure |

Disclosure compliance audits matter because GREC can sanction the broker when an agent's file lacks Form 700. Brokers who implement [transaction handoff checklists](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) as a standard operating procedure catch these gaps before closing.

## Penalties and Enforcement: What Non-Compliance Actually Costs

GREC's enforcement arm is the Georgia Real Estate Commission Investigations Division. Complaints can be filed by consumers, other licensees, or initiated by GREC itself during audits. The disciplinary process begins with an investigation, moves to a citation or consent order for minor violations, and escalates to a formal hearing for serious offenses.

Monetary fines under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25 cap at $1,000 per violation per transaction, but the real damage comes from license suspension or revocation. A suspended agent cannot earn commissions, cannot attend closings, and cannot represent clients — effectively killing their pipeline. GREC publishes all disciplinary actions in its quarterly Sanction Report, which means your violation becomes a matter of public record.

Civil liability runs parallel to regulatory action. An agent who violates BRRETA's disclosure requirements may face a private lawsuit for damages, including commission disgorgement. Georgia courts have held that failure to present Form 700 can void the brokerage agreement entirely, leaving the agent with no legal claim to compensation.

Beyond individual liability, Errors and Omissions insurance policies often exclude coverage for regulatory violations. This means a fine from GREC plus a civil judgment can hit an agent's personal assets. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond a single lost commission when compliance failures are involved.

## Post-NAR Settlement Impacts on Georgia Practice

The 2024 NAR settlement reshaped buyer-agent compensation nationally, and Georgia agents must now grapple with how these changes interact with state-specific rules. GAR updated Form F301 (Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement) to require explicit written agreement on buyer-agent compensation before an agent shows properties. This aligns with BRRETA's existing requirement for written brokerage agreements but adds granularity around the compensation disclosure.

**Agents who show properties without a signed F301 now risk both a GREC violation and a breach of MLS participation rules.** FMLS and Georgia MLS both updated their cooperative compensation policies in 2025, removing blanket offers of buyer-agent compensation from listing data. The practical effect: agents must negotiate and document their fee before touring homes, or risk working for free.

This shift makes commission documentation more important than ever. Brokers need to verify that every buyer file contains a signed F301 with a specific compensation amount or method before any showing activity occurs. Agents who treat this as a formality rather than a legal requirement will find themselves uncompensated when disputes arise.
