## The Real Breakdown of Illinois Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026

Every Illinois transaction involves a closing cost negotiation, and the answer to "Illinois who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026" is never as simple as custom alone dictates. Illinois is an attorney-state with layered municipal taxes, transfer stamp ordinances that shift by ZIP code, and contract forms that place the burden of specificity squarely on the agents drafting the deal. If you get sloppy with cost allocation language, you risk blown closings, disciplinary complaints, and malpractice exposure.

The Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation (IDFPR) oversees licensee conduct under the Illinois Real Estate License Act of 2000 (225 ILCS 454). Agents who misrepresent or omit closing cost terms can face sanctions ranging from fines to license revocation. This post maps out who actually pays what, the forms that govern allocation, and where agents most frequently get it wrong.

## Standard Cost Allocation: Buyer Side vs. Seller Side

**Illinois has no single statute that assigns every closing cost to a specific party — allocation is primarily contract-driven.** The Multi-Board Residential Real Estate Contract (version 7.0, commonly called the "Multi-Board 7.0") and the Illinois REALTORS Residential Real Estate Contract are the two dominant forms, and each handles cost allocation slightly differently in their boilerplate language.

Here is how costs typically break down across the state:

| Closing Cost | Customary Payer | Statutory Basis or Custom |
|---|---|---|
| State transfer tax ($0.50 per $500) | Seller | 35 ILCS 200/31-10 |
| County transfer tax ($0.25 per $500) | Seller | County ordinance (Cook, DuPage, etc.) |
| Chicago city transfer stamp ($5.25 per $500) | Seller | Chicago Municipal Code 3-33-030 |
| Chicago CTA tax ($1.50 per $500) | Buyer | Chicago Municipal Code 3-33-030(C) |
| Owner's title insurance policy | Seller (northern IL) / Buyer (southern IL) | Custom, negotiable |
| Lender's title policy | Buyer | Contract term |
| Recording fees | Split (deed to seller, mortgage to buyer) | Contract term |
| Survey | Buyer or Seller depending on form | Contract term |

Notice that "custom" appears often. That is precisely the problem — agents treat custom as law and skip contract specifics. When the deal falls apart over a $2,400 survey neither party agreed to pay, the listing agent's phone rings first.

## Transfer Taxes: The Statutory Layer Agents Must Get Right

Illinois imposes a state-level real estate transfer tax under 35 ILCS 200/31-10, assessed at $0.50 per $500 of consideration. Counties impose their own stamp under the same chapter. The statute itself does not designate who pays; it taxes the "privilege of transferring title" and the seller is the customary obligor because the seller conveys title.

**Chicago adds complexity that trips up even experienced agents.** The city levies both a seller-side transfer stamp ($5.25 per $500) and a buyer-side CTA/affordable housing tax ($1.50 per $500) under Chicago Municipal Code 3-33-030. Since January 2025, the tiered "Bring Chicago Home" structure was struck down by Cook County Circuit Court, returning Chicago to its flat-rate system. Agents must verify current rates at closing because municipal tax litigation remains active.

Outside Chicago, municipalities like Evanston, Oak Park, and several collar-county villages impose their own transfer taxes with varying payer designations. Always confirm the local ordinance — do not assume the seller pays just because that is Chicago custom.

## Forms and Contract Provisions That Control Allocation

The [Multi-Board Residential Real Estate Contract 7.0](https://www.illinoisrealtors.org) includes specific paragraphs addressing closing cost allocation. Paragraph 11 covers title evidence and designates responsibility for the title commitment and policy. Paragraph 13 addresses transfer taxes and stamps. Paragraph 15 details survey obligations.

Illinois REALTORS also publishes its own Residential Real Estate Contract (Form 6.1), which uses slightly different numbering but addresses the same categories. Agents working in markets where both forms circulate — particularly the collar counties — must read the specific paragraphs of whichever form the parties selected rather than relying on memory from a different form version.

The attorney modification letter, generated during the five-business-day attorney review period, is where most cost allocation changes occur in practice. Attorneys routinely shift survey costs, proration methods, and title charges through these letters. Agents who do not read and track modification letters [miss critical deal changes](/blog/where-deals-break-down) that alter net sheets and estimated proceeds.

## What Happens When Agents Get It Wrong: Liability and Consequences

IDFPR has authority under 225 ILCS 454/20-20 to discipline licensees for incompetence, negligence, or misrepresentation. Incorrectly representing who pays a transfer tax — or drafting a net sheet that omits a municipal stamp — can trigger a consumer complaint and formal investigation.

Beyond regulatory risk, agents face civil liability for negligent misrepresentation. Illinois courts have held that buyers and sellers can sue their own agents for inaccurate closing cost estimates that materially changed the economics of the deal. The measure of damages is typically the difference between what the client expected to pay and what they actually owed at closing.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate practical consequence. If a buyer arrives at closing and discovers an unexpected $8,000 CTA tax line item, they may refuse to close. The resulting fallout — extension fees, rate-lock expirations, potential earnest money disputes — cascades into [the true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) that far exceeds the original oversight.

## Five Common Mistakes Agents Make on Illinois Closing Costs

**First, agents assume Chicago transfer tax rules apply statewide.** The CTA tax is Chicago-specific. Agents listing in Schaumburg or Naperville who include a "buyer CTA tax" line on their estimates confuse clients and signal unfamiliarity with the local market.

Second, agents fail to update net sheets after attorney review modifications. The original contract might assign the survey to the buyer, but the seller's attorney agrees to provide it during review. If the listing agent never updates the seller's estimated net, the seller discovers a surprise deduction at closing.

Third, agents conflate title insurance customs between northern and southern Illinois. In Cook, DuPage, Lake, and Kane counties, the seller customarily pays for the owner's policy. South of I-80, many markets default to the buyer paying. Agents relocating or working across regions must verify the local custom and specify it in the contract rather than leaving it to assumption.

Fourth, agents neglect to disclose negotiable costs as negotiable. Under the IDFPR's interpretation of Article 20 of the License Act, agents owe a duty of honest dealing. Telling a buyer that "the seller always pays the survey" when the contract says otherwise is a misrepresentation, even if the agent believes it to be customary.

Fifth, agents forget municipal water and sewer certifications. Many Illinois municipalities require paid-in-full water certificates before transfer. The cost is modest — often $50 to $200 — but the timeline to obtain one can be two to three weeks. Agents who do not calendar this requirement risk delaying closings over a bureaucratic bottleneck that [proper deadline tracking eliminates](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated managing brokers carry supervisory liability under 225 ILCS 454/20-20(a)(31) for their sponsored licensees' conduct. A pattern of inaccurate closing cost representations across an office is not just an agent problem — it is a brokerage compliance failure.

**Brokers should audit three specific areas quarterly.** First, review a sample of net sheets and buyer estimates against final closing disclosures (the TRID Closing Disclosure or the Illinois equivalent HUD-1 equivalent for cash deals). Variance above five percent on fees the agent controlled or represented deserves a coaching conversation. Second, confirm that agents are using current form versions. The Multi-Board 7.0 replaced 6.1 and includes updated allocation language; agents using outdated forms expose the brokerage to liability. Third, verify that agents document transfer tax research for every municipality involved in their transactions, not just the ones where they typically work.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Risk if Ignored |
|---|---|---|
| Net sheet vs. Closing Disclosure variance | Quarterly sampling | Misrepresentation claims, E&O exposure |
| Contract form version verification | Every transaction | Unenforceable terms, regulatory complaint |
| Municipal transfer tax documentation | Every transaction | Surprise charges, deal cancellation |
| Attorney review letter tracking | Every transaction | Missed cost shifts, client disputes |
| Water/sewer certificate calendaring | Every transaction | Closing delays, commission clawback |

Brokerages that build these audits into their transaction management workflow reduce E&O claims and create a paper trail that demonstrates supervisory diligence if IDFPR ever investigates.

## Prorations and Credits That Blur the Line

Real property taxes in Illinois are paid in arrears, creating proration calculations that effectively shift costs between parties at closing. The standard approach — using 110 percent of the prior year's tax bill as the proration estimate — is baked into Paragraph 14 of the Multi-Board 7.0. But agents must understand that this is an estimate, and the contract should specify whether a re-proration will occur after final tax bills issue.

**Failure to address re-proration language is a silent liability.** If the actual tax bill comes in significantly higher than the 110 percent estimate, the buyer absorbs the difference unless the contract includes a re-proration clause. Agents representing buyers in areas with recent reassessments — particularly south suburban Cook County — should advocate for re-proration protection.

Seller credits toward buyer closing costs are another area requiring precision. FHA, VA, and conventional loans each cap seller contributions differently (6 percent, 4 percent, and 3 to 9 percent depending on down payment). An agent who negotiates a 7 percent seller credit on a conventional loan with 10 percent down has just written an unworkable term that the lender will reject, sending the deal back to negotiation days before closing.
