## Understanding Massachusetts Commission & Licensing Rules 2026

The Massachusetts commission & licensing rules 2026 represent a regulatory framework that demands precision from every licensee in the Commonwealth. Following the NAR settlement implementation and state-level amendments to 254 CMR 2.00, agents face tighter documentation requirements, more explicit compensation disclosure obligations, and heightened scrutiny from the Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons. Ignoring these rules does not just risk a fine—it puts entire transactions and careers at stake.

Massachusetts operates under a regulatory structure that differs meaningfully from most states. The Board of Registration, housed within the Division of Professional Licensure, enforces rules codified in Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 112, Sections 87PP through 87DDD, and the administrative regulations in 254 CMR. Every agent and broker operating in the state must understand both the statutory framework and the practical documentation that keeps them compliant.

## Licensing Structure and Renewal Requirements

Massachusetts issues two primary license categories: Salesperson and Broker. A salesperson license requires completion of 40 hours of pre-licensing education from a Board-approved school, passage of the state and national portions of the PSI exam, and affiliation with a licensed broker. Broker applicants must hold an active salesperson license for at least three years and complete an additional 40 hours of broker-specific coursework.

**License renewal occurs on a biennial cycle, and the Board does not grant grace periods for expired licenses.** Salespersons must complete 12 hours of continuing education per renewal cycle, which now includes a mandatory NAR ethics training module. Brokers must complete 12 hours as well, with at least 3 hours dedicated to supervisory responsibilities and Massachusetts-specific regulatory updates.

| Requirement | Salesperson | Broker |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-licensing education | 40 hours | 40 additional hours |
| Experience prerequisite | None | 3 years as active salesperson |
| CE per renewal cycle | 12 hours | 12 hours (3 hrs supervision) |
| Renewal period | Every 2 years | Every 2 years |
| Exam administrator | PSI | PSI |

Failing to renew on time means your license lapses. Operating on a lapsed license constitutes unlicensed activity under M.G.L. Chapter 112 Section 87RR, carrying fines of up to $2,500 per transaction and potential criminal misdemeanor charges.

## Commission Payment Rules and Written Agreements

Massachusetts law is unambiguous: commission payments must flow through the broker. Under M.G.L. Chapter 112 Section 87AAA, no salesperson may receive compensation for brokerage services from any party other than their supervising broker. This rule applies regardless of whether the commission originates from a seller, buyer, cooperating brokerage, or referral network.

Since the NAR settlement took effect, Massachusetts agents must execute written buyer representation agreements before providing brokerage services. The [Massachusetts Association of Realtors](https://www.marealtor.com/) updated its standard forms in late 2024, including the MAR Buyer Agency Agreement (Form 201) and the MAR Compensation Disclosure Form (Form 206). These forms explicitly state the amount or rate of compensation the buyer's agent will receive and identify who pays it.

**On the listing side, the Standard Listing Agreement (MAR Form 101) must clearly state the total commission rate and specify whether any portion will be offered to a cooperating buyer's agent.** The shift away from blanket MLS cooperative compensation offers means agents cannot rely on MLS data alone to confirm their payment. Written confirmation—signed before services begin—is now the standard.

Seller concessions toward buyer-agent compensation remain permissible, but they must be documented in the purchase and sale agreement. Agents who verbally agree to commission splits without written documentation are [making the same mistakes that cause deals to break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) across the country.

## Disclosure Obligations and Required Forms

Massachusetts imposes layered disclosure requirements that touch agency relationships, material facts, and compensation terms. The key forms agents must know cold include:

The Mandatory Licensee-Consumer Relationship Disclosure (Form 301) must be presented at the first substantive contact with any party. This form explains the types of agency relationships available in Massachusetts—seller's agent, buyer's agent, dual agent, and facilitator—and must be signed by the consumer and retained in the transaction file.

The Agency Disclosure and Election Form (Form 302) is executed when the consumer elects a specific agency relationship. For buyer agents, this pairs with the Buyer Agency Agreement (Form 201) to formalize both the relationship and the compensation structure.

Under 254 CMR 2.05, dual agency requires informed written consent from both parties. Massachusetts permits dual agency but restricts it heavily—both buyer and seller must sign a Dual Agency Consent Form (Form 303) acknowledging the limitations on fiduciary duties. Agents who fail to obtain this consent face disciplinary proceedings and deal rescission.

| Form | Purpose | When Required |
|---|---|---|
| MAR Form 101 | Standard Listing Agreement | At listing execution |
| MAR Form 201 | Buyer Agency Agreement | Before providing buyer services |
| MAR Form 206 | Compensation Disclosure | Before showing properties |
| MAR Form 301 | Licensee-Consumer Relationship Disclosure | First substantive contact |
| MAR Form 302 | Agency Disclosure and Election | When agency relationship is formed |
| MAR Form 303 | Dual Agency Consent | Before acting as dual agent |

These forms are not optional documentation add-ons. They are regulatory requirements with teeth.

## Common Mistakes Massachusetts Agents Make

The Board of Registration publishes enforcement actions quarterly, and patterns emerge. Here are five specific errors that generate the most complaints and disciplinary proceedings.

**First: Failing to present the Licensee-Consumer Relationship Disclosure (Form 301) at first substantive contact.** Agents routinely skip this step during open houses or initial phone consultations, assuming it only matters once a formal relationship begins. The regulation is clear—first substantive contact triggers the obligation, and an open house conversation about a buyer's needs qualifies.

Second: Accepting referral fees or bonuses directly rather than routing them through the supervising broker. Even a $500 builder incentive paid to an agent's personal account violates M.G.L. Chapter 112 Section 87AAA. The Board treats this identically to undisclosed compensation.

Third: Operating under an expired or inactive license during a transaction. Agents who close a deal after their license lapses—even by a single day—face the prospect of having their commission disgorged and the transaction voided at the buyer's or seller's request. This links directly to [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) on administrative tasks that carry real consequences.

Fourth: Misrepresenting the commission structure verbally without matching the written agreement. Post-settlement, any discrepancy between what an agent tells a buyer about compensation and what the signed Buyer Agency Agreement states creates grounds for a Board complaint and potential UDAP (Unfair and Deceptive Acts and Practices) liability under M.G.L. Chapter 93A.

Fifth: Failing to maintain copies of all transaction documents for the required three-year retention period mandated by 254 CMR 2.05(4). The Board audits randomly, and missing files generate automatic violations regardless of whether the underlying transaction had any issues.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers carry explicit liability under Massachusetts law for the actions of their affiliated agents. Under 254 CMR 2.05, a broker's supervisory responsibilities include maintaining complete transaction files, reviewing all agreements before execution, and confirming that every affiliated agent holds a current, active license.

**Brokers should conduct quarterly audits of three critical areas: form completion, license status, and compensation documentation.** A single agent operating on a lapsed license exposes the entire brokerage to enforcement action. The Board has assessed fines against brokerages ranging from $1,000 to $10,000 for systematic supervisory failures.

Every buyer representation agreement and listing agreement should pass through broker review before the agent presents it to a consumer. The broker must verify that commission terms comply with current law, that agency disclosures were properly timed and executed, and that no prohibited dual agency situation exists without written consent. Relying on agents to self-police these requirements is the [same reason why checklists beat memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) in every high-stakes compliance environment.

Brokers must also maintain an up-to-date roster of affiliated agents with the Board, updating within 10 days of any agent joining or leaving the brokerage. Failure to update this roster is a frequent citation in Board enforcement actions and creates confusion about who was authorized to act on the brokerage's behalf during a given transaction.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

The Board of Registration has a graduated enforcement toolkit. For first-time paperwork violations—missing a Form 301, for instance—agents typically receive a letter of reprimand and a requirement to complete additional continuing education. Repeat offenses escalate to fines, mandatory supervision periods, and license suspension.

More serious violations carry steeper consequences. Unlicensed activity triggers fines up to $2,500 per occurrence under M.G.L. Chapter 112 Section 87RR. Misrepresentation of compensation terms can result in license revocation and civil liability under Chapter 93A, which allows treble damages and attorney's fees.

Transaction-level consequences matter equally. A buyer who discovers their agent lacked a valid license or failed to disclose a dual agency relationship can petition to rescind the purchase and sale agreement. Courts have consistently upheld rescission in these cases, leaving the agent and brokerage liable for the buyer's damages including inspection costs, appraisal fees, and mortgage application expenses.
