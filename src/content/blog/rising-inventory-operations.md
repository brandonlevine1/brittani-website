The housing market just handed brokerages a stress test, and most of them are going to fail it quietly. Active listings rose 30.6 percent year-over-year in April 2026 according to Realtor.com's Monthly Housing Trends Report, pushing national inventory to levels not seen since early 2020. More listings mean more contracts, and more contracts expose every brittle process your back office has been patching together with shared drives and good intentions.

This is not a piece about whether inventory growth is sustainable. It is about what happens inside your operation when the contract count doubles and nobody added headcount.

## The Volume Problem Nobody Budgeted For

**Most brokerages built their transaction workflows during a period of historically low inventory.** Between 2021 and early 2024, the playbook was simple: fewer deals, higher price points, lean admin teams. That math worked when an agent closed eight sides a year.

Now the math is breaking. NAR's latest Existing Home Sales report shows pending transactions up 6.1 percent nationally through Q1 2026, with Sun Belt markets like Phoenix, Tampa, and Raleigh seeing double-digit jumps. The agents who were doing eight sides are suddenly doing twelve or fourteen, and every one of those files needs disclosure tracking, deadline management, and compliance documentation.

The brokerages that scaled their ops during the lean years did so by cutting transaction coordinators, relying on agents to self-manage files, or outsourcing to part-time virtual assistants with no licensing context. Those choices are about to compound.

## Why the 2024 Settlement Changes Make This Worse

The practice changes that took effect after the NAR settlement in August 2024 added new documentation requirements at the buyer-agent engagement stage. Every buyer representation agreement is now a compliance artifact that needs to be stored, tracked for expiration, and matched to closing records. That was manageable at low volume.

At higher volume, the coordination burden multiplies in a non-linear way. A team doing 20 buy-side transactions a month now has 20 representation agreements with varying term lengths, 20 sets of state-specific disclosure obligations, and 20 potential audit points. The [Florida Real Estate Commission's updated guidance on buyer brokerage agreements](https://www.myflrec.com/) alone runs to several pages of required language.

Agents will not track this themselves. If your ops infrastructure assumes they will, you are building compliance risk into every file.

## The Counterargument: "We'll Just Hire When We Need To"

The strongest pushback I hear from broker-owners is that staffing solves this problem. Hire another TC when volume ticks up, let them go if it slows down. It sounds rational.

**Here is why it fails in practice: the labor market for experienced transaction coordinators is brutally thin right now.** The same inventory surge creating your volume spike is creating it for every brokerage in your market simultaneously. You are not the only one posting that job listing.

Even if you find the hire, onboarding a TC takes 30 to 60 days before they are handling files independently. In a fast-moving market, 60 days of growing backlog creates agent attrition. The cost of losing a productive agent because their files kept falling through the cracks dwarfs the salary of the TC you should have had in place three months earlier.

## What "Operations-Ready" Actually Looks Like at Scale

The brokerages handling this well share a few characteristics I have observed directly. They treat their transaction pipeline like a production system, not a filing cabinet. They have clear intake rules: when a contract is executed, what happens in the first 24 hours is defined, assigned, and tracked without anyone sending a reminder email.

They separate the creative work agents do — negotiating, advising, prospecting — from the procedural work that follows contract execution. The procedural layer is standardized. It does not depend on any single person remembering a deadline.

They also audit their own files proactively, not just when a state regulator or E&O carrier asks. Quarterly self-audits catch the patterns that become violations later. If you want a framework for building that audit habit, [this breakdown of common compliance gaps](/blog/compliance-audit-checklist) is a useful starting point.

## The Hidden Cost: Agent Experience and Retention

**Volume without operational support does not just create compliance risk — it destroys agent satisfaction.** A 2025 WAV Group study found that 41 percent of agents who switched brokerages cited "lack of transaction support" as a primary reason. Not commission splits. Not brand. Support.

When your top producer is spending three hours a week chasing HOA docs and lender status updates, they are doing math in their head. They are calculating whether the brokerage down the street would give them those hours back. The answer is usually yes, because somebody in your market has already figured this out.

Retention is an ops problem disguised as a culture problem. You cannot brunch your way out of a broken transaction workflow.

## Where Technology Fits — and Where It Doesn't

Technology is not a magic fix for broken processes. If your current workflow is chaos, adding software on top produces automated chaos. I have seen brokerages adopt three different platforms in two years and still lose files because nobody defined who owns what step.

The correct sequence is: define the process, assign accountability, then choose tools that enforce the process. A shared Google Drive with good naming conventions and a disciplined TC will outperform a $50,000 platform that nobody configured properly.

That said, once your process is defined, the right technology makes it possible to scale without linear headcount growth. Automated deadline tracking, intelligent document routing, and exception-based alerts mean your TC handles 40 files instead of 20 — without burning out. The [operational differences between high-volume and low-volume teams](/blog/scaling-transaction-ops) come down to this exact principle.

## The Next Six Months Will Sort the Market

Inventory is not going back down to 2022 levels. The Federal Reserve's decision to hold rates steady through Q1 2026 has kept enough buyers active that rising supply is converting to rising transaction counts, not just rising days-on-market. Brokerages that treat this as a temporary blip will be the ones scrambling in Q4.

The brokerages that win the next cycle are making infrastructure investments right now, before the pain is acute. They are defining their transaction workflows in writing. They are stress-testing whether their current stack can handle 50 percent more volume without adding headcount proportionally.
