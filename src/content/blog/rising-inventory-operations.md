The brokerages that scaled down their transaction coordination teams during the 2022-2024 slowdown are about to pay for it. Active listings have surged 30.6 percent year-over-year as of Q1 2026, according to [Realtor.com's Monthly Housing Trends Report](https://www.realtor.com/research/topics/housing-supply/), and pending sales are following suit. More contracts in the pipeline sounds like good news until your back office starts cracking under the weight.

## The Volume Spike Is Already Here

NAR's latest Existing Home Sales data from April 2026 shows closed transactions up 11 percent nationally compared to the same period last year. That number lags the contract volume hitting brokerages right now by 30 to 45 days. If you manage a team of ten or more agents, you are likely already feeling the compression in your operations workflow.

**This is not a theoretical problem — it is a staffing and systems crisis unfolding in real time.** The average transaction coordinator at a mid-size brokerage handles 25 to 35 files simultaneously. When volume jumps by double digits in a single quarter, the math breaks before anyone raises a flag.

The brokerages that thrived during the 2020-2021 boom had already invested in their ops infrastructure before it was tested. The ones that scrambled — hiring TCs at a premium, missing disclosure deadlines, fumbling contingency removals — lost agents to competitors who could actually close on time.

## Why Staffing Alone Will Not Fix This

Hiring another TC takes four to six weeks when you factor in recruiting, onboarding, and the ramp-up period before they handle files independently. The current market is not giving you that runway. Contracts are hitting desks now, and the inspection periods, title orders, and lender coordination cannot wait for your new hire to learn your file naming conventions.

Even if you do hire fast, the labor market for experienced transaction coordinators has tightened considerably. A recent survey from the Real Estate Transaction Standard (RETS) working group found that TC salaries rose 18 percent between 2024 and 2025 in the top 20 metros. That trend has not reversed.

The staffing play is necessary but insufficient. You need systems that reduce the per-file burden on every person touching a transaction, not just more people absorbing the same inefficiencies at scale.

## The Compliance Load Has Gotten Heavier

Post-settlement practice changes from the NAR litigation — specifically the requirements around buyer representation agreements and compensation disclosure — added documentation steps to every single transaction starting in August 2024. Those steps did not disappear. They compounded with existing state-level requirements.

**If your TC is still manually tracking which forms apply to which transaction type in which jurisdiction, you are one missed disclosure away from a regulatory complaint.** The Florida Real Estate Commission, for example, issued 14 percent more disciplinary actions in 2025 than 2024, with documentation failures being the leading category.

> "The Commission has observed a pattern of non-compliance with disclosure timing requirements, particularly in high-volume brokerages where administrative oversight has not kept pace with transaction growth." — FREC 2025 Annual Report, Section IV

This is not about bad actors. It is about good brokerages running outdated processes at a volume those processes were never designed to handle.

## What an Operations Stack Should Actually Do in 2026

The term "operations stack" gets thrown around loosely, but here is what it should mean for a brokerage processing 50 or more transactions per month. It should automatically route tasks based on transaction milestones. It should flag compliance deadlines before they become compliance failures. And it should reduce the number of manual touches between contract execution and closing.

Most brokerages cobble together a CRM, a transaction management platform, a shared drive, and a group chat. The connective tissue between those tools is a human being copy-pasting data and hoping nothing falls through. That worked at 30 closings a month. It does not work at 70.

The brokerages I have spoken with over the past quarter that are handling the volume increase without adding headcount share one trait: they automated the repetitive coordination layer — deadline tracking, document requests, status updates to agents — before the volume arrived. They treated operations technology as infrastructure, not as a nice-to-have line item.

## The Counterargument: "We Have Always Managed Surges"

The strongest pushback I hear from veteran broker-owners goes something like this: "We handled 2021 just fine with the team we had." Fair enough. But that argument ignores two things that have changed materially since then.

First, the compliance burden is heavier post-settlement. Every transaction carries more documentation requirements than it did three years ago. Second, agent expectations around operational support have shifted permanently. The [2025 NAR Member Profile](https://www.nar.realtor/research-and-statistics/research-reports/member-profile) showed that 62 percent of agents ranked "transaction support quality" as a top-three factor in brokerage selection, up from 41 percent in 2021.

You might have survived 2021. But your agents had fewer options then, and the regulatory environment was simpler. Treating this cycle like the last one is a bet against clearly observable changes in the market.

## The Cost of Doing Nothing Is Measurable

A missed contingency deadline does not just risk a deal — it risks an E&O claim. The average cost of a real estate errors and omissions claim settled out of court sits between $15,000 and $35,000 according to data from the Rice Insurance Group's 2025 claims analysis. One bad quarter of sloppy file management can wipe out the margin on dozens of closed transactions.

**Agent attrition is the less visible but more expensive failure mode.** When your top producers start hearing from recruiters at brokerages that offer faster, cleaner transaction support, the conversation is already half over. You do not lose agents because of commission splits alone — you lose them because closing a deal at your shop feels harder than it should.

The math here is not complicated. If your operations cannot absorb a 20 percent volume increase without degrading service quality, you have a fragility problem that rising inventory will expose.

## Where the Smart Money Is Going

The brokerages investing right now are not just buying more software seats. They are rethinking which parts of the transaction lifecycle require human judgment and which parts are pure process execution — document collection, deadline calculation, status communication, compliance verification. The latter category is where automation delivers immediate returns without sacrificing quality.

I have watched this play out across dozens of brokerages we work with at [brittani.ai](/pricing), where the focus is specifically on removing the manual coordination burden from TCs and agents so they can spend their time on the parts of the job that actually require expertise. The volume spike rewards brokerages that invested in this layer early and punishes those that waited for the pain to become undeniable.

If you are a broker or team lead reading this and your current plan for handling 40 percent more contracts is "we will figure it out" — that is not a plan. Audit your operations stack this week. Count the manual steps between ratified contract and clear-to-close. If that number has not decreased in the past 12 months while your volume has increased, you already know what needs to change. The inventory is not waiting for you to catch up, and neither are your agents.
