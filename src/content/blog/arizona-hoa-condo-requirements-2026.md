## Why Arizona HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention

Roughly 70 percent of homes sold in the Phoenix metro sit inside a homeowners association or condominium regime. That single statistic makes **Arizona HOA & condo disclosure rules 2026** one of the most transaction-critical compliance areas for every agent and broker operating in the state. Miss a form, blow a deadline, or confuse the statute that applies, and the deal can unravel with legal exposure landing squarely on you.

Arizona treats condominiums and planned communities under two separate statutory frameworks. Conflating the two is the fastest way to hand a buyer an extended cancellation window they should never have received—or worse, deny them one the law guarantees. This post breaks down both frameworks, the specific forms you should be using, and the enforcement consequences that follow non-compliance.

## The Two Statutes You Cannot Confuse

Arizona splits its HOA disclosure obligations along property-type lines. Condominiums fall under the Arizona Condominium Act, codified at [A.R.S. Title 33, Chapter 9](https://www.azleg.gov/arsDetail/?title=33). Planned communities—meaning single-family subdivisions, townhome projects, and mixed-use developments with a mandatory association—fall under the Planned Community Act at A.R.S. Title 33, Chapter 16.

| Requirement | Condominiums (A.R.S. 33-1260) | Planned Communities (A.R.S. 33-1806) |
|---|---|---|
| Resale disclosure required | Yes | Yes |
| Delivery deadline from HOA | 10 days after written request | 10 days after written request |
| Buyer cancellation period | 5 days after receipt | 5 days after receipt |
| Statutory damages for late delivery | Actual damages against HOA | Actual damages against HOA |
| Covers reserve study info | Yes | Yes |
| Covers pending litigation | Yes | Yes |

The buyer's right to cancel is not optional or waivable in the contract. It exists by statute, and any contract language that attempts to eliminate it is unenforceable. Your job as the listing or buyer agent is to make sure the disclosure package arrives, gets delivered to the buyer in a documented manner, and that the cancellation clock starts on a date you can prove.

## What Goes Into the Resale Disclosure Package

The resale disclosure package is produced by the HOA or its management company, not by the listing agent. However, agents are responsible for requesting it in a timely manner and confirming it contains the items mandated by statute. Under A.R.S. 33-1260(A) for condos, the package must include the declaration, bylaws, rules, most recent audited financial statement, current budget, reserve study summary, and a statement of any pending special assessments or litigation.

**For planned communities under A.R.S. 33-1806, the required contents mirror the condo statute but also include a disclosure of any fees the association charges for transferring ownership.** Transfer fees have become a point of contention since some management companies bundle administrative charges that exceed what the statute contemplates. If the package arrives incomplete, you have an obligation to flag the gap before presenting it to your buyer client.

The Arizona Association of REALTORS publishes the Homeowners Association Disclosure form and the Condominium/Townhouse Resale Disclosure Advisory, which are companion documents agents use inside the AAR purchase contract. These are not substitutes for the statutory resale package—they supplement it with transaction-specific advisories for your buyer.

## Key AAR Forms and Where They Fit

Agents working within the AAR contract ecosystem rely on specific form numbers that align with HOA disclosure obligations. The AAR Residential Resale Real Estate Purchase Contract includes Section 4(d) addressing HOA disclosures, and the related addenda further define the process.

The HOA Addendum (AAR form) is attached when the property belongs to any mandatory association. It establishes who orders the resale disclosure, who pays for it, and the deadlines for delivery and buyer review. The Condominium Resale Disclosure Advisory alerts buyers to their 5-day cancellation right and reminds agents that the clock does not start until actual receipt.

**If you use a non-AAR contract—common in investor transactions or commercial-residential hybrids—you still must satisfy the statutory requirements.** The AAR forms simply package them conveniently. Operating outside those forms does not relieve you of the disclosure obligation, and the Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE) will not accept "we used a different contract" as a defense for non-compliance.

For agents who manage [high-volume pipelines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), tracking which transactions require HOA disclosures versus condo disclosures versus neither is where systematic workflows prevent costly errors.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Liability for HOA disclosure failures in Arizona comes from three directions simultaneously: the buyer, the ADRE, and the broker's own errors-and-omissions policy. A buyer who never received the resale disclosure package can cancel the contract at any time before closing—because their 5-day window never began. That alone can kill a deal days before the scheduled close of escrow.

The ADRE treats disclosure failures as potential violations of A.R.S. 32-2153, which defines unprofessional conduct for licensees. Substantiated complaints can result in a letter of concern, required continuing education, probation, civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation, or license suspension. The commissioner has broad discretion, and repeat offenders face escalating consequences.

From an E&O standpoint, failing to request or deliver HOA disclosures constitutes a professional negligence claim that most carriers will initially defend but may ultimately decline to indemnify if the failure was procedural rather than substantive. The result: personal financial exposure for the agent and potentially the broker.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Arizona HOA Disclosures

**First, agents routinely order the resale disclosure package too late.** Management companies have 10 calendar days to produce the package, and some push that deadline to the last hour. If you wait until the inspection period is underway to order it, your buyer may not receive it until after other contingencies expire—creating scheduling conflicts that force extensions or terminations.

Second, agents confuse the buyer's HOA cancellation right with the inspection contingency. These are independent timelines. A buyer can approve inspections and still cancel within 5 days of receiving the HOA disclosure. Listing agents who pressure buyers to waive both simultaneously are misrepresenting the statutory framework.

Third, some agents accept a verbal assurance from the seller that "there's no HOA" without verifying title. Many Arizona properties belong to a master-planned community association even when the homeowner believes they are unencumbered. A title commitment will reveal recorded CC&Rs, but agents should also run a search through the ADRE's [HOA registry](https://boa.az.gov/) or contact the county recorder's office directly.

Fourth, agents fail to document the date of buyer receipt. Email delivery with a read receipt, a signed acknowledgment line on the AAR Advisory, or a transaction management timestamp all work. Handing a physical packet to a buyer at a showing without any record creates an impossible-to-prove delivery date if a dispute arises later.

Fifth, listing agents occasionally redact or omit portions of the resale package they consider unfavorable—pending litigation summaries, for example. This exposes the agent to fraud claims separate from the disclosure violation itself, and it will not survive even minimal discovery in a civil proceeding.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carry supervisory liability under A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(21) for the actions of their licensees. A broker who has no system for verifying HOA disclosure compliance across active transactions is building a ticking liability portfolio. Here is what your audit framework should include.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Resale disclosure ordered within 3 days of contract | Every HOA transaction | No order confirmation in file by Day 4 |
| Buyer receipt documented with date | Before cancellation period expires | Missing signature or timestamp |
| Correct statute referenced (condo vs. planned community) | File review at contract execution | Wrong AAR addendum attached |
| Package completeness verified by agent | Upon receipt from management company | Missing financial statements or reserve study |
| Cancellation period tracked on timeline | Ongoing until period expires | No calendar entry or TC tracking |

Brokers should also enforce a policy requiring agents to attach a copy of the full resale disclosure to the transaction file—not just the cover sheet. If a complaint lands on your desk 18 months post-closing, you need the complete document to mount a defense. Relying on the management company's records is insufficient because companies change, merge, or lose data.

For brokerages running dozens of concurrent closings, the tracking burden is where many [fall behind without automated systems](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory). A single missed cancellation deadline across a 50-agent office can generate a complaint that attaches to the broker's license.

## How Cancellation Rights Interact With Earnest Money

Arizona's HOA cancellation right is unconditional during the 5-day window. The buyer does not need to provide a reason, and the earnest money must be returned in full. This catches some listing agents off guard—particularly when the buyer cancels on Day 4 of the HOA review period after waiving their inspection contingency earlier in the transaction.

The release of earnest money following an HOA-based cancellation should be processed through escrow using standard mutual release procedures. If the seller disputes the cancellation, the funds may be held pending resolution, but the statute is clear: a timely cancellation within the 5-day window entitles the buyer to their deposit back. Agents who advise sellers to contest a legitimate statutory cancellation are inviting a regulatory complaint.

**Understanding how these timelines interact with [other deal-critical deadlines](/blog/where-deals-break-down) is what separates experienced agents from those who end up in mediation.** The HOA disclosure period does not pause or toll other contractual deadlines unless the contract specifically provides for it—and the standard AAR contract does coordinate them, but only if you attached the correct addendum.
