## Understanding Arizona Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

Arizona earnest money & escrow laws 2026 carry real teeth for agents who ignore the details. The Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE) and the Arizona Association of REALTORS (AAR) have built a framework that puts specific deposit timelines, disbursement rules, and escrow handling standards into both statute and contract forms. Mishandling these rules does not just kill deals—it exposes agents and their brokerages to disciplinary action under A.R.S. Title 32, Chapter 20.

The stakes are not abstract. In fiscal year 2025, ADRE opened dozens of complaint investigations tied to trust fund mishandling and earnest money disputes. For 2026, the regulatory posture has not softened, and the AAR contract revisions effective January 2026 added clarifying language around deposit timelines that every licensee must internalize.

## Statutory Framework: A.R.S. 32-2151 Through 32-2174

**Arizona Revised Statutes Section 32-2151.01 defines the fiduciary duties that govern how agents handle client funds, including earnest money.** It mandates that licensees act with reasonable care and deal honestly with all parties. When combined with A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(3), which prohibits commingling or misappropriating client funds, the statute creates a bright line: earnest money is never the agent's money to hold loosely.

A.R.S. 32-2174 specifically governs trust accounts. If a brokerage elects to hold earnest money (rather than directing it to a title or escrow company), the designated broker must maintain a trust account at a federally insured financial institution in Arizona. The account must be non-interest-bearing unless the client provides written authorization, and all deposits and withdrawals must be recorded in a trust account log that ADRE can audit at any time.

The [Arizona Department of Real Estate's Commissioner's Rules](https://azre.gov/) (A.A.C. R4-28-1101 through R4-28-1103) further specify recordkeeping requirements, including monthly reconciliations and documentation retention for five years. This five-year window means a sloppy deposit made in 2026 can surface in a 2031 audit.

## The AAR Forms That Control the Process

Arizona agents overwhelmingly use AAR standardized forms, and the earnest money lifecycle runs through several of them. The primary contract is the **Residential Resale Real Estate Purchase Contract**, and its Section 2 spells out earnest money amount, form of payment, deposit timeline, and the identity of the escrow or title company that will hold the funds.

Here is a breakdown of the key forms involved in earnest money transactions:

| Form | Number | Function |
|------|--------|----------|
| Residential Resale Purchase Contract | AAR RRPC | Establishes earnest money amount, deposit deadline, and escrow holder |
| Buyer's Notice of Cancellation | AAR BNC | Triggers earnest money return when buyer cancels within contractual rights |
| Cancellation of Contract / Earnest Money Release | AAR CC | Mutual agreement on who receives earnest money after cancellation |
| Cure Notice / Seller's Instruction to Escrow | AAR CNSI | Seller demands release after buyer default and cure period expiration |
| Pre-Qualification Form | AAR PQF | Documents buyer financial capacity, often reviewed alongside deposit amount |

Section 2a of the RRPC states that earnest money must be deposited with the escrow company within three business days of contract acceptance. Agents who treat this as a soft suggestion rather than a contractual obligation create cancellation rights for the opposing party and potential ADRE complaints.

## Escrow Handling: Who Holds, Who Disburses

**In Arizona, the norm is for a licensed escrow agent at a title company to hold earnest money, not the listing or buyer's brokerage.** This differs from states where broker trust accounts are standard. Arizona's Title Insurance and Trust statutes (A.R.S. Title 20, Chapter 15) require escrow companies to be licensed by the Arizona Department of Insurance and Financial Institutions (DIFI).

Once deposited, the escrow company becomes a neutral stakeholder. It cannot release funds to either party without mutual signed instructions or a court order. This means even if both agents agree verbally that the buyer should get the money back, nothing moves without proper executed paperwork—typically the AAR CC form with both buyer and seller signatures.

When disputes arise and neither party will sign a release, the escrow company may interplead the funds into court under A.R.S. 12-1801. This deposits the money with the court and lets a judge decide. Agents should understand that interpleader actions can take months, and neither party has access to the funds during that period. For a comparison of how [escrow regulations work in other states](/blog/washington-escrow-regulations-2026), the differences in holder identity and disbursement triggers are significant.

## Liability When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences for mishandling earnest money in Arizona fall into three categories: regulatory discipline, civil liability, and deal collapse.

**ADRE can suspend or revoke a license under A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(3) for commingling or converting trust funds.** The Commissioner can also impose civil penalties up to $1,000 per violation and require restitution. In serious cases, criminal charges for theft or fraud under A.R.S. 13-1802 apply—particularly when an agent diverts earnest money for personal use.

Civil liability goes beyond the agent. Brokers face vicarious liability for their agents' trust fund violations under A.R.S. 32-2155. A buyer whose earnest money was lost or mishandled can sue both the agent and the brokerage. The damages are not limited to the deposit amount; consequential damages for a lost deal, increased purchase prices, or temporary housing costs can multiply exposure.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk. Under the AAR RRPC, if earnest money is not deposited within the three-business-day window, the seller may cancel the contract by delivering written notice before the deposit is made. Agents who let that window slip hand the other side an exit they may not have otherwise had.

## Common Mistakes Arizona Agents Make With Earnest Money

**First, agents routinely miscalculate the three-business-day deposit deadline.** They count calendar days instead of business days, or they start counting from the date of the last signature rather than the date of "acceptance" as defined in the contract. The AAR contract defines acceptance as the moment the final party signs and delivers—not when the other agent sees the notification. A Friday evening acceptance means the clock starts Monday.

Second, agents accept earnest money directly from buyers without immediately delivering it to the escrow company. Even holding a check overnight in a desk drawer creates a commingling risk if the brokerage does not have a properly established trust account. The safest practice is directing buyers to deliver funds straight to escrow, but when agents receive checks, they must deliver them within the contractual timeline.

Third, agents use informal communications—text messages, verbal confirmations—to "release" earnest money without executing the proper AAR CC form. Escrow companies will not act on a text message, and agents who tell their clients "it's handled" without paperwork create confusion and complaints.

Fourth, agents fail to document the form of payment. The AAR contract requires specifying whether the deposit is a personal check, cashier's check, wire transfer, or other instrument. If a buyer writes a personal check that bounces, the agent who did not verify funds has a problem—and the seller may have a cancellation right under the RRPC's default provisions.

Fifth, agents neglect to confirm receipt with the escrow company. Dropping a check in the mail or handing it to a receptionist without getting a written confirmation of deposit leaves no paper trail. When disputes arise weeks later, the absence of a deposit receipt becomes a credibility issue in mediation or arbitration. ## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Arizona carry personal liability for their agents' trust fund handling. A.R.S. 32-2151.02 places supervisory duties squarely on the broker, and ADRE audits target brokerage-level compliance first.

**Brokers should audit these five areas quarterly at minimum:**

| Audit Area | What to Check | Risk If Missed |
|------------|---------------|----------------|
| Deposit timeline compliance | Transaction files showing date of acceptance vs. date of escrow receipt | Contract cancellation rights, ADRE complaint |
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly bank statements matched to ledger entries | ADRE fine, license suspension |
| Form execution | Signed AAR CC or BNC in every cancelled file | Disputed funds, interpleader costs |
| Agent receipt of funds | Any instance where agent received check before forwarding to escrow | Commingling allegation |
| Escrow confirmation documentation | Written receipt from escrow company in every active file | No proof of deposit if challenged |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance are gambling. A systematic file review process—whether manual or automated—is the only defensible approach. Many brokerages are now integrating [transaction coordination tools](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) to catch these issues before they become regulatory problems.

The ADRE conducts both scheduled and random trust account audits. During an audit, investigators will request the trust account journal, bank statements, reconciliation records, and individual transaction files. If discrepancies exceed $500 or persist across multiple months, the brokerage faces immediate corrective action and potential disciplinary proceedings.

## Earnest Money Disputes and the AAR Mediation Requirement

Section 8 of the AAR RRPC contains a binding mediation clause. Before either party can pursue arbitration or litigation over earnest money, they must attempt mediation through an agreed-upon mediator. If a party refuses to mediate or participate in good faith, they forfeit the right to recover attorney fees even if they prevail in subsequent proceedings.

This mediation-first structure means agents need to document everything meticulously. The party with better records—deposit receipts, communication logs, signed cancellation notices—typically prevails. Agents who skip paperwork hand the opposing side leverage in mediation by creating ambiguity about timelines and intent.

When mediation fails, the AAR contract defaults to binding arbitration unless both parties elect litigation. Earnest money amounts under $10,000 often settle in arbitration within 60 to 90 days. Larger amounts or complex fact patterns may push parties into Superior Court via the interpleader process.
