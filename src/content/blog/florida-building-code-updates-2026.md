## Florida Building Code Updates 2026: The Changes That Will Hit Your Transactions

The Florida building code updates 2026 cycle represents one of the most consequential shifts in residential construction standards since the post-Hurricane Andrew reforms of 1992. The Florida Building Commission adopted the 8th Edition (2023) of the Florida Building Code effective December 31, 2023, and several amendments taking effect in 2025 and 2026 directly impact resale transactions, not just new construction. If you hold an active license under the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR), these changes affect how you list, disclose, and close deals across every county.

This is not just a builder's problem. Agents who fail to understand the interplay between code updates, disclosure obligations, and inspection contingencies are exposing themselves and their brokerages to liability that no errors-and-omissions policy was designed to absorb.

## What Actually Changed in the 8th Edition and Its 2026 Amendments

The Florida Building Commission, operating under Florida Statute 553.73, is the sole authority for adopting and amending the statewide building code. The 8th Edition incorporated the 2021 International Codes with Florida-specific amendments, and additional rule changes filed under FAC 61G20 took effect incrementally through 2025 and into 2026.

**The most transaction-relevant changes fall into three categories: structural integrity requirements for condominiums (SB 4-D compliance), wind mitigation standards for roof-to-wall connections, and energy efficiency mandates under the updated Florida Energy Conservation Code.** Each of these generates paperwork, inspection requirements, or disclosure obligations that land squarely on your desk.

For single-family residential, the updated wind speed maps now require higher design pressures in certain coastal zones. A home that was code-compliant when built in 2005 may now sit in a zone where new construction demands impact-rated glazing or enhanced roof decking attachment. That distinction matters the moment a buyer's inspector references "current code" in a report.

## How SB 4-D Reshapes Condo Transactions

Senate Bill 4-D, signed in 2022 and now fully in its enforcement phase, requires milestone structural inspections for condominiums three stories or higher that are 30 years old (or 25 years old within three miles of the coast). The Structural Integrity Reserve Study (SIRS) must be completed by December 31, 2024, for associations that did not previously waive reserves.

| Requirement | Pre-SB 4-D | Post-SB 4-D (2026 Enforcement) |
|---|---|---|
| Milestone Inspection | Not required | Mandatory at 30 years (25 coastal) |
| Reserve Study | Could be waived by vote | SIRS cannot be waived for structural items |
| Disclosure to Buyer | Limited HOA docs | Full SIRS and inspection reports required |
| Agent Obligation | Deliver HOA docs per contract | Confirm SIRS completion; disclose known deficiencies |
| Penalty for Association Non-Compliance | None | DBPR enforcement, potential receivership |

When you list a condo unit in a building subject to SB 4-D, the buyer is entitled to the milestone inspection report and the SIRS under [Florida Statute 718.111](https://www.flsenate.gov/Laws/Statutes/2024/718.111). Failing to provide these documents during the inspection period is not just a negotiation issue—it is a disclosure failure that can unwind a deal or trigger a FREC complaint.

## Disclosure Obligations and the Forms You Must Use

Florida Realtors provides the Seller's Property Disclosure (SPD-1) form, which is the standard vehicle for communicating known material defects. Unlike some states that mandate seller disclosures by statute, Florida relies primarily on case law—specifically the *Johnson v. Davis* (1985) standard—to impose disclosure duties on sellers and their agents.

**When building code updates create new compliance gaps in existing properties, agents must determine whether those gaps constitute "known material defects" requiring disclosure.** The answer depends on whether the seller or agent has actual knowledge. An unpermitted addition that violates current code is disclosable. A roof that met code when installed but would not meet today's standards is generally not, unless an inspector has flagged it.

The AS IS Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase (FR/BAR AS IS-6) does not eliminate disclosure obligations. Agents routinely confuse "as-is" with "no disclosure required," but Florida courts have consistently held that selling as-is does not waive the duty to disclose known latent defects. If you know the property has a code violation, it goes on the SPD-1 regardless of contract form.

For transactions involving older properties where code compliance is ambiguous, consider attaching a Code Compliance Addendum or referencing the specific inspection reports in the Special Clauses section of the contract. Document everything in writing to your transaction file, because verbal disclosures carry zero weight in a FREC disciplinary hearing.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Building Code Issues

**Mistake 1: Confusing "grandfathered" with "compliant."** A property built under the 5th Edition Florida Building Code is not required to be retrofitted to 8th Edition standards absent a triggering event (substantial improvement exceeding 50% of market value, or change of occupancy classification). But agents frequently tell buyers a home is "up to code" when they mean it was up to code at the time of construction. That distinction matters in disclosure and in negotiation.

Mistake 2: Ignoring unpermitted work visible in listing photos. If your listing has an enclosed patio, a converted garage, or an added bathroom that does not appear in the property appraiser's records, you have a problem. Under F.S. 475.25(1)(b), FREC can discipline agents for misrepresentation or failure to disclose known facts. Posting photos of unpermitted space without disclosure is affirmative misrepresentation.

Mistake 3: Failing to verify four-point inspection results against code requirements. Insurance companies in Florida increasingly require four-point inspections (roof, electrical, plumbing, HVAC) for homes over 20 years old. When a four-point reveals knob-and-tube wiring or a roof with fewer than five years of remaining life, agents sometimes bury the report in the file without disclosing the findings to prospective buyers. This creates liability that follows you long after closing.

Mistake 4: Not distinguishing between local amendments and statewide code. Under F.S. 553.73(4), local governments can adopt amendments that are more stringent than the statewide code if they demonstrate a local need. Miami-Dade County maintains its own Product Approval system (Miami-Dade NOA). An agent listing property in an area with local amendments who represents the property as meeting "Florida code" without specifying local requirements is making an incomplete—and potentially misleading—statement.

Mistake 5: Relying on the buyer's agent to catch disclosure gaps. Your fiduciary duty as a listing agent is independent of the buyer's agent's diligence. If your transaction falls apart because undisclosed code violations surface during inspection, the listing agent faces the FREC complaint, not the buyer's agent. The [cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond a single commission check.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers operating under F.S. 475.245 bear direct supervisory liability for their agents' disclosure failures. A FREC investigation into a code-related misrepresentation will examine whether the brokerage had policies in place to prevent the violation. If you manage agents, here is what your compliance audit should cover.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| SPD-1 Completion | Every listing file contains a completed SPD-1 or written seller refusal | Per listing |
| Permit Verification | Agent confirmed permit history via local building department or SunBiz | Per listing |
| SB 4-D Compliance (Condos) | SIRS and milestone inspection reports in file for applicable units | Per condo listing |
| Four-Point Inspection Disclosure | Results communicated in writing to buyer's agent | Per transaction (homes 20+ years) |
| Unpermitted Improvement Protocol | Agent documented investigation of property appraiser discrepancies | Per listing with visible additions |

**Brokers should implement a transaction file checklist that flags building-code-sensitive items before a listing goes active.** Waiting until the inspection contingency period to address code issues means you are managing a crisis rather than preventing one. A system like what is described in [why checklists beat memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) is not optional for high-volume offices dealing with Florida's aging housing stock.

Training new agents on these requirements should happen during onboarding, not after their first FREC complaint. Firms that build code-awareness into their [agent training timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) see fewer E&O claims and fewer deals collapsing at the inspection stage.

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When You Get This Wrong

The consequences of non-compliance with disclosure obligations tied to building code issues operate on three levels. First, the transaction itself: a buyer who discovers undisclosed code violations post-closing can pursue rescission under Florida common law, forcing the return of the property and all closing costs. Second, FREC discipline: under F.S. 475.25(1)(b), the Florida Real Estate Commission can impose fines up to $5,000 per violation, suspend or revoke a license, and require additional education. Third, civil liability: agents can be named individually in fraud or negligent misrepresentation claims, and Florida courts have awarded damages that dwarf the original commission.

The 2026 code amendments amplify this exposure because more properties now trigger inspection requirements that generate discoverable documents. Once a milestone inspection or SIRS exists, it becomes a "known" fact for anyone in the transaction chain who has access to it. Claiming ignorance after your seller's HOA distributed the report at a board meeting will not survive even the most basic discovery process.
