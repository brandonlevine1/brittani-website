## Understanding Pennsylvania FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

Foreign sellers own more Pennsylvania real estate than most agents realize. According to the National Association of Realtors' 2025 Profile of International Transactions, foreign nationals held approximately $42 billion in U.S. residential property across just the top ten metro areas—and Philadelphia ranks consistently among them. If you list or co-broke a property where the seller holds foreign status, you need to understand Pennsylvania firpta withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 before you reach the settlement table.

FIRPTA—the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act—requires buyers to withhold a percentage of the gross sale price when the seller is a "foreign person" as defined under IRC Section 1445. Pennsylvania layers its own nonresident withholding on top of the federal requirement. The dual obligation catches agents off guard, especially when they assume the title company or settlement agent handles everything automatically.

**This is not a set-it-and-forget-it compliance task.** Agents who ignore their role in the withholding chain risk personal liability, deal cancellations, and disciplinary action from the Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission (PREC). The stakes escalated further after PREC issued updated guidance in late 2025 clarifying agent responsibilities during foreign-seller transactions.

## Federal FIRPTA Withholding: The Baseline Obligation

Under IRC Section 1445, any buyer acquiring a U.S. real property interest from a foreign person must withhold 15% of the amount realized (the gross sale price, not the net proceeds). The withholding is remitted to the IRS using Form 8288 (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Certain Dispositions by Foreign Persons) and Form 8288-A (Statement of Withholding on Dispositions by Foreign Persons). Both forms must be filed within 20 days of the closing date.

Three exceptions reduce or eliminate the federal withholding. First, if the property will be used as the buyer's residence and the sale price is $300,000 or less, no withholding is required. Second, if the price falls between $300,001 and $1,000,000 and the buyer intends to reside there, the rate drops to 10%. Third, a seller who obtains a [withholding certificate from the IRS](https://www.irs.gov/individuals/international-taxpayers/firpta-withholding) (Form 8288-B application) before closing can reduce the amount to reflect actual tax liability rather than the flat percentage.

The seller's agent should flag early—ideally during listing intake—whether the seller holds a green card, is a U.S. citizen, or qualifies as a "foreign person." Waiting until one week before settlement to discover foreign status is a recipe for a collapsed timeline, which ties directly into [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) in the first place.

## Pennsylvania's Separate Nonresident Withholding Under 72 P.S. Section 7324

Pennsylvania does not piggyback on the federal FIRPTA rate. Instead, the Commonwealth imposes its own withholding under 72 P.S. Section 7324, which requires the buyer (or settlement agent acting on the buyer's behalf) to withhold Pennsylvania personal income tax at the rate of 3.07% on the estimated gain from the sale when the seller is a nonresident individual or entity. The relevant form is PA REV-799, the Pennsylvania Nonresident Withholding statement, which the settlement agent files with the Pennsylvania Department of Revenue.

**Agents often conflate "foreign person" with "nonresident," but these are distinct categories.** A Canadian citizen who lived in a Philadelphia condo for four years may be a foreign person for federal FIRPTA purposes yet qualify as a Pennsylvania resident for state withholding purposes. Conversely, a U.S. citizen who moved to New Jersey last year is not a foreign person under FIRPTA but is a nonresident for PA state withholding. Both layers require separate analysis.

The state withholding can be waived if the seller files PA Schedule NRH (Declaration of Estimated Income) proving the gain will be zero or minimal, and the Department of Revenue approves the request before closing. Agents need to build this lead time into the transaction timeline—typically 30 to 45 days.

## Who Holds the Liability: A Comparison Table

The liability chain under FIRPTA and Pennsylvania nonresident withholding confuses many agents. Here is how responsibility breaks down:

| Party | Federal FIRPTA Liability | PA Nonresident Withholding Liability | Primary Form |
|---|---|---|---|
| Buyer | Liable for full 15% if not withheld | Liable for 3.07% on gain if not withheld | IRS Form 8288 / PA REV-799 |
| Settlement Agent | Liable if instructed to withhold and fails | Liable if acting on buyer's behalf and fails | Same as above |
| Listing Agent | No direct withholding liability | No direct withholding liability | None, but disclosure duties apply |
| Buyer's Agent | No direct withholding liability | No direct withholding liability | None, but fiduciary duties apply |
| Seller | Owes underlying tax; can apply for refund of overpayment | Owes PA income tax on gain | IRS Form 8288-B / PA REV-799 |

Note that "no direct withholding liability" does not mean zero risk for agents. PREC can pursue disciplinary action under 63 P.S. Section 455.604 for failure to disclose material facts or for incompetence in handling a transaction that falls apart due to undisclosed withholding obligations.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Foreign Seller Transactions

Five errors appear repeatedly in Pennsylvania foreign-seller deals, and any one of them can unravel a closing or trigger regulatory scrutiny.

**First, agents fail to ask the withholding question during listing intake.** The seller's citizenship or residency status is a material fact affecting net proceeds. If you wait until the title search to surface this issue, you have already lost weeks that the seller needs to apply for a withholding certificate or PA Schedule NRH exemption.

Second, agents confuse a seller's Taxpayer Identification Number with proof of non-foreign status. Having an ITIN does not make a seller a U.S. person. Only a valid Form W-9 signed under penalties of perjury—or a separate affidavit of non-foreign status (sometimes called a FIRPTA affidavit)—satisfies the buyer's due diligence requirement under IRC Section 1445(b)(2).

Third, agents miscalculate the withholding amount by applying the percentage to net proceeds rather than the gross sale price. Federal FIRPTA is explicit: 15% of the "amount realized," which means the full contract price before deductions for commissions, transfer taxes, or liens. Getting this wrong shortchanges the IRS remittance and exposes the buyer to a deficiency assessment.

Fourth, agents neglect to coordinate the federal and state timelines. IRS Form 8288-B applications for reduced withholding can take 90 days to process. The PA Department of Revenue processes Schedule NRH requests on a different timeline. Running both in parallel—starting at contract execution—is the only way to avoid a settlement delay. This kind of timeline management is exactly [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) when no one owns the compliance calendar.

Fifth, agents assume the title company or settlement attorney will catch everything. Pennsylvania is an attorney-settlement state in practice (though not strictly by statute), and most title companies do flag FIRPTA. But if the seller provides a false affidavit and the agent had reason to know the seller was foreign—say, the listing mentions "overseas relocation"—the agent's silence creates exposure.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under 49 Pa. Code Section 35.242, which requires brokers of record to supervise all licensees in their office and ensure compliance with applicable law. A foreign-seller transaction that goes sideways can trigger a PREC investigation into the brokerage's systems, not just the individual agent.

**Brokers should audit three specific checkpoints in every file involving a nonresident or foreign seller.** First, confirm that the listing agent documented the seller's citizenship and residency status at intake—ideally on a standardized internal form, not just a verbal note. Second, verify that a FIRPTA affidavit or withholding certificate application appears in the file at least 60 days before the estimated settlement date. Third, ensure the HUD-1 or ALTA settlement statement reflects the correct withholding amounts remitted to both the IRS and the Pennsylvania Department of Revenue.

| Audit Checkpoint | When to Verify | Red Flag If Missing |
|---|---|---|
| Seller residency/citizenship documentation | Listing agreement execution | Agent never asked; no form in file |
| FIRPTA affidavit or Form 8288-B application | Within 14 days of executed contract | Seller refused or delayed; no follow-up documented |
| PA REV-799 or Schedule NRH filing | 30-45 days before settlement | Settlement agent unaware of nonresident status |
| Form 8288 and 8288-A remittance confirmation | Within 20 days post-closing | No IRS confirmation in file |

Brokers who implement a standardized foreign-seller checklist—separate from their general [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist)—reduce the chance of a compliance gap reaching the settlement table. The checklist should live in the transaction management system, not in an agent's memory.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, Liability, and License Risk

The IRS does not treat FIRPTA withholding failures lightly. If the buyer fails to withhold and remit, the IRS can assess the full 15% against the buyer, plus interest at the federal short-term rate plus three percentage points, plus a penalty of up to 25% for late payment under IRC Section 6651. The buyer then has a claim against the seller—but good luck collecting from someone who has already repatriated proceeds overseas.

On the state side, the Pennsylvania Department of Revenue can assess the 3.07% withholding deficiency against the buyer or settlement agent, add a 5% per month late-filing penalty (capped at 25%), and charge interest at the rate published annually in the Pennsylvania Bulletin. For a $500,000 sale with $200,000 in gain, the state withholding owed is $6,140—not a catastrophic number, but one that no buyer wants to discover post-closing.

For agents, the professional risk is distinct from the tax liability. PREC can impose fines up to $10,000 per violation, suspend or revoke a license, or require additional education under 63 P.S. Section 455.604. A pattern of negligence in foreign-seller transactions—especially if a buyer suffers financial harm—elevates a simple compliance miss into a licensure threat.
