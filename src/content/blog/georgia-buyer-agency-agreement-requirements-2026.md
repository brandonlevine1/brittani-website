## Why Georgia Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

The rules governing buyer representation in Georgia have shifted more in the past eighteen months than in the prior decade. Understanding Georgia buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 is no longer optional knowledge—it is a practice-or-perish mandate for every agent holding an active license under the Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC). The combination of the NAR settlement terms, revised GAR forms, and GREC's existing statutory framework creates a three-layer compliance obligation that catches agents off guard with surprising frequency.

Georgia operates under O.C.G.A. Title 43, Chapter 40—the Georgia Real Estate License Law—and GREC Rule 520-1. Together, these statutes and rules define brokerage relationships, disclosure timing, and the fiduciary duties owed to buyers. The Georgia Association of REALTORS (GAR) publishes the standardized forms that most agents use to memorialize these relationships, and the 2025-2026 revision cycle introduced meaningful changes to both language and execution requirements.

If you have been treating the buyer agency agreement as a formality, this is the year that assumption backfires. GREC complaint filings related to undisclosed or improperly documented buyer agency rose 34 percent between 2024 and 2025 according to the Commission's annual enforcement summary.

## The Statutory Framework: O.C.G.A. 43-40-25 and GREC Rule 520-1-.07

**Georgia law does not technically require a written buyer agency agreement before all contact, but it absolutely requires disclosure of brokerage relationships at the earliest practicable time.** O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(a) mandates that licensees disclose, in writing, who they represent before engaging in any substantive discussion about a specific property. GREC Rule 520-1-.07 further specifies that agents must present the brokerage relationships disclosure form prior to performing any brokerage services.

The distinction matters because the NAR settlement (effective August 2024) layered an additional requirement on top of Georgia's state law: before touring a home listed on any MLS participating in the settlement, a buyer's agent must have a written agreement with the buyer that includes specific terms about compensation. This is not a GREC rule—it is an MLS participation rule—but the practical effect is identical to a legal mandate because agents cannot access MLS data without agreeing to it.

Georgia agents therefore face two triggers. First, the state-law trigger under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25: disclose the relationship in writing at earliest practicable time. Second, the MLS-rule trigger: execute a written buyer representation agreement before touring. The stricter of the two controls—and right now, that means a signed agreement before any property showing.

## The Forms: GAR F201, F202, and the Brokerage Relationships Disclosure

Georgia agents working within the GAR form ecosystem will primarily handle three documents when onboarding a buyer client.

| Form Number | Form Title | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| GAR F201 | Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement | Grants one brokerage exclusive right to represent the buyer for a defined term |
| GAR F202 | Non-Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement | Allows the buyer to work with multiple brokerages simultaneously |
| GAR F301 | Brokerage Relationships in Real Estate Transactions | Statutory disclosure of available relationship types, required before services begin |

GAR F201 remains the most commonly used form. Its 2025 revision added explicit compensation disclosure language, a defined scope-of-services section, and a conspicuous termination-date field that cannot be left blank without rendering the form incomplete. The compensation section now must state, in specific dollar or percentage terms, what the buyer will owe the brokerage—and it must clarify whether seller concessions or listing-broker-offered compensation can offset that amount.

GAR F301 is not the agreement itself but is required in tandem. Think of F301 as the menu and F201 or F202 as the order. Presenting F301 without obtaining a signed F201 or F202 before touring is the single most common compliance gap agents fall into.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Georgia Buyer Agreements

Agents who handle [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously are especially prone to these errors. The five most frequent mistakes GREC investigators flag are consistent across brokerage size and market area.

**First, agents present GAR F301 but never follow up with a signed F201 or F202.** They treat the disclosure as the agreement. It is not. The disclosure explains relationship types; it does not create one. Without a signed brokerage agreement, the agent has no enforceable commission claim and no documented fiduciary framework.

Second, agents leave the termination date blank or insert an unreasonably long term. GREC examiners look for agreements that extend beyond six months without evidence of renegotiation. A blank date makes the entire agreement unenforceable under basic contract law because it lacks a definite term.

Third, agents fill in compensation fields with vague language like "as negotiated" or "TBD." Post-settlement MLS rules require a specific, objective amount. GREC's position, consistent with O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(b)(5), is that compensation terms must be clear enough for the buyer to understand exactly what they owe before signing.

Fourth, agents use outdated form versions. GAR updates forms annually, and the 2025-2026 versions contain mandatory language changes. Using a 2023 F201 in 2026 is not just sloppy—it may omit required disclosures that expose the brokerage to liability.

Fifth, agents obtain signatures electronically but fail to deliver a copy to the buyer within the timeframe specified in GREC Rule 520-1-.10, which requires delivery of signed documents "promptly." Brokerages that rely on platforms without automatic delivery confirmation create an audit trail gap that regulators exploit.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance in Georgia operate on three tracks: regulatory, civil, and transactional.

On the regulatory track, GREC can issue a citation, impose fines up to $5,000 per violation under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(c), require continuing education remediation, suspend a license, or revoke it entirely. The Commission's disciplinary database shows that buyer-agency documentation failures are the third most common basis for formal complaints, behind trust account violations and misrepresentation.

On the civil track, an agent without a signed buyer agency agreement has an extremely weak position in any compensation dispute. Georgia courts have consistently held that real estate commission claims require a written agreement satisfying the Statute of Frauds (O.C.G.A. 13-5-30). No signed F201 means no enforceable commission—period. Additionally, if a buyer alleges that an agent provided advice or toured properties without disclosing the agency relationship, the agent faces potential liability for breach of fiduciary duty and negligent misrepresentation.

On the transactional track, a missing or defective buyer agency agreement can trigger deal cancellation. Listing agents and their brokers increasingly demand proof of a valid buyer representation agreement before accepting offers, particularly when the offer includes a request for seller-paid buyer-agent compensation. Without documentation, the offer may be rejected outright or the closing delayed while compliance gaps are remediated—exactly the kind of scenario that leads to [blown closings](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under O.C.G.A. 43-40-18(d) for the acts of their affiliated licensees. A broker who fails to ensure that buyer agency agreements are properly executed risks personal disciplinary action, even if the broker never met the buyer.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Form version | Confirm all agents use current GAR F201/F202 editions | Quarterly |
| Signed before touring | Verify execution date precedes first showing date in MLS records | Per transaction |
| Compensation specificity | Ensure dollar or percentage amount is stated, not "TBD" | Per transaction |
| Termination date | Confirm a definite end date exists and does not exceed policy limits | Per transaction |
| Buyer copy delivery | Confirm delivery timestamp in transaction management system | Per transaction |
| F301 paired with F201/F202 | Both must be present in the file | Per transaction |

**Brokers should build a weekly file-review cadence into their supervision workflow.** Waiting until closing to check for a buyer agency agreement is too late—if the agreement is defective, the commission is already at risk and the GREC exposure is baked in. A [weekly pipeline review](/blog/weekly-pipeline-review-system) that includes document verification at the "showing commenced" stage catches problems while they are still fixable.

Brokers must also maintain a written policy—ideally in the office policy manual—specifying that no agent may tour a property with a buyer until GAR F201 or F202 is fully executed, delivered, and uploaded to the brokerage's transaction management platform. GREC investigators routinely ask for this policy during audits, and its absence shifts liability squarely onto the broker.

## Practical Implementation for 2026

The shift toward mandatory written buyer agreements has forced Georgia agents to rethink their onboarding sequence. The most effective approach treats the buyer consultation as a structured event with a clear deliverable: a signed agreement before anyone gets in a car to look at homes.

Start every buyer relationship with GAR F301. Explain the three relationship types (customer, client with designated agent, client with the brokerage). Then transition directly into the F201 or F202 discussion. The compensation conversation is no longer optional or deferrable—it happens at this meeting or it does not happen at all.

Agents who resist this sequence often cite the fear of losing buyers who "just want to look at a few houses first." The data from Georgia MLS systems tells a different story: agents who present the agreement confidently at the first meeting convert at roughly the same rate as those who delay, but they eliminate the downstream compliance risk entirely. The conversation is only awkward if the agent treats it as an apology rather than a professional standard.
