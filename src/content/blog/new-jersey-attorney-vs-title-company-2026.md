## Understanding New Jersey Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026

The question of "New Jersey attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026" surfaces in nearly every transaction because New Jersey straddles the line between attorney-state and title-company-state more than almost any other jurisdiction. Unlike Pennsylvania or Connecticut, where one path dominates, New Jersey relies on customary practice, attorney review clauses, and overlapping regulatory frameworks that leave agents confused about what is legally required versus what is merely expected. Getting this wrong exposes agents to liability claims, deal collapses, and regulatory scrutiny from the New Jersey Real Estate Commission.

New Jersey is classified as an "attorney review" state rather than a strict "attorney-only" state. The distinction matters enormously. No statute explicitly mandates that an attorney attend the closing table, yet the standard New Jersey Association of Realtors (NJAR) contract — Form 118 — contains a built-in attorney review clause that makes legal counsel a practical necessity for both parties.

## The Attorney Review Clause: Where the Obligation Lives

**The three-business-day attorney review period embedded in the NJAR Form 118 contract is the mechanism that functionally requires attorney involvement.** During this window, either party's attorney can disapprove the contract, propose modifications, or void the agreement entirely. If neither party engages an attorney within those three days, the contract becomes binding without modification.

This clause traces its authority to the New Jersey Supreme Court's decision in *In re Opinion No. 26 of the Committee on the Unauthorized Practice of Law*, 139 N.J. 323 (1995). That opinion drew the line between what real estate agents can do — fill in blanks on approved forms — and what constitutes the unauthorized practice of law. Drafting contract riders, resolving title issues, and preparing deeds all fall on the attorney side.

The New Jersey Division of Consumer Affairs enforces unauthorized practice of law violations through N.J.S.A. 2C:21-22, which carries penalties including fines up to $10,000 and potential criminal charges for repeat offenders.

## What Title Companies Can and Cannot Do in New Jersey

Title companies perform an essential but bounded role in New Jersey closings. They search title, issue title insurance commitments and policies, prepare HUD-1 or ALTA settlement statements, and hold escrow funds. What they cannot do is provide legal advice, draft deeds, interpret contract language, or resolve disputes between parties.

| Function | Attorney | Title Company |
|----------|----------|---------------|
| Review and modify contract | Yes | No |
| Draft deed | Yes | No |
| Issue title insurance | No (unless also a title agent) | Yes |
| Prepare settlement statement | Yes | Yes |
| Resolve title defects requiring legal action | Yes | No |
| Conduct closing | Yes | Yes (with limitations) |
| Provide legal advice to parties | Yes | No |

In South Jersey, title-company-conducted closings are more common, reflecting Philadelphia-area customs where title companies traditionally ran settlements. In North Jersey, attorney-conducted closings dominate. Regardless of geography, the legal boundaries remain the same statewide.

**The New Jersey Department of Banking and Insurance (DOBI) regulates title companies under N.J.S.A. 17:46B-1 et seq., the Title Insurance Act of 1975.** Title agents must hold a valid license and follow rate filing requirements. But DOBI's jurisdiction does not authorize title companies to practice law, and agents who assume a title company alone satisfies all closing requirements are making a dangerous assumption.

## What Agents Are Allowed to Do at Closing

Real estate licensees in New Jersey operate under the authority of N.J.A.C. 11:5-6, the regulations governing licensed activities. Agents can present approved standard forms, explain general transaction timelines, and coordinate between parties. They cannot interpret legal documents, advise clients on contract modifications, or draft any legal instrument.

The NJAR Form 118 and Form 125 (the counteroffer form) represent the boundaries of what agents fill in. Any modification beyond those form fields — including rider language, contingency wording, or non-standard clauses — requires attorney involvement. When agents draft custom language, they risk both a complaint to the Real Estate Commission and an unauthorized practice of law referral.

Agents should also understand that their role at the closing table is observational and supportive, not directive. Handing a client a pen and saying "sign here" while explaining what the document means crosses the line into legal advice territory. The safest practice is to defer all document explanations to the attorney or, where no attorney is present, to remain silent on legal implications.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make Regarding Attorney and Title Requirements

**First, agents frequently tell clients that attorney representation is "optional" without explaining the consequences.** While technically no statute forces a buyer to hire an attorney, failing to do so means the attorney review period passes without protection. When problems surface later — boundary disputes, undisclosed liens, contract ambiguities — the client has no recourse through attorney review and may direct their frustration (and their lawsuit) toward the agent who minimized the need for counsel.

Second, agents in South Jersey sometimes assume that because the title company is "handling everything," no attorney involvement is needed at all. Title companies cannot prepare deeds in New Jersey without attorney supervision. If a title company prepares a deed without attorney oversight, both the title company and the agent who facilitated that arrangement face regulatory exposure.

Third, agents confuse the attorney review period deadline with the inspection contingency deadline. These are separate timelines with separate consequences. Missing the three-business-day attorney review window eliminates contract modification rights entirely, while inspection deadlines vary by contract terms. Conflating the two leads to [missed deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) that kill deals or expose clients to binding terms they never reviewed.

Fourth, agents occasionally recommend specific attorneys without disclosing referral relationships. N.J.A.C. 11:5-6.1 requires disclosure of any financial interest or referral arrangement. RESPA Section 8 also prohibits kickbacks for settlement service referrals. The penalty structure includes fines, license suspension, and potential federal liability.

Fifth, some agents attempt to "speed up" closings by coordinating directly with the title company to schedule a closing date before attorney review concludes. This creates chaos when the attorney disapproves or modifies the contract after the closing date is already set, undermining [transaction transparency for clients](/blog/transaction-transparency-for-clients) and damaging the agent's credibility.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.1 for the conduct of their affiliated licensees. A single agent who drafts unauthorized contract language or misrepresents attorney requirements can expose the entire brokerage to disciplinary action by the New Jersey Real Estate Commission.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Frequency |
|-------------|---------------|-----------|
| Attorney review compliance | Confirm review period tracked on every file | Every transaction |
| Form usage | Verify only NJAR-approved forms (118, 125) used | Monthly sample |
| Custom language | Flag any rider or addendum not drafted by attorney | Every transaction |
| Referral disclosures | Confirm RESPA-compliant disclosure on file | Quarterly review |
| Closing coordination | Verify closing not scheduled before attorney review concludes | Every transaction |

Brokers should implement a file review protocol that flags transactions where no attorney appears on either side. While not technically illegal, these files carry elevated risk. A [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) that includes attorney engagement confirmation helps ensure nothing slips between contract execution and closing.

Failure to supervise can result in fines against the broker personally, suspension of the brokerage license, and civil liability if a client suffers damages traceable to inadequate oversight. The Real Estate Commission has historically taken supervisory failures seriously, particularly when a pattern of non-compliance emerges across multiple transactions.

## Liability When Things Go Wrong

If an agent advises a client to skip attorney review and the client later discovers a material contract defect, the agent faces professional liability exposure. Errors and omissions insurance may cover the defense costs, but policy exclusions often apply to acts that constitute unauthorized practice of law. This means the agent could be personally liable for damages without insurance protection.

Deal cancellations triggered by attorney review disputes are not the agent's fault — they are a contractual right. But when an agent fails to properly calendar the three-day window, or fails to inform the client that the period is running, the resulting fallout can include commission disputes, client complaints to the Commission, and reputational damage that [costs far more than the blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) itself.

The [New Jersey Real Estate Commission](https://www.njconsumeraffairs.gov/rec/) publishes disciplinary actions quarterly. Agents found to have engaged in unauthorized practice of law or failed to disclose material information about closing requirements appear in these public records, which are searchable by consumers and competing brokerages.
