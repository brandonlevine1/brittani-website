## Understanding Washington Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026

Wire fraud losses in real estate topped $446 million nationally in 2023, according to FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center data, and Washington state ranked among the top ten for reported incidents. The Washington wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 framework places specific duties on agents, brokers, and escrow officers to warn consumers before funds move. Failing to meet these obligations exposes licensees to disciplinary action, civil liability, and broken deals.

Washington's approach blends statutory duty under RCW 18.86 (the Real Estate Brokerage Relationships Act) with industry-standard forms developed by the Northwest Multiple Listing Service. The Washington Department of Licensing (DOL) treats wire fraud advisories as part of the agent's fiduciary duty of care, meaning omission is not merely a best-practice failure but a potential license violation. Agents who treat this as optional paperwork are misreading the regulatory environment.

## The Legal Framework: RCW 18.86 and DOL Guidance

**Washington's statutory foundation for wire fraud duties flows from RCW 18.86.050, which requires agents to exercise reasonable skill and care in all aspects of a transaction.** The DOL's 2025 advisory bulletin specifically identified wire fraud warnings as falling within this statutory duty. While no single statute uses the phrase "wire fraud advisory," the regulatory interpretation is clear: agents who fail to warn clients about wire fraud risks are breaching their duty of care.

The Washington Real Estate Commission, operating under the DOL, has signaled through continuing education requirements and disciplinary precedent that wire fraud prevention is a licensee obligation rather than an optional courtesy. WAC 308-124C outlines standards of practice, and the Commission has cited these standards in at least two published disciplinary actions involving agents who failed to provide adequate warnings before closing.

Escrow agents operating under the Escrow Agent Registration Act (RCW 18.44) carry their own independent obligations, but that does not relieve real estate licensees of their duties. The two frameworks operate in parallel, not as substitutes.

## Required Forms and How to Use Them

The NWMLS Form 22WF (Wire Fraud Advisory) is the primary document agents use to satisfy their disclosure obligation. This form explicitly warns buyers and sellers that wire instructions can be intercepted, advises them to verify all wiring details by phone using a number they independently locate, and confirms they received this warning before sending funds.

Form 22WF must be signed by all parties and retained in the transaction file. The timing matters: agents should present this form at or before the mutual acceptance stage, not days before closing when funds are already being assembled. Presenting the form at the last minute undermines its protective purpose and creates liability gaps if a client is already compromised.

| Form | Purpose | When to Present | Who Signs |
|------|---------|----------------|-----------|
| NWMLS Form 22WF | Wire Fraud Advisory | At or before mutual acceptance | All parties |
| NWMLS Form 22E | Closing entity selection | During offer preparation | Buyer (typically) |
| Firm-specific wire protocol | Internal compliance | At onboarding | Agent acknowledgment |

Some brokerages supplement Form 22WF with proprietary wire fraud acknowledgment documents. These additional forms do not replace the NWMLS standard but layer on firm-specific protocols such as callback verification procedures or designated communication channels for wiring information.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**The consequences of skipping wire fraud protocols range from license discipline to six-figure civil judgments.** The DOL can impose fines, require additional education, suspend, or revoke a license under RCW 18.85.361 for violations of the standards of practice. A single omission on a transaction where fraud actually occurs can trigger all of these simultaneously.

Civil liability is the larger financial exposure. If a buyer loses $400,000 to a business email compromise attack and the agent never provided Form 22WF or any verbal warning, the agent faces a negligence claim with strong plaintiff footing. E&O carriers have begun inserting wire fraud protocol compliance as a condition of coverage, meaning agents who skip the form may find themselves uninsured at the moment they need coverage most.

Deal cancellation is also on the table. When fraud occurs mid-transaction and the buyer's funds are unrecoverable, the deal collapses. The listing agent's seller loses their buyer, timelines reset, and the market position may have shifted. Everyone loses, and the fingers point at whoever broke the chain of warnings.

For additional context on how [earnest money handling intersects with wire protocols](/blog/washington-earnest-money-rules-2026), agents should understand that the same verification standards apply to initial deposits wired to escrow.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Wire Fraud Prevention

The first and most frequent error is treating Form 22WF as closing paperwork rather than an early-transaction document. Agents who bundle it into the signing stack three days before closing have already missed the window where the warning actually protects their client. By that point, buyers may have already received and acted on fraudulent instructions.

The second mistake is relying on email to transmit legitimate wire instructions. Agents who forward wiring details from the escrow company to clients via email are creating exactly the vulnerability that fraudsters exploit. The correct protocol is to advise clients to call the escrow company directly using a phone number from the company's official website, never from an email.

Third, agents fail to document verbal warnings. A phone call telling the buyer to "be careful with wire instructions" means nothing in a courtroom without documentation. The form exists for a reason: it creates a dated, signed record that the warning occurred.

Fourth, some agents assume the escrow officer handles everything and that their own advisory duty is satisfied by selecting a reputable closing company. This misreads the law. The agent's duty under RCW 18.86 is independent of the escrow company's obligations under RCW 18.44.

Fifth, agents operating on teams sometimes assume the transaction coordinator or team lead handled the wire fraud advisory. Delegation without verification is a compliance gap. The licensed agent whose name appears on the transaction bears the duty regardless of internal team structure.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

**Designated brokers carry supervisory liability under RCW 18.85.275 and must ensure their agents comply with wire fraud protocols on every transaction.** This is not a suggestion; it is a statutory obligation that flows from the broker's duty to supervise licensees affiliated with their firm.

Brokers should build wire fraud compliance into their transaction audit checklist. At minimum, the audit should confirm that Form 22WF was signed by all parties, that it was presented before or at mutual acceptance, and that the file contains no evidence the agent transmitted wire instructions via email.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag if Missing |
|------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Form 22WF signed and dated | Every transaction | Immediate compliance gap |
| No wire instructions sent via agent email | Spot-check quarterly | Potential liability exposure |
| Agent wire fraud training current | Annual review | Firm policy violation |
| Firm wire protocol acknowledgment on file | At agent onboarding | Supervisory failure |
| Client callback verification documented | Every funded transaction | Protocol breakdown |

Brokers who discover gaps should not wait for a complaint. Corrective action plans, additional training requirements, and escalating discipline for repeat offenders protect the firm and demonstrate good faith if a claim eventually surfaces. The DOL looks favorably on firms that self-police rather than ignoring systemic failures.

Washington's [agency disclosure requirements](/blog/washington-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) already place documentation burdens on brokers, and wire fraud compliance should be treated with the same rigor as those fiduciary disclosures.

## How Wire Fraud Actually Happens in Washington Transactions

The typical attack pattern has not changed much since 2020, but it has grown more sophisticated in targeting. A bad actor compromises an email account belonging to the agent, escrow officer, or one of the parties. They monitor the conversation, learn the deal timeline, and send a spoofed email with altered wire instructions at the exact moment the buyer expects to receive them.

In Washington, the risk concentrates around the final funding stage when buyers wire their down payment and closing costs to escrow. The amounts are large, the timeline is tight, and buyers are emotionally invested in not delaying closing. This psychological pressure makes them less likely to question wire instructions that look legitimate.

The FBI's Seattle field office has specifically flagged Washington's high-volume markets — King County, Pierce County, and Clark County — as frequent targets. Agents working in these areas should treat wire fraud prevention not as a compliance checkbox but as active client protection during the most financially vulnerable moment of the transaction.
