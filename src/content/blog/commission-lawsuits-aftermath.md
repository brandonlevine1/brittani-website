## The Settlement Didn't Kill Buyer Agency — It Exposed Who Was Coasting

The Sitzer/Burnett verdict and the resulting [NAR settlement agreement](https://www.nar.realtor/the-facts/nar-settlement-faqs) did not destroy the real estate industry. What they did was remove the single mechanism that allowed marginal agents to collect full commissions without articulating their value. Two years into the new rules, the data is clear: agents who can demonstrate specific, contractual value to buyers are thriving, and those who relied on the MLS cooperative compensation field as an unspoken entitlement are either adapting or exiting.

NAR's own membership rolls tell the story. From a peak of 1.56 million in October 2022, membership fell to approximately 1.45 million by early 2026. That is not a collapse. It is a correction concentrated among agents who closed fewer than four transactions per year.

## The Structural Shift Everyone Predicted (and Few Prepared For)

**The mandatory buyer representation agreement is the single biggest operational change most brokerages have faced since the TRID disclosure timelines took effect in 2015.** Before August 2024, a buyer could walk into an open house, start working with the listing agent's colleague, and never sign anything until contract. Now, a written agreement must be in place before any touring begins.

This sounds simple on paper. In practice, it means every buyer-facing agent needs a polished value proposition, a clear fee structure, and the administrative discipline to execute agreements before showing a single property. The brokerages that had already trained agents on [proper agency disclosures](/blog/texas-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) adapted fastest because their teams already had a disclosure-first workflow.

The ones caught flat-footed were the shops where agents treated paperwork as an afterthought — something to worry about once a buyer got serious. That era is over.

## Who Is Winning and Why

Two models are emerging as clear winners in the post-settlement market. The first is the high-service team that bundles transaction coordination, market analysis, and negotiation into a flat or tiered fee. The second is the lean solo agent who charges a lower percentage but communicates exactly what that percentage covers.

| Model | Typical Buyer Fee Structure | Value Proposition | Avg. Transactions per Agent (2025) |
|-------|---------------------------|-------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Full-Service Team | 2.5%-3% or flat fee ($8K-$15K) | End-to-end coordination, inspections, timelines managed | 18-24 |
| Lean Solo Agent | 1.5%-2% or hourly + closing bonus | Advisory and negotiation only; client handles admin | 12-16 |
| Discount/Rebate Brokerage | 1% or rebate model | MLS access, contract writing, minimal guidance | 30+ (high volume, low touch) |
| Struggling Traditional Agent | "Whatever the seller offers" | Undefined; reactive | 4-6 (declining) |

The struggling traditional model is not viable anymore because "whatever the seller offers" is no longer a valid answer to the question a buyer now must ask: what am I paying you, and why?

## The Counterargument Worth Taking Seriously

The strongest pushback I hear from veteran brokers goes something like this: the settlement harmed consumers more than it helped them because buyers — especially first-time buyers — now face friction in hiring representation, and some are going unrepresented as a result. There is evidence supporting this concern. A RealTrends analysis from Q1 2026 noted that unrepresented buyer transactions rose from roughly 10% to 14% nationally.

That is a real problem, and I do not dismiss it. But the solution is not to revert to opaque compensation structures. The solution is better communication, clearer agreements, and services that justify the cost transparently. The old system subsidized bad agents at the expense of consumer understanding. A 4-point increase in unrepresented buyers is a transition cost, not a permanent condition — particularly as buyer education improves and agents get better at articulating their role before the first showing.

## What the Data Says About Seller Concessions

**Sellers did not stop contributing to buyer-agent compensation — they just started doing it differently.** According to data aggregated by Redfin and reported in their Q4 2025 market update, approximately 62% of closed transactions still involved some form of seller concession that partially or fully covered the buyer-agent fee. The mechanism changed; the economics shifted less dramatically than doomsayers predicted.

| Metric | Pre-Settlement (2023) | Post-Settlement (2025-2026) |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------------|
| Avg. buyer-agent compensation | 2.55% | 2.30% (when paid via concession) |
| Transactions with seller concession toward buyer agent | ~88% (via MLS field) | ~62% (via contract negotiation) |
| Buyers paying agent directly (no seller concession) | ~5% | ~22% |
| Avg. days on market (national) | 34 | 38 |

The 26-point drop in seller-funded buyer-agent compensation represents real money leaving agents' pockets in aggregate. But per-transaction, the decline is modest — roughly 25 basis points for agents who successfully negotiate concessions into the purchase agreement. The agents losing out are the ones who never learned to negotiate this term because they never had to.

## Operational Adaptations That Actually Work

The brokerages pulling ahead share a few operational traits. First, they have systematized their onboarding process so that buyer representation agreements are discussed at initial consultation, not as a last-minute hurdle. Second, they have invested in [training new agents on timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) and compliance from day one rather than treating administrative competence as something learned through osmosis.

Third — and this is where the real differentiation happens — winning teams have separated the advisory role from the administrative role. The agent focuses on client relationship, negotiation, and market expertise. The transaction management, deadline tracking, and [multi-deal coordination](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) lives in a system rather than in someone's memory.

This separation is not new. Top producers have used transaction coordinators for years. What is new is that the economic pressure from compressed margins makes this separation mandatory rather than optional.

## The Margin Pressure Is Real, So Stop Pretending Otherwise

**A 25 basis-point decline in average compensation does not sound catastrophic until you do the math at scale.** For a team closing 200 transactions annually at an average price of $425,000, that is approximately $212,500 less gross commission income per year. That number forces hard conversations about headcount, marketing spend, and which operational tasks deserve human attention versus automation.

This is where the broker's role becomes genuinely strategic. The brokerages that will dominate the next five years are the ones building infrastructure that lets agents spend 80% of their time on revenue-generating activities — client acquisition, negotiation, showing property — and 20% or less on administration. The math does not work any other way when margins tighten.

## Where This Goes Next

The DOJ has made clear that it is watching the industry's adaptation closely. The consent decree with NAR includes ongoing monitoring provisions, and several state legislatures are considering additional disclosure requirements for buyer representation agreements. Agents who treat the August 2024 changes as the final word rather than the opening chapter are going to get caught flat-footed again.

The agents and brokers who are winning right now share one trait: they treat administrative excellence as a competitive advantage rather than a cost center. They are not manually chasing deadlines or [wondering where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) — they have systems that handle the operational load so they can focus on the relationship and negotiation skills that justify their fee in a world where clients now see exactly what they are paying. Tools like [brittani.ai](https://brittani.ai/pricing) exist precisely for this moment, handling the transaction coordination layer so that agents can put their energy where it actually earns the commission they now have to explicitly defend.

The brokerages that build this infrastructure now — whether through dedicated TCs, intelligent automation, or both — will absorb the market share shed by the agents who could not articulate their value when someone finally asked them to.
