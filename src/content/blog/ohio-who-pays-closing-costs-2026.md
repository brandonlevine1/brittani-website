## The Question Every Ohio Agent Should Be Able to Answer Cold

Understanding **Ohio who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026** is not optional knowledge for licensed agents — it is the baseline for writing clean offers and setting accurate net-sheet expectations. Misquoting cost allocation kills deals, triggers license complaints, and erodes client trust faster than almost any other transactional error. Ohio does not have a single statute that assigns every closing cost to one party, which means the purchase contract and local custom drive most of the allocation.

The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing (under the Ohio Department of Commerce) enforces agent conduct through ORC Chapter 4735 and the Ohio Administrative Code 1301:5-6. Agents who misrepresent closing cost responsibilities — even unintentionally — can face disciplinary action under ORC 4735.18(A)(6) for dishonest dealing and ORC 4735.18(A)(9) for failure to disclose material facts. This article breaks down exactly what each party typically pays, which forms control the allocation, and where agents and brokers most often get it wrong.

## The Forms That Control Allocation

Ohio agents primarily use forms published by the Ohio Association of Realtors (OAR) or local board equivalents. The two forms most relevant to closing cost allocation are:

OAR Form 101 (Residential Purchase Agreement) contains Section 9, which addresses closing costs, credits, and seller concessions. This section includes pre-printed language assigning standard costs and blank fields for negotiated credits. When agents leave Section 9 incomplete or contradicted by a separate addendum, title companies flag the discrepancy — and deals stall.

OAR Form 102 (Counter Offer) and OAR Form 105 (Addendum to Purchase Agreement) are where renegotiated cost splits typically land after the initial offer. Agents should reference the exact line items being reallocated rather than writing vague language like "seller to pay buyer's closing costs." A better practice is specifying dollar caps and identifying which costs the credit applies toward, because [deals break down at exactly this point](/blog/where-deals-break-down) when lenders reject open-ended credits that exceed their allowable limits.

## Ohio Conveyance Fees and Transfer Taxes — The Non-Negotiable Line

ORC 322.02 establishes the mandatory real property conveyance fee. The base rate is $1 per $1,000 of the property's sale price (after subtracting the first $100,000 in some county permissive tax structures). Counties may levy an additional permissive fee under ORC 322.02(C), and some municipalities impose their own transfer taxes on top.

> "The fee shall be charged to and collected from the grantor..." — ORC 322.02(A)

This statutory assignment means the seller pays the conveyance fee unless the contract explicitly shifts it to the buyer. Even when parties agree contractually to reassign it, the county auditor still collects from the grantor's proceeds at closing. Agents who promise buyers they won't owe transfer tax need to verify their local municipality hasn't enacted a separate buyer-side fee — Cleveland Heights and Lakewood, for example, have historically imposed additional local transfer obligations.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make With Closing Cost Allocation

**Mistake 1: Writing "seller to pay all closing costs" without a dollar cap.** Lenders — particularly FHA and VA — cap seller concessions at specific percentages (typically 3-6% depending on loan type and down payment). When the contract exceeds these caps, the lender rejects the credit and the deal must be renegotiated or falls apart. Agents should always specify a maximum dollar amount and tie credits to eligible cost categories.

Mistake 2: Failing to update the Closing Disclosure review. Ohio attorneys or title companies prepare the CD, but the agent has a fiduciary duty under OAC 1301:5-6-05 to ensure their client understands the costs. Agents who never review the preliminary CD with their client before the closing date expose themselves to post-closing complaints. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends well beyond the single transaction — it includes referral damage and potential board complaints.

Mistake 3: Confusing "seller concessions" with "seller credits toward purchase price." These are not interchangeable terms. A seller concession reduces the buyer's out-of-pocket closing costs. A price reduction lowers the purchase price (and potentially the appraised value baseline). Mixing these up on OAR Form 101 creates underwriting confusion and can trigger a second appraisal if the lender suspects price inflation.

Mistake 4: Ignoring county-specific customs on title insurance payment. In northeast Ohio counties (Cuyahoga, Summit, Lake), sellers customarily pay the owner's title policy. In some central and southern Ohio counties, the buyer pays. Agents relocating clients across county lines who assume uniform custom create inaccurate net sheets and angry clients at closing.

Mistake 5: Not documenting verbal agreements in writing. Ohio's Statute of Frauds (ORC 1335.05) requires real estate agreements to be in writing. A verbal agreement that "the seller will cover the termite inspection" means nothing if it never makes it onto Form 105 or into a signed addendum. Agents who rely on "we agreed on the phone" have zero enforceability.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under ORC 4735.18(H) for their agents' transactional conduct. A closing cost error that results in a buyer shortfall at the closing table reflects directly on the brokerage. Here is what managing brokers should be reviewing:

Every purchase agreement leaving the office should have Section 9 of OAR Form 101 fully completed — no blank lines, no ambiguous language. Brokers who spot "seller pays closing costs" without a dollar figure should reject the form before submission. This single audit step prevents more deal collapses than almost any other compliance check.

Preliminary net sheets prepared by agents should be cross-referenced against the title company's preliminary CD at least five days before closing. Brokers should require agents to submit proof (email confirmation or file note) that they walked the client through cost allocation before the final walkthrough. This protects the brokerage if a post-closing complaint is filed with the Ohio Division of Real Estate.

| Audit Point | Frequency | Risk if Skipped |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Section 9 completion on Form 101 | Every offer | Deal renegotiation, client shortfall |
| Seller concession cap verification | Every financed offer | Lender rejection, delayed closing |
| CD review confirmation in file | 5 days pre-close | License complaint, E&O claim |
| Conveyance fee accuracy on net sheet | Every listing | Seller surprise, trust account discrepancy |
| Addendum language for cost shifts | Every counter | Unenforceability under ORC 1335.05 |

Brokers who build these checkpoints into their [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) reduce compliance exposure significantly. The Ohio Division of Real Estate has increased auditing frequency since 2024, and sloppy file documentation is the most common trigger for formal investigations.

## Liability, Fines, and What Happens When It Goes Wrong

The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing can impose penalties ranging from a formal reprimand to license revocation under ORC 4735.051. Monetary fines can reach $2,500 per violation for individuals. Brokerages face separate fines and potential trust account audits if closing cost misrepresentations resulted in improper fund disbursement.

Beyond regulatory action, agents face civil liability. Ohio courts have upheld negligent misrepresentation claims against agents who provided inaccurate closing cost estimates that caused buyers to arrive at closing without sufficient funds. The buyer's damages typically include costs of extending rate locks, additional rent paid due to delayed closings, and occasionally earnest money forfeiture if the deal dies.

**E&O insurance does not always cover closing cost misrepresentation if the carrier determines the error resulted from willful negligence or failure to follow brokerage procedures.** Agents should confirm with their E&O provider exactly what transactional errors fall within policy coverage — many are surprised to learn that "administrative mistakes" are excluded under certain policy endorsements.

The [Ohio Division of Real Estate's enforcement page](https://com.ohio.gov/divisions-and-programs/real-estate-and-professional-licensing/real-estate-education-and-licensing/enforcement) publishes disciplinary actions quarterly. Reviewing recent cases reveals that closing cost disputes appear in roughly one out of every eight formal complaints — a ratio that should concern any agent who treats cost allocation as an afterthought.
