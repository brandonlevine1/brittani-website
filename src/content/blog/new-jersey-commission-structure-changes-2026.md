## The New Rules Are Not Optional

The New Jersey commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 have reshaped how every licensed agent in this state handles buyer compensation, listing agreements, and disclosure obligations. If you closed transactions before August 2024 without a signed buyer representation agreement, that era is finished. The New Jersey Real Estate Commission (NJREC) has made clear through updated administrative code guidance and enforcement bulletins that agents operating under the old cooperative compensation model face real regulatory consequences.

This is not a philosophical shift you can ease into over time. The settlement terms, combined with New Jersey's own statutory framework under N.J.S.A. 45:15-1 et seq. and the administrative rules at N.J.A.C. 11:5-6.1, create a compliance floor that agents either meet or risk their license over. Every showing, every listing appointment, and every offer submission now carries documentation requirements that did not exist three years ago.

## How the NAR Settlement Changed Commission Flow in New Jersey

**The core prohibition is simple: MLS systems can no longer be used to communicate offers of buyer agent compensation.** New Jersey MLS providers, including the Garden State MLS and the New Jersey MLS (NJMLS), stripped cooperative compensation fields from their platforms in alignment with the national settlement terms. This means any buyer agent compensation must now be negotiated outside the MLS ecosystem entirely.

In practice, this creates a two-track system. Sellers who want to attract buyer-represented offers can still agree to contribute toward buyer agent fees, but that agreement happens during the offer negotiation phase or through off-MLS marketing channels. The buyer agent's fee must be documented in a written buyer representation agreement before a showing occurs, and any seller contribution must be separately memorialized in the purchase contract.

New Jersey's standard forms have been updated to reflect this. NJ REALTORS released revised versions of Form 118 (Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement) and Form 140 (Commission Disclosure Statement) in late 2024, with further clarifications published in early 2025 addressing common compliance gaps.

## Required Forms and Documentation

The paperwork burden has increased measurably. Here is what agents must now have executed and in file for every transaction:

| Form | Purpose | When Required | Who Signs |
|------|---------|---------------|-----------|
| Form 118 (Revised 2025) | Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement | Before first showing | Buyer and buyer agent |
| Form 140 | Commission Disclosure Statement | At or before contract signing | All parties |
| Form 125A (Revised 2025) | Exclusive Listing Agreement | At listing appointment | Seller and listing agent |
| Addendum C-1 | Seller Contribution to Buyer Agent Fees | With offer submission | Seller (if applicable) |

Form 118 now requires a specific dollar amount or percentage that the buyer agrees to pay their agent. Vague language like "customary commission" or "to be determined" will not satisfy the requirement. The NJREC's March 2025 compliance bulletin explicitly stated that open-ended compensation terms in buyer agreements violate N.J.A.C. 11:5-6.4(a), which mandates that all material terms of representation be clearly stated before services commence.

Form 140 serves as the transaction-level disclosure that shows every party exactly who is paying whom, and how much. This form must be provided even in transactions where the seller is contributing to the buyer agent's fee, because the buyer still needs to see the full picture of compensation flowing through the deal. Agents who skip this form or fill it out after closing are exposing themselves to [the kind of deal breakdowns](/blog/where-deals-break-down) that result in regulatory complaints.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**The penalties in New Jersey are not theoretical.** The NJREC has the authority under N.J.S.A. 45:15-17 to suspend or revoke licenses, impose fines of up to $10,000 per violation, and require mandatory continuing education as a condition of license reinstatement. Since the settlement took effect, the Commission has opened investigations into agents who conducted showings without signed buyer agreements and into brokerages whose listing agreements lacked proper commission disclosures.

Beyond regulatory action, there is significant civil liability exposure. A buyer who discovers post-closing that their agent received undisclosed compensation from the seller can pursue claims for breach of fiduciary duty and fraud. New Jersey courts have consistently held that real estate agents owe their clients the highest duty of disclosure under the common law agency principles codified in N.J.A.C. 11:5-6.1.

Deal rescission is also on the table. If a buyer or seller can demonstrate that they would not have entered the transaction had they understood the commission structure, they may seek contract voidance under New Jersey's Consumer Fraud Act (N.J.S.A. 56:8-1 et seq.). This creates a scenario where an agent's compliance failure does not just cost them their commission—it unwinds the entire closing and potentially triggers damages.

## Common Mistakes Agents Are Making Right Now

After reviewing enforcement patterns and speaking with transaction coordinators handling New Jersey files, five errors appear repeatedly.

**First, agents are using outdated Form 118 versions that predate the 2025 revisions.** The older form did not require a specific compensation amount and instead allowed for "negotiable" language. Files submitted with pre-revision forms are being flagged during brokerage audits and, in some cases, returned by title companies that have updated their compliance checklists.

Second, buyer agents are presenting offers that include seller-paid commission without a corresponding Addendum C-1. The offer itself may reference a seller contribution in the financing section, but without the standalone addendum, there is no clear acceptance by the seller of the obligation. This creates an ambiguity that has already resulted in commission disputes at closing tables across Bergen and Essex counties.

Third, some agents are treating verbal agreements as sufficient for showings. The settlement and NJREC guidance are explicit: the written buyer agreement must be signed before touring any property. A text message or email confirming the relationship does not satisfy the [regulatory requirement for formal documentation](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory), and agents relying on informal confirmations are one complaint away from a license hearing.

Fourth, listing agents are failing to explain to sellers that they can no longer guarantee buyer agent compensation through the MLS. Sellers who listed properties before August 2024 sometimes expect that their listing agreement's cooperative compensation clause still applies upon relisting. It does not. Every new listing requires a fresh Form 125A with updated language reflecting the current rules.

Fifth, agents are not tracking their [deadlines and disclosure windows](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) carefully enough. The Form 140 must be delivered at or before contract execution—not at the inspection, not at the title commitment, not at closing. Late delivery is treated the same as non-delivery for enforcement purposes.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under N.J.S.A. 45:15-17(n) for the commission-related conduct of their affiliated agents. This is not an abstract risk. When an agent violates commission disclosure rules, the NJREC can and does bring parallel actions against the supervising broker, particularly when the violation reflects a systemic gap rather than a one-off error.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Risk if Missing |
|------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Signed Form 118 in every buyer file | Per transaction, before first showing | License suspension for agent; fine for broker |
| Form 140 delivered before contract execution | Per transaction | Civil liability, deal rescission risk |
| Form 125A with compliant compensation language | At listing, no cooperative comp references | MLS violation, seller complaint |
| Addendum C-1 when seller contributes | With every applicable offer | Commission dispute at closing |
| Agent CLE records on settlement compliance | Annually | NJREC audit finding, enhanced monitoring |

Brokers should implement a file review checkpoint at the offer stage, not just at closing. By the time a deal reaches the title company, a missing Form 118 or improperly completed Form 140 represents a compliance failure that has already occurred—potentially weeks earlier. The most effective brokerages in New Jersey have moved to real-time transaction monitoring where forms are verified against deal milestones as they happen.

Quarterly training sessions on commission structure compliance should be documented and retained. The NJREC's 2025 enforcement guidance specifically references broker training programs as a mitigating factor in penalty determinations. Brokers who cannot demonstrate that they educated their agents on the new rules will face steeper sanctions when violations surface.

## How Buyer Agent Compensation Actually Gets Negotiated Now

The practical mechanics of negotiation have shifted dramatically. A buyer agent who wants the seller to cover their fee must now request it explicitly in the offer, typically through Addendum C-1 or comparable language in the contract's terms section. The seller can accept, reject, or counter this request just like any other contract term.

This means buyer agents must have the compensation conversation with their clients before writing an offer. If the buyer agreement specifies a 2.5% fee and the seller only agrees to contribute 2%, the buyer is contractually responsible for the remaining 0.5%. Agents who fail to explain this to buyers upfront are generating complaints and, in several reported cases, being forced to credit their own commission to cover the gap at closing.

The [New Jersey Real Estate Commission](https://www.nj.gov/dobi/division_rec/) has published a consumer-facing FAQ on its website explaining that buyers should understand they may be responsible for their agent's fee. Agents should direct clients to this resource during the buyer consultation and document that the discussion occurred.
