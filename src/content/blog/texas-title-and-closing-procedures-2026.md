## Understanding Texas Title & Closing Procedures 2026

Texas title & closing procedures 2026 remain governed by a layered framework of state statutes, Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC) rules, and Texas Department of Insurance (TDI) regulations that together dictate how agents, title companies, and lenders move a transaction from executed contract to recorded deed. Unlike attorney-closing states, Texas relies almost exclusively on title companies and escrow officers to facilitate settlement. That distinction matters because it shifts specific procedural burdens onto agents who must ensure the right forms arrive at the right desks on time.

**Failing to understand these procedures does not just embarrass you at the closing table—it exposes your license and your brokerage to enforcement action.** TREC's rules under Title 22, Texas Administrative Code Chapter 535, set the standard of competence expected of licensees. Ignorance of the closing process is not a defense when a complaint lands on an investigator's desk.

This post breaks down the forms, timelines, common pitfalls, and broker-level audit responsibilities that define Texas residential closings in 2026.

## The TREC Contract Forms That Drive Closing

Every residential closing in Texas starts with the promulgated contract forms mandated under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.155. Agents must use TREC-promulgated forms unless the transaction falls into a recognized exception (such as custom commercial deals or transactions involving a licensed Texas attorney drafting the contract). The most commonly used form is the One to Four Family Residential Contract (Resale), designated TREC 20-17.

Paragraph 6 of TREC 20-17 controls the title and closing mechanics. It specifies who furnishes the title policy, which title company handles escrow, and the deadlines for title commitment delivery and objection periods. Paragraph 9 governs the closing date, possession terms, and the obligation to deliver executed documents to the title company.

Additional forms agents encounter during the closing phase include the TREC Amendment (TREC 39-9), the TREC Notice of Buyer's Termination (TREC 38-7), and the Third Party Financing Addendum (TREC 40-9) which dictates lender-related closing conditions. Each of these must be executed correctly and delivered to the title company to avoid last-minute surprises.

## Title Commitment: The Document That Controls Everything

The title commitment is the title company's conditional promise to issue a title insurance policy at closing, and its contents dictate whether the transaction can proceed cleanly. In Texas, title insurance rates and forms are promulgated by the Texas Department of Insurance under Title 28, Texas Administrative Code Chapter 9. Agents cannot shop rates—premiums are set by TDI.

A standard Texas title commitment contains three schedules. Schedule A identifies the parties, the property, and the type of policy to be issued. Schedule B-1 lists the requirements that must be satisfied before closing (paying off existing liens, obtaining releases, correcting legal descriptions). Schedule B-2 lists exceptions to coverage that will survive into the final policy—easements, restrictive covenants, and any title defects the company will not insure against.

| Component | What It Contains | Agent Action Required |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Schedule A | Parties, property description, policy amount | Verify names match contract exactly |
| Schedule B-1 | Requirements to close (lien payoffs, documents needed) | Confirm seller is aware of obligations |
| Schedule B-2 | Exceptions to coverage (easements, deed restrictions) | Review with buyer; object within deadline |
| Tax Certificate | Current and delinquent tax status | Confirm proration calculations |

**Agents who never read the title commitment are gambling with their clients' interests and their own license.** The objection period in Paragraph 6D of the TREC contract gives the buyer a finite window to raise concerns. If an agent misses that deadline, the buyer loses the right to object and is stuck with whatever exceptions exist.

## The T-47 Affidavit and Survey Requirements

Texas has a unique mechanism that allows parties to avoid ordering a new survey: the T-47 Affidavit. This form, promulgated by the Texas Department of Insurance, allows the seller to swear that no changes have been made to the property's boundaries, improvements, or fences since the existing survey was prepared. When paired with an existing survey, the title company can issue coverage without requiring a fresh survey.

The T-47 must be notarized and is typically prepared by the title company for the seller's signature. However, agents should advise sellers early in the process that this document will be required. If the seller has added a pool, extended a fence, or built a structure since the last survey, the T-47 cannot be truthfully signed, and a new survey becomes mandatory.

Survey costs in Texas commonly range from $400 to $700 for a standard residential lot. Under the TREC contract's Paragraph 6C, the parties negotiate who pays. If neither box is checked and the contract is silent, disputes arise—and agents bear responsibility for the oversight.

## The Closing Disclosure and TRID Timing Rules

Although TRID (TILA-RESPA Integrated Disclosure) is federal law under 12 CFR Part 1026, it directly impacts Texas closing timelines. The Closing Disclosure must be delivered to the buyer at least three business days before consummation. Any material change to the CD (an APR increase exceeding tolerances, a change in loan product, or the addition of a prepayment penalty) triggers a new three-day waiting period.

Texas agents frequently misunderstand that "closing day" and "consummation" are not always identical. Consummation under TRID occurs when the borrower becomes contractually obligated on the loan—which in Texas typically happens when the buyer signs the note at the closing table. This means the three-day clock runs backward from the signing appointment, not from recording or funding.

| TRID Milestone | Federal Requirement | Texas Practical Effect |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Loan Estimate delivery | Within 3 business days of application | Sets initial fee tolerances |
| Closing Disclosure delivery | 3 business days before consummation | Determines earliest possible signing date |
| Material CD change | Triggers new 3-day wait | Can push closing past contract deadline |
| Funding | After lender authorization | Same day or next day after signing in Texas |
| Recording | After funding confirmed | Deed filed with county clerk |

Agents who schedule closings without confirming the CD delivery date with the lender routinely cause blown deadlines. If the closing date in Paragraph 9 of the contract passes without an executed amendment extending it, either party may terminate. Understanding this interplay between [federal timing rules and contract deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) is non-negotiable.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Texas Closings

**Mistake 1: Failing to deliver the executed contract to the title company promptly.** Texas title companies cannot begin the title search or open escrow without the fully executed contract. Agents who sit on signed contracts for days—or worse, send unsigned pages—delay the entire timeline and compress the title commitment review period.

**Mistake 2: Ignoring the earnest money deposit deadline.** Under the standard TREC contract, earnest money must be delivered to the escrow agent within the number of days specified (typically 3 days after the effective date). Failure to deliver earnest money does not automatically void the contract in Texas, but it does constitute a default that exposes the buyer to termination and potential damages under Paragraph 15.

**Mistake 3: Mishandling the option period termination.** The unrestricted right to terminate under Paragraph 23 of the TREC contract expires at 5:00 PM local time on the final day of the option period. Agents who send termination notices after this deadline—even by minutes—have missed it. The buyer is then bound to perform or face default.

**Mistake 4: Failing to verify wire instructions independently.** Wire fraud remains the single largest financial crime vector in Texas residential real estate. Agents who forward wire instructions via email without verbally confirming them with the title company's escrow officer expose their clients and themselves to catastrophic loss. TREC has issued [guidance on wire verification protocols](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) and brokerages increasingly face liability for inadequate training on this issue.

**Mistake 5: Not confirming the survey or T-47 status until the week of closing.** By that point, if a new survey is needed, there is rarely enough time to complete one. This mistake alone accounts for a disproportionate number of delayed closings in the DFW and Houston metro markets.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Broker responsibility in Texas extends beyond recruiting and training. Under TREC Rule 535.2, the broker is responsible for the acts of sponsored agents performed within the scope of their license. When a closing falls apart due to an agent's procedural failure, the broker's supervision practices come under scrutiny.

Brokers should audit contract files for three specific compliance points before closing. First, confirm that the correct TREC promulgated form was used and that no unauthorized modifications were made to pre-printed language. Second, verify that all addenda are properly referenced in the contract and that deadlines (option period, financing contingency, title objection period) are calendared. Third, ensure that earnest money receipts match the contract terms and that the title company has confirmed receipt.

**Beyond file audits, brokers must enforce written policies on wire fraud prevention, document retention, and closing preparation timelines.** The cost of a single blown closing due to agent negligence often exceeds the commission earned on the transaction—a reality examined in detail in [this analysis of deal failure costs](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing). TREC can impose administrative penalties up to $5,000 per violation under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.702, and repeated violations trigger license suspension or revocation proceedings.

## Liability Exposure When Procedures Fail

Texas courts have consistently held that real estate agents owe fiduciary duties to their clients, and procedural failures during the closing process can constitute a breach. In addition to TREC disciplinary action, agents face civil liability for negligence, breach of fiduciary duty, and violations of the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA) under Texas Business & Commerce Code Chapter 17.

A DTPA claim can result in treble damages if the court finds the agent's conduct was committed knowingly. Even an innocent mistake—like failing to relay a title commitment exception to a buyer—can form the basis of a negligence claim if the buyer suffers financial harm as a result. Professional liability insurance (E&O coverage) mitigates the financial impact but does not prevent the reputational damage or TREC investigation that typically accompanies a formal complaint.

> Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.652(b)(1) authorizes TREC to suspend or revoke a license for "negligence or incompetence in performing an act for which a person is required to hold a license."

Agents should treat every closing file as a potential exhibit in a disciplinary hearing. Document every communication, calendar every deadline, and confirm every instruction in writing. The agents who [track their active deals systematically](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) are the ones who avoid becoming cautionary tales.

## Staying Current With Texas Title & Closing Procedures 2026

The regulatory environment in Texas does not stand still. TDI periodically revises promulgated title insurance forms and rates—most recently adjusting the basic premium rate schedule effective January 2026. TREC's forms committee updates promulgated contract forms on a rolling basis, and agents are responsible for using the current version from the effective date forward. Using an outdated form is a violation of TREC Rule 535.146 and can void contractual provisions or trigger disciplinary complaints.

Agents who build compliance into their daily workflow—rather than cramming before CE deadlines—consistently outperform on client satisfaction and deal closure rates. Texas title & closing procedures 2026 reward precision, punish delay, and demand that agents treat the title commitment, the closing disclosure, and every TREC-promulgated form as the binding legal instruments they are. The agents who internalize this reality close more deals, face fewer complaints, and keep their licenses intact.
