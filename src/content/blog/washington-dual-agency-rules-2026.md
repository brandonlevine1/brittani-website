## Understanding Washington Dual Agency & Designated Agency Rules 2026

Washington dual agency & designated agency rules 2026 remain one of the most misunderstood areas of state licensing law, and the consequences for getting them wrong land squarely on the agent and the managing broker. The state permits dual agency but wraps it in strict disclosure requirements, reduced fiduciary duties, and consent mandates that trip up even experienced licensees. If your brokerage handles any volume of in-house transactions, this is the regulatory framework that should keep you up at night.

The governing statute is [RCW 18.86](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=18.86), the Washington Real Estate Brokerage Relationships Act. It defines three lawful agency relationships: seller agency, buyer agency, and dual agency. Designated brokerage (sometimes called designated agency in other states) operates differently here than in Virginia or Wisconsin, and confusing the two frameworks is a common source of compliance failures.

## How Washington Defines Dual Agency Under RCW 18.86

**Dual agency in Washington arises whenever a single licensee or a single brokerage firm represents both the buyer and the seller in the same transaction.** This is not limited to a single agent writing both sides. If two agents within the same firm each represent opposing parties, the firm itself is a dual agent unless designated broker procedures are properly followed.

RCW 18.86.060 spells out what a dual agent cannot do: advocate for one party's price position over the other's, disclose confidential information from one party to the other without written authorization, or recommend terms that benefit one side at the expense of the other. The statute reduces the agent's obligations from full advocacy to a neutral facilitation role.

This is where Washington departs from states like Virginia, which has more explicit [designated agency protections](/blog/virginia-dual-agency-rules-2026) built into its statutory framework. In Washington, the managing broker must actively designate separate licensees and build information barriers, or the entire firm defaults to dual agency status.

## The Designated Broker's Role and Firm-Level Designation

Washington uses the term "designated broker" to refer to the individual who manages the firm, not to the concept of designating separate agents. This creates confusion. When two affiliated licensees represent opposing parties, the designated broker must implement procedures that prevent confidential information from crossing between the two sides.

The Washington Department of Licensing (DOL) has clarified through advisory guidance that firms must maintain written office policies specifying how information barriers operate. These policies must be documented and available for audit. Without them, any in-house transaction reverts to dual agency by default, requiring full dual agency disclosure and consent from both parties.

**Brokers who rely on informal "don't talk to each other" instructions are exposing the firm to regulatory action and civil liability.** The DOL expects written protocols, training records, and acknowledgment signatures from affiliated licensees who participate in opposing-side transactions.

## Required Forms and Disclosure Timing

Washington agents must deliver agency disclosures using the pamphlet prescribed under RCW 18.86.120. The pamphlet itself is published by the DOL and titled "The Law of Real Estate Agency." It must be provided at the earliest of: the first substantive contact, an open house sign-in, or the initial showing.

For dual agency specifically, agents must use NWMLS Form 22DA, which provides the written consent mechanism for both buyer and seller. This form must be signed before or at the time the dual agency relationship arises. It cannot be presented retroactively at closing as a paperwork cleanup item.

| Form | Purpose | Timing | Who Signs |
|------|---------|--------|-----------|
| DOL Agency Pamphlet | General agency relationship education | First substantive contact | Buyer or Seller (acknowledgment) |
| NWMLS Form 22DA | Dual agency consent and disclosure | When dual agency arises | Both Buyer and Seller |
| NWMLS Form 41 | Agency disclosure (buyer or seller) | Before services begin | Agent and Client |
| Firm Office Policy | Information barrier documentation | Ongoing | All affiliated licensees |

The timing requirement is not flexible. If a buyer's agent discovers mid-transaction that a listing is held by their own firm, the dual agency disclosure must happen before the next material step, whether that is drafting an offer, scheduling an inspection, or sharing confidential pricing intelligence.

## Liability Exposure When Agents Fail to Comply

**The consequences of non-compliance stack: regulatory discipline, transaction rescission, and civil damages can all apply simultaneously.** Under RCW 18.86.031, the DOL can impose fines up to $5,000 per violation, suspend or revoke a license, and require additional education hours. These penalties apply to both the individual licensee and the designated broker who failed to supervise.

On the civil side, an undisclosed dual agency creates a voidable transaction. Either party can seek rescission, meaning the deal unwinds entirely. Courts have also awarded damages for breach of fiduciary duty where agents failed to maintain neutrality after disclosure or where disclosure came too late to constitute informed consent.

The Washington Supreme Court addressed fiduciary obligations in dual agency scenarios in *Short v. Wells Fargo Bank*, reinforcing that reduced duties under RCW 18.86.060 do not eliminate the duty of good faith. An agent who tilts the playing field, even subtly, remains liable despite holding signed consent forms.

Insurance carriers have also taken notice. E&O claims involving undisclosed or poorly documented dual agency rank among the most frequent and expensive claim categories in Washington, according to data from major real estate E&O providers.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Dual Agency in Washington

Three to five errors appear repeatedly in DOL disciplinary actions and civil litigation. Recognizing them now saves careers and commissions later.

The first mistake is treating Form 22DA as a formality rather than a substantive consent process. Agents slide the form across the table with six other documents at signing, and clients initial without understanding what they are consenting to. The DOL has issued guidance stating that consent must be "informed," meaning the agent must explain the limitations on advocacy before the form is signed.

The second mistake is failing to recognize when dual agency arises within a firm. An agent who refers a buyer to their colleague's listing often assumes the two agents operate independently. Without documented information barriers and managing broker oversight, that transaction is a dual agency situation for the entire firm. The [agency disclosure requirements in Washington](/blog/washington-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) apply at the firm level, not just the individual level.

The third mistake is continuing to advocate after dual agency consent is obtained. Agents who tell their buyer client "I think we can get them down another $15,000" after signing Form 22DA are violating the neutrality requirement of RCW 18.86.060. This behavior generates the majority of dual agency complaints to the DOL.

The fourth mistake involves confidential information leakage. An agent who knows the seller's bottom line from a prior conversation cannot un-know it. When that agent then represents both sides, the seller's confidential pricing strategy is compromised unless the agent can demonstrate they maintained strict confidentiality. Documentation of what information was received and when becomes the agent's only defense.

The fifth mistake is relying on verbal disclosure without written confirmation. Washington law requires written consent. A phone conversation explaining dual agency, no matter how thorough, does not satisfy the statutory requirement.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers carry direct liability under RCW 18.85.361 for the actions of their affiliated licensees. This is not vicarious liability in the traditional sense; it is a direct statutory obligation to supervise. Auditing dual agency compliance should be a standing item in every brokerage's transaction review process.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|------------|---------------|-----------|
| Form 22DA presence in file | Signed by both parties, dated correctly | Every dual agency transaction |
| Timing documentation | Disclosure delivered before or at dual agency trigger | Every dual agency transaction |
| Information barrier policy | Written, signed by all licensees, current | Annually and at onboarding |
| Training records | Dual agency module completion | Annually |
| Communication logs | No advocacy language post-disclosure | Random quarterly audit |

Brokers should also flag any transaction where the listing agent and buyer's agent share office space, even if they are on different teams. Physical proximity increases the risk of inadvertent information sharing. The DOL does not accept "I didn't mean to tell them" as a defense.

File review software can catch missing forms, but it cannot catch improper timing. That requires reviewing email timestamps, MLS activity logs, and offer submission dates against the Form 22DA execution date. If the form was signed after the offer was submitted, the consent was too late. ## Distinguishing Washington's Framework From Other States

Agents licensed in multiple states frequently import assumptions that do not apply in Washington. Virginia, for example, has a statutory [designated agency framework](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) that explicitly separates agent duties at the individual level without firm-level dual agency arising. Washington's framework does not work that way. The firm is the agent, and individual licensees are its representatives.

Wisconsin offers yet another variation, requiring [specific timing and documentation](/blog/wisconsin-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) that differs from Washington's approach. Agents relocating from other markets or handling referrals across state lines must study Washington's statute independently rather than assuming reciprocity in agency law concepts.

| Feature | Washington | Virginia |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Dual agency permitted | Yes, with written consent | Yes, with written consent |
| Designated agency at firm level | Not explicitly statutory; requires broker procedures | Explicitly statutory under Code of Virginia 54.1-2130 |
| Information barrier requirement | DOL advisory guidance, not codified | Statutory requirement |
| Consent form | NWMLS Form 22DA | VAR Form 0520 |
| Managing broker liability | Direct under RCW 18.85.361 | Supervisory under VREB regulations |

This table underscores why agents cannot rely on general industry knowledge. Washington's framework places an outsized burden on the designated broker to create and enforce policies that the statute itself does not spell out in granular detail.

The 2026 regulatory environment in Washington has not relaxed any dual agency requirements. If anything, increased DOL enforcement actions over the past 18 months signal that the department is actively auditing brokerages and responding to consumer complaints with heavier penalties. Agents who treat Form 22DA as just another piece of paper, or brokers who lack written information barrier policies, are operating on borrowed time.

Washington dual agency & designated agency rules 2026 demand proactive compliance systems, not reactive paperwork fixes.
