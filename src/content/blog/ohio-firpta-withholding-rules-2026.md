## Understanding Ohio FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

Failing to identify a foreign seller before closing day is one of the fastest ways to blow a deal in Ohio. The Ohio FIRPTA withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 impose federal withholding obligations under Internal Revenue Code Section 1445, layered on top of Ohio's own nonresident withholding under Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Section 5747.071. Agents who miss either layer expose their buyers—and themselves—to IRS penalties, delayed closings, and potential license discipline from the Ohio Division of Real Estate.

This post breaks down exactly what Ohio agents need to know, what forms to prepare, and where liability lands when things go wrong.

## The Federal Layer: IRC Section 1445 and Form 8288

Under IRC Section 1445, any buyer who acquires a U.S. real property interest from a "foreign person" must withhold 15 percent of the amount realized at closing. The term "foreign person" includes nonresident aliens, foreign corporations, foreign partnerships, foreign trusts, and foreign estates. The determination is made at closing, not at listing.

**The buyer—not the agent, not the title company—bears primary statutory liability as the "withholding agent."** In practice, most Ohio closings handled through attorneys or title companies delegate the mechanical filing, but the legal obligation never shifts. The withholding is remitted to the IRS using Form 8288 (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Certain Dispositions by Foreign Persons) and Form 8288-A (Statement of Withholding on Dispositions by Foreign Persons), both due within 20 days of the transfer date.

Reduced withholding is available. If the buyer will use the property as a personal residence and the price is between $300,001 and $1,000,000, the rate drops to 10 percent. At $300,000 or below with the same buyer-use requirement, the rate is zero. Sellers can also apply for a withholding certificate on IRS Form 8288-B before closing to reduce or eliminate withholding—but processing takes 90 days or more.

## Ohio's State-Level Nonresident Withholding: ORC 5747.071

Many agents treat FIRPTA as the only obligation. That is wrong. Ohio imposes its own withholding on pass-through entities and nonresident sellers, including foreign persons, through ORC Section 5747.071. The Ohio Department of Taxation administers this, and the withholding rate is generally applied against the estimated Ohio-source income from the transaction.

The title company or closing attorney typically handles the state filing using Ohio Form IT 1140 (for pass-through entities) or by deducting the estimated tax and remitting it with the seller's Ohio IT 1040. If the seller qualifies for an exemption or can demonstrate no Ohio tax liability, they may submit documentation to avoid state-level withholding—but that must happen before funds disburse.

|---|---|---|
| Governing Law | IRC Section 1445 | ORC 5747.071 |
| Standard Rate | 15% of amount realized | Based on estimated gain |
| Primary Liable Party | Buyer | Buyer/withholding agent |
| Key Form | IRS Form 8288 / 8288-A | Ohio IT 1140 or IT 1040 |
| Filing Deadline | 20 days post-closing | Per Ohio filing schedule |

## What Agents Face If They Fail to Comply

Real estate agents are not the statutory withholding agents under FIRPTA—buyers are. But that distinction provides almost no practical protection for agents who ignore their duty to disclose known facts about a transaction. Under Ohio agency law and the Ohio Administrative Code (OAC) 1301:5-1-03, agents owe a duty of reasonable care to all parties.

**If you know or should know a seller is a foreign person and you fail to flag it, you are exposed on multiple fronts.** First, the buyer may sue for negligent misrepresentation if they face IRS penalties because no one raised the FIRPTA issue. Second, the Ohio Division of Real Estate, housed under the Ohio Department of Commerce, can initiate disciplinary proceedings for failure to disclose material facts. Third, your errors-and-omissions policy may deny coverage if you knew about the foreign status and did nothing.

From the deal mechanics side, a last-minute FIRPTA discovery routinely causes closing delays of weeks or months while the parties scramble for withholding certificates or renegotiate net proceeds. The buyer can walk if contingency periods have lapsed without resolution. Meanwhile, commissions evaporate.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make with Foreign Sellers

Mistake one: assuming a seller with a U.S. mailing address is not a foreign person. FIRPTA defines foreign status by tax residency, not physical address. A Canadian citizen who owns a rental condo in Cleveland and mails from an Ohio address is still a foreign person unless they hold a green card or meet the substantial presence test.

Mistake two: relying on the title company to "handle it" without verifying that the title company actually knows the seller's status. Many Ohio title companies do not proactively request a FIRPTA affidavit unless prompted. If no one asks the question, no one catches the issue.

Mistake three: accepting a seller's verbal claim of U.S. citizenship without obtaining the Non-Foreign Affidavit under IRC Section 1445(b)(2). The affidavit—commonly called the FIRPTA affidavit or certificate of non-foreign status—must include the seller's name, taxpayer identification number, address, and a statement under penalty of perjury that the seller is not a foreign person. Without it in writing, the buyer has no statutory protection from withholding liability.

Mistake four: failing to account for FIRPTA withholding in the listing presentation or net sheet. If a foreign seller expects to walk away with the full sale price, the 15 percent federal withholding is a rude surprise. Agents who discuss this early retain their credibility and avoid blown deals.

Mistake five: confusing withholding with the seller's final tax liability. The 15 percent is not a tax—it is an advance payment. Many foreign sellers owe less than the withheld amount and can file for a refund. Agents who explain this distinction correctly keep deals together when sellers balk at the numbers.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Ohio brokerages bear supervisory liability under ORC 4735.18 and OAC 1301:5-1-02 for the actions of their affiliated agents. A broker who fails to implement reasonable systems for FIRPTA compliance risks regulatory discipline, civil claims, and loss of trust accounts. Here is what a broker audit protocol should include.

**Every listing file involving a seller who is not a natural-born or naturalized U.S. citizen should contain either a signed Non-Foreign Affidavit or documentation that FIRPTA withholding procedures were initiated.** Brokers should include a FIRPTA checklist item in their transaction management system that triggers at the listing intake stage—not at closing.

Brokers should also verify that their preferred title companies and closing attorneys maintain updated FIRPTA procedures. A quarterly confirmation email to your title partners costs nothing and prevents liability gaps. Beyond that, training logs should show that agents received FIRPTA education at least annually, documented in the brokerage's continuing education records.

## Reduced Withholding and Exemptions Available in Ohio

Not every foreign seller transaction triggers the full 15 percent. IRC Section 1445(b) provides several exemptions that agents should understand—even though the legal application falls to attorneys and CPAs. The most common: if the property sells for $300,000 or less and the buyer signs an affidavit stating they intend to use it as a residence for at least 50 percent of the days it is in use during each of the first two 12-month periods after transfer, withholding is zero.

For sales between $300,001 and $1,000,000 with the same buyer-use affidavit, withholding drops to 10 percent. Above $1,000,000, the full 15 percent applies regardless of buyer intent. The seller can also apply for early reduction through [IRS Form 8288-B](https://www.irs.gov/forms-pubs/about-form-8288-b), but agents should warn sellers that processing timelines from the IRS often exceed 90 days—sometimes stretching past six months.

At the state level, Ohio exemptions under ORC 5747.071 may apply if the seller can demonstrate that no Ohio income tax is owed on the transaction gain, or if the transaction qualifies under specific pass-through entity provisions. These exemptions require documentation submitted to the closing agent before disbursement.

## Practical Steps Agents Should Take Starting at Listing Intake

The single most effective practice is asking the right question at the right time. During listing intake, ask every seller directly: "Are you a U.S. citizen or permanent resident alien?" Document the answer. If the answer is no—or if the seller hesitates—flag the file immediately and involve the closing attorney or title company.

Build FIRPTA into your listing presentation. Show the foreign seller an adjusted net sheet that accounts for both the 15 percent federal withholding and potential Ohio state withholding. Explain that these are advance payments, not penalties, and that refunds may be available after filing. This conversation prevents deal-killing surprises at the closing table.
