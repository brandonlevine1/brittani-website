## Why South Carolina Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026 Still Trip Up Experienced Agents

The South Carolina lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 remain one of the most frequently botched compliance steps in residential transactions across the state. Unlike state-specific disclosure forms, lead paint disclosure is a federal mandate that operates alongside South Carolina's own property condition disclosure framework. Getting it wrong does not just risk a scolding from your broker — it opens the door to six-figure liability.

**The disclosure obligation traces back to the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992, codified at 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d, and its implementing regulations at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H.** The EPA and HUD jointly enforce these rules, and they apply uniformly whether you are selling a single-family bungalow in Greenville or a multi-unit investment property in downtown Columbia. South Carolina does not have a separate state-level lead paint statute that overrides or modifies the federal requirements, which means agents must look to the federal framework as the controlling authority.

The South Carolina Association of Realtors (SCR) publishes standardized forms that satisfy federal requirements, but using the correct form is only the first step. Timing, delivery method, and documentation all matter — and the 2026 enforcement environment has only gotten stricter following increased HUD audit activity across the Southeast.

## Which Properties Trigger the Disclosure Obligation

Not every transaction requires a lead paint disclosure. The rule is straightforward: any residential dwelling constructed before 1978 must include the disclosure at or before the time a purchase contract is signed. The 1978 cutoff reflects the year the Consumer Product Safety Commission banned lead-based paint in residential use.

Several categories of property are exempt under 24 CFR 35.82. These include housing certified as lead-free by an accredited inspector, zero-bedroom units (studios and dormitories), short-term vacation rentals of 100 days or fewer, and housing exclusively designated for elderly or disabled residents where no children reside. Foreclosure sales are not exempt — a misconception that persists in some South Carolina brokerages.

For agents working the historic districts of Charleston, Beaufort, or Camden, the pre-1978 threshold captures the vast majority of listed inventory. If you regularly list in these markets, the lead paint disclosure should be reflexive — not an afterthought triggered by a transaction coordinator flagging a missing document three days before closing.

## The Forms South Carolina Agents Must Use

**The standard form in South Carolina is SCR Form 520, titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards."** This form tracks the language required by 24 CFR 35.92 and includes the seller's disclosure of known lead-based paint or hazards, a statement regarding available records or reports, the buyer's acknowledgment of receipt, and the required 10-day inspection opportunity notice.

| Form Component | Seller's Responsibility | Buyer's Responsibility |
|---|---|---|
| Known lead-based paint presence | Disclose all known information | Acknowledge receipt of disclosure |
| Available reports or records | Provide copies to buyer | Confirm receipt of records |
| 10-day inspection period | Allow opportunity (or negotiate alternative) | Elect to conduct or waive inspection |
| EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" | Provide to buyer before contract ratification | Acknowledge receipt of pamphlet |
| Agent certification | Listing agent signs confirming compliance | Buyer's agent signs confirming delivery |

Both agents — listing side and buyer side — must sign the form. This dual-signature requirement trips up agents who assume only the listing agent bears responsibility. Under 24 CFR 35.92(b), the buyer's agent must certify that they informed the buyer of their rights, and the listing agent must certify that the seller was informed of their obligations.

The [EPA pamphlet](https://www.epa.gov/lead/protect-your-family-lead-your-home) must be the current version. Outdated pamphlets do not satisfy the regulatory requirement, and the EPA periodically updates its content. Confirm you are distributing the most recent edition before relying on bulk copies sitting in your office supply closet.

## Timing and the 10-Day Inspection Window

Federal law entitles the buyer to a 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at the buyer's expense before becoming obligated under the contract. This period can be shortened, lengthened, or waived entirely — but only by mutual written agreement between buyer and seller.

**The 10-day clock does not automatically start at contract ratification.** It begins when the buyer receives the disclosure, the EPA pamphlet, and any existing lead paint reports. If your listing agent delivers these documents two days after the buyer signs the offer, the 10-day window starts at that later delivery date — which can push closing timelines or create inspection conflicts with the general due diligence period under the South Carolina contract.

South Carolina's standard Residential Contract of Sale (SCR Form 310) includes a due diligence period that often runs concurrently with the lead paint inspection window. Agents should coordinate these timelines explicitly in the contract rather than assuming they overlap. A buyer who waives their general due diligence contingency has not automatically waived their separate federal right to a lead paint inspection unless the waiver is documented on SCR Form 520.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Fines, Liability, and Deal Cancellation

The penalties for failing to comply with lead paint disclosure requirements operate on multiple levels. Federal civil penalties under Section 1018 of the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act allow fines of up to $19,507 per violation (as adjusted for inflation through 2026). Criminal penalties for knowing violations can reach $19,507 per day of violation plus imprisonment.

| Consequence Type | Triggering Event | Potential Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Contract rescission | Failure to deliver disclosure before contract signing | Buyer voids contract; earnest money returned |
| Federal civil penalty | Any disclosure violation identified by HUD/EPA audit | Up to $19,507 per violation |
| Treble damages (civil suit) | Buyer discovers undisclosed lead hazard post-closing | Three times actual damages plus attorney fees |
| Criminal prosecution | Knowing and willful failure to disclose | Up to $19,507/day plus imprisonment |
| License discipline | SC Real Estate Commission complaint | Reprimand, fine, suspension, or revocation under S.C. Code 40-57-145 |

Beyond federal exposure, the South Carolina Real Estate Commission (SCREC) can pursue disciplinary action under S.C. Code Section 40-57-145 for failing to comply with applicable federal or state disclosure requirements. A substantiated complaint can result in fines, mandatory education, suspension, or license revocation. The Commission does not need to prove buyer harm — the failure to follow the disclosure procedure itself constitutes a violation.

In practice, the most common consequence agents face is contract rescission. A buyer who did not receive a proper lead paint disclosure before signing can cancel the deal and recover their earnest money deposit. This outcome is particularly damaging in competitive markets where the seller has already purchased another home contingent on the first closing.

## Common Mistakes South Carolina Agents Make

**First, many listing agents fail to provide the disclosure before the buyer signs the offer.** The federal rule requires delivery at or before the time the purchaser is obligated under a contract. Slipping the form into the closing package does not satisfy this requirement. If the buyer has already signed and the disclosure arrives later, the contract is voidable at the buyer's election.

Second, agents routinely forget to include both agent signatures on SCR Form 520. The buyer's agent must sign the certification confirming they informed the buyer of their rights, and the listing agent must sign confirming the seller was advised. A form with only one agent signature is incomplete and non-compliant. This error often surfaces in dual agency situations where a single agent assumes one signature covers both roles — it does not.

Third, some agents mark the disclosure as "not applicable" for condominiums or townhouses built before 1978. The exemption for certified lead-free properties requires a formal inspection report from an accredited inspector — not an assumption based on renovations or the age of interior finishes. A property that was repainted in 2015 is not exempt merely because the visible paint is new.

Fourth, agents neglect to provide buyers with copies of existing lead paint inspection reports that the seller possesses. Under 24 CFR 35.88, the seller must disclose all available records and reports pertaining to lead-based paint in the property. If the seller had an inspection done five years ago — even one showing no lead — that report must be provided.

Fifth, and perhaps most damaging long-term, agents fail to retain the signed disclosure for the required three-year period. Federal regulations at 24 CFR 35.92(c) mandate retention for three years from completion of the sale. If a buyer files a complaint 18 months post-closing and the brokerage cannot produce the signed form, the agent has no documentary defense. This requirement applies independently of South Carolina's general [seller disclosure retention obligations](/blog/south-carolina-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026).

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers-in-charge at South Carolina firms carry supervisory liability for their agents' disclosure compliance. Under S.C. Code Section 40-57-137, the broker-in-charge is responsible for ensuring that all licensees under their supervision comply with applicable laws and regulations. A pattern of lead paint disclosure failures can result in disciplinary action against the broker's license, not just the individual agent.

**Every brokerage operating in South Carolina should maintain a transaction audit checklist that flags pre-1978 properties at listing intake.** The year built should trigger an automatic workflow step requiring completion of SCR Form 520 before any offer is presented. Relying on agents to self-identify which transactions require the disclosure produces inconsistent results — particularly among newer licensees who may not have encountered the requirement during pre-licensing education.

Brokers should also audit file retention practices quarterly. The three-year retention requirement means that closed transaction files from 2023 forward must still contain legible, fully executed copies of SCR Form 520 with both agent signatures. Digital storage satisfies the requirement, but the files must be retrievable and intact. If your brokerage uses transaction management software, confirm that lead paint disclosures are categorized as a mandatory document type that cannot be bypassed.

Additionally, brokers should verify that their agents are distributing the current EPA pamphlet. The pamphlet was last substantively revised in 2021, and some brokerages still circulate the 2012 version. An outdated pamphlet creates a technical compliance gap that, while rarely enforced in isolation, weakens the brokerage's position if other disclosure failures surface in the same transaction. Understanding [agency disclosure obligations](/blog/south-carolina-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) alongside lead paint requirements gives brokers a more complete compliance picture.

## How This Intersects With South Carolina's Property Condition Disclosure

South Carolina requires sellers to complete a Seller's Disclosure Statement under S.C. Code Section 27-50-40, which asks about known material defects but does not specifically address lead-based paint in the detail required by federal law. The state disclosure form and the federal lead paint disclosure serve different functions and one does not substitute for the other.

Agents occasionally assume that checking a box on the state property condition form about paint condition satisfies the federal lead paint requirement. It does not. The federal disclosure requires a specific form, specific language, delivery of the EPA pamphlet, and the 10-day inspection opportunity — none of which are embedded in South Carolina's state disclosure framework. Treating these as separate obligations with separate forms and separate timelines prevents the kind of conflation that leads to compliance gaps. For agents working across state lines, the approach differs from states like [Tennessee](/blog/tennessee-lead-paint-disclosure-2026), which similarly layers federal requirements atop state-specific forms.
