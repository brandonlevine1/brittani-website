## Understanding Florida Dual Agency & Designated Agency Rules 2026

Florida handles agency relationships differently than most states, and that distinction trips up agents who have relocated from other markets. The core rule under **Florida dual agency & designated agency rules 2026** is deceptively simple: dual agency does not exist in Florida law. The state eliminated it years ago through Chapter 475 of the Florida Statutes, replacing it with a transaction broker model that carries its own set of duties and disclosure deadlines.

Agents who assume they can represent both sides the way they might in New York or Texas are walking into a compliance minefield. Florida's framework creates three possible brokerage relationships — single agent, transaction broker, and no brokerage relationship — plus the narrow designated sales associate exception. Getting the paperwork wrong on any of these doesn't just risk a FREC complaint; it can unwind a closed deal.

## The Three Brokerage Relationships Under Chapter 475

**Florida Statute 475.278 establishes that every licensee operates as a transaction broker by default unless a different relationship is established in writing.** This presumption catches new agents off guard. If you never sign a single agent agreement, you are already functioning as a transaction broker with limited duties to both parties.

A single agent owes fiduciary duties — loyalty, confidentiality, obedience, full disclosure, and accounting — to one party only. The moment that same brokerage might represent the other side, the single agent relationship must be terminated and a transition notice must be delivered and signed before the licensee acts in a different capacity.

The no brokerage relationship option is rare in practice. It means the licensee provides factual information without any duties beyond honesty and fair dealing. Florida Statute 475.278(4) governs this category, and it requires written disclosure at or before the time the licensee provides real estate services.

| Relationship Type | Statutory Basis | Duties Owed | Written Disclosure Required? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single Agent | F.S. 475.278(3) | Full fiduciary | Yes, before services begin |
| Transaction Broker | F.S. 475.278(2) | Limited (honesty, disclosure of material facts, no confidentiality of price) | Yes, but presumed by default |
| No Brokerage Relationship | F.S. 475.278(4) | Honesty and fair dealing only | Yes, before services begin |
| Designated Sales Associate | F.S. 475.2755 | Full fiduciary to assigned party | Yes, with asset qualification and consent |

## Designated Sales Associates: Florida's Alternative to Dual Agency

Because dual agency is banned, the Florida Legislature created the designated sales associate (DSA) structure under [Section 475.2755 of the Florida Statutes](http://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&URL=0400-0499/0475/Sections/0475.2755.html). This allows a single brokerage to assign one agent to represent the buyer and another to represent the seller, each owing full single-agent fiduciary duties to their respective client.

**The catch: DSA applies only when both the buyer and seller each have assets of at least $1 million.** The statute does not limit this to net worth — it refers to total assets. But the threshold still eliminates most residential transactions from eligibility, making DSA primarily a tool for commercial deals and high-net-worth clients.

Both parties must provide written consent to the designated sales associate arrangement. The broker acts as a neutral party and cannot share confidential information between the two designated agents. If your brokerage handles [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) involving the same buyer pool and seller pool, internal information barriers become critical.

## The Transition Notice: Timing and Form Requirements

When a single agent discovers a conflict — say, a brokerage listing attracts an offer from another client of the same brokerage — the single agent relationship must end before anyone acts as a transaction broker. Florida law mandates a written Transition to Transaction Broker Notice, delivered to and signed by the party losing fiduciary representation.

The Florida Realtors and Florida Bar jointly publish the CRSP-16 form series covering residential sale and purchase agreements, but many brokerages use their own FREC-compliant transition notice templates. The key requirement isn't the form number; it's the timing. The notice must be signed before the licensee performs any act in the new capacity. Performing a single act as a transaction broker before getting the transition signed is a violation of Chapter 475.

Agents who miss this window face more than a slap on the wrist. FREC treats unauthorized dual representation as a serious offense because it means fiduciary duties were owed to one party while the agent effectively served both. This creates grounds for contract rescission and civil suits by the disadvantaged party. If you're unsure about [where deals commonly break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) due to compliance gaps, this transition moment is ground zero in Florida.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Florida Agency Disclosures

**First, agents assume the transaction broker disclosure is optional because it's the default.** While F.S. 475.278(2) creates the presumption, FREC rules still require agents to provide written notice of transaction broker duties. Many brokerages include this language in the listing agreement or buyer-broker agreement, but if the form language is buried or missing, the agent hasn't satisfied the requirement.

Second, agents deliver the single agent disclosure too late. The statute says disclosure must be made "before or at the time of entering into a listing agreement, before showing property, or before an offer is made." Sliding a disclosure form across the table at the closing is not compliance — it is evidence of a violation.

Third, agents use the designated sales associate structure in transactions that don't meet the asset threshold. Either they never confirm both parties qualify or they misunderstand "assets of $1 million" as household income or property value. FREC has investigated cases where agents treated a standard residential transaction as DSA-eligible without documentation.

Fourth, agents fail to transition from single agent to transaction broker before attending an open house for their own listing while simultaneously working with an unrepresented buyer. The moment that buyer expresses interest and the agent begins providing guidance, the single agent relationship with the seller is compromised if no transition has been documented.

Fifth, agents operating under a no brokerage relationship inadvertently provide opinions about pricing, negotiation strategy, or inspection responses — all of which exceed the limited duties allowed under F.S. 475.278(4) and create an implied agency relationship.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear ultimate responsibility for agency compliance within their brokerage under F.S. 475.25(1)(u), which allows FREC to discipline a broker for failing to maintain and properly supervise licensees. A broker who ignores systemic disclosure failures risks their own license, not just the agent's.

**Every brokerage should audit three things quarterly: timing of disclosure delivery, proper form usage, and DSA qualification documentation.** Timing is the hardest to verify after the fact. If your transaction files only contain the signed form without a date stamp or evidence of when it was presented, you have a gap that FREC investigators will fill with the least favorable assumption.

| Audit Point | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Single Agent Disclosure | Signed before services began | Date on form is same as or after first showing |
| Transition Notice | Signed before transaction broker acts were performed | No transition form in file despite in-house double-end |
| DSA Qualification | Written asset confirmation from both parties | No documentation or parties clearly below threshold |
| Default Transaction Broker | Written notice provided despite presumption | No disclosure language in any agreement or standalone form |
| No Brokerage Relationship | Services limited to factual information | File contains pricing advice, CMAs, or negotiation emails |

Brokers should also build a [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) that flags agency disclosure as the first compliance gate before any file moves to contract stage. Without that checkpoint, files reach closing with missing or defective disclosures that no one catches until a post-closing audit — or worse, a FREC complaint.

## Penalties and Liability for Non-Compliance

FREC operates under the Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) and has broad authority to discipline licensees under F.S. 475.25. Penalties for agency disclosure violations include administrative fines up to $5,000 per count, mandatory education courses, license suspension for up to ten years, and permanent revocation for repeat or egregious violations.

Civil liability compounds the regulatory exposure. A buyer or seller who was never informed of the brokerage relationship — or who lost fiduciary protection without proper notice — can pursue damages in court. Florida courts have allowed claims for breach of fiduciary duty, fraud by omission, and negligent misrepresentation when agency disclosures were missing or defective.

Contract rescission is the nuclear option. If a court determines that a party's consent to the transaction was based on a misunderstanding of who represented them, the deal can be unwound. Title insurance policies often exclude coverage for fraud or undisclosed agency conflicts, meaning the fallout lands squarely on the agent and brokerage. Knowing the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) should motivate every licensee to treat disclosure timing as non-negotiable.
