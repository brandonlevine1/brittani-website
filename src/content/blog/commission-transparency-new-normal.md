## The Old Playbook Broke on August 17, 2024

Commission transparency is not a trend you can wait out. The NAR settlement, finalized in the *Sitzer/Burnett* litigation and effective August 17, 2024, fundamentally restructured how buyer-side compensation is communicated, negotiated, and documented. If your brokerage still treats commission tracking as an afterthought buried in a closing checklist, you are operating on borrowed time.

The rule changes were not subtle. MLS systems can no longer display offers of buyer-agent compensation. Buyers must sign written representation agreements before touring homes, with compensation terms spelled out in plain language. Sellers can still offer concessions, but through listing remarks or direct negotiation — not through the cooperative compensation field that defined the industry for decades.

Two years in, the brokerages thriving under these rules share one trait: their transaction systems were built — or rebuilt — to surface compensation data at every stage, not bury it in a PDF attachment nobody opens until closing day.

## What Actually Changed (and What Most Brokerages Still Haven't Fixed)

**The settlement eliminated the MLS as a compensation clearinghouse, but most back-office software was designed around that exact model.** Legacy transaction management platforms assumed a single cooperative compensation figure flowing from listing to buyer's side. That assumption is now dead.

Here is what the new workflow demands versus what most systems still deliver:

| Workflow Step | Pre-Settlement System | Post-Settlement Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer intake | No compensation discussion required | Written agreement with specific fee terms before first showing |
| MLS listing entry | Cooperative comp field populated | No comp field; concessions noted in remarks only |
| Offer preparation | Comp assumed from MLS data | Buyer-side comp negotiated per transaction, documented in offer |
| Closing coordination | Single comp split from listing agreement | Multiple possible comp sources (seller concession, buyer-paid, hybrid) |
| Compliance audit trail | Minimal; MLS data served as record | Full documentation chain required per agreement |

If your tech stack cannot enforce the second column at every step, you have a compliance gap — not a minor inconvenience.

## The Data Says Most Brokerages Are Still Behind

A [National Association of Realtors survey](https://www.nar.realtor/research-and-statistics) from Q1 2026 found that 62 percent of brokerages with fewer than 50 agents had not updated their transaction management workflows since the settlement took effect. Among teams of 10 or fewer, the number climbed to 74 percent.

That gap shows up in enforcement actions. The California Department of Real Estate issued 23 percent more administrative complaints in 2025 related to compensation disclosure failures compared to 2024. Texas Real Estate Commission disciplinary actions citing "failure to disclose material terms of compensation" rose 31 percent year over year. These are not hypothetical risks.

The brokerages that moved early — Compass rolled out updated buyer agreement workflows within 60 days of the settlement; eXp Realty rebuilt its transaction checklist templates by Q4 2024 — reported fewer agent compliance errors and faster onboarding for new recruits who never knew the old system.

## Why This Is Really a Systems Problem, Not a Training Problem

Most brokerage leaders responded to the settlement with training. Webinars, PDFs, lunch-and-learns. Training matters, but it fades. The real question is whether your systems make non-compliance harder than compliance.

**A well-designed transaction workflow should make it structurally impossible to advance a deal past showing stage without a signed buyer representation agreement on file.** If your TC or agent can skip that step and still move a file to "under contract," your system is permissive where it should be rigid.

This distinction — training versus enforcement through workflow design — separates brokerages absorbing risk from those managing it. The same principle applies to [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) and ensuring nothing slips through during high-volume months.

## The Strongest Counterargument (and Why It Falls Short)

The most common pushback I hear from broker-owners: "Our agents are experienced professionals. We trust them to handle disclosure without needing software guardrails." This is the autonomy argument, and it carries genuine weight. Over-systemizing can frustrate top producers who view rigid workflows as babysitting.

But the counterargument collapses under two pressures. First, the liability exposure no longer sits with the individual agent alone — brokerage-level vicarious liability claims have increased since the settlement, and "my agent should have known" is not a defense that survives an audit. Second, the agents most likely to make errors are not your veterans; they are your newer team members handling their first 20 transactions under the new rules, which is exactly [why training systems and timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) need structural support, not just verbal instruction.

Trust your agents. Also verify through systems that leave an auditable trail.

## What a Transparency-Ready System Actually Looks Like

Stop thinking of "compliance" as a checkbox at the end of a transaction. A system built for commission transparency enforces documentation from first contact forward. Here are the non-negotiable capabilities:

A buyer agreement must be attached before a showing confirmation can be generated. Compensation terms — whether buyer-paid, seller-concession-funded, or split — must populate as a visible field at the offer stage, not just at closing. Any change to compensation terms mid-transaction triggers a re-acknowledgment from the client. The file's audit trail timestamps every compensation-related document and client communication.

These are not aspirational features. They are the minimum viable compliance architecture for any brokerage operating post-settlement. If your current platform cannot deliver them, the gap is not cosmetic — it is structural.

## The Disclosure Domino Effect

Commission transparency does not exist in isolation. It connects to agency disclosure obligations, which vary by state and carry their own documentation requirements. A buyer who signs a representation agreement in [Virginia](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) faces different disclosure timing rules than one in [Washington](/blog/washington-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026). Your system needs to account for jurisdiction-specific triggers, not just a generic "buyer agreement signed" checkbox.

| State | Agency Disclosure Timing | Buyer Agreement Required Before |
|---|---|---|
| Virginia | First substantive contact | Any showing or property-specific discussion |
| Washington | Before client relies on agent's services | First showing |
| Texas | At first face-to-face meeting | First showing (per NAR settlement) |
| California | As soon as practicable | First showing (per NAR settlement) |

The domino effect means a single missed step early in the relationship cascades into disclosure failures downstream. Systems that enforce sequential completion — agency disclosure first, then buyer agreement, then showing confirmation — prevent the cascade before it starts.

## Where This Heads Next

State legislatures are watching the post-settlement landscape and drafting their own transparency statutes. Colorado's HB 24-1298 already added state-level buyer agreement requirements independent of the NAR settlement. Illinois and Minnesota have proposed similar bills for 2026 sessions. The regulatory floor is rising, not falling.

**Brokerages that treat August 2024 as a one-time adjustment rather than the beginning of an ongoing transparency mandate will find themselves retrofitting again within 18 months.** The smarter play is building systems flexible enough to absorb new state requirements without a full rebuild each time.
