## Ohio 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: The State-Level Rules Agents Overlook

Most Ohio agents understand the federal mechanics of a 1031 exchange: sell investment property, identify a replacement within 45 days, close within 180 days, defer capital gains. Far fewer understand the Ohio 1031 exchange state tax implications 2026 that can derail a deal or create post-closing liability for the seller, the buyer, and sometimes the agent. Ohio's conformity to IRC Section 1031 is not absolute, and the state's withholding rules for nonresident sellers add a layer that catches even experienced licensees off guard.

The Ohio Department of Taxation enforces rules under Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Chapter 5747 that govern income tax treatment of real property dispositions. When a 1031 exchange involves a nonresident seller, the transferee must either withhold state income tax or obtain a qualifying exemption affidavit. Failing to address these requirements at the transaction level means the deal may close with outstanding tax obligations—and the finger-pointing starts immediately after.

## How Ohio Conforms to Federal 1031 Rules—and Where It Diverges

**Ohio generally follows federal tax treatment of like-kind exchanges under ORC Section 5747.01, which defines "adjusted gross income" by reference to the Internal Revenue Code.** This means a properly structured 1031 exchange that defers federal capital gains will typically defer Ohio state income tax as well. The state does not impose a separate gain recognition event for exchanges that meet IRC Section 1031 requirements.

However, conformity has limits. Ohio enacted its own version of the Commercial Activity Tax (CAT) under ORC Chapter 5751, which applies to gross receipts rather than net income. A 1031 exchange does not exempt a taxpayer from CAT obligations on gross receipts generated from the relinquished property sale. Agents advising clients who hold property through pass-through entities should flag this early because it blindsides sellers who assume full deferral across all Ohio taxes.

The other divergence that matters in 2026 involves Ohio's municipal income taxes. Ohio's roughly 600 municipal taxing jurisdictions operate under ORC Chapter 718, and most do not explicitly conform to IRC Section 1031. A seller exchanging property located in Columbus, Cleveland, or Cincinnati may still owe municipal income tax on the gain, even if the state and federal taxes are deferred. This is not something an agent can resolve—but it is something an agent must surface so the client consults a tax professional before signing the purchase agreement.

## Nonresident Withholding: The Form That Kills Deals

Ohio imposes a withholding obligation on real property transfers involving nonresident sellers under [ORC Section 5747.40](https://codes.ohio.gov/ohio-revised-code/section-5747.40). The transferee (buyer) must withhold a percentage of the sale price unless the seller qualifies for an exemption. For 2026, the withholding rate remains at the lesser of 3.99% of the gain or an amount based on the sale price, as specified by the Ohio Department of Taxation.

| Scenario | Withholding Required? | Form Used | Who Files |
|----------|----------------------|-----------|-----------|
| Nonresident seller, no exchange | Yes | IT 1140 | Buyer/transferee |
| Nonresident seller, valid 1031 exchange | Exempt if affidavit provided | IT 1140 (with exemption notation) | Buyer/transferee |
| Ohio resident seller | No | None | N/A |
| Entity seller (nonresident members) | Depends on pass-through structure | IT 1140 or IT 4708 | Buyer or entity |

The exemption affidavit is the critical document. If the nonresident seller certifies that the transaction qualifies under IRC Section 1031 and will result in no recognized gain for Ohio purposes, the buyer is relieved of the withholding obligation. But here is where agents get burned: if that exchange later fails—because the seller misses the 45-day identification window or the 180-day closing deadline—the withholding obligation snaps back into existence. The buyer who relied on the affidavit may suddenly owe the Ohio Department of Taxation money they never collected.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Make on 1031 Exchanges

Agents do not prepare tax returns, but they do control the information flow that determines whether a transaction is structured correctly from the start. These five errors appear repeatedly in Ohio transactions involving like-kind exchanges.

**First, agents fail to ask whether the seller is an Ohio resident at the listing appointment.** Residency determines withholding obligations, and discovering a seller's out-of-state domicile two days before closing leaves no time to obtain a proper exemption affidavit or coordinate with the qualified intermediary (QI). The listing agent should confirm residency status at the time of engagement and document it in writing.

Second, agents confuse "investment property" with "property the seller happens to own." IRC Section 1031 requires both the relinquished and replacement properties to be held for productive use in a trade or business or for investment. A seller who flipped a property within 12 months, or who lived in it part-time without reporting rental income, may not qualify. Agents who market a listing as "1031-eligible" without understanding the seller's holding purpose expose themselves to misrepresentation claims.

Third, agents assume the title company or closing attorney handles all 1031 compliance automatically. Ohio is an attorney-closing state in many counties, but neither the attorney nor the title company is obligated to verify exchange eligibility or file IT 1140. The responsibility for withholding falls on the buyer, and the responsibility for surfacing the issue falls on whichever professional is managing the transaction timeline—often the agent. If you are tracking multiple active deals simultaneously, this kind of detail slips through the cracks fast, as we have [written about before](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals).

Fourth, agents neglect to confirm that the QI is properly bonded and meets Ohio's expectations for escrow handling. Ohio does not license QIs, but if the intermediary mishandles funds or becomes insolvent, the exchange fails and the seller recognizes gain immediately—including for Ohio tax purposes.

Fifth, agents fail to document the 45-day and 180-day deadlines in their transaction management systems. Missing a deadline by even one day destroys the exchange, triggers gain recognition, and potentially activates the withholding requirement that was waived based on the seller's exemption affidavit.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers supervising Ohio agents who handle investment property transactions carry a supervisory responsibility under ORC Section 4735.18 and Ohio Administrative Code Rule 1301:5-1-01. A broker cannot prepare tax advice, but a broker absolutely can—and should—require that agents follow documented procedures when a 1031 exchange is involved.

| Broker Audit Item | Why It Matters | Frequency |
|-------------------|---------------|-----------|
| Seller residency confirmed in writing | Determines IT 1140 obligation | Every listing involving investment property |
| QI engagement letter in file | Proves exchange was properly initiated | Before relinquished property closes |
| 45-day and 180-day deadlines calendared | Missed deadlines kill exchanges retroactively | At listing and under contract |
| Exemption affidavit in closing file | Protects buyer from withholding liability | Before closing |
| Municipal tax flag documented | Prevents client surprise post-closing | At listing |

**Brokers should build a compliance checkpoint into their transaction review process that fires the moment a listing is tagged as "investor-owned" or "1031 intended."** This is not optional diligence—it is risk management. If an agent closes a deal without collecting the withholding or obtaining the affidavit, and the Ohio Department of Taxation comes after the buyer, the buyer's attorney will look at who managed the transaction and ask why nobody flagged the requirement. That [chain of accountability](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) needs to be airtight.

The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, which operates under the Ohio Department of Commerce, can investigate complaints related to agent negligence in transaction management. While a missed withholding form is technically a tax issue, the failure to inform a client about a known material obligation can constitute a violation of the agent's fiduciary duty under ORC Section 4735.62.

## Liability Exposure When Compliance Fails

The consequences of getting this wrong are not theoretical. Under ORC 5747.40(C), a transferee who fails to withhold is personally liable for the amount that should have been withheld, plus interest and penalties. If the seller provided a fraudulent exemption affidavit, the buyer has a defense—but proving fraud requires litigation, time, and money that most buyers did not budget for.

For agents, the exposure is indirect but real. An agent who knew or should have known that a withholding obligation existed—and who failed to advise the parties—faces potential negligence claims, E&O insurance claims, and disciplinary action. The Ohio Real Estate Commission has the authority to suspend or revoke a license for "incompetency" under ORC 4735.18(A)(6), and repeated failures to address known compliance issues in investment transactions can trigger that standard.

Deal cancellation is another risk. Buyers who discover mid-transaction that they will be required to withhold nearly 4% of the purchase price—and escrow it to the Ohio Department of Taxation—sometimes walk away rather than take on the liability. Agents who surface this early protect the deal. Agents who ignore it create the conditions for a [blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) that costs everyone time and money.

## Planning for 2026: Legislative and Regulatory Updates

Ohio's General Assembly has not introduced legislation in 2026 that would decouple the state from federal 1031 treatment, but agents should monitor House Bill activity through the Ohio Legislature's website. Several states have considered partial decoupling in recent sessions, and Ohio's budget pressures make this a perennial policy discussion.

The Ohio Department of Taxation updated its withholding guidance for tax year 2026 in Information Release IT 2026-01, which reaffirmed the withholding rates and clarified the affidavit process for exchanges. Agents should verify that any exemption affidavit templates used by their title companies or attorneys reflect the current-year guidance. Outdated forms may not satisfy the Department's requirements, leaving the buyer exposed.

> "The transferee shall withhold from the sale proceeds an amount equal to the applicable withholding percentage...unless the transferor provides an affidavit that the transfer qualifies for nonrecognition treatment under Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code." — ORC 5747.40(A)

Municipal tax conformity remains the biggest gray area. Agents operating in jurisdictions with a municipal income tax should maintain a reference list of which cities conform to IRC 1031 and which do not. The Regional Income Tax Agency (RITA) administers taxes for many Ohio municipalities and publishes guidance on its website, but each city's ordinance controls.
