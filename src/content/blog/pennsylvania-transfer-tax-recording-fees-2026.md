## Pennsylvania Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026: The Numbers That Kill Deals

Miscalculating transfer tax is one of the fastest ways to delay a closing in Pennsylvania. Agents who quote the wrong figures on a buyer's estimated settlement statement create last-minute cash shortfalls that ripple through the entire transaction. Understanding the Pennsylvania transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 is not optional—it is the mechanical knowledge that separates agents who close on time from those who scramble at the eleventh hour.

Pennsylvania's realty transfer tax framework is governed primarily by Article XI-C of the Tax Reform Code of 1971, codified at 72 P.S. § 8101-C through § 8114-C. The Pennsylvania Department of Revenue administers the state portion, while each county's Recorder of Deeds handles local collection and recording. Every agent operating in the Commonwealth needs to understand both layers and how they interact with the standard Agreement of Sale (PAR Form ASR) and the settlement sheet calculations that title companies produce.

## The Two-Layer Tax Structure: State and Local

**Pennsylvania imposes a total realty transfer tax of 2% on the value of real estate conveyed by deed, split evenly between state and local governments.** The state collects 1% under 72 P.S. § 8102-C, and the municipality or school district collects an additional 1%. In Philadelphia, the combined rate jumps to 4.278% because the city layers its own tax on top of the standard county/state split.

The taxable value is the higher of the actual consideration paid or the property's assessed fair market value as adjusted by the common level ratio factor published annually by the State Tax Equalization Board. This means that below-market-value transfers—common in family deals or distressed sales—still get taxed on the adjusted assessed value unless a specific statutory exclusion applies.

Exclusions exist under 72 P.S. § 8102-C.3 for transfers between certain family members, transfers to or from governmental bodies, and transfers resulting from corporate mergers that meet specific criteria. Agents often misapply these exclusions, which triggers deficiency notices months after closing.

## Recording Fee Breakdown by Document Type

Recording fees in Pennsylvania are set at the county level, not by state statute, though 42 Pa.C.S. § 21051 authorizes the fee structure. Each Recorder of Deeds office publishes its own schedule, and the variation across Pennsylvania's 67 counties catches out-of-area agents off guard.

| Document Type | Typical Fee Range (2026) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Deed (first page) | $30–$75 | Includes UPI surcharge in some counties |
| Each additional page | $3–$5 | Montgomery County charges $4/page |
| Mortgage recording | $45–$90 | Some counties add $10 satisfaction tracking fee |
| Mortgage satisfaction | $20–$35 | Must be recorded within 60 days per 21 P.S. § 722 |
| Affidavit of value | No separate fee | Filed with deed; required for transfer tax calculation |

**The Recorder of Deeds will reject any deed submitted without a completed Statement of Value (Form REV-183) unless the transfer qualifies for a listed exclusion.** That form—REV-183—is where agents and their title companies declare the consideration and calculate the transfer tax due. Missing or incorrectly completed REV-183 forms account for a significant portion of recording rejections statewide.

Agents should also be aware that some counties have adopted electronic recording through platforms like eRecording Partners Network or Simplifile, which charge technology fees of $3–$7 per document on top of the county recording fee. These fees show up on the settlement statement and must be disclosed to buyers and sellers.

## Form Numbers Every Pennsylvania Agent Should Know

The paperwork trail for transfer tax compliance is short but unforgiving. Here are the critical forms:

Form REV-183 (Realty Transfer Tax Statement of Value) must accompany every deed presented for recording unless the transfer is wholly exempt. The form requires the property address, tax parcel number, sale price, assessment ratio, names of grantors and grantees, and the specific exclusion code if applicable. Filing an incomplete REV-183 results in the deed being kicked back by the Recorder.

Form REV-183A applies to partial interest transfers and is less common in residential transactions but relevant for investment property syndications. PAR Form ASR (Agreement of Sale for residential properties) includes transfer tax allocation language in its standard paragraphs—specifically Paragraph 13—that defaults to a 50/50 split unless the parties amend it. Agents who forget to address this paragraph during negotiation often discover at settlement that their client assumed the other party was paying the full tax.

The [Pennsylvania Department of Revenue's realty transfer tax page](https://www.revenue.pa.gov/TaxTypes/RTT/Pages/default.aspx) provides current common level ratio factors, exclusion codes, and fillable forms. Bookmark it.

## What Happens When Agents Get It Wrong

Failure to properly account for transfer tax does not just cause closing delays—it can expose agents to liability claims. When a buyer shows up at settlement expecting to pay $5,000 in closing costs and the actual figure is $7,400 because the agent underestimated transfer tax, that agent faces a client who may refuse to close. A [blown closing costs the listing agent, the buyer's agent, and both brokerages](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) in tangible ways: expired rate locks, renegotiated purchase prices, and reputational damage.

Under the Pennsylvania Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. § 455.604, agents have a duty to exercise reasonable skill and care. Repeatedly miscalculating transfer tax on estimated settlement statements could be treated as incompetence by the Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission during a complaint investigation. The Commission can issue fines up to $10,000 per violation, suspend licenses, or require additional education.

**If the deed is recorded with an underpayment of transfer tax, the Department of Revenue can pursue the deficiency against both the buyer and seller under 72 P.S. § 8110-C.** Penalties include interest at the prevailing rate plus a potential 25% fraud penalty if the underpayment is deemed intentional. The agent who prepared the erroneous figures is not statutorily liable for the tax itself, but the aggrieved party can pursue a civil claim for negligent misrepresentation.

## Five Common Mistakes Agents Make on Transfer Tax

**First, agents routinely forget that Philadelphia has a different rate.** The combined city/state transfer tax in Philadelphia is 4.278%, not 2%. Agents who work the suburbs and occasionally handle a city deal apply the wrong rate with alarming frequency.

Second, agents misapply the family exclusion. Transfers between siblings, for example, are not exempt under 72 P.S. § 8102-C.3. Only transfers between parent and child, grandparent and grandchild, or between spouses qualify. An agent who tells a client their brother-to-brother transfer is tax-free has just cost that client thousands of dollars and a potential recording rejection.

Third, agents neglect to verify the common level ratio. The State Tax Equalization Board updates this ratio annually, and it determines whether a below-market transfer triggers tax on the assessed value rather than the actual sale price. Using last year's ratio on this year's transaction produces an incorrect REV-183.

Fourth, agents assume recording fees are uniform. An agent who regularly closes in Chester County and quotes those fees for a Delaware County transaction will be off—sometimes by $30–$50 per document. That adds up when a transaction involves a deed, mortgage, and multiple rider pages.

Fifth, agents fail to confirm the transfer tax split during contract negotiations. Paragraph 13 of PAR Form ASR defaults to a 50/50 split, but buyers in competitive markets sometimes agree to pay the seller's share. If the agent does not explicitly confirm this arrangement in writing, disputes arise at the settlement table. Tracking these details across [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) requires a system, not memory.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under RELRA § 455.604(a)(12). If an agent in their office consistently miscalculates transfer tax or fails to include REV-183 forms in transaction files, the broker faces Commission scrutiny during audits. The Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission requires brokers to maintain transaction records for three years, and those records must include evidence that the correct transfer tax was collected and remitted.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| REV-183 in every file | Signed form with correct exclusion code or tax amount | Missing form or blank consideration field |
| Transfer tax split matches contract | Paragraph 13 of ASR or addendum language | Settlement statement differs from contract |
| Recording fee accuracy | Correct county fee schedule applied | Fees from wrong county or outdated schedule |
| Philadelphia transactions | 4.278% rate applied | Standard 2% rate used for city properties |
| Common level ratio | Current year's STEB ratio on REV-183 | Prior year ratio or no ratio applied |

Brokers should build a quarterly file review into their compliance calendar. Waiting until a Commission audit to discover that half your agents are filing incomplete REV-183 forms is a losing strategy. Creating a [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) that includes transfer tax verification prevents these errors from reaching the settlement table.

## Exemptions That Actually Apply in 2026

Not every transfer triggers the 2% tax. The most commonly applicable residential exemptions include transfers by will or intestate succession, transfers between spouses (including those incident to divorce under a court order), and transfers to a trust where the grantor retains beneficial interest. Each exemption requires a specific code on Form REV-183, and the Recorder of Deeds staff will reject filings that claim an exemption without the correct code.

Short sales and foreclosure transfers have their own complications. A sheriff's sale transfer is generally taxable, but the tax is calculated on the upset price, not the original mortgage balance. REO-to-buyer transfers following foreclosure are fully taxable at the sale price. Agents handling distressed properties must verify the taxable base with the title company before quoting costs to buyers.
