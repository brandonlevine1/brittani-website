## The Settlement Changed Everything Except What People Expected

The NAR settlement did not destroy buyer agency, collapse commission rates to zero, or trigger the mass exodus of agents that industry doomsayers predicted. What it actually did — twelve months after the August 17, 2024 implementation date — was dump an unprecedented documentation burden onto the people who manage the file: transaction coordinators, team leads, and operations staff. The agents who predicted business-as-usual were half right; the business is roughly the same, but the paperwork trail behind it grew by a third.

Data from the [National Association of Realtors' own post-settlement FAQ updates](https://www.nar.realtor/the-facts/nar-settlement-faqs) confirms that written buyer agreements, compensation disclosure addenda, and revised MLS input rules now touch every single transaction side. That is not a cosmetic change. It is an operational one, and the teams absorbing it deserve a clear-eyed assessment of what the first year actually looked like.

## Commission Rates Barely Moved — But the Paper Trail Exploded

**Average buyer-side commission in Q1 2026 landed at 2.55%, compared to 2.63% in Q1 2024 according to RealTrends Verified data.** That eight-basis-point decline is well within normal market fluctuation. The predicted race to the bottom simply did not materialize in most metros.

What did materialize was a documentation explosion. Every buyer engagement now requires a written agreement specifying compensation terms before a single showing occurs. Every offer must address how the buyer agent gets paid — whether through seller concession, direct buyer payment, or a hybrid. Every file must be auditable against MLS rules that prohibit blanket cooperative compensation offers.

For brokerages running fifteen or more concurrent transactions, that means fifteen additional documents per file that did not exist eighteen months ago. The agents barely notice because someone else manages the file. That someone else noticed immediately.

## What Transaction Teams Actually Handle Now Versus Before

The operational delta is clearest when you compare pre-settlement and post-settlement file management responsibilities side by side.

| Task | Pre-Settlement (Before Aug 2024) | Post-Settlement (Current) |
|------|----------------------------------|---------------------------|
| Buyer compensation documentation | None required in file | Written buyer agreement mandatory before showings |
| MLS compensation field review | Cooperative comp auto-populated | Must verify no prohibited offers; field removed or restricted |
| Offer-stage comp terms | Typically handled via MLS | Must be explicitly stated in purchase contract or addendum |
| Disclosure of agent compensation to buyer | Informal or at closing | Written disclosure required upfront |
| Compliance audit trail | Minimal | Full paper trail required for broker review |

That table represents real hours. Teams that once opened a file, confirmed MLS cooperation, and moved on now run a compliance check at engagement, at offer, and again before closing. The [role of a transaction coordinator](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) expanded overnight without a corresponding expansion in pay or headcount at most brokerages.

## The Written Buyer Agreement Problem Nobody Solved

Written buyer agreements existed before the settlement. Twelve states already required them. The difference now is that every MLS participant nationwide must secure one before property tours begin — and the agreements must state specific compensation terms, not open-ended ranges.

This creates a friction point that teams report as the single biggest operational headache of year one. Agents forget to send them. Buyers balk at signing before they have seen a house. Agreements expire mid-search and nobody flags the lapse until the offer stage. The file is then non-compliant, and the TC or team lead has to pause the transaction to fix it.

**Tracking these agreements across multiple active deals is where most teams describe their workflow breaking down.** A brokerage with eight buyer agents running four deals each has thirty-two buyer agreements in various states of validity at any given time. That is a tracking problem, not a knowledge problem, and it is exactly the kind of operational gap that [becomes visible when you manage concurrent files](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals).

## The Strongest Counterargument: "This Is Just Growing Pains"

The most reasonable pushback I hear from broker-owners is that year one is always the hardest. Forms will stabilize. Agents will internalize the new habits. State associations will issue standardized templates that reduce confusion. Give it another eighteen months and the compliance burden will feel as routine as lead paint disclosures.

I take this argument seriously because it has historical precedent. TRID felt apocalyptic in 2015 and became background noise by 2017. But there is a structural difference here. TRID added steps to the lender's workflow. The NAR settlement added steps to the agent's workflow — and agents do not do their own paperwork. The burden does not land on the person whose behavior needs to change. It lands on the operations team that compensates for unchanged behavior. Until brokerages invest in either [better training around timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) or better systems to catch lapses automatically, the "growing pains" theory assumes a behavior change that the incentive structure does not reward.

## Seller-Side Impacts Are Real But Quieter

Most post-settlement analysis focuses on the buyer side because that is where the rules changed most visibly. But listing teams absorbed hits too. Sellers now receive more questions about whether they will offer concessions covering buyer-agent fees. Listing agents must counsel clients on strategic concession amounts without running afoul of steering concerns. The listing input itself changed — fields that once advertised cooperative compensation are gone or restricted depending on the MLS.

| Seller-Side Change | Operational Impact |
|---|---|
| No cooperative comp in MLS | Listing TC must verify compliant MLS input at listing launch |
| Concession strategy conversations | Listing agent needs comp data by price point; TC may prepare comps |
| Buyer requests for seller-paid agent fees | Additional addendum at offer stage; TC tracks terms |
| Disclosure obligations | Varies by state; some now require [explicit seller disclosure updates](/blog/virginia-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) referencing compensation |

The net effect is more decision points earlier in the listing process, each generating a document or conversation that the operations team must track.

## Who Won Year One

If there is a winner after twelve months, it is the mid-size brokerage that already ran a tight transaction management operation. Teams with established [handoff checklists](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) and compliance workflows absorbed the new requirements with minimal disruption. They added line items to existing processes rather than building processes from scratch.

**The losers were high-volume teams with informal systems — the ones running deals through text threads and shared drives with no version control.** Those teams hit compliance failures, missed buyer agreement expirations, and burned hours reconstructing paper trails for broker audits. Several prominent brokerages disclosed increased E&O claims in Q4 2025 earnings calls, though none attributed them directly to settlement-related lapses on the record.

## Year Two Demands Systems, Not Heroics

The first year rewarded individual effort — TCs staying late, team leads manually checking files, brokers personally auditing MLS inputs. Year two will not sustain that pace. Transaction volume is projected to increase as rates stabilize, and the documentation requirements will not shrink. The teams that thrive will be the ones that automate compliance tracking rather than relying on human memory.
