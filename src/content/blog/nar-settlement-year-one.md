## The Sky Did Not Fall — But the Paperwork Did Multiply

The NAR settlement, finalized in March 2024 and effective August 17 of that year, was supposed to upend residential real estate. One year in, the honest assessment is more mundane: transaction teams absorbed a meaningful but manageable increase in administrative complexity, while the feared exodus of buyer agents never materialized. The structural shift is real, but it landed on operations staff — not on the front page.

That distinction matters for anyone running a brokerage or leading a team. The agents who adapted fastest were the ones whose back-office systems could handle new document types without missing a beat. The ones still struggling are the ones who treated the settlement as a PR problem rather than a workflow problem.

## What the Numbers Actually Show

**National commission averages moved, but not dramatically.** RealTrends' [2025 year-end data](https://www.nar.realtor/research-and-statistics) showed average total commissions settling around 4.6%, down from the pre-settlement norm of roughly 5.0%. That is a real compression — roughly $2,400 less per transaction on a $600,000 home — but it did not trigger the 30-40% agent attrition some analysts forecasted.

The more telling number is transaction volume per surviving agent. NAR's own membership data shows a decline from 1.56 million members in early 2024 to approximately 1.44 million by Q1 2026. That 8% attrition is notable, but most of those exits were part-time agents doing fewer than four deals annually. For established teams, the competitive environment actually improved slightly.

## The Buyer Agency Agreement Became a Workflow Chokepoint

The single largest operational change was the mandatory written buyer agreement before any property tour. This was not conceptually difficult — many states already required something similar — but it introduced a hard dependency into the showing workflow that most teams had never encountered.

For transaction coordinators, this meant a new checkpoint: verifying that a signed buyer agreement existed, matched the property being shown, and contained compliant compensation terms before the agent even turned the lockbox. Teams that relied on verbal commitments or handshake arrangements with buyers suddenly had a compliance gap measured in hours, not days. The [training demands on new agents](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) became substantially more front-loaded as a result.

**The teams that struggled most were high-volume listing teams with buyer agents operating semi-independently.** Without a centralized system catching missing agreements before showing confirmations went out, these teams faced MLS compliance flags within the first 90 days. Several large brokerages reported internal audit failure rates above 20% during Q4 2024.

## Compensation Disclosure Added Another Layer

The second major change — removing cooperative compensation offers from MLS fields — sounds simple on paper. In practice, it created a documentation cascade. Sellers who wanted to offer buyer agent compensation could still do so, but the offer had to live outside the MLS, typically in a separate listing addendum or through direct agent-to-agent communication.

For transaction teams, this meant tracking compensation agreements as a distinct document category. Where a TC previously confirmed commission splits in a single MLS printout, they now cross-reference the buyer agreement, the seller's concession offer (if any), and the purchase contract's compensation terms. One missed document and the file is incomplete at closing.

This reality is why [understanding where deals actually break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) has become more urgent. The failure points shifted upstream — not at inspection or appraisal, but at the agreement stage.

## The Strongest Counterargument: Nothing Fundamentally Changed

The most credible pushback against the "operational burden" narrative comes from brokerages in states like Oregon, Colorado, and Texas where buyer agency agreements were already standard practice. Their argument: we have been doing this for years. The settlement just made everyone else catch up.

That is a fair point, and it is partially correct. But it misses scale effects. Even in those states, the MLS compensation field removal was new, and the enforcement mechanisms — particularly around MLS compliance audits — tightened significantly. The Oregon Association of Realtors reported a 34% increase in compliance-related inquiries to their legal hotline between September 2024 and March 2025. "Already doing it" and "doing it under audit scrutiny" are different operational realities.

## Who Won Year One

| Category | Winners | Losers |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Agent Type | Full-time buyer specialists with clear value propositions | Part-time agents who relied on cooperative comp as a given |
| Brokerage Model | Flat-fee and team-based models with strong TC support | Traditional splits with minimal back-office infrastructure |
| Technology | Document management and compliance tracking tools | Generic CRMs with no transaction workflow capability |
| Consumer Outcome | Buyers who now understand what they are paying for | Buyers who assumed agent services were free |

The pattern is consistent: organizations with systematized transaction workflows absorbed the new requirements without drama. Those running on ad-hoc processes burned agent hours on paperwork that should have been handled once, correctly, by someone whose job is specifically [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals).

## What Year Two Looks Like

**The DOJ's ongoing scrutiny suggests further changes are possible, not guaranteed.** The antitrust division has made clear it did not consider the NAR settlement a final resolution. Proposed rules around pocket listings and office-exclusive inventory may add another documentation requirement in 2027. Smart teams are building flexible compliance workflows now rather than retooling every twelve months.

State-level divergence is also accelerating. Texas updated its [agency disclosure requirements](/blog/texas-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) in ways that go beyond federal settlement terms. Virginia and Washington have introduced state-specific buyer agreement addenda. For multi-state teams, the compliance matrix just got wider.

## The Operational Takeaway

The NAR settlement did not destroy the industry. It did permanently increase the documentation overhead per transaction by roughly 15-20%, concentrated at the front end of the buyer relationship. That is not a crisis — it is an infrastructure problem.

Teams that invested in transaction coordination capacity during 2024 and 2025 are now running at parity or better. Those still asking agents to self-manage compliance paperwork are bleeding productive hours. The brokerages that treat this as a systems problem rather than a training problem will own the next three years.
