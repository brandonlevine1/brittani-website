The NAR settlement did not blow up the residential transaction the way headlines predicted—it quietly rearranged the paperwork stack, added a compliance layer, and shifted the discomfort from listing appointments to buyer consultations. After twelve months of living under the new rules that took effect in August 2024, the data tells a story less dramatic than the op-eds of 2023 but more operationally significant than most brokerages have acknowledged internally. Transaction teams—coordinators, team leads, and the admins who keep files moving—absorbed most of the friction, and too few leaders have stopped to ask whether their systems actually adapted or just survived.

## The Settlement Terms That Matter Operationally

For anyone who skipped the 200-page [practice changes document from NAR](https://www.nar.realtor/the-facts/nar-settlement-faqs), two rules reshaped day-to-day workflows more than all others combined. First, offers of buyer-agent compensation can no longer appear in MLS fields. Second, buyers must sign a written representation agreement before touring any property with an agent.

Those two changes sound simple on paper. In practice, they created a cascade of new documents, new conversations at intake, and new failure points that surface mid-transaction when a file reaches the coordinator's desk incomplete.

**The written buyer representation agreement is now the single most common missing document in transaction files.** A February 2026 survey by T3 Sixty found that 34 percent of buyer-side files opened in Q4 2025 arrived at the TC or compliance desk without a properly executed agreement attached. That is not a training problem—it is a systems problem.

## What The Data Shows After Twelve Months

RealTrends published its first post-settlement compensation study in March 2026, analyzing 148,000 closed transactions across 22 states between August 2024 and January 2026. The headline finding: average buyer-agent compensation dropped from 2.55 percent to 2.31 percent. That 24-basis-point decline matters at scale, but it did not trigger the agent exodus that doomsayers predicted.

More relevant for transaction teams is what shifted inside the file itself. Here is how the pre- and post-settlement transaction file compares for a standard residential purchase:

| Document / Step | Pre-Settlement (Before Aug 2024) | Post-Settlement (Aug 2024 Onward) |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer representation agreement | Often informal or signed at offer | Required before first showing |
| MLS cooperative compensation field | Populated by listing agent | Eliminated from MLS |
| Compensation negotiation | Rarely in purchase contract | Frequently a contract term or addendum |
| Seller concession for buyer-agent fee | Uncommon | Increasing in 40+ percent of transactions (RealTrends) |
| Agency disclosure timing | Varied by state | Tightened in 14 states post-settlement |

That table reveals something brokers should internalize: the compliance surface area per file grew by roughly 25 percent without a corresponding increase in TC staffing at most firms. The work did not disappear—it migrated.

## Who Actually Absorbed The New Work

**Transaction coordinators and team administrators became the de facto compliance backstop for the new documentation requirements.** Agents adapted their buyer consultations over several months, but the verification burden—confirming signed agreements exist, checking that compensation terms match what appears in the contract, flagging files where concession language conflicts with the representation agreement—landed squarely on the operations side.

This tracks with what we hear from brokerages running 30 to 100 transactions per month. The agents learned the new script. The TCs learned to catch everything the agents still forget. A [well-designed transaction handoff process](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) is no longer optional; it is the only mechanism that prevents these new gaps from compounding into closing delays.

## The Buyer Consultation Became A Compliance Event

Before August 2024, a buyer consultation was primarily a relationship-building exercise. Now it carries legal weight. The moment an agent shows a home without a signed agreement, the brokerage faces potential MLS violation and—depending on the state—a licensing complaint.

This transformed the buyer onboarding sequence from a soft-skill moment into a hard-deadline event. For teams tracking [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), the question shifted from "Did the agent meet with the buyer?" to "Is the executed agreement uploaded before the first tour date?" That distinction matters enormously when you are coordinating 15 buyer files simultaneously.

Fourteen states updated their agency disclosure rules within the first nine months of the settlement taking effect. [State-level agency requirements](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) now vary more dramatically than they did two years ago, and multi-state brokerages face a patchwork of timing mandates and form versions that did not previously exist.

## The Strongest Counterargument—And Why It Falls Short

The most credible pushback on the "settlement changed everything" narrative goes like this: commission compression was already underway, buyer agreements were already standard in many states, and good TCs were already catching documentation gaps. The settlement, this argument holds, merely formalized existing best practices.

There is truth in that. Texas and several other states already required written buyer agreements. High-performing teams already ran tight onboarding checklists. But formalizing a best practice across 1.5 million NAR members is materially different from having it exist in pockets of excellence. The difference is enforcement. MLS systems now reject files that would have passed twelve months earlier. Compliance reviewers at brokerages now reject files that would have closed unremarked. The margin for error compressed industry-wide, and that compression hit operations teams hardest because they sit at the enforcement checkpoint.

## Commission Negotiation Moved Into The Contract

Perhaps the least discussed operational shift: buyer-agent compensation is now frequently negotiated inside the purchase agreement rather than established in advance via the MLS. This means TCs reviewing contracts must now verify not only price, earnest money, and contingency deadlines, but also whether the compensation terms align with the separate buyer representation agreement.

| Scenario | Pre-Settlement Workflow | Post-Settlement Workflow |
|---|---|---|
| Seller offers buyer-agent comp | Visible in MLS; no contract language needed | Must appear as contract term or seller concession |
| Buyer pays own agent directly | Rare; minimal documentation | Requires explicit agreement language; TC must verify funding source |
| Compensation mismatch between agreement and contract | Uncommon edge case | Flagged in roughly 12 percent of files per WAV Group Q1 2026 audit |
| TC review of compensation terms | Not standard practice | Now a required compliance checkpoint at most brokerages |

That 12 percent mismatch rate from WAV Group's audit should concern every team lead reading this. A compensation discrepancy discovered at closing creates a last-minute scramble that can delay funding or, worse, trigger a post-closing dispute.

## What Needs To Change In Year Two

The firms that adapted fastest shared one trait: they treated the settlement as an operations problem, not a sales problem. They updated their intake forms within weeks. They added a compensation-verification step to their TC checklists. They built the buyer agreement into their CRM workflows as a hard gate rather than a reminder.

**The brokerages still struggling twelve months later are the ones that treated the settlement as a one-time training event instead of a permanent workflow redesign.** A single all-hands meeting in July 2024 did not prepare anyone for the daily grind of catching missing agreements, reconciling compensation terms, and managing the expanded documentation load across dozens of concurrent files.
