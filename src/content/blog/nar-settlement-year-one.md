## The Verdict After Twelve Months

The NAR settlement was supposed to blow up the traditional commission model. One year in, the honest assessment is this: it changed paperwork and process more than it changed economics. Total commission dollars paid by sellers declined modestly—around half a percentage point on average according to RealTrends Verified data from Q1 2026—but the operational burden on transaction teams exploded. If you run a team or manage a brokerage's back office, the settlement didn't rewrite your revenue model so much as it rewired your compliance obligations.

That distinction matters because it tells you where to invest attention. The brokers who panicked about margin compression twelve months ago are mostly fine on revenue. The ones who failed to retool their transaction workflows are drowning in rejected offers, unsigned agreements, and audit exposure.

## What the Settlement Actually Required

For anyone who needs a refresher without the legal jargon: the [Burnett v. NAR settlement](https://www.nar.realtor/the-settlement), finalized in August 2024 and effective since August 17, 2024, imposed two structural changes. First, offers of buyer-agent compensation can no longer appear on MLS listings. Second, agents working with buyers must execute a written buyer representation agreement before touring properties.

**Neither of those rules said a single word about who ultimately pays.** They addressed transparency and decoupling—making sure buyers know what their agent costs and that compensation is negotiated rather than assumed. The sky-is-falling predictions about buyer agents going unpaid were always overblown, and the data from the first full year confirms it.

What the settlement did create is a documentation gauntlet. Every transaction now carries at least one additional required agreement that must be signed, dated, and filed before the first showing—not just before an offer, before a showing. That timing requirement is where deals have actually broken down.

## The Paperwork Multiplier Effect

Transaction coordinators and team admins saw their per-file workload increase by an estimated 20-30 minutes per buyer-side deal, based on reporting from TC associations and brokerage operations surveys conducted by T3 Sixty in early 2026. That half-hour isn't glamorous. It consists of chasing buyer agreements, verifying compensation terms match between the buyer rep agreement and the purchase contract, and confirming that any seller concession language doesn't contradict MLS rules.

Here is where the real operational shift lives:

| Task | Pre-Settlement | Post-Settlement |
|------|---------------|-----------------|
| Buyer rep agreement required | Only in some states/brokerages | Universal before first showing |
| Compensation source documented in file | Rarely explicit | Required in every transaction |
| MLS compliance check on commission language | Not applicable | Required before listing goes live |
| Offer rejection risk from paperwork gaps | Low | Moderate to high |
| Audit exposure per file | Minimal | Elevated for 18 months post-close |

That table is not theoretical. Teams that handle [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously felt the pain immediately in Q4 2024 and Q1 2025 when the new rules collided with seasonal volume spikes.

## Commission Compression: Real but Modest

The headline everyone wanted to write was "commissions crater." The reality is more nuanced. Average total commission rates dropped from approximately 5.3% to approximately 4.8% nationally, per data compiled by Redfin and corroborated by brokerage earnings calls from Anywhere, Compass, and eXp in Q1 2026. That is a real decline. It is not a collapse.

**Buyer-side commission averages dropped from roughly 2.6% to 2.3%, with significant variance by market.** Luxury markets in coastal metros saw sharper declines. Middle-market suburban transactions stayed closer to historical norms because sellers still overwhelmingly chose to offer concessions that covered buyer agent fees—just through different contractual vehicles.

The shift was less about total dollars and more about who initiates the conversation. Before the settlement, compensation was embedded and invisible to most buyers. Now it is discussed upfront, which has created friction in the buyer consultation but hasn't caused mass defection from using buyer agents.

## The Strongest Counterargument

Critics—including DOJ officials who filed a statement of interest in the case—argue that one year is too short to judge structural impact. They point out that consumer awareness of the changes remains low, citing a Clever Real Estate survey from March 2026 showing 58% of recent buyers were unaware they could negotiate their agent's fee separately from the home price. The argument follows that once awareness rises, downward pressure on buyer-side commissions will accelerate meaningfully.

This is a credible argument, and I take it seriously. But awareness campaigns have been running for a full year now, and the market has not moved dramatically. The more likely trajectory is a slow grind downward—another quarter-point over three to five years—rather than a sudden repricing event. Transaction teams should plan for gradual margin erosion, not an overnight reckoning. That means building operational efficiency now rather than waiting for a crisis that may arrive as a slow leak instead of a burst pipe.

## What Top Teams Changed Operationally

The brokerages and teams that adapted fastest shared three traits. They standardized their buyer consultation process into a repeatable system with scripts, disclosures, and agreements preloaded. They assigned explicit ownership of the buyer rep agreement to a specific person—usually the TC or a showing assistant—rather than leaving it to the agent's memory. And they built audit trails that could survive a compliance review eighteen months after closing.

**Teams that treated the settlement as a paperwork problem outperformed those who treated it as a commission problem.** The commission question largely resolved itself through market forces. The paperwork question required deliberate operational redesign.

One brokerage in North Carolina reported reducing buyer-side file errors by 40% after implementing a pre-showing checklist that mirrors the [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) approach—assigning every document a responsible party and a deadline before the first appointment is set.

## Where Deals Actually Broke Down

The failure mode most TCs reported in Year One was not commission disputes at closing. It was [deals breaking down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) in week one because the buyer rep agreement was missing, incomplete, or contradicted the terms in the offer. Listing agents began rejecting offers that lacked clear documentation of buyer-side compensation sources, not out of malice but out of liability fear.

| Failure Point | Frequency Pre-Settlement | Frequency Post-Settlement |
|--------------|-------------------------|--------------------------|
| Missing buyer rep agreement at offer | Rare (state-dependent) | Common (12-15% of initial submissions per TREC reports) |
| Compensation mismatch between agreements | Almost nonexistent | Moderate (8-10% of files flagged in audits) |
| Buyer walking due to fee confusion | Very rare | Slightly elevated but still under 3% |
| Listing agent rejecting offer for documentation gaps | Negligible | Reported in 6-9% of multi-offer situations |

These numbers come from state regulatory reports and brokerage compliance officers interviewed by Inman and HousingWire throughout late 2025 and early 2026. They point to a clear conclusion: the settlement's biggest impact on deal flow is procedural, not economic.

## What Year Two Will Demand

The DOJ has signaled continued scrutiny. Several state legislatures—including those in [Texas](/blog/texas-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) and [Virginia](/blog/virginia-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026)—are considering additional disclosure requirements that layer on top of the settlement terms. Compliance complexity will increase, not decrease, in 2026 and 2027.

For brokers and team leads, the actionable move right now is to audit your buyer-side file process with the same rigor you apply to listing disclosures. Assign ownership. Set deadlines. Automate reminders. If your current system still relies on agents self-managing their buyer agreements, you are one busy month away from an audit finding or a rejected offer that costs a client.
