## Virginia Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Rules Agents Actually Need to Know

The question of **Virginia attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026** trips up even experienced agents because the answer is not a clean either-or. Virginia occupies a middle ground among states: it does not require an attorney to sit at the closing table, but it does require attorney involvement behind the scenes for specific legal functions. Misunderstanding where that line falls creates real liability for agents who steer clients toward noncompliant settlement services.

This post breaks down what Virginia statute actually says, what forms document these choices, and what mistakes get agents and brokers in trouble with the Virginia Real Estate Board (VREB) and the Virginia State Bar (VSB).

## The Statutory Framework: CRESPA and the Virginia Code

Virginia's Consumer Real Estate Settlement Protection Act, codified at Virginia Code Section 6.2-1600 through 6.2-1616, governs who may conduct real estate settlements. Under CRESPA, a "settlement agent" must be one of the following: a Virginia-licensed attorney, a title insurance company, or a title insurance agent registered with the Virginia State Corporation Commission (SCC). No one else may lawfully handle escrow funds or conduct settlement.

Separately, the Virginia State Bar's Unauthorized Practice of Law (UPL) Committee has issued opinions — most notably UPL Opinion 183 — clarifying that title examination constitutes the practice of law. This means a title company's non-attorney staff cannot independently search and certify title without attorney supervision. The distinction matters: a title company can run closing, but cannot perform the legal analysis of title without a lawyer's oversight.

**Agents who refer clients to settlement providers should verify both the CRESPA registration and the attorney-supervision arrangement before the referral.** Failing to do so does not violate VREB regulations directly, but it exposes the agent to negligence claims if the settlement later unravels due to an improperly certified title.

## Attorney States vs. Title Company States — Where Virginia Falls

Virginia is commonly classified as a "hybrid" or "title company state with attorney oversight." Here is how it compares to neighboring jurisdictions:

| Requirement | Virginia | West Virginia | Maryland | North Carolina |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Attorney must attend closing | No | No | No | Yes (for deed preparation) |
| Attorney must supervise title exam | Yes (VSB UPL 183) | No | No | Yes |
| Settlement agent must be CRESPA-licensed | Yes | N/A (different statute) | N/A | N/A |

| SCC registration required for title agents | Yes | No | No | No |

This table clarifies why agents moving between Virginia and neighboring states get confused. Virginia's requirements are statute-specific, and the relevant regulatory bodies — the SCC for licensing, the VSB for practice-of-law boundaries, and the VREB for agent conduct — each enforce different pieces of the puzzle.

## What Forms Document the Settlement Agent Selection

On the Virginia REALTORS residential contract of purchase (Form 600), Paragraph 10 addresses settlement. The parties designate the settlement agent and settlement location here. Agents must ensure the named entity holds a valid CRESPA license; writing in an unlicensed company name does not automatically void the contract, but it creates a title-insurance coverage gap that can delay or kill the deal at the eleventh hour.

**Form 600's Paragraph 10 is also where agents confirm whether the buyer or seller is selecting — and paying for — the settlement agent.** This is a negotiation point, not a legal mandate. However, VREB Regulation 18 VAC 135-20-280 prohibits agents from receiving undisclosed compensation for settlement referrals. If you refer a client to a specific title company and receive anything of value in return, you must disclose it in writing under both RESPA Section 8 and VREB rules.

Additionally, agents listing properties often encounter the [Virginia seller disclosure requirements](/blog/virginia-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) well before settlement. Getting disclosure documents filed correctly early prevents last-minute title issues that force attorney intervention at closing.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make at or Before Closing

**Mistake 1: Assuming "title company" means no attorney is involved.** Agents sometimes tell buyers they are saving money by using a title company instead of an attorney, implying no legal professional is part of the transaction. In Virginia, a licensed attorney must still supervise the title examination. If the title company lacks this arrangement, the title certification is defective. The buyer's lender will likely catch this and refuse to fund.

**Mistake 2: Failing to verify CRESPA registration.** The SCC maintains a public database of registered settlement agents. Agents who skip this step — particularly when working with smaller, independent title shops — risk steering clients to an unregistered provider. If the provider mishandles escrow funds, the agent's errors-and-omissions policy may not cover the resulting claim because the agent facilitated an unlawful settlement.

**Mistake 3: Preparing legal documents without authorization.** Some agents draft addenda that effectively modify title-related obligations or create lien-release language. Under VSB guidelines, this crosses into unauthorized practice of law. The proper course is to have the settlement attorney or the parties' own counsel prepare such documents. Agents who draft legal instruments face VSB complaints and potential VREB disciplinary action under 18 VAC 135-20-300.

**Mistake 4: Missing the wet-signature requirement for deeds.** Virginia Code Section 55.1-612 requires that deeds be signed and acknowledged before a notary. Agents occasionally schedule fully remote closings without confirming that the settlement agent has arranged for a Virginia-commissioned notary or that the transaction qualifies for remote online notarization (RON) under Virginia Code Section 47.1-2. A deed executed without proper notarization is unrecordable, halting the transaction.

**Mistake 5: Ignoring agency disclosure timing at settlement.** Virginia Code Section 54.1-2138 requires disclosure of agency relationships. Agents who have not properly documented agency before closing — using the [Virginia agency disclosure requirements](/blog/virginia-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) — may face VREB sanctions, and the settlement attorney may refuse to proceed until the documentation is in order.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2130 and VREB Regulation 18 VAC 135-20-160. When it comes to settlement-related compliance, brokers should focus audits on four areas.

First, verify that every transaction file contains proof the selected settlement agent holds a current CRESPA license. A simple screenshot from the SCC database, dated within 30 days of settlement, is sufficient. Second, confirm that no undisclosed affiliated business arrangements (ABAs) exist between agents and settlement providers. VREB treats undisclosed ABAs as a violation of 18 VAC 135-20-280, carrying fines up to $2,500 per occurrence.

Third, audit agent communications to ensure no one is offering legal opinions about title defects, lien priority, or deed language. These conversations should be redirected to the settlement attorney. Fourth, brokers should maintain a written office policy specifying that agents may not schedule settlement with any provider not on an approved list — or, at minimum, not without broker pre-approval.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Consequence of Failure |
|---|---|---|
| CRESPA license verification | SCC database confirmation in file | Potential E&O denial; VREB fine |
| ABA disclosure | Written disclosure to buyer/seller | VREB fine up to $2,500; RESPA violation |
| UPL boundary compliance | Agent emails/texts for legal advice | VSB complaint; VREB disciplinary hearing |
| Notarization/RON compliance | Deed acknowledgment records | Unrecordable deed; deal collapse |
| Agency disclosure at settlement | Signed 54.1-2138 form in file | VREB sanction; potential contract rescission |

Brokers who treat these audits as quarterly rather than annual are far less likely to face board complaints. A [weekly pipeline review system](/blog/weekly-pipeline-review-system) that includes a settlement-compliance checkpoint is one way to build this into existing workflows without adding a standalone audit burden.

## Liability When Things Go Wrong

If an agent directs a client to an unlicensed settlement provider and funds are misappropriated, the agent may face both civil liability and VREB discipline. Virginia Code Section 6.2-1616 imposes penalties on unlicensed settlement activity, but injured consumers often sue the referring agent under negligence theories. The argument: a licensed professional should have known the provider was unregistered.

**VREB can impose fines, license suspension, or revocation under 18 VAC 135-20-300 for conduct that endangers clients or violates statutory requirements.** Even where the agent did not personally mishandle funds, the failure to verify licensure or the provision of legal advice outside the agent's scope can trigger these penalties. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) goes well beyond a single commission check — it includes legal defense costs, increased E&O premiums, and reputational damage that compounds over years.

Deal cancellation is also on the table. Under Form 600, if settlement cannot occur because the designated agent is not legally qualified, the non-defaulting party may terminate and recover deposits. Agents who created the problem by recommending an unqualified provider have limited defenses.

## The 2026 Landscape: Remote Closings and Evolving Standards

Virginia expanded its RON statutes in recent years, and as of 2026, remote online notarization is fully available for real estate transactions under Virginia Code Section 47.1-2 et seq. This does not eliminate the attorney-supervision requirement for title examination — it only changes how signatures and acknowledgments are captured. Agents must not conflate "remote closing" with "no attorney needed."

The Virginia State Bar continues to monitor title-related UPL issues, and proposed 2026 guidance suggests tighter enforcement around title companies operating without clearly documented attorney supervision. Agents should expect settlement companies to begin providing written confirmation of their supervisory attorney arrangement. Ask for it proactively; if a title company cannot produce this documentation, treat it as a red flag.
