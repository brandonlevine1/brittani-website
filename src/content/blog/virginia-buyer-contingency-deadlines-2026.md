## Understanding Virginia Buyer Contingency Deadlines 2026

Missed deadlines kill deals. In Virginia's residential market, the interplay between buyer contingency deadlines and contract performance obligations follows rules that are simultaneously rigid and agent-dependent—meaning your competence as a licensee is the only thing standing between a smooth closing and a complaint filed with the Virginia Real Estate Board. Virginia buyer contingency deadlines 2026 remain governed by the same statutory framework that has created liability traps for agents since the Virginia Residential Property Disclosure Act was last amended, but the stakes grow sharper each year as enforcement actions increase and transaction volume picks up.

The Virginia Association of Realtors (VAR) publishes standardized forms that most MLS-affiliated brokerages mandate. These forms contain blank fields where agents insert specific day-counts—and those blanks are where errors live. Unlike states with statutory default periods baked into code, Virginia places the burden on the drafting agent to get the math right, confirm the dates in writing, and deliver notices within the window.

If you are managing multiple active transactions simultaneously, the margin for error compounds quickly. Understanding how each contingency operates on paper versus in practice separates agents who close consistently from agents who face Board discipline under [Virginia Code Section 54.1-2131](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title54.1/chapter21/section54.1-2131/) and its associated regulations.

## The Core Forms That Govern Buyer Contingencies

**VAR Form 600 (Residential Contract of Purchase) is the backbone of nearly every buyer-side contingency deadline in the Commonwealth.** This form contains the home inspection contingency, financing contingency, appraisal contingency, and property condition provisions, each with negotiable day-count blanks that agents fill in at contract drafting. VAR Form 600 was updated for 2025-2026 use, and the current version includes revised language on electronic delivery that affects when a deadline clock starts ticking.

Beyond the primary purchase contract, several companion forms carry their own independent deadlines. VAR Form 610 (Home Inspection Contingency Addendum) provides additional specificity around inspection periods, repair negotiations, and the buyer's right to terminate. VAR Form 620 addresses the financing contingency with specific performance benchmarks the buyer must meet.

| Form | Purpose | Key Deadline Field | Typical Day Count |
|------|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| VAR 600 | Primary Purchase Contract | Multiple contingency blanks | Varies by negotiation |
| VAR 610 | Home Inspection Contingency | Days for inspection completion and buyer response | 7-10 days common |
| VAR 620 | Financing Contingency Addendum | Loan approval deadline | 21 days common |
| VAR 630 | Appraisal Contingency | Days after appraisal delivery for buyer decision | 3-5 days common |
| VAR 640 | Sale of Buyer's Property Contingency | Days for buyer to secure ratified contract on existing home | 30-60 days common |

Each form's deadline operates independently unless the contract explicitly ties them together. An agent who assumes the inspection deadline and the financing deadline share a common trigger date—they do not—creates exposure for the buyer and for themselves.

## How Deadlines Are Calculated Under Virginia Contract Law

Virginia follows the general principle that the day of ratification is Day 0, and counting begins the following calendar day. This seems straightforward until you factor in VAR's specific provisions about weekends and holidays. If the final day of a contingency period falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or state-recognized legal holiday, the deadline automatically extends to the next business day.

**Ratification itself is a contested moment in many transactions.** Under VAR Form 600, ratification occurs when the last party (or their agent) receives the fully executed contract. Electronic delivery is valid under Virginia's Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (Virginia Code Section 59.1-479 et seq.), but the timestamp of receipt—not sending—controls when the clock starts. An email sent at 11:58 PM on a Tuesday but not opened or received by the other party's agent until Wednesday morning creates an ambiguity that agents must resolve prospectively, not retroactively.

The distinction between "business days" and "calendar days" is another trap. VAR forms primarily use calendar days for contingency periods but reference business days in certain notice provisions. Agents who conflate the two—or who assume all day-counts work the same way—routinely miscalculate deadlines by one to three days, which is enough to waive a contingency or forfeit earnest money.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

The consequences of missing or mismanaging a buyer contingency deadline in Virginia fall into three categories: transactional, regulatory, and civil.

On the transactional side, a missed inspection contingency deadline typically means the buyer has waived their right to terminate based on property conditions. The buyer proceeds obligated to purchase, or they breach the contract and forfeit their earnest money deposit. Neither outcome serves the client, and both reflect directly on the agent's competence.

Regulatory exposure comes through the Virginia Real Estate Board, which operates under the Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR). The Board enforces [18 VAC 135-20-300](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/admincode/title18/agency135/chapter20/section300/), which requires licensees to act with "reasonable skill and care." A pattern of missed deadlines or a single egregious failure can result in investigation, mandatory continuing education, fines up to $2,500 per violation, license suspension, or revocation. These are not theoretical outcomes—the Board publishes disciplinary actions quarterly.

Civil liability is the third vector. A buyer who loses their right to terminate because their agent missed a deadline may pursue a negligence claim against the agent and the agent's brokerage. Errors and Omissions insurance typically covers these claims, but premiums rise and coverage disputes emerge when the insurer argues the agent's error was a known, preventable mistake rather than a judgment call gone wrong.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Virginia Contingency Deadlines

**The first and most frequent error is leaving day-count blanks unfilled on VAR forms.** When an agent submits a ratified contract with an empty contingency field, the contingency either does not exist (exposing the buyer) or becomes legally ambiguous (exposing everyone). The VAR forms are designed so that a blank field means the provision does not apply—a fact that surprises agents who assumed a "reasonable" default would be implied by courts. Virginia courts do not imply contingency periods that parties failed to specify.

The second common mistake is confusing the inspection period with the repair negotiation period. VAR Form 610 creates two distinct windows: the period during which the buyer must complete inspections and the separate period during which the buyer must deliver a written list of requested repairs or elect to terminate. Agents who wait until the last day of the inspection window to request repairs often discover they have conflated two sequential deadlines into one.

Third, agents frequently fail to deliver written notice of contingency satisfaction or termination in the manner specified by the contract. VAR Form 600 requires written notice delivered to the other party's agent (or designated recipient) before the deadline expires. A phone call does not satisfy this requirement. A text message may not satisfy it depending on what the parties agreed regarding acceptable delivery methods in the contract's delivery provisions section.

Fourth, agents miscalculate the financing contingency deadline by assuming it starts from the date the buyer submits their loan application rather than from ratification. The clock on VAR Form 620 runs from ratification, not from the buyer's lender engagement date. This misunderstanding leads agents to believe they have additional days when they do not.

Fifth, agents fail to [track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) with adequate systems, leading to calendar conflicts where two transactions have contingency deadlines on the same day and one inevitably gets missed. Virginia does not recognize "I was busy with another client" as a defense to a missed deadline.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under Virginia Code Section 54.1-2130 for the acts of their licensees. A broker who fails to implement reasonable systems for deadline tracking faces the same regulatory exposure as the agent who missed the deadline. The Board has historically held that "adequate supervision" requires more than periodic file reviews—it requires proactive systems.

| Broker Responsibility | Minimum Standard | Best Practice |
|----------------------|------------------|---------------|
| Contract review at ratification | Verify all contingency blanks are completed | Same-day audit of form accuracy and deadline calculation |
| Deadline tracking system | Calendar or spreadsheet accessible to broker | Automated alerts 72, 48, and 24 hours before each deadline |
| Notice delivery verification | File copy of all contingency notices | Timestamped confirmation of delivery method and receipt |
| Agent training on form updates | Annual CE on VAR form changes | Quarterly in-house training with form-specific scenarios |
| File audit frequency | Monthly random sampling | Weekly review of all pending files within contingency windows |

**Brokers should audit every ratified contract within 24 hours of execution to confirm that contingency deadlines are properly filled, mathematically correct, and entered into the brokerage's tracking system.** The [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) between agent and transaction coordinator—or between listing side and buyer side—is where most deadline tracking failures originate.

Virginia DPOR has increased its focus on brokerage-level compliance since 2024, and brokers who rely solely on agents to self-manage deadlines face escalating risk. The regulatory theory is straightforward: if your agent missed a deadline because your brokerage lacks a tracking system, your supervision was inadequate.

## Virginia Buyer Contingency Deadlines 2026 and the Electronic Delivery Problem

The shift toward electronic transactions has created a secondary compliance layer for Virginia contingency deadlines that many agents underestimate. VAR Form 600's delivery provisions section allows parties to specify acceptable methods: personal delivery, mail, facsimile, or electronic transmission. When parties select electronic transmission, the timestamp of receipt controls—not the timestamp of sending.

This distinction matters because email delivery receipts, read receipts, and platform timestamps vary by system. An agent who sends a contingency termination notice at 11:45 PM on the deadline day via email has technically met their obligation only if the recipient's system records receipt before midnight. If the email lands in a spam filter or the recipient's server delays delivery, the notice may be untimely.

**The safest practice is to deliver all contingency-related notices no later than 5:00 PM on the deadline day and to obtain affirmative written confirmation of receipt from the other party's agent.** This is not required by the forms, but it eliminates the delivery-timing disputes that generate complaints to the Board and small-claims litigation over earnest money.

Agents who want to understand [why deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) at the contingency stage should start with their own delivery habits. The substance of a timely decision means nothing if the notice conveying that decision arrives one hour late.

## Building a System That Protects Your License

Deadline management is not a skill problem—it is a systems problem. The agents who face Board complaints are rarely incompetent; they are disorganized or over-leveraged with too many active files and no automated safeguards. The solution is not to work harder but to build redundancy into your tracking workflow.

Every contingency deadline in a Virginia transaction should exist in at least two independent systems: the agent's personal calendar and a brokerage-level tracking tool that the supervising broker can access. Single-point-of-failure systems—where only the agent knows the deadline—violate the spirit of Virginia's supervisory requirements and create practical risk when agents get sick, go on vacation, or simply forget.
