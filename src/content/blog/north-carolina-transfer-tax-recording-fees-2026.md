# North Carolina Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026: What Every Agent Must Know Before Closing

Getting the numbers wrong on transfer taxes or recording fees does not just embarrass you at the closing table—it can delay funding, trigger compliance flags, and cost your client real money. Understanding North Carolina transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 is non-negotiable for any agent preparing accurate settlement statements or advising sellers on net proceeds. The state has not changed its core excise tax rate in years, but the details around exemptions, local surcharges, and recording page fees still trip up experienced agents every quarter.

This post breaks down the statutory framework, the exact dollar figures you need for 2026 closings, and the compliance traps that expose agents and brokers to liability.

## The Statutory Foundation: N.C. Gen. Stat. 105-228.30

North Carolina's real estate excise tax is governed primarily by Article 8E of Chapter 105 of the North Carolina General Statutes. Specifically, N.C. Gen. Stat. 105-228.30 imposes a tax on each instrument conveying an interest in real property at the rate of $1.00 per $500 of the consideration or value of the interest conveyed. This has been the rate since the General Assembly standardized it decades ago, and no legislative changes have been enacted for 2026.

**The tax applies to deeds, deeds of trust (only the underlying transfer), and any other instrument that conveys title.** Mortgages themselves are not subject to excise tax—only the conveyance instrument. If the consideration includes assumption of an existing loan balance, that amount is included in the taxable base.

One wrinkle agents forget: the tax applies to each $500 or fraction thereof. A sale price of $350,250 means you calculate on $350,500, yielding $701.00 in excise tax, not $700.50.

## Recording Fee Breakdown for 2026

Recording fees in North Carolina are set by N.C. Gen. Stat. 161-10 and are collected by the county Register of Deeds. The fee structure applies per instrument, and most counties follow the state schedule without local deviation on base recording fees.

| Fee Type | Amount (2026) | Statute |
|----------|--------------|---------|
| First page of any instrument | $26.00 | N.C.G.S. 161-10(a)(1) |
| Each additional page | $4.00 | N.C.G.S. 161-10(a)(1) |
| Deed of Trust (first page) | $26.00 | N.C.G.S. 161-10(a)(1) |
| Each additional page (DOT) | $4.00 | N.C.G.S. 161-10(a)(1) |
| Plat recording | $21.00 per sheet | N.C.G.S. 161-10(a)(6) |
| Satisfaction/Cancellation | $26.00 | N.C.G.S. 161-10(a)(1) |

These fees are typically charged to the buyer for the deed and deed of trust recording, while the seller covers any required satisfaction of existing liens. Agents who estimate recording costs on a multi-page deed of trust should assume 15-25 pages for a standard residential loan package, which pushes recording fees for that single document to $82-$122.

The North Carolina Real Estate Commission does not regulate recording fees directly, but expects agents to provide accurate cost estimates on the NCAR/NCBA Offer to Purchase and Contract (Form 2-T) and the associated due diligence forms. Inaccurate estimates can constitute a misrepresentation under [21 NCAC 58A .0104](https://www.ncrec.gov/Commission/Rules).

## Local Transfer Tax Exceptions: The Counties You Must Flag

Most North Carolina counties collect only the state excise tax at the $1.00 per $500 rate. However, the General Assembly has granted a handful of counties authority to impose an additional land transfer tax under special legislation. As of 2026, the following counties collect a local surcharge in addition to the state rate.

**If you sell property in the Outer Banks region or the northeastern coastal counties, you must adjust your excise tax calculations upward by 50%.** Failing to account for this on a $600,000 beach house sale means a $200 shortfall at closing—enough to stall recording if the closing attorney's office does not catch it first.

Agents practicing in these counties should confirm the surcharge applies before drafting net sheets. The total excise tax on a $600,000 sale in Dare County is $1,800, not the $1,200 a seller might expect based on the standard rate alone.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make on Transfer Tax and Recording Fees

Five errors show up repeatedly in North Carolina closings, and most of them stem from agents relying on outdated assumptions or failing to verify county-specific requirements.

**First, agents routinely miscalculate the excise tax by ignoring the "fraction thereof" rule.** A sale price of $425,100 requires calculation on $425,500 (the next $500 increment), producing $851.00 in excise tax. Many agents calculate on $425,000 flat and quote $850.00. The one-dollar difference rarely kills a deal, but it reflects sloppy practice that compounds across multiple line items.

Second, agents in the northeastern coastal counties forget the local land transfer tax surcharge. This is not an obscure rule—Dare County alone accounts for thousands of residential closings annually. Quoting the wrong net to a seller on a $900,000 Nags Head listing means a $150 surprise at closing.

Third, agents assume recording fees are a flat rate rather than a per-page charge. A deed of trust with 22 pages costs $110.00 to record (first page at $26.00 plus 21 additional pages at $4.00 each), not the $26.00 some agents assume. When buyers are tight on cash to close, a $75+ underestimate on recording fees can trigger a funding shortfall.

Fourth, agents fail to identify excise tax exemptions that apply. Under N.C. Gen. Stat. 105-228.29, certain transfers are exempt from excise tax, including transfers between spouses incident to divorce, transfers to or from governmental units, and transfers where no consideration is exchanged. Agents sometimes advise sellers they owe excise tax on an interfamily transfer that actually qualifies for exemption—or worse, fail to flag a non-exempt transfer as taxable.

Fifth, listing agents produce net sheets that omit the seller's share of recording fees for satisfaction of existing liens. The seller typically pays to record the satisfaction of their existing deed of trust, and that $26.00-$30.00 charge should appear on every preliminary net sheet. Small amounts, but they add up to credibility with clients who notice discrepancies at the closing table.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers-in-charge carry supervisory responsibility under 21 NCAC 58A .0110 for ensuring their agents provide accurate representations to clients. When it comes to transfer tax and recording fee compliance, this means specific audit points cannot be delegated away.

**Brokers should spot-check net sheets and preliminary cost estimates on at least a quarterly basis, focusing on agents who work in multiple counties.** An agent who splits time between Wake County (standard rate) and Dare County (surcharge rate) is the most likely to produce an incorrect estimate. A systematic review process—even a random sample of five net sheets per month—catches patterns before they become client complaints.

Brokers also need to verify that contract modifications in Form 2-T Paragraph 8 are intentional and properly initialed. If an agent shifts excise tax responsibility to the buyer without clear negotiation documentation, the broker faces potential license discipline under N.C. Gen. Stat. 93A-6(a)(1) for failing to supervise. Our post on [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) covers several scenarios where fee miscommunication triggers last-minute disputes.

Additionally, brokers should maintain an updated reference sheet of county-specific rates and recording fee schedules. The North Carolina Register of Deeds Association publishes updates, but individual county offices remain the authoritative source. A centralized compliance resource—whether a shared document or an automated system—prevents agents from relying on memory.

For brokerages managing high transaction volumes, tracking these details manually across dozens of agents becomes unsustainable. ## Liability Exposure When Agents Get It Wrong

The consequences of misquoting transfer taxes or recording fees range from mild embarrassment to actual financial liability. At the closing table, if excise tax is underfunded, the Register of Deeds will refuse to record the deed under N.C. Gen. Stat. 161-10. This means the transaction does not close on time, which can trigger per-diem charges, rate lock expirations, and breach-of-contract claims.

> N.C. Gen. Stat. 105-228.30(b): "An instrument subject to the tax imposed by this section may not be registered or recorded by the register of deeds unless the tax has been paid."

If a seller relies on an agent's net sheet and arrives at closing expecting $15,000 more than they actually receive—because the agent omitted the Dare County surcharge on a $5 million oceanfront sale ($5,000 difference)—the agent faces a potential negligent misrepresentation claim. The North Carolina Real Estate Commission can also initiate disciplinary proceedings under N.C. Gen. Stat. 93A-6(a)(1) for making "any misrepresentation" in connection with a real estate transaction.

Financial exposure aside, repeated errors erode referral networks faster than any market downturn. Closing attorneys in North Carolina handle the actual recording and tax payment, but they base their preliminary figures on the information agents provide regarding sale price, exemptions, and allocation of costs.
