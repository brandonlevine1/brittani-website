## Understanding Massachusetts Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Agents closing deals across the Commonwealth in 2026 need precise knowledge of the Massachusetts transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 to avoid rejected recordings, delayed closings, and uncomfortable post-settlement phone calls from title attorneys. The fee structure in Massachusetts is governed primarily by Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 64D, which establishes the deed excise tax, and by the individual Registries of Deeds that set recording fees for each county. Getting these numbers wrong does not just annoy a closing attorney—it can stall a transaction long enough to blow a rate lock or violate a contractual deadline.

Massachusetts operates 21 Registries of Deeds across its 14 counties, and some counties maintain separate registries for registered versus recorded land. Each registry sets its own page fees and ancillary charges, though the excise tax rate is uniform statewide with one notable exception. Agents who work multiple counties need to track these differences or risk under-quoting closing costs to clients.

## The Deed Excise Tax: Rates, Calculations, and the Barnstable Exception

**The Massachusetts deed excise tax is set at $4.56 per $1,000 of consideration under M.G.L. Chapter 64D, Section 1.** On a $600,000 sale, that equals $2,736 owed to the Commonwealth. The tax must be paid at the time of recording, and the Registry will not accept the deed without accompanying excise stamps or their electronic equivalent.

Barnstable County stands alone with a higher combined rate of $7.14 per $1,000, which includes the standard $4.56 state excise plus a $2.58 surcharge under the Cape Cod and Islands Land Bank Act (M.G.L. Chapter 293). The same $600,000 transaction in Barnstable County costs the seller $4,284 in deed excise. Agents listing properties on the Cape must account for this difference in their net sheets from day one.

The tax is calculated on the full consideration, meaning the total purchase price including any assumed mortgages. There is no deduction for existing liens being paid off. Exemptions exist for certain government transfers, tax title proceedings, and conveyances between spouses incident to divorce under M.G.L. Chapter 64D, Section 1, but these exemptions do not apply to standard residential sales.

## Recording Fees at the Registry of Deeds

Recording fees in Massachusetts are separate from the deed excise tax and apply to every document submitted for recording—deeds, mortgages, discharges, homestead declarations, and municipal lien certificates. The base recording fee is $75 for the first page of most documents, with additional pages costing $25 each. These fees are set by [M.G.L. Chapter 36, Section 13](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleIV/Chapter36/Section13) and apply uniformly across all county registries.

**Mortgage recording carries its own separate structure that agents frequently miscalculate.** A new mortgage filing requires the $75 base fee, a $25 per additional page charge, and—most critically—a separate deed excise on the mortgage note itself if the note exceeds the prior encumbrance. That mortgage excise is $2.28 per $500 of the new debt (the "intangible tax" under M.G.L. Chapter 64D, Section 8), though it only applies to the amount of new money.

Here is what a typical buyer-side recording package looks like in cost:

| Document | Base Fee | Per Additional Page | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------------------|-------|
| Deed | $75 | $25 | Plus excise stamps |
| Mortgage | $75 | $25 | Plus mortgage excise if applicable |
| Homestead Declaration | $35 | — | Flat fee |
| Municipal Lien Certificate | $75 | $25 | Filed by seller's attorney |
| Discharge of Mortgage | $75 | $25 | Filed by seller's lender |

Agents should note that registered land (Land Court property) filings go through the Land Registration Office and carry slightly different procedural requirements, including the filing of a Certificate of Title. The fees are comparable, but the forms differ.

## Forms Every Agent Should Know

The primary form tied to the transfer tax is the Statement of Consideration, commonly referenced as Form REA-2. This document is filed with the deed at the Registry and declares the sale price, the excise amount paid, and the parties involved. Without it, the registrar will reject the deed package. The seller's closing attorney typically prepares this form, but listing agents should verify it appears in the closing package during final review.

For transactions involving trusts, agents will encounter the Certificate of Trust form required under M.G.L. Chapter 184, Section 35. This is not a tax form, but it must accompany the deed when a trust is the grantor. Missing this form is one of the most common reasons deeds get kicked back from the Registry, adding days to a closing.

Buyer's agents should confirm that Form 1099-S reporting is handled by the closing attorney. While this is an IRS requirement rather than a state one, the Massachusetts Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons expects agents to ensure proper coordination of all closing-related documentation. Failure to do so can trigger questions during a board audit.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Transfer Taxes and Recording Fees

**First, agents routinely forget to adjust net sheets for Barnstable County's higher rate.** A seller expecting $2,736 in excise who learns at closing that the actual amount is $4,284 will blame their listing agent, not the statute. This $1,548 difference on a $600,000 property is material, and it comes straight out of the seller's proceeds.

Second, agents confuse who is responsible for the mortgage recording excise. The buyer pays this tax because it applies to their new loan, but listing agents sometimes quote sellers for this cost on preliminary net sheets pulled from outdated templates. The mortgage excise applies only to new debt above the prior encumbrance threshold, and the calculation is the buyer's attorney's responsibility.

Third, agents working with [transaction coordinators](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) sometimes fail to communicate whether the property is registered land or recorded land. The distinction affects which registry office processes the filing, which forms are required, and whether a Land Court examiner must approve the deed before it can be registered. Getting this wrong does not just delay recording—it can mean the entire closing package must be reassembled.

Fourth, agents neglect to account for the municipal lien certificate (MLC) fee in their pre-listing cost estimates. Massachusetts requires an MLC to confirm all municipal charges are paid before conveyance. The fee varies by municipality (typically $25 to $150), and some towns take two to three weeks to issue them. Missing this timeline can push a closing date, which is particularly dangerous when rate locks are expiring.

Fifth, newer agents assume excise tax exemptions apply to scenarios they do not cover. A transfer from an individual to their own LLC, for example, does not qualify for the spousal or divorce exemption. The full $4.56 per $1,000 applies, and the Registry will demand payment before recording.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers in Massachusetts carry affirmative supervisory duties under 254 CMR 2.00, the regulations promulgated by the Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons. Those duties extend to ensuring that agents under their supervision provide accurate closing cost estimates and coordinate properly with closing attorneys on tax and recording matters.

**Brokers should audit net sheets and buyer cost estimates for correct excise tax calculations at least quarterly.** A pattern of errors across an office suggests a training gap, and the Board can cite a broker for inadequate supervision under M.G.L. Chapter 112, Section 87AAA. The penalties include license suspension, fines up to $1,000 per violation, and mandatory continuing education remediation.

Specifically, brokers should verify that their agents distinguish between the deed excise (seller's responsibility) and the mortgage excise (buyer's responsibility). They should also confirm that agents working in Barnstable County use the $7.14 rate rather than the statewide $4.56 figure. A simple spot-check of three to five recent transaction files per agent per quarter is sufficient to catch systemic problems.

The difference between a smooth recording and a rejected deed package often comes down to whether someone verified the correct county fee schedule before preparing closing figures.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

A rejected deed recording means the buyer does not hold legal title of record. Title insurance companies will not issue a final owner's policy until the deed is properly recorded with all excise taxes paid. During the gap between closing and successful recording, the buyer is exposed to intervening liens, judgment attachments, or bankruptcy filings against the seller.

If an agent provides materially inaccurate closing cost figures that cause a buyer or seller financial harm, the agent faces potential liability under consumer protection statutes (M.G.L. Chapter 93A) and disciplinary action from the Board. The Board has historically treated repeated fee miscalculations as evidence of incompetence, which can result in license conditions or revocation proceedings.

Deal cancellation is also a real risk. When recording fees or excise taxes are understated and a seller refuses to cover the difference, the transaction can fall apart at the closing table. Massachusetts purchase and sale agreements typically do not include a contingency for "recording fee errors," meaning the party at fault may face breach of contract claims. Agents who consistently [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) due to documentation errors compound this risk.
