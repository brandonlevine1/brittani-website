## Arizona Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Definitive Answer

The question of Arizona attorney vs. title company — who must be at closing 2026 generates more confusion among agents than almost any other procedural issue in the state. Arizona is firmly a "title company state," meaning no statute requires an attorney to be present at the closing table for a residential real estate transaction. That distinction matters for your workflow, your liability exposure, and the advice you give clients who ask whether they should hire counsel.

Unlike states such as Georgia, Massachusetts, or South Carolina where attorney involvement is mandated by law or longstanding custom, Arizona places the closing function squarely in the hands of licensed title agencies and escrow officers. The statutory authority for this framework flows from Arizona Revised Statutes Title 20 (Insurance) and Title 6 (Banks and Financial Institutions), which together regulate who may conduct escrow and issue title insurance in the state.

## The Statutory Framework Governing Arizona Closings

**Arizona does not have a "closing attorney" requirement anywhere in its revised statutes.** The Arizona Department of Insurance and Financial Institutions (ADIFI) regulates title agencies under A.R.S. 20-1562 through 20-1599, which establishes licensing, bonding, and operational requirements for title insurers and agents. Escrow agents operating outside of title companies fall under A.R.S. 6-841 through 6-846, supervised by the same department.

The Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE), operating under A.R.S. 32-2101 et seq., governs agent conduct but does not dictate who presides over the closing itself. ADRE's jurisdiction ends at agent behavior—how you represent clients, handle documents, and disclose material facts—not at who sits at the head of the settlement table.

This creates a clean division of authority. Title companies prepare the settlement statement, manage the escrow account, record documents, and disburse funds. Agents facilitate the transaction, ensure contract compliance, and coordinate timelines.

## What Title Companies Actually Do at an Arizona Closing

An Arizona title company performs functions that in attorney-required states would be split between counsel and a settlement agent. The title officer conducts the title search, issues the preliminary title report, and clears exceptions before closing. The escrow officer manages the escrow account under fiduciary obligations established by A.R.S. 6-843, which mandates segregation of funds and specific disbursement timelines.

At the closing table, the escrow officer walks parties through the signing package, explains recording procedures, and confirms wire receipt. They do not provide legal advice—a critical distinction that agents must reinforce with clients. When a buyer asks the escrow officer to interpret a contract clause or explain the legal effect of a deed restriction, that question falls outside the officer's scope.

This is precisely where attorneys enter the picture voluntarily. Either party may retain counsel at any point, and sophisticated transactions—commercial deals, trusts, 1031 exchanges, boundary disputes—often warrant attorney involvement even though it is not mandated. Your job as an agent is to know when to recommend counsel without practicing law yourself.

## The AAR Contract Forms and Closing Procedures

The Arizona Association of REALTORS (AAR) Residential Resale Real Estate Purchase Contract is the standard form used in the vast majority of residential transactions statewide. Section 6 of the current contract addresses title and escrow, specifying that the parties will designate a title and escrow company to handle closing.

| Form / Section | Purpose | Closing Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| AAR Residential Resale Purchase Contract, Section 6 | Designates title/escrow company | Determines who conducts closing |
| AAR Buyer Advisory (standalone form) | Advises buyer to seek independent professionals | Recommends attorney consultation for legal questions |
| ADRE Disclosure Report (Public Report) | Subdivision disclosure for new builds | Must be delivered before closing on new construction |
| AAR Escrow/Title Instruction Addendum | Provides escrow instructions to title company | Governs disbursement, prorations, and recording |
| HUD/CFPB Closing Disclosure (federal) | Itemizes final costs | Required 3 business days before closing under TRID |

The AAR Buyer Advisory explicitly recommends that buyers consult with an attorney regarding legal questions about the transaction. This form is not optional—ADRE expects agents to deliver it, and failure to do so exposes you to [compliance issues that parallel those seen in other states](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) around document delivery failures.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make Around Closing in Arizona

**Mistake one: telling clients they do not need an attorney without qualification.** You cannot practice law. Saying "you don't need a lawyer" constitutes legal advice about whether legal representation is necessary. The correct approach is to explain that Arizona does not require attorney presence at closing but that clients are free to—and in complex situations should—retain one.

Mistake two: failing to review the preliminary title report with the buyer before the Title Objection Deadline specified in the contract. Under the AAR Residential Resale Purchase Contract, the buyer has a limited number of days (specified in Section 6) to object to title exceptions. If you let that deadline pass without flagging easements, CC&R issues, or liens to your client, you have not fulfilled your fiduciary duty under [A.R.S. 32-2153](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/32/02153.htm), which defines grounds for license discipline.

Mistake three: assuming the title company will catch contract compliance errors. The escrow officer's job is to follow escrow instructions, not to enforce your contract deadlines. If you miss a contingency deadline, the title company will not stop the clock for you.

Mistake four: directing clients to a specific title company without disclosing an affiliated business arrangement. RESPA Section 8 violations carry penalties up to $10,000 per violation and up to one year of imprisonment. Arizona agents with ownership stakes in title companies must provide the Affiliated Business Arrangement Disclosure form at or before the time of referral.

Mistake five: signing documents on behalf of a client without a properly executed power of attorney that the title company has pre-approved. Title companies in Arizona routinely reject POA documents that were not reviewed at least 48 hours before closing, killing deals at the last minute.

## Liability Exposure When Things Go Wrong

ADRE can discipline agents under A.R.S. 32-2153 for conduct that constitutes negligence, incompetence, or dishonesty in a transaction. Penalties range from a letter of concern to license revocation, with monetary fines up to $10,000 per violation as established under A.R.S. 32-2154.

| Violation Type | Potential Consequence | Regulatory Body |
|---|---|---|
| Practicing law (advising on legal sufficiency of documents) | License discipline, civil liability | ADRE, Arizona State Bar |
| RESPA kickback / undisclosed affiliation | $10,000 fine, criminal prosecution | CFPB, ADRE |
| Failure to deliver Buyer Advisory | Complaint, potential discipline | ADRE |
| Missing title objection deadline (agent negligence) | E&O claim, breach of fiduciary duty | Civil court, ADRE |
| Unauthorized notarization or POA execution | Transaction rescission, license action | ADRE, title underwriter |

The distinction between agent liability and title company liability is not always clean. If a deal collapses because the agent selected an unlicensed or financially unstable escrow company, the agent's E&O carrier will scrutinize whether due diligence was performed. Agents carry a recommendation obligation even though clients ultimately choose.

When [deadlines slip through the cracks](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), the resulting liability often lands on the agent who was responsible for calendar management rather than on the title company. This is a recurring pattern in ADRE complaints—agents assume someone else is tracking timelines when no one actually is.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Arizona bear supervisory responsibility under A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(21) for the conduct of their licensees. This is not theoretical—ADRE regularly names brokers in disciplinary actions when systemic compliance failures exist in a brokerage.

**Brokers should audit whether agents are delivering the AAR Buyer Advisory on every transaction.** This form contains the critical language recommending attorney consultation and professional inspections. If your office does not have a system verifying delivery with a signed acknowledgment, you are exposed.

Second, brokers must confirm that agents are not making undisclosed referrals to affiliated title companies. An annual audit of agent referral patterns compared against disclosed affiliations will surface potential RESPA issues before they become federal enforcement actions.

Third, brokers should verify that agents are reviewing preliminary title reports within the contractual timeline and documenting client communication about exceptions. A simple note in the transaction file showing that the agent discussed the prelim with the buyer on a specific date creates a defensible record.

Finally, brokers need training protocols that clarify the line between transaction coordination and legal advice. When an agent explains a deed of trust to a buyer, they risk crossing into unauthorized practice of law under Arizona Supreme Court Rule 31 (now Rule 75 after the 2023 renumbering). Training should include specific scenarios and approved language that agents can use without exposure.

## Arizona Attorney vs. Title Company — Who Must Be at Closing 2026: The Bottom Line

The answer has not changed with the calendar year: Arizona requires a licensed title agency or escrow company at closing, not an attorney. But understanding this baseline requirement is only the starting point for agents and brokers who want to [avoid the operational breakdowns that kill transactions](/blog/where-deals-break-down).
