## The Settlement Changed Everything—Here Is Where New York Stands

The New York commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 are no longer theoretical. They are actively enforced, and agents who treat them as optional are losing deals and facing disciplinary proceedings. Since the practice changes took effect in August 2024, the New York Department of State (DOS) Division of Licensing Services has folded these requirements into its existing supervisory framework under Article 12-A of the Real Property Law.

New York was already one of the stricter states for agency disclosure. The settlement layered federal practice changes on top of state-specific mandates, creating a compliance environment that demands precision from both listing and buyer agents. Brokers who assumed their existing paperwork would suffice have discovered painful gaps during audits.

This post breaks down the specific forms, statutes, enforcement risks, and operational adjustments every New York licensee needs to internalize before their next transaction.

## How the NAR Settlement Intersects with New York State Law

**The NAR settlement prohibits offers of buyer-agent compensation through any MLS.** That single rule eliminated a workflow that most New York agents had relied on for decades. Compensation is still negotiable—sellers can still contribute to buyer-agent fees—but the mechanism must change.

New York's Real Property Law Section 443 already required written disclosure of agency relationships before "substantive contact" with a consumer. The DOS mandates the use of the Disclosure Regarding Real Estate Agency Relationships form at first substantive contact. Post-settlement, this disclosure alone is insufficient; agents must also secure a written buyer representation agreement that explicitly states compensation terms before touring any property.

The regulatory bodies enforcing compliance include the New York DOS, the New York State Association of Realtors (NYSAR), and local MLS systems like OneKey MLS and the Greater Capital Association MLS. Each has issued updated rules of participation reflecting the settlement terms.

## Required Forms and Documentation in New York

New York does not have a single state-mandated buyer agency form. Instead, agents typically rely on NYSAR Standard Form 801 (Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement) or brokerage-specific versions drafted by counsel. Regardless of the template, the agreement must contain three elements: the duration of representation, the specific amount or rate of buyer-agent compensation, and a clear statement that fees are not set by law.

| Form / Document | Purpose | When Required |
|---|---|---|
| RPL 443 Disclosure Form | Agency relationship disclosure | First substantive contact |
| NYSAR Form 801 or equivalent | Buyer representation agreement | Before touring any property |
| NYSAR Form 803 (Non-Exclusive Buyer Agency) | Alternative buyer agreement | Before touring, if non-exclusive |
| Listing Agreement (exclusive right to sell) | Seller compensation terms | At listing execution |
| MLS Compensation Addendum (if used) | Documents off-MLS comp arrangements | At offer negotiation or before |

For listing agents, the standard exclusive right to sell agreement must no longer include a field authorizing the MLS to broadcast an offer of buyer-agent compensation. Any seller contribution to buyer-agent fees should be handled through offer negotiations or a separate written agreement between the parties.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**Non-compliance triggers consequences at three levels: regulatory, contractual, and financial.** The DOS can bring disciplinary action under 19 NYCRR Part 175, which governs the conduct of real estate licensees. Violations of RPL 443 disclosure requirements carry penalties including fines up to $1,000 per violation and license suspension or revocation for repeated offenses.

At the MLS level, OneKey MLS and other New York systems have implemented their own enforcement mechanisms. Agents who publish compensation offers in listing remarks, broker-to-broker fields, or any MLS-accessible data face fines starting at $500 and escalating to suspension of MLS privileges. These fines are separate from DOS penalties.

Contractually, a buyer who was never presented with a compliant representation agreement could challenge the enforceability of any compensation clause. If a deal closes and the buyer later argues they never agreed to the fee structure, the agent's brokerage may face disgorgement of the commission. Several New York attorneys have already raised this theory in post-closing disputes.

> "Failure to obtain a written buyer representation agreement prior to showing property exposes the licensee to disciplinary action under Article 12-A and potential civil liability for the commission received." — New York DOS Guidance Bulletin, 2024

## New York Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026: Common Mistakes Agents Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in DOS complaints and MLS violation logs since the settlement took effect.

**First, agents tour properties before securing a signed buyer agreement.** The most common violation is also the most basic. Agents assume verbal consent or a text message exchange satisfies the requirement. It does not. The agreement must be written, signed, and specify compensation before any showing occurs.

Second, listing agents embed compensation language in MLS remarks fields. Phrases like "generous co-broke" or "2.5% to buyer's agent" in public or agent-only remarks violate settlement terms. OneKey MLS has flagged thousands of listings since August 2024 for this exact issue.

Third, agents fail to distinguish between the RPL 443 disclosure and the buyer representation agreement. These are separate documents with separate legal functions. Presenting only the 443 disclosure does not satisfy the compensation transparency requirement imposed by the settlement.

Fourth, brokerages use outdated form templates that reference "cooperating compensation" or "MLS-offered commission." Any language suggesting the MLS is a vehicle for compensation offers must be removed. Forms referencing pre-settlement practices create liability even if the dollar amount is appropriate.

Fifth, agents neglect to document the source of buyer-agent compensation in the purchase contract. When sellers agree to contribute to buyer-agent fees, that agreement should appear in writing—either in the contract itself or in a signed addendum—before closing. Oral agreements between cooperating brokers no longer provide sufficient protection.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers carry personal liability under RPL Section 441 for the conduct of their sponsored agents. The DOS does not accept "I didn't know my agent skipped that step" as a defense. Brokerage-level compliance requires systematic audits, not occasional spot-checks.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer agreements on file | Signed before first showing, compensation specified | Every new buyer engagement |
| Listing agreement language | No MLS compensation offer language | Every new listing |
| MLS compliance | No comp language in remarks or data fields | Weekly review of active listings |
| RPL 443 disclosure timing | Documented at first substantive contact | Every transaction file |
| Agent training records | Settlement-compliant practices covered | Quarterly minimum |

**Brokers should implement a file review checkpoint before any offer is submitted.** If the buyer representation agreement is missing or deficient at the offer stage, the transaction should not proceed until the gap is corrected. Waiting until closing to discover a missing agreement converts a fixable problem into a potential commission dispute.

Training matters too. The DOS requires 22.5 hours of continuing education per renewal cycle, and several approved providers now include settlement-compliance modules. Brokers who mandate attendance—and document it—create an affirmative defense if an agent later claims ignorance. ## Compensation Negotiation Strategies That Stay Compliant

The settlement did not eliminate seller-paid buyer-agent compensation. It eliminated the MLS as the advertising mechanism. Agents who understand this distinction can still structure transactions where sellers contribute to buyer-agent fees—they simply must do so through direct negotiation rather than blanket MLS offers.

Buyer agents should discuss compensation with their clients at the agreement stage, explaining the range of possible outcomes: the buyer pays directly, the buyer requests a seller concession in the offer, or a combination. Documenting this conversation in the buyer agreement itself creates clarity and reduces post-closing disputes.

Listing agents can still advise sellers that offering to contribute to buyer-agent fees may attract more buyer interest. That advice belongs in the listing presentation, not the MLS. If a seller decides to offer such a contribution, it should be communicated through direct agent-to-agent contact after an inquiry is made about the property, or structured as a response to a buyer's offer terms.
