## Michigan Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026: The Numbers That Actually Matter

Getting the Michigan transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 right is not a nicety—it is a gating requirement for every residential closing in the state. A single miscalculation delays recording, clouds title, and exposes the listing agent, buyer's agent, and their brokers to liability. The numbers themselves are straightforward, but the statutory framework around them trips up experienced agents more often than you might expect.

Michigan imposes transfer taxes at two levels: the State Real Estate Transfer Tax (SRETT) under MCL 207.525 and the County Real Estate Transfer Tax under MCL 207.501. Both must be paid before the Register of Deeds will accept a deed for recording. Recording fees are set separately by each county's Register of Deeds under MCL 565.651 and vary modestly across the state's 83 counties.

## State and County Transfer Tax Rates Explained

**The state transfer tax rate is $3.75 per $500 of consideration, which translates to $7.50 per $1,000 of the sale price.** The county transfer tax rate is $0.55 per $500—or $1.10 per $1,000. Combined, the parties owe $8.60 per $1,000 on every taxable transfer.

On a $350,000 residential sale, that means $3,010 in total transfer tax. On a $225,000 sale, it is $1,935. These amounts are calculated on the full consideration stated in the deed, not a rounded figure, and the Register of Deeds will reject any deed where the tax tendered does not match the stated value.

| Fee Component | Rate per $1,000 | On $250,000 Sale | On $400,000 Sale |
|---|---|---|---|
| State Transfer Tax (MCL 207.525) | $7.50 | $1,875.00 | $3,000.00 |
| County Transfer Tax (MCL 207.501) | $1.10 | $275.00 | $440.00 |
| Combined Transfer Tax | $8.60 | $2,150.00 | $3,440.00 |

Exemptions exist under MCL 207.526 (state) and MCL 207.505 (county). Transfers between spouses, transfers to trusts where the grantor is the sole beneficiary, and certain foreclosure-related conveyances may qualify. But claiming an exemption that does not apply carries a penalty of ten times the unpaid tax under MCL 207.525(3).

## Recording Fees Across Michigan Counties

Recording fees in Michigan are governed by MCL 565.651, which sets statutory maximums and allows counties to adopt a fee schedule within those bounds. Most counties charge a base fee for the first page of a recorded document and a per-page fee for additional pages, plus a $4.00 Remonumentation Act surcharge under MCL 54.265.

As of 2026, the majority of Michigan counties charge $30 for the first page and $3 per additional page for standard documents. Some counties—Wayne, Oakland, and Macomb among them—publish separate schedules for non-standard documents such as land contracts, plats, and discharge documents. Always confirm the current schedule with the specific Register of Deeds office before closing.

Beyond the deed itself, agents should account for recording of the mortgage (charged to the buyer), any discharge of a prior mortgage, and any ancillary instruments like affidavits of survivorship. Each recorded instrument incurs its own fee. Missing one on the closing statement creates a shortage that the title company must cover—and then chase after closing.

## Forms Every Michigan Agent Must Know

Michigan real estate transactions require specific state-prescribed forms tied to transfer taxes and recording. Misidentifying or omitting one of these forms will halt recording.

**Form L-4260, the Property Transfer Affidavit, is required under MCL 211.27a(10) for every transfer of ownership.** It must be filed with the local assessor within 45 days of the transfer date. Failure to file triggers a penalty of $5 per day, up to $200, assessed against the buyer. Agents who neglect to advise their clients about this form—or who conflate it with the transfer tax affidavit attached to the deed—create post-closing headaches that erode client trust.

The Real Estate Transfer Valuation Affidavit (state form) is the document attached to the deed that declares the consideration paid and whether an exemption applies. This is distinct from L-4260. The county Register of Deeds will not record the deed without it.

Additionally, Michigan Administrative Code R 211.951 governs the format requirements for documents presented for recording—margin specifications, font size minimums, and paper dimensions. A deed that does not conform will be returned, delaying the transaction and potentially triggering [the kind of blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) that costs agents referrals and reputation.

## What Happens When Agents Get It Wrong

Non-compliance is not theoretical. Michigan's statutory penalties are specific and enforceable.

If the correct transfer tax is not tendered, the deed is rejected at the Register of Deeds counter. The conveyance is not on record. The buyer does not have a recorded chain of title, which means title insurance policies cannot be issued—or if issued, must be revoked. This is a deal-killing outcome.

If an agent or party claims a transfer tax exemption fraudulently or erroneously, MCL 207.525(3) imposes a penalty of ten times the tax that should have been paid. On a $300,000 transaction, an improper exemption claim could trigger a $25,800 penalty. That is not a fine the broker absorbs quietly.

> "Any person who makes a false statement on the transfer tax affidavit is liable for a penalty equal to 10 times the amount of tax due." — MCL 207.525(3)

Beyond statutory penalties, an agent who consistently miscalculates closing costs or fails to advise on required filings risks a complaint to the Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA). Under the Occupational Code (MCL 339.2512), incompetence in performing licensed activities is grounds for disciplinary action, including license suspension. The stakes make it worth [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) with enough precision that nothing slips.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Michigan Transfer Taxes and Recording Fees

Five errors appear repeatedly in Michigan residential transactions, and each one is preventable.

**First, agents conflate the Property Transfer Affidavit (L-4260) with the Transfer Valuation Affidavit attached to the deed.** These are two separate documents with different filing destinations. The L-4260 goes to the local assessor; the valuation affidavit goes with the deed to the Register of Deeds. Mixing them up means one or both obligations go unfulfilled.

Second, agents estimate transfer tax on a rounded purchase price rather than the actual consideration. Michigan calculates tax on the full stated consideration. A $347,500 sale is taxed on $347,500—not $348,000, not $347,000. Rounding in either direction produces an incorrect tender and a rejected deed.

Third, agents fail to verify county-specific recording fees before preparing the closing disclosure. While most counties align at $30 for the first page, ancillary fees for non-standard instruments vary. A land contract recording in Ingham County does not cost the same as a warranty deed recording in Marquette County. Verifying the [county's published schedule](https://www.michigan.gov/treasury/local/register-of-deeds) before preparing numbers prevents last-minute adjustments that unsettle buyers.

Fourth, agents assume the buyer is responsible for the transfer tax because it appears on the buyer's side of the HUD or closing disclosure. In Michigan, custom and statute place the transfer tax obligation on the seller. The purchase agreement can shift it, but absent explicit language, the seller pays.

Fifth, agents neglect to confirm that the prior mortgage discharge will be recorded simultaneously or promptly after closing. An unrecorded discharge clouds title and can delay the buyer's refinancing for months. This is an operational detail that agents often delegate entirely to the title company without follow-up—a habit that [why checklists beat memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) makes clear is risky.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under MCL 339.2512e for the acts of their agents. That means transfer tax and recording fee errors are not just agent problems—they land on the broker's desk.

**Brokers should audit every purchase agreement to confirm that transfer tax responsibility is clearly assigned.** Ambiguity in this area leads to closing-day disputes that can delay recording and trigger client complaints. A quarterly file review that checks for explicit transfer tax language in every executed contract is the minimum standard.

Brokers should also confirm that agents are advising clients in writing about the L-4260 filing requirement. A simple addendum or closing instruction letter documenting this advice protects the brokerage from claims that the buyer was never told about the 45-day filing deadline or the $5/day penalty.

Training on exemption claims deserves particular attention. An agent who casually tells a divorcing couple that their quitclaim deed is "exempt" without verifying the specific conditions of MCL 207.526(a) exposes the brokerage to the ten-times penalty. Brokers should require agents to route any exemption claim through the title company's underwriting department before the deed is prepared.

Finally, brokers should track rejected recordings. If a pattern emerges—certain agents repeatedly submitting non-conforming documents—that signals a training gap that must be closed before LARA intervenes. Documentation of corrective action taken protects the broker's license in any subsequent audit.

Agents entering the second half of 2026 should pull current fee schedules directly from their county Register of Deeds offices rather than relying on title company rate cards from prior years.
