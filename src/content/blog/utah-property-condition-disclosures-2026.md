## Understanding Utah Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026

The rules governing Utah property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 have not loosened, even as market speed has increased. Agents who treated disclosure as a checkbox exercise in previous years are finding themselves on the wrong end of complaints to the Utah Division of Real Estate. This post breaks down the statutory framework, the specific forms you need, the liability exposure you face, and the mistakes that keep showing up in disciplinary actions.

Utah's disclosure regime sits primarily within Utah Code Title 57, Chapter 1, and is enforced by the Utah Division of Real Estate under the Department of Commerce. The [Utah Division of Real Estate](https://realestate.utah.gov/) maintains the regulatory authority to investigate complaints, suspend licenses, and impose fines. Unlike states with lengthy multi-page mandatory disclosure packets, Utah's approach places heavy responsibility on agents to understand what "material defect" means and to ensure sellers don't dodge the obligation.

## The Statutory Framework: What the Law Actually Says

**Utah Code Section 57-1-37 is the backbone of residential property condition disclosure in this state.** It requires sellers of residential property (one to four units) to provide a written disclosure to buyers before or at the time an offer is accepted. The statute does not prescribe a single state-mandated form, but it sets the floor for what must be disclosed: known material defects affecting the physical condition of the property.

Environmental conditions fall under the broader "material defect" umbrella. Radon levels, known asbestos-containing materials, underground storage tanks, lead-based paint (for pre-1978 homes), proximity to Superfund sites, and contaminated groundwater all qualify. If the seller knows about it and it affects the property's value or desirability, it must be disclosed.

Utah Code Section 61-2f-401 addresses agent conduct specifically. It makes it a violation for a licensee to fail to disclose material facts that the licensee knew or should have known. This is the statute that regulators cite when agents face disciplinary action for incomplete or absent disclosures.

## Forms and Documents Agents Must Use

The Utah Association of Realtors publishes the Seller's Property Condition Disclosure form, commonly referenced as UAR Form 25. This is the standard form used across most Utah brokerages and MLS systems. It covers structural components, plumbing, electrical, HVAC, water systems, and a dedicated section on environmental hazards including radon, mold, asbestos, and lead paint.

For properties built before 1978, agents must also comply with federal lead-based paint disclosure requirements under 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d. This involves a separate EPA-mandated disclosure form and the delivery of the "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" pamphlet. Utah agents handling older housing stock should pair this with the state-level condition disclosure — they are not interchangeable. For deeper coverage of that obligation, see our breakdown of [Utah lead paint disclosure requirements](/blog/utah-lead-paint-disclosure-2026).

| Form / Document | Source | When Required | Who Completes It |
|---|---|---|---|
| UAR Form 25 (Seller's Property Condition Disclosure) | Utah Association of Realtors | Before or at offer acceptance | Seller, with agent guidance |
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | Federal EPA / HUD | Pre-1978 residential properties | Seller and agent |
| Radon Disclosure Addendum (if applicable) | Brokerage-specific or UAR | When radon testing results exist | Seller |
| Environmental Site Assessment Summary | Third-party inspector | When commercial/mixed-use triggers apply | Buyer or seller, depending on contract |

**Agents do not fill out the condition disclosure on behalf of the seller.** You provide the form, explain why it matters, and review the completed version for obvious gaps or contradictions. Writing in answers for a seller — even with their verbal permission — creates liability that attaches directly to your license.

## Liability When Disclosure Fails

The consequences of non-compliance operate on multiple levels. A buyer who discovers an undisclosed material defect after closing can pursue rescission of the contract, damages, or both. Under Utah Code 57-1-37, the seller bears primary liability, but agents are not shielded if they had knowledge or should have had knowledge of the defect.

The Utah Division of Real Estate can impose administrative penalties under Utah Code 61-2f-503 including fines up to $5,000 per violation, license suspension, and license revocation. In egregious cases — particularly those involving environmental contamination that poses health risks — the Division has referred matters to the Utah Attorney General for civil enforcement.

Beyond regulatory action, agents face errors-and-omissions claims. E&O carriers routinely deny coverage when an agent affirmatively concealed a defect or participated in filling out the disclosure form inaccurately. The exposure here is not theoretical: multiple Utah disciplinary orders in 2024 and 2025 involved agents who knew about water intrusion or radon levels and failed to ensure proper disclosure.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Utah Disclosures

Three to five errors appear with disturbing regularity in Utah Division of Real Estate complaint files. Recognizing these patterns is the fastest way to avoid becoming a case study.

**First, agents accept an incomplete disclosure form and submit it with the transaction file anyway.** When a seller leaves environmental questions blank — not marking "yes," "no," or "unknown" — that is not a completed disclosure. Blank fields invite post-closing claims because buyers can argue the omission was intentional concealment. Agents must send the form back to the seller for completion before delivering it to the buyer's side.

Second, listing agents fail to reconcile the disclosure with what they observe during the listing appointment. If you walk through a property and notice staining on basement walls, efflorescence on foundation concrete, or the distinctive smell of mold, you have a duty to inquire further. Turning a blind eye does not protect you — it increases your exposure under Section 61-2f-401's "should have known" standard.

Third, agents conflate "as-is" sales with disclosure-free sales. Selling a property as-is does not eliminate the disclosure obligation. The seller still must disclose known defects. The "as-is" clause simply means the seller is not agreeing to make repairs — it does not waive their duty to inform the buyer about conditions they already know exist.

Fourth, agents assume the home inspection replaces seller disclosure. These serve entirely different functions. The inspection is the buyer's independent investigation. The disclosure is the seller's affirmative representation of what they know. A clean inspection does not cure a fraudulent or incomplete disclosure, and a damning inspection does not relieve the seller of their separate duty.

Fifth, buyer's agents fail to advise clients to actually read the disclosure carefully before waiving contingencies. When buyers sign off on due diligence deadlines without reviewing the disclosure in detail, the buyer's agent has missed an opportunity to flag potential environmental or structural concerns that warrant further investigation. This matters especially in [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) where volume increases the risk of skipping steps.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers carry supervisory liability under Utah Code 61-2f-401(4). If an agent in your brokerage consistently submits transaction files without completed disclosures, the Division can and will hold the broker accountable. Passive oversight is not a defense.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure form completeness | Every field answered (yes/no/unknown) | Blank environmental section |
| Delivery timing | Disclosure delivered before or at offer acceptance | Disclosure dated after mutual acceptance |
| Lead paint compliance | Separate federal form present for pre-1978 | Missing form or missing buyer acknowledgment signature |
| As-is transactions | Disclosure still present in file | Agent note stating "no disclosure needed — as-is sale" |
| Agent observations vs. disclosure | Photos/notes from listing match seller statements | Visible water damage in listing photos but "no" checked on disclosure |

Brokers should build a quarterly file review process that specifically pulls disclosure documents and compares them against listing photos, inspection reports (if available in file), and MLS remarks. Discrepancies between what is publicly visible and what the seller disclosed represent the highest-risk exposure for the entire brokerage.

Training is the other half of enforcement. Annual compliance training that walks agents through real Utah Division of Real Estate disciplinary cases — not just abstract rules — creates lasting behavioral change. Agents remember the story of a license suspended over a basement flood that was painted over far more than they remember a statute citation. ## Environmental Hazards Specific to Utah Properties

Utah's geography and development history create environmental disclosure situations that agents in other states rarely encounter. Radon is prevalent along the Wasatch Front, particularly in Salt Lake, Davis, and Utah counties. The EPA classifies much of this corridor as Zone 1, meaning predicted indoor radon levels frequently exceed the 4.0 pCi/L action level.

Properties near former mining operations — common in Park City, Tooele County, and parts of Summit County — may sit on or near tailings piles containing arsenic, lead, and other heavy metals. Sellers who know about nearby mining activity or who have received environmental notices from the Utah Department of Environmental Quality must disclose this information.

Underground storage tanks (USTs) are another recurring issue, especially on properties that previously operated as gas stations, dry cleaners, or agricultural operations. The Utah Division of Environmental Response and Remediation maintains a database of known UST sites. Agents listing properties with commercial history should check this database and advise sellers to disclose any known contamination or remediation activity.

## Connecting Disclosures to the Broader Transaction Workflow

Disclosure failures rarely happen in isolation. They correlate with disorganized transaction management — missed deadlines, documents filed out of order, and agents juggling too many deals without systematic tracking. The disclosure form should be one of the first documents a listing agent delivers to the seller, ideally at or before the listing agreement signing. Waiting until an offer arrives creates time pressure that breeds shortcuts.

**Buyer's agents should calendar a specific deadline for disclosure review that falls well before the due diligence expiration.** This gives the buyer time to request clarification, order additional inspections, or negotiate based on disclosed conditions.
