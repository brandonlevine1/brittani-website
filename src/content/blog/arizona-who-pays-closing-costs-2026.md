# Arizona Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026: What Agents Must Get Right

The question of Arizona who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 sounds straightforward until a deal falls apart over a $1,200 escrow fee nobody accounted for. Arizona does not have a statute mandating which party pays which closing cost, which means every allocation lives or dies inside the purchase contract. That flexibility gives agents power — and plenty of room to create liability if they handle it carelessly.

This post breaks down the current fee landscape specific to Arizona residential transactions, the AAR forms that control allocation, the statutory framework agents operate within, and the consequences of getting it wrong.

## How Arizona Closing Costs Are Actually Split

Unlike states that assign costs by statute, Arizona relies almost entirely on contract terms and county custom. The Arizona Association of Realtors (AAR) Residential Resale Real Estate Purchase Contract — the form most agents work from — addresses cost allocation primarily in Section 3 (Escrow, Title, and Closing) and Section 6 (Additional Terms and Conditions).

**In most Maricopa, Pima, and Pinal County transactions, the customary split looks like this:**

| Cost Item | Typically Paid By Buyer | Typically Paid By Seller |
|-----------|:-----------------------:|:------------------------:|
| Owner's title insurance policy | | X |
| Lender's title insurance policy | X | |
| Escrow/settlement fee | Split 50/50 | Split 50/50 |
| Recording fees (deed) | | X |
| Recording fees (mortgage/deed of trust) | X | |
| Transfer taxes | None (Arizona has no state transfer tax) | None |
| HOA transfer/disclosure fees | | X |
| Loan origination and discount points | X | |
| Home warranty (if offered) | | X |

These are customs, not mandates. Agents in Coconino, Yavapai, or Mohave counties may encounter different local norms. The critical point: if the contract does not specify who pays, the title company defaults to county custom, and disputes arise when one party assumed something the contract never confirmed.

## The AAR Forms That Control Allocation

The AAR Residential Resale Real Estate Purchase Contract (updated annually; agents should confirm they are using the 2026 revision) is the primary vehicle for allocating costs. Section 3c explicitly addresses title and escrow charges. Section 6l covers seller concessions toward buyer closing costs, expressed as a fixed dollar amount or percentage of the purchase price.

The AAR Buyer's Inspection Notice and Seller's Response (BINSR) form can also introduce cost shifts if repair credits are structured as closing cost concessions rather than price reductions. Agents must understand the tax and lender implications of each approach.

For new construction, the AAR Pre-Closing Walk-Through form (separate from the BINSR) does not directly allocate costs, but agents working builder transactions should note that builders often dictate cost splits in their proprietary addenda, overriding typical county custom.

**When representing buyers requesting seller concessions, agents must verify the buyer's lender will permit the concession amount.** Conventional loans cap seller concessions at 3% for down payments under 10%, 6% for down payments between 10-25%, and 9% for down payments above 25%. FHA caps at 6%, and VA allows up to 4% in specific concession categories.

## Statutory Framework and Regulatory Oversight

Arizona Revised Statutes Title 32, Chapter 20 governs real estate licensee conduct. While no single statute dictates closing cost allocation, A.R.S. 32-2153 outlines grounds for license discipline, including misrepresentation, negligence, and failure to disclose material facts. An agent who tells a buyer "the seller always pays this fee" without verifying the contract language could face a complaint under this provision.

The [Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE)](https://azre.gov) enforces licensing standards and investigates complaints. Under Commissioner's Rule R4-28-1101, licensees must exercise reasonable skill and care — which includes accurately representing cost responsibilities in writing.

A.R.S. 33-422 governs the Seller Property Disclosure Statement (SPDS), and while it does not directly address closing costs, agents sometimes conflate disclosure obligations with cost obligations. They are not the same. Cost allocation is contractual; disclosure is statutory.

For agents working with [wire transfers and escrow procedures](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process), understanding how closing funds flow in Arizona's escrow-state model is directly relevant. Arizona uses independent escrow companies or title companies acting as escrow agents, not attorneys, to close transactions.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Closing Cost Allocation

**First, agents frequently leave Section 3c of the AAR contract incomplete or unchanged from the pre-populated defaults without discussing the implications with their client.** This is not a fill-in-the-blank exercise — it is a negotiation point that affects net proceeds and out-of-pocket costs.

Second, listing agents sometimes verbally promise to "cover buyer closing costs" during negotiations without specifying a cap or detailing which costs. When the buyer's closing disclosure arrives with $14,000 in charges and the seller expected $8,000, the agent who made the vague promise owns the problem.

Third, buyer's agents fail to coordinate concession amounts with the buyer's lender before submitting offers. A concession that exceeds lender guidelines will either kill the deal or require a last-minute contract amendment — neither of which inspires client confidence.

Fourth, agents confuse price reductions with closing cost credits. A $10,000 price reduction and a $10,000 seller credit toward closing costs produce different outcomes for the buyer's loan-to-value ratio, appraisal gap risk, and the seller's net sheet. Treating them as interchangeable is negligent.

Fifth, agents working transactions in counties outside Maricopa assume that Maricopa customs apply everywhere. In some rural Arizona counties, the buyer customarily pays the owner's title policy. An agent who drafts a contract based on Maricopa norms without confirming local custom has exposed their client — and themselves — to a dispute.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Arizona carry supervisory liability under A.R.S. 32-2153(A)(19) for failing to adequately supervise licensees. This is not theoretical — the ADRE has disciplined brokers for agent errors on cost allocation that resulted in client harm.

Brokers should audit three specific areas in every transaction file. First, confirm that Section 3c and Section 6l of the AAR contract are fully completed with specific dollar amounts or clearly stated terms — not left blank or filled with ambiguous language like "per custom." Second, verify that any seller concession is within the buyer's lender guidelines before the contract is ratified. A post-ratification discovery that the concession exceeds limits creates amendment pressure and potential cancellation.

Third, review net sheets prepared by agents for accuracy. The ADRE does not require agents to prepare net sheets, but many brokerages mandate them as a best practice. An inaccurate net sheet that misallocates costs — showing the buyer paying a fee the contract assigns to the seller, for example — can constitute misrepresentation even if the contract itself is correct.

**Brokers operating teams should pay particular attention to newer agents who have not yet internalized county-specific customs.** A team lead closing 80 transactions a year in Scottsdale may not catch that their new agent just drafted a Prescott contract using Maricopa assumptions. Standardized checklists help, but they do not replace file-level review. Understanding [why checklists beat memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) is one thing — implementing them at the brokerage level is another.

## Liability When Agents Get It Wrong

If an agent misrepresents closing cost responsibilities — even unintentionally — the consequences scale with the harm. At minimum, the agent may face a demand to contribute personally to cover the disputed costs. Errors and omissions insurance may or may not cover the claim depending on whether the insurer classifies it as negligence versus a contractual promise.

At the ADRE level, a substantiated complaint under A.R.S. 32-2153 can result in a consent order, fine (typically $1,000-$10,000 per violation), mandatory education, or license suspension. The ADRE publishes disciplinary actions, which means the agent's reputation takes a public hit regardless of the financial penalty.

**Deal cancellation is the most common immediate consequence.** When a buyer discovers at the closing table that they owe $4,000 more than their agent represented, they may exercise their right to cancel if the contract has been breached. Even if cancellation does not occur, the commission dispute that follows — where the aggrieved party demands the agent forfeit their fee to cover the shortfall — is ugly, time-consuming, and rarely worth the fight.

Agents who find themselves [managing deadline-sensitive compliance tasks](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) across multiple files simultaneously are the ones most likely to make allocation errors under time pressure. The fix is not working harder — it is building systems that flag incomplete contract sections before ratification.

## Negotiation Realities for Arizona Agents in 2026

The 2026 market in Arizona has shifted buyer expectations around closing cost assistance. With mortgage rates remaining elevated compared to the 2020-2021 period, buyers are more frequently requesting seller concessions to buy down their rate. This means Section 6l of the AAR contract is seeing heavier use than in prior years.

Listing agents need to counsel sellers that a concession request is not a sign of a weak buyer — it is a financing strategy. The net effect to the seller of a $8,000 concession on a $450,000 purchase price versus a straight $442,000 offer depends on appraisal risk, days on market, and competing offers. Agents who default to "just reject concession requests" are leaving deals on the table.

For buyer's agents, structuring concession requests properly means specifying in the contract exactly which costs the credit applies to. Writing "seller to credit buyer $10,000 toward closing costs and prepaids" is clearer and more enforceable than "seller to assist with buyer costs." Precision protects both parties and reduces title company confusion at settlement.
