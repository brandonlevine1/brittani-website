## Understanding New York Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026

The question of New York who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is not merely academic for licensed agents. It directly shapes how you advise clients during contract negotiations, how you structure offers, and how you avoid regulatory exposure. Getting this wrong does not just embarrass you at the closing table — it can trigger Department of State investigations and civil liability.

New York is one of the most expensive states to close a real estate transaction. Between transfer taxes, mansion taxes, mortgage recording taxes, and attorney fees, total closing costs routinely reach 8-10% of the purchase price in New York City and 3-5% upstate. Agents who cannot articulate exactly which party bears which cost lose credibility fast, and credibility is what gets you referrals.

This post breaks down the statutory obligations, the negotiable fees, the forms you must use correctly, and the mistakes that get agents into trouble.

## Statutory Framework: Who Owes What by Law

**New York law assigns certain closing costs to specific parties by statute, regardless of what the contract says.** The most important of these is the real estate transfer tax under New York Tax Law Article 31, Section 1402. The grantor (seller) is the party legally obligated to pay the state transfer tax of $2 per $500 of consideration (0.4%) — or $4 per $500 for residential properties over $3 million in certain counties.

New York City layers its own transfer tax on top, governed by NYC Administrative Code Title 11, Chapter 21. The city rate is 1% for residential sales under $500,000 and 1.425% for sales at or above $500,000. Again, the seller is the statutory obligor.

On the buyer's side, the mansion tax (Tax Law Section 1402-a) applies to residential purchases of $1 million or more. This is a buyer obligation by statute and cannot be contractually shifted to the seller without triggering potential gift tax implications. The mortgage recording tax under Tax Law Section 253 is also a buyer cost — currently 1.8% of the mortgage amount in NYC (split between state and city portions) and 1.05% in most other New York counties.

## Forms and Documents Agents Must Know

**Every residential transaction in New York involves specific forms that allocate or disclose closing costs, and agents must ensure these are completed accurately.** The NYSBA/REBNY Contract of Sale is the dominant contract form in New York City transactions. Its Rider provisions (typically Paragraphs 34-50 in most attorneys' riders) address cost allocations including who pays for title searches, surveys, and transfer taxes.

For co-op transactions, the Aztech Recognition Agreement and the co-op's own application package govern additional fees like flip taxes and move-in/move-out deposits. Agents representing buyers in co-ops must review the proprietary lease and offering plan to identify hidden costs that affect the buyer's bottom line.

The RP-5217 form (Real Property Transfer Report) must be filed with the county clerk at closing. This form, required by Real Property Tax Law Section 333, reports the sale price and is used to calculate the transfer tax. Errors on the RP-5217 delay recording and can result in rejected deeds.

For agency disclosure, agents must still deliver the DOS-1736 (Agency Disclosure Form) at first substantive contact. While this form does not directly address closing costs, it establishes the fiduciary relationship that obligates you to advise your client accurately about financial matters — including cost allocation.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Closing Costs

Agents blow closing cost discussions in predictable ways. Here are five errors that surface repeatedly in Department of State disciplinary proceedings and contract disputes.

**First, telling a buyer client that "the seller pays closing costs" as a blanket statement.** This is flatly wrong in New York. Buyers in NYC routinely pay more in closing costs than sellers when the mansion tax and mortgage recording tax apply. An agent who makes this claim on a $1.5 million purchase has just understated the buyer's costs by $40,000 or more.

Second, failing to distinguish between statutory obligations and contractual allocations. When a buyer asks the seller to "pay closing costs," agents often draft vague contract language that does not specify which costs. Attorneys will reject ambiguous riders, and deals stall. The better practice is to identify specific dollar amounts or specific line items the seller agrees to cover, written directly into the rider of the Contract of Sale.

Third, ignoring the [true cost implications when a deal falls apart](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) because a buyer was not prepared for the financial reality. Agents who skip the closing cost conversation early in the relationship end up scrambling at contract signing. By then, the emotional investment makes clients feel trapped rather than informed.

Fourth, neglecting to account for co-op and condo board fees. Many NYC co-op boards charge buyers an application fee ($500-$1,000), a move-in deposit ($500-$2,000), and sometimes a credit check fee. Condo buildings frequently charge working capital contributions (often 2 months of common charges) that buyers do not anticipate. These are not listed on standard closing cost estimate templates because they vary by building.

Fifth, miscalculating the mortgage recording tax by applying the wrong rate. In NYC, the combined rate is 1.8% for loans under $500,000 and 1.925% for loans at or above $500,000. Outside the city, the rate is typically 1.05%. Agents who use a flat percentage regardless of geography mislead their clients materially.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Supervising brokers in New York carry direct liability under Real Property Law Section 441(1) for the conduct of their sponsored salespersons. This is not abstract — the Department of State has revoked broker licenses for failure to supervise agents who misrepresented closing costs to consumers.

Brokers should audit three things quarterly. First, review the pre-contract disclosures agents provide to buyer clients. If your agents are using generic "closing cost estimate" sheets from 2019 that do not reflect current mansion tax brackets, you have a compliance gap. Second, check that every transaction file contains a signed DOS-1736 dated at or before first substantive contact. Third, confirm that agents are not making written closing cost guarantees in marketing materials or emails — any written estimate should include a disclaimer that actual costs depend on contract terms, lender requirements, and applicable taxes.

Brokers should also build internal training around [how deadlines affect deal outcomes](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) and ensure agents understand that closing cost miscommunication is a leading cause of last-minute deal collapses. A buyer who shows up to closing $25,000 short because their agent quoted the wrong mortgage recording tax rate is a buyer who files a complaint.

The penalty structure under Real Property Law Section 441-c includes fines up to $1,000 per violation, license suspension, and revocation. For brokers, the DOS can also impose a requirement to retake qualifying coursework or add supervised periods before reinstatement.

## Negotiation Strategies That Work in 2026

The 2026 New York market has shifted buyer expectations around closing cost credits. With mortgage rates remaining elevated, buyers increasingly ask sellers to contribute toward closing costs rather than reduce the purchase price. This strategy preserves the appraised value while reducing the buyer's cash-to-close requirement.

**Agents representing sellers should understand that a closing cost credit is economically identical to a price reduction from the seller's perspective, but it changes the buyer's qualification math significantly.** A $15,000 seller credit on a $750,000 sale does not reduce the sale price (protecting comps), but it reduces the buyer's upfront cash need by that same amount. Smart listing agents use this as a negotiation tool rather than reflexively rejecting credit requests.

For agents managing multiple active transactions, keeping track of which deals have negotiated cost splits, which have statutory-only allocations, and which are approaching contract deadlines requires systematic [pipeline management](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals). A missed rider amendment about who pays transfer tax creates chaos at the closing table that reflects poorly on everyone involved.
