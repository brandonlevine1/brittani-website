## Understanding New York FIRPTA Withholding Rules for Foreign Sellers 2026

Every year, agents lose deals — or worse, face personal liability — because they mishandle foreign seller withholding requirements. The New York FIRPTA withholding rules for foreign sellers 2026 remain one of the most misunderstood compliance areas in residential and commercial transactions across the state. Getting this wrong does not just embarrass your brokerage; it can result in the IRS assessing the full withholding amount against your buyer client.

FIRPTA — the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act, codified at [26 U.S.C. Sections 1445 and 897](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/1445) — requires that when a foreign person disposes of a U.S. real property interest, the buyer must withhold a percentage of the gross sales price and remit it to the IRS. New York layers its own state-level withholding on top of that federal obligation, creating a dual compliance burden that agents must understand even if they are not the ones filing the forms.

## Federal Withholding: The 15% Baseline

**The default FIRPTA withholding rate is 15% of the gross amount realized on the sale.** This is not 15% of the gain — it is 15% of the entire purchase price. On a $2 million Brooklyn brownstone, that means $300,000 held back from the seller's proceeds at closing.

The withholding obligation falls on the "transferee," which is the buyer. The buyer's closing attorney, title company, or escrow agent typically handles the mechanical remittance, but the legal liability stays with the buyer if withholding is not properly made. The IRS requires that the withheld funds be submitted using Form 8288 (U.S. Withholding Tax Return for Certain Dispositions by Foreign Persons) and Form 8288-A (Statement of Withholding on Dispositions by Foreign Persons) within 20 days after the transfer date.

There are narrow exceptions. If the property will be used as the buyer's personal residence and the price is at or below $300,000, no withholding is required. If the price falls between $300,001 and $1,000,000 and the buyer intends to reside there, withholding drops to 10%.

## New York State's Separate Withholding Layer

Most agents know about federal FIRPTA. Fewer understand that New York imposes its own parallel withholding under New York Tax Law Section 1446. This statute requires estimated income tax withholding on gains from the disposition of New York real property by nonresidents — including foreign nationals.

| Obligation | Federal (FIRPTA) | New York State |
|---|---|---|
| Governing law | 26 U.S.C. § 1445 | NY Tax Law § 1446 |
| Withholding rate | 15% of gross price (default) | Estimated gain-based calculation |
| Filing form | IRS Forms 8288 / 8288-A | Form IT-2663 (individuals) or IT-2664 (entities) |
| Filing deadline | 20 days after transfer | Must be filed and approved before closing |
| Liable party | Buyer (transferee) | Buyer / closing agent |

**The critical difference for agents: New York's Form IT-2663 must be submitted to the Department of Taxation and Finance before closing, not after.** The state will not issue a receipt of estimated tax payment unless the form is filed and processed in advance. If the form is submitted late, the closing can stall or the title company may refuse to disburse proceeds.

For entity sellers — LLCs, partnerships, or corporations — Form IT-2664 applies instead. Many foreign investors hold New York real property through single-member LLCs, which means agents must identify the entity structure early in the listing process.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Flag a Foreign Seller

Agents are not directly responsible for calculating or remitting FIRPTA withholding. But your fiduciary and disclosure obligations do not disappear because a tax lawyer files the forms. If you are the listing agent and you know — or should reasonably know — that your seller is a foreign person, failing to disclose this to the buyer's side can result in serious consequences.

The buyer's attorney will request an Affidavit of Non-Foreign Status from the seller. If the seller cannot sign it truthfully, FIRPTA withholding applies. When this surfaces late in the transaction, deals fall apart. Buyers recalculate their cash-to-close, sellers refuse the reduced proceeds, and the contract collapses days before the scheduled closing.

From a liability standpoint, if the buyer's side can demonstrate that the listing agent concealed the seller's foreign status, the agent faces potential claims of fraudulent misrepresentation. Under New York Real Property Law Section 443, agents owe disclosure duties that extend to material facts affecting the transaction — and a $300,000 withholding obligation on a $2 million sale is undeniably material. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) in the late stages makes this risk tangible for any agent who has lived through a blown closing.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With FIRPTA in New York

**First, agents assume that a seller with a U.S. address is not a foreign person.** FIRPTA defines "foreign person" as a nonresident alien individual, a foreign corporation, a foreign partnership, a foreign trust, or a foreign estate. A seller who has lived in Manhattan for a decade but never obtained a green card or U.S. citizenship is still a foreign person under FIRPTA. Residency for income tax purposes and immigration status are not the same thing.

Second, listing agents fail to raise the question early. The time to determine foreign status is during the listing intake — not at the attorney review stage. By the time the buyer's counsel requests the non-foreign affidavit, you should already have the answer documented. Agents who follow a disciplined [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) catch this at intake rather than scrambling at closing.

Third, agents confuse the federal withholding certificate application (Form 8288-B) with an exemption. A seller can apply to the IRS for a reduced withholding certificate if the actual tax liability will be less than 15% of the gross price. But Form 8288-B takes six to twelve weeks to process, and the closing cannot proceed without either the full withholding or an approved certificate. Agents who promise sellers they can "get around" the 15% without understanding the timeline create unrealistic expectations and delayed closings.

Fourth, agents overlook the New York State filing lead time. Form IT-2663 requires advance submission. The Department of Taxation and Finance typically needs at least 15 business days to process. If the contract has a 30-day closing, the math does not work unless the form is submitted immediately after contract execution.

Fifth, agents fail to coordinate between the seller's tax advisor and the buyer's closing attorney. FIRPTA compliance requires cooperation from both sides of the transaction, and agents who silo communication — or assume the attorneys will handle everything without prompting — are the ones whose deals crater at the eleventh hour. The [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) extends far beyond a single commission check.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers in New York carry supervisory liability under 19 NYCRR Part 175, the regulations governing licensed real estate brokers. If an agent in your office repeatedly mishandles foreign seller transactions, the Department of State can hold you accountable for inadequate supervision.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Listing intake form | Does it include a foreign person status question? | Every new listing |
| File documentation | Is a signed non-foreign affidavit or FIRPTA acknowledgment in the file? | Pre-contract |
| Timeline tracking | Was Form IT-2663/IT-2664 submitted with adequate lead time? | Before closing |
| Agent training records | Have agents completed annual FIRPTA compliance training? | Annually |
| Communication logs | Did the agent notify the buyer's side of foreign status early? | At offer acceptance |

**Brokers should mandate that every listing file contains either a signed FIRPTA Affidavit of Non-Foreign Status or a written acknowledgment that the seller is foreign and withholding obligations apply.** There is no middle ground. A missing document means an unauditable file, and an unauditable file means uncontrolled risk.

Beyond file audits, brokers should verify that their office's standard contract addenda include FIRPTA disclosure language. The New York State Association of Realtors (NYSAR) provides recommended language, but many offices still use outdated templates that omit state-level withholding references entirely.

## Reducing Withholding: The Form 8288-B Process

Foreign sellers are not forced to accept the full 15% withholding if their actual gain is significantly lower. IRS Form 8288-B allows the seller to apply for a Withholding Certificate that reduces or eliminates the federal withholding amount. The application must demonstrate the seller's maximum tax liability, and the IRS will issue a certificate specifying the reduced amount.

The problem is timing. The IRS advises applicants to file Form 8288-B as early as possible — ideally the day the contract is signed. Processing takes a minimum of 90 days in most cases as of early 2026. If the certificate is not issued by closing, the full 15% must still be withheld and deposited. The seller can later claim a refund when filing their U.S. tax return, but they will be out the capital in the interim.

> "The transferee must withhold the full amount required under section 1445(a) unless a withholding certificate is received from the IRS prior to the date of transfer." — Treasury Regulation § 1.1445-3(a)

For agents, the practical takeaway is straightforward: if your foreign seller wants reduced withholding, the 8288-B application must go out within days of contract execution, and the closing timeline may need to accommodate the IRS processing period.
