## Pennsylvania Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026: The Allocation Framework Agents Need

The question of Pennsylvania who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 trips up agents more often than anyone in the industry wants to admit. Unlike states where custom dictates almost everything, Pennsylvania's allocation depends on a layered mix of statute, contract language, and local practice that varies sharply between Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, and the commonwealth's 65 other counties. Getting the split wrong does not just embarrass you at the closing table — it can blow a deal, trigger a commission dispute, or land you in front of the Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission (PREC).

This post breaks down the statutory framework, the specific PAR forms that control cost allocation, the liability exposure agents face, and what managing brokers need to enforce across their teams heading into the second half of 2026.

## The Statutory Backbone: Transfer Taxes and Recording Fees

**Pennsylvania imposes a combined 2% Realty Transfer Tax on every residential sale — 1% to the state under 72 P.S. § 8102-C and 1% to the local municipality.** The statute itself does not mandate which party pays; it simply establishes joint and several liability, meaning the Department of Revenue can collect from either party if the tax goes unpaid. In practice, the Agreement of Sale dictates the split, and the longstanding custom in most PA counties is a 50/50 division.

Philadelphia layers on an additional 4.278% city transfer tax under the Philadelphia Code § 19-1402, making total transfer taxes in the city 6.278%. Montgomery, Delaware, and Chester counties stick closer to the 2% state-plus-local formula but occasionally see municipal surcharges. Agents who work across county lines must verify the local rate for every transaction rather than assume uniformity.

Recording fees — paid to the county Recorder of Deeds — are governed by 42 Pa.C.S. § 21051 and vary by county. Buyers typically pay for recording the deed and mortgage; sellers pay to record the mortgage satisfaction. These are small line items individually, but they add up and create confusion when agents lump them into vague "closing cost" language without specifying who covers what.

## The PAR Forms That Control Cost Allocation

The Pennsylvania Association of Realtors (PAR) Agreement of Sale — Residential (Form ASR, current revision effective January 2026) addresses closing costs primarily in Paragraphs 12 and 13. Paragraph 12 covers the seller's costs — mortgage payoff, pro-rated taxes, the seller's share of transfer tax, and any seller-paid title insurance premium. Paragraph 13 addresses the buyer's costs — lender fees, the buyer's share of transfer tax, recording charges, and the cost of the owner's title insurance policy if the buyer elects one.

**Paragraph 23 (Seller Assist) is where most negotiation happens.** This provision allows a seller credit toward the buyer's closing costs, often expressed as a fixed dollar amount or percentage of the sale price. Agents must confirm the credit does not exceed the buyer's lender cap — FHA allows up to 6%, VA up to 4%, and conventional loans typically cap at 3% for down payments under 10%.

Amendments to cost allocation must be memorialized on PAR Form AM (Amendment to Agreement of Sale). Verbal agreements or email confirmations without a signed amendment are unenforceable and create liability exposure. If a dispute arises over who owes what at settlement, the title company will follow the executed contract language — not your text thread with the cooperating agent.

For agents who [struggle with deadline management on multi-form transactions](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines), building a checklist that matches each cost item to a specific paragraph number eliminates ambiguity before it becomes a settlement-day crisis.

## What Happens When Agents Get It Wrong

The Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission, operating under the Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. § 455.101 et seq., has authority to discipline licensees for misrepresentation, negligence, and failure to account for money received in a real estate transaction. Under [PREC's administrative regulations at 49 Pa. Code § 35.284](https://www.dos.pa.gov/ProfessionalLicensing/BoardsCommissions/RealEstateCommission/Pages/default.aspx), agents must provide accurate disclosure of all fees and costs associated with the transaction.

If a listing agent tells a seller that "the buyer always pays for title insurance" without disclosing that the allocation is negotiable, that agent has made a material misrepresentation. Penalties under RELRA include fines up to $10,000 per violation, license suspension, and mandatory continuing education. In egregious cases — especially those involving undisclosed dual benefits — PREC can revoke a license entirely.

**Civil liability compounds the regulatory risk.** A buyer who discovers post-closing that they paid costs the contract allocated to the seller has grounds for a breach claim against the seller and potentially a negligence or fraud claim against the agent who drafted or failed to review the settlement statement. Title companies carry errors and omissions insurance for exactly this scenario, but agents should not rely on the title company as a backstop for their own contractual oversights.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate practical consequence. When a buyer receives their Closing Disclosure three days before settlement and sees unexpected costs, they can exercise their right to delay closing or, if the discrepancy is significant enough to constitute a material breach, invoke their termination rights under the Agreement of Sale.

## Five Common Mistakes Pennsylvania Agents Make

**First, agents frequently confuse "customary" with "required."** No Pennsylvania statute mandates that transfer tax be split 50/50. If your seller client wants the buyer to pay the full 2%, they can propose that — and the buyer can accept, reject, or counter. Telling clients something is "required by law" when it is actually custom exposes you to misrepresentation claims.

Second, agents fail to reconcile the seller assist with lender limits before executing the Agreement of Sale. A $15,000 seller credit on a $250,000 sale is 6% — fine for FHA but problematic for conventional financing with less than 25% down. When the lender rejects the credit amount, the deal stalls, and [blown closings cost everyone money and reputation](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Third, agents in multi-county practices apply Philadelphia norms to suburban transactions or vice versa. The 6.278% total transfer tax in Philadelphia creates materially different net sheets than the 2% in Lancaster County. An agent who prepares a net sheet using the wrong transfer tax rate will either overpromise the seller's proceeds or understate the buyer's costs.

Fourth, agents neglect to update the cost allocation when the transaction changes from conventional to FHA financing mid-contract. A financing switch often triggers a change in allowable seller credits, and the original Agreement of Sale language may no longer be compliant with the new lender's requirements. PAR Form AM must be executed to reflect the updated terms.

Fifth, listing agents fail to disclose to sellers that the settlement fee is negotiable. Many title companies will split the fee or assign it entirely to the buyer, but agents who always default to "seller pays half" without exploring alternatives leave money on their client's net sheet.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in Pennsylvania carry supervisory liability under 49 Pa. Code § 35.242, which requires brokers to "exercise adequate supervision over the activities of their licensees." That supervision obligation extends to reviewing cost allocation language in every Agreement of Sale before it becomes fully executed.

| Audit Item | What Broker Should Verify | Frequency |
|-----------|--------------------------|-----------|
| Transfer tax split language | Matches county rate and is explicitly stated in ¶12/13 | Every transaction |
| Seller assist amount | Does not exceed lender-specific cap for buyer's loan type | Every transaction with assist |
| PAR Form AM usage | Any mid-contract cost changes are formally amended | Spot-check weekly |
| Net sheet accuracy | Agent's estimated seller proceeds match actual county transfer tax rates | Monthly review |
| Settlement statement review | Final ALTA matches executed contract cost allocation | Pre-closing |

Brokers who [rely on memory rather than systematic checklists](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) will inevitably miss discrepancies that compound into liability. A quarterly file audit focusing specifically on Paragraphs 12, 13, and 23 of PAR Form ASR catches most allocation errors before they reach settlement.

PREC's investigative process typically begins with a consumer complaint filed online. Brokers should maintain documentation showing supervisory review of cost disclosures — a date-stamped note in the transaction file confirming the broker reviewed the cost allocation language is minimal but meaningful protection.

## Negotiation Realities Heading Into Late 2026

Pennsylvania's 2026 market has shifted from the extreme seller dominance of 2021-2022, which means buyers are successfully negotiating seller assists and non-standard cost allocations far more frequently. Agents on both sides need to treat Paragraph 23 as an active negotiation tool rather than a throwaway provision.

For buyer's agents, framing a seller assist request in terms of net-to-seller rather than gross credit amount tends to get faster acceptance. A seller who sees "net proceeds of $285,000 after a $7,500 credit" responds differently than one who sees "$7,500 off the price."

For listing agents, the key discipline is ensuring your seller understands that agreeing to cover the buyer's title insurance, for example, shifts roughly $2,000-$4,000 depending on sale price. That number must appear on the estimated net sheet before the seller signs the Agreement of Sale — not as a surprise at the closing table.
