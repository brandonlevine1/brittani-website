The direction is unmistakable: state legislatures are expanding what sellers must disclose, shortening the timelines for delivery, and raising the penalties for noncompliance. Since 2023, at least nine states have introduced or passed bills that add new mandatory disclosure categories — from climate risk and insurance history to past renovation permits and HOA litigation. If your brokerage hasn't updated its disclosure workflows in the last eighteen months, you are already behind.

## The Wave Started With Climate, But It Didn't Stop There

**California's AB 1525, effective January 2025, required sellers to disclose whether a property sits in a state-designated climate risk zone and whether they have received any climate-related insurance cancellation notices.** Colorado followed months later with HB 25-1198, adding flood and wildfire insurance claim history to its seller's property disclosure form. Oregon's 2025 legislative session produced SB 847, which mandates disclosure of any known soil instability or past landslide activity within 1,000 feet.

These aren't isolated actions. They reflect a coordinated lobbying push by consumer advocacy groups and state insurance commissioners who are tired of buyers discovering risk factors after closing. The [National Association of Realtors' 2025 legislative tracker](https://www.nar.realtor/political-advocacy/legislative-and-regulatory-policy) shows disclosure-related bills introduced in 23 states during the 2025-2026 legislative cycle — up from 14 in 2023-2024.

The pattern is clear: what was once considered "nice to have" transparency is becoming statutory obligation.

## Beyond Climate: Permits, Repairs, and Association Disputes

Climate risk gets the headlines, but the less-publicized expansion is happening around renovation history and HOA conflicts. Texas SB 312, signed in June 2025, now requires sellers to disclose any unpermitted structural modifications they are aware of — a significant shift from the state's historically seller-friendly posture. Florida's revised Form CRSP-16, updated by the Florida Real Estate Commission in late 2025, added three new line items related to active or resolved HOA/COA litigation.

Illinois went further. Its amended Residential Real Property Disclosure Act now requires sellers to state whether any insurance claim was denied in the prior five years and the reason for denial. That's not just "did you have water damage" — it's "did your insurer refuse to cover it, and why."

For brokers managing multiple transactions, these additions create a compliance surface that changes state by state and month by month. A form that was correct in Q1 may be deficient by Q3.

## The Enforcement Shift: From Civil Remedy to Regulatory Action

**What makes this cycle different from previous disclosure expansions is where enforcement is landing.** Historically, disclosure failures surfaced as post-closing civil claims between buyer and seller. The listing agent was occasionally dragged in, but regulatory boards rarely got involved unless fraud was alleged.

That dynamic is shifting. The Colorado Division of Real Estate issued guidance in March 2025 stating that a licensee's failure to advise clients about new disclosure obligations could constitute a violation of the competency standard under Rule E-35. Washington's Department of Licensing revised its disciplinary matrix in 2025 to include "failure to ensure timely and complete seller disclosure delivery" as a stand-alone category.

This means the risk has moved upstream — from a buyer's attorney sending a demand letter to a state board opening an investigation. For team leads managing newer agents, [training on disclosure compliance](/blog/agent-training-compliance-basics) is no longer optional continuing education. It's a liability firewall.

## The Counterargument: Are We Over-Regulating Sellers?

The strongest pushback comes from property rights advocates and some broker associations who argue that expanding disclosure forms creates a paradox. The more items you add, the more likely sellers are to check "unknown" on every line — rendering the form meaningless. There's also a legitimate concern about seller fatigue producing inaccurate or careless responses that expose everyone to more litigation, not less.

I take this seriously. The Texas Association of Realtors raised exactly this point in their comment letter on SB 312, noting that lengthy forms can reduce the quality of individual responses. They're not wrong about the behavioral reality.

But the answer isn't to resist disclosure expansion — it's to build better systems around it. When forms get longer and more jurisdiction-specific, the operational response has to be process-driven rather than memory-driven. The agents and brokers who treat disclosure as a checklist they'll "remember to update" are the ones ending up in front of review boards. Those who build repeatable, tracked workflows around form delivery and completion are the ones sleeping soundly.

## Timelines Are Compressing Too

**It's not just the content of disclosures that's expanding — it's the speed at which they must be delivered.** Georgia's 2025 amendment to O.C.G.A. 44-1-16 shortened the seller disclosure delivery window from "prior to acceptance" (a standard with generous interpretation) to "within 48 hours of listing activation on any MLS." That's a meaningful operational change for listing agents who previously treated disclosure as a post-showing, pre-offer task.

Minnesota's updated rules require that any material change to a previously delivered disclosure — say, a new insurance claim filed during the listing period — must be communicated to all parties within 72 hours of the seller becoming aware. That puts a continuing obligation on the agent to check in with the seller, not just at listing but throughout the transaction.

If your [transaction coordination process](/blog/transaction-coordination-workflow) doesn't include automated prompts for disclosure updates at defined intervals, you're exposed.

## What This Means for Multi-State Brokerages

The compliance burden falls disproportionately on brokerages operating across state lines. A team doing business in three states might now face three different disclosure forms, three different delivery timelines, and three different penalty structures — all updated on different legislative calendars.

NAR's state-by-state disclosure comparison, last updated in Q4 2025, already runs to 47 pages. That's a reference document, not an operational tool. The operational question is: how does your team ensure the right form, with the right fields, gets to the right party, at the right time, with a delivery receipt that holds up under scrutiny?

Manual tracking worked when disclosure was a single static form per state. It does not work when forms update mid-year and delivery windows shrink to 48 hours.

## The Brokers Who Adapt Fastest Will Separate From the Pack

This regulatory wave isn't reversing. Consumer protection lobbies have momentum, state insurance departments are aligned, and legislators have discovered that expanding disclosure is politically painless — it's pro-consumer and costs the state nothing to implement. The only cost falls on practitioners who fail to keep pace.

The actionable move is this: audit your disclosure process now, not after your next transaction stumbles. Map every state you operate in, identify the most recent form revision date, and confirm your delivery workflow produces a timestamped compliance trail.
