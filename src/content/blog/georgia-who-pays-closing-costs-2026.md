# Georgia Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026: What Every Agent Must Get Right

The question of Georgia who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is one that surfaces in nearly every listing presentation and buyer consultation across the state. Yet agents still fumble it, sometimes at the cost of a deal and sometimes at the cost of a license. Georgia's attorney-close requirement, its specific transfer tax statute, and the Georgia Association of Realtors (GAR) contract forms create a framework that differs meaningfully from neighboring states.

This post breaks down who is customarily responsible for each line item, the forms that memorialize those obligations, and the consequences agents face when they get it wrong.

## The Legal Framework Governing Closing Costs in Georgia

**Georgia does not have a single statute that assigns every closing cost to one party.** Instead, responsibility flows from three sources: state statute, contract terms, and local custom. O.C.G.A. 48-6-1 governs the real estate transfer tax. O.C.G.A. 44-14-13 governs title examination requirements. And because Georgia is an attorney-close state per the [Georgia Supreme Court's unauthorized practice of law opinions](https://www.grec.state.ga.us/), the presence of a settlement attorney adds a fee layer that does not exist in non-attorney states.

The Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) regulates agent conduct under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25. GREC does not dictate who pays what, but it does require agents to accurately represent material facts about a transaction, including cost allocation. Misleading a client about their financial obligations at closing is a licensable offense.

Contract terms override custom. If the GAR F20 Purchase and Sale Agreement specifies that the buyer pays the transfer tax, that term controls regardless of what is "normal" in a given county. Agents must read the actual contract language rather than relying on assumptions carried over from the last deal.

## Standard Cost Allocation: Buyer vs. Seller

The table below reflects customary allocation in most Georgia metro and suburban markets. Every item is negotiable, but this represents the default starting position agents encounter on GAR forms.

| Cost Item | Customary Payer | Approximate Amount | Governing Reference |
|---|---|---|---|
| Real estate transfer tax | Seller | $1.00 per $1,000 of sale price | O.C.G.A. 48-6-1 |
| Owner's title insurance | Seller | $800–$2,500 (varies by price) | Custom / Contract |
| Lender's title insurance | Buyer | $400–$1,200 | Lender requirement |
| Attorney/closing fee | Buyer | $800–$1,500 | Attorney-close state custom |
| Intangible tax on new mortgage | Buyer | $1.50 per $500 of loan amount | O.C.G.A. 48-6-61 |
| Recording fees (deed) | Buyer | $10–$25 per page | County clerk schedule |

| HOA transfer/estoppel fee | Seller | $150–$500 | HOA governing docs |

Note that metro Atlanta transactions often deviate from this chart, particularly when sellers offer concessions to attract buyers in a shifting market. Agents should never present this allocation as fixed law. It is custom backed by contract language, and either party may propose changes through the appropriate GAR amendment form.

## Key GAR Forms That Document Cost Allocation

**The GAR F20 Purchase and Sale Agreement is where closing cost responsibility is first established.** Paragraph 5 of the F20 addresses the purchase price and financing terms, including any seller contributions toward buyer closing costs. Paragraph 8 covers closing details including who selects the closing attorney.

When parties agree to seller-paid closing costs, that concession should appear in the Special Stipulations section of the F20 or via the GAR F803 Special Stipulations Exhibit. Vague language such as "seller to help with costs" invites disputes. The concession should state a dollar cap or percentage and specify whether it applies to recurring or non-recurring costs.

If the parties renegotiate cost allocation after binding agreement—say, following an inspection—agents should use the GAR F524 Amendment to Address Concerns with Property. This form allows both sides to modify financial terms without voiding the contract. Agents who attempt to renegotiate costs through informal emails or text messages risk creating unenforceable side agreements that a closing attorney will reject at settlement.

For deals involving [transaction coordination support](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator), tracking which amendment modified cost terms is essential because settlement attorneys will reconcile the Closing Disclosure against every executed contract document.

## What Happens When an Agent Gets It Wrong

GREC's disciplinary authority under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25(a) covers misrepresentation, negligent omission, and failure to account for funds. An agent who tells a seller "you won't owe anything at closing" without verifying actual cost allocation is making a material misrepresentation. The consequences escalate based on harm.

A first offense involving an honest mistake may result in a citation and mandatory continuing education. Repeated misrepresentations or cases involving consumer financial harm can trigger license suspension, fines up to $5,000 per violation, or revocation. GREC publishes disciplinary actions on its website, meaning the reputational damage compounds the financial penalty.

Beyond GREC, agents face civil liability. If a buyer loses earnest money because the seller refused to honor an improperly documented concession, the agent who failed to memorialize it may be named in a small claims or superior court action. Errors and omissions insurance covers some of these situations, but policies typically exclude intentional misrepresentation. Agents should review the [true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) to understand how a single documentation failure cascades into lost commissions, legal fees, and client relationships.

## Five Common Mistakes Georgia Agents Make on Closing Costs

**First, agents frequently confuse the intangible tax with the transfer tax.** The transfer tax under O.C.G.A. 48-6-1 is traditionally a seller cost. The intangible tax under O.C.G.A. 48-6-61, levied on the new mortgage amount, is a buyer cost. Swapping these on a net sheet misleads the client about their bottom line by potentially thousands of dollars on high-value transactions.

Second, agents quote seller concessions without confirming lender limits. FHA allows up to 6% in seller concessions, conventional loans cap at 3% for buyers putting less than 10% down, and VA allows up to 4% in certain categories. An agent who writes a 5% seller concession into a conventional contract for a buyer with 5% down has created a term that the lender will reject, forcing a last-minute renegotiation or deal collapse.

Third, agents fail to specify whether the owner's title insurance premium is the seller's or buyer's responsibility. In many Georgia counties, custom places it on the seller, but custom is not law. If the F20 is silent on this point and the parties disagree, the closing attorney cannot disburse without resolution. This is exactly [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down).

Fourth, agents neglect HOA transfer fees. Georgia HOAs routinely charge $150–$500 for document preparation and account transfers. If neither party's agent accounts for this charge, it surfaces on the Closing Disclosure three days before closing and triggers a dispute over who writes the check.

Fifth, agents use outdated net sheet calculators that do not reflect 2026 county recording fee schedules or updated intangible tax thresholds. A net sheet is only as accurate as the data feeding it.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers have supervisory duties under O.C.G.A. 43-40-18 and GREC Rule 520-1-.07. Those duties extend to ensuring agents accurately represent closing cost obligations. A broker who rubber-stamps files without reviewing cost allocation language in contracts faces vicarious liability and potential GREC action against their firm license.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Seller concession documentation | F20 Paragraph 5 or Special Stipulations | Concession exceeds lender-allowed maximum |
| Net sheet accuracy | Compare to Closing Disclosure | Variance greater than $500 unexplained |
| Transfer tax calculation | Sale price times $1.00 per $1,000 | Math error or wrong party charged |
| Amendment trail | F524 or F803 used for post-binding changes | Cost changes documented only in email |
| Attorney selection compliance | F20 Paragraph 8 | Agent selecting attorney without buyer consent |

Brokers should require agents to submit net sheets alongside executed contracts for review before the file reaches the closing attorney. This catches errors early enough to resolve without delaying settlement. A quarterly audit of closed transaction files—comparing final Closing Disclosures against original contract terms—identifies systemic training gaps in the office.

## Negotiation Realities in the 2026 Georgia Market

The 2026 market in Georgia has shifted cost negotiations toward more creative structures. Builders in suburban Atlanta corridors like Forsyth and Gwinnett counties are offering closing cost credits in lieu of price reductions, which changes how agents must document these credits on GAR forms. A builder credit that does not appear in the contract but only on the builder's internal worksheet is invisible to the appraiser and the lender, creating compliance risk.

Resale transactions in intown Atlanta neighborhoods are seeing buyers request that sellers cover the attorney fee—a departure from the traditional buyer-pays custom. This is perfectly legal but must be explicitly stated in the contract. Agents who assume the closing attorney will "just know" to charge the seller are setting up a dispute at the settlement table.

The bottom line for agents working Georgia transactions in 2026: document every cost allocation term in writing, use the correct GAR form, and verify that lender guidelines support any concession structure before going under contract. The question of Georgia who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is never answered by custom alone—it is answered by what the signed contract says and whether that contract aligns with regulatory and lender requirements.
