# Indiana Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026: What Every Agent Must Know

The Indiana commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 have reshaped how buyer agents get paid, how listing agents present offers, and how brokerages document every dollar of compensation in a transaction. If you hold an Indiana real estate license and have not updated your pre-showing processes, your listing presentation scripts, or your brokerage compliance files since August 2024, you are already behind. The consequences range from deal cancellations to disciplinary action by the Indiana Real Estate Commission (IREC).

This post breaks down exactly what changed, what forms you need, what penalties you face, and what your managing broker should be auditing right now.

## How the NAR Settlement Changed Indiana Commission Practices

The NAR settlement, finalized in late 2024, eliminated blanket offers of buyer-agent compensation through any NAR-affiliated MLS. In Indiana, that means the statewide MIBOR MLS, the Indiana Regional MLS, and every local board-operated system stopped displaying seller-offered buyer-agent commissions. The prohibition is structural, not optional.

**Indiana agents must now treat buyer-agent compensation as a separate, negotiated agreement between the buyer and their agent.** The seller can still contribute to that compensation, but the mechanism changed. Sellers may offer concessions through listing remarks or direct communication, but the MLS field for buyer-agent commission no longer exists.

The Indiana Real Estate Commission issued [Guidance Bulletin 2024-03](https://www.in.gov/pla/professions/real-estate/) clarifying that the state's existing agency disclosure requirements under Indiana Code 25-34.1-10 already mandated written agreements, but the settlement raised the enforcement bar. IREC now treats missing or deficient buyer agreements as a disciplinary priority.

## Written Buyer Agency Agreements: Indiana's New Non-Negotiable

Before the settlement, many Indiana agents operated under informal buyer relationships, sometimes touring five or six homes before getting anything signed. That practice is dead.

Under the settlement's practice changes, a buyer's agent must have a signed, written agreement before any property tour. The agreement must state the specific compensation the buyer's agent will receive, and it cannot be left open-ended or say "whatever the seller offers." Indiana Code 25-34.1-10-9.5 reinforces this by requiring written disclosure of the agent's compensation terms prior to providing brokerage services.

The Indiana Association of Realtors (IAR) updated its standard forms in response. IAR Form 56 (Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement) now includes explicit fields for fixed-fee amounts, percentage rates, or hourly compensation structures. IAR Form 57 (Non-Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement) mirrors these fields for agents working under non-exclusive arrangements. Both forms require the buyer's initials next to the compensation disclosure paragraph.

| Requirement | Before Settlement | After Settlement (2026) |
|---|---|---|
| Written buyer agreement timing | Often delayed until offer stage | Required before any showing |
| Compensation disclosure | Vague or unstated | Must specify exact amount or rate |
| MLS-published buyer-agent commission | Standard practice | Prohibited |
| Seller concession for buyer-agent pay | Automatic via MLS | Must be negotiated off-MLS |
| Form used | IAR Form 56 (older version) | IAR Form 56 (revised 2024+) |

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**Non-compliance triggers a chain of consequences that can destroy a deal and a career.** The Indiana Real Estate Commission has authority under IC 25-34.1-10-12 to impose sanctions including license suspension, revocation, and civil penalties up to $10,000 per violation.

Beyond IREC discipline, a buyer who was never presented with a written agreement can argue the agent's compensation was never authorized. This opens the door to commission disgorgement, meaning a court can order the agent to return every dollar earned on the transaction. Indiana appellate courts have historically sided with consumers when disclosure duties were not met.

At the deal level, title companies in Indiana have started flagging transactions where the buyer-agent compensation documentation is missing from the closing file. Several major Indiana title underwriters now require a copy of the signed buyer agreement before issuing clear-to-close. If you cannot produce it, the closing gets delayed or canceled.

There is also a federal antitrust dimension. The entire point of the NAR settlement was to resolve claims of anticompetitive behavior. An agent who steers buyers away from properties where no seller concession is offered, or who inflates compensation demands without written justification, risks individual antitrust exposure under the Sherman Act.

## Common Mistakes Indiana Agents Are Making Right Now

After eighteen months of living under the new rules, patterns of non-compliance have emerged. Here are the errors showing up most frequently in IREC complaint files and brokerage audits.

**First, agents are using outdated forms.** Some Indiana agents are still printing the pre-2024 version of IAR Form 56, which does not include the required specificity in compensation terms. The old form allowed language like "as offered through the MLS," which is now functionally meaningless and legally insufficient.

Second, agents are treating verbal agreements as sufficient for initial showings. The rules require a written, signed agreement before touring. Telling a buyer "we'll sign the paperwork later" and then showing three homes creates liability the moment you walk through the first door. IREC considers that an unauthorized provision of brokerage services.

Third, some listing agents are attempting to communicate buyer-agent compensation through MLS agent-only remarks. This violates the settlement terms even if it does not appear in public-facing fields. The prohibition covers all MLS fields, public and private. [Understanding where deals commonly break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) helps illustrate why these documentation gaps create cascading failures at closing.

Fourth, buyer agents are failing to present multiple compensation options. The settlement encourages competition on fees, and IREC has signaled that agreements offering only one take-it-or-leave-it rate may face scrutiny if a pattern of complaints emerges.

Fifth, agents are neglecting to update buyer agreements when compensation terms change mid-transaction. If a seller offers a concession that alters the buyer's obligation, the buyer agreement must be amended. IAR Form 56A (Amendment to Buyer Agency Agreement) exists for exactly this purpose, and skipping it creates a mismatch between the agreement and the closing statement.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Managing brokers in Indiana carry supervisory liability under IC 25-34.1-10-10. If an agent in your brokerage closes a deal without a compliant buyer agreement, IREC can sanction the broker independently of the agent.

Here is a minimum audit checklist for Indiana brokerages operating under the 2026 rules:

| Audit Item | Frequency | Consequence of Failure |
|---|---|---|
| Signed buyer agreement on file before first showing | Every transaction | IREC fine, commission disgorgement risk |
| Compensation terms match closing disclosure | Every transaction | Title company rejection, consumer complaint |
| IAR Form 56/57 version date is 2024 or later | Quarterly form review | Inadequate disclosure, potential voiding |
| IAR Form 56A filed for any mid-deal compensation change | As triggered | Misrepresentation claim, E&O exposure |
| Agent training documentation on settlement compliance | Annual minimum | Supervisory liability under IC 25-34.1 |

## Seller-Side Changes: Listing Agent Obligations

Listing agents are not exempt from the settlement's operational shifts. While the listing side has fewer new forms to manage, the disclosure obligations changed.

Indiana sellers must be informed that they are not required to offer any compensation to a buyer's agent. This disclosure should be documented during the listing presentation and memorialized in the listing agreement. IAR Form 46 (Exclusive Right to Sell Listing Agreement) was updated in 2024 to include a seller acknowledgment section covering this point.

If a seller chooses to offer buyer-agent compensation anyway, the listing agent must ensure that offer is communicated outside the MLS. Common methods include listing remarks on the brokerage's own website, direct agent-to-agent communication, or terms included in the seller's counter-offer. The key constraint is that no MLS-affiliated platform carries the offer.

Listing agents should also prepare sellers for buyers who submit offers requesting the seller cover their agent's fee as a concession. This is increasingly common, and sellers who refuse may limit their buyer pool. Framing it as a net-proceeds conversation rather than a "paying the other side's agent" conversation tends to produce better outcomes.

## Dual Agency and Compensation Conflicts Under the New Rules

Indiana permits dual agency under IC 25-34.1-10-9, but the compensation rules make dual agency transactions more complex than before. When a single agent represents both sides, the buyer agreement and listing agreement must not create conflicting compensation obligations.

> "A licensee shall not collect compensation from more than one party to a transaction without the written consent of all parties." — Indiana Code 25-34.1-10-12(a)(14)

An agent serving as a dual agent must clearly document which party is paying what, and both parties must sign off.
