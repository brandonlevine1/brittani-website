## Why Michigan Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Wire fraud losses in real estate topped $446 million nationally in 2023, and Michigan ranked among the top 15 states for reported incidents according to FBI IC3 data. Understanding Michigan wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 is not optional—it is a baseline expectation from your broker, your title partners, and the Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA). Agents who treat this as a checkbox exercise are the ones most likely to face a disciplinary complaint after a client's earnest money disappears into a spoofed account.

The Michigan Occupational Code (MCL 339.2512) grants LARA authority to discipline licensees who fail to exercise reasonable skill and care. Courts and administrative law judges have increasingly interpreted "reasonable skill" to include wire fraud education and disclosure, particularly after industry associations formalized advisory forms in 2023 and 2024. If your compliance routine has not changed since then, you are already behind.

## The Regulatory Framework: Statutes and Bodies That Govern You

Michigan does not have a single standalone wire fraud prevention statute the way some states handle seller disclosure. Instead, enforcement flows through several overlapping authorities. LARA's Bureau of Professional Licensing oversees agent conduct under the Occupational Code. The Michigan Realtors association publishes standardized transaction forms, including the Wire Fraud Warning Advisory (MR-7040), which has become the de facto compliance benchmark even though it is technically voluntary.

**The Real Estate Stabilization Act and LARA Rule 339.22301 require licensees to act with competence and disclose material risks to clients.** Wire fraud is now treated as a material risk in any transaction involving electronic fund transfers. Title companies operating under Michigan's Title Insurance Act (MCL 500.7401 et seq.) have their own verification protocols, but those do not relieve agents of their independent duty to warn and verify.

The [Michigan LARA licensing page](https://www.michigan.gov/lara/bureau-list/bpl/occ/prof/real-estate) publishes enforcement actions quarterly. In 2025, two agents received formal citations partly tied to inadequate client communication about wire fraud risks during transactions that resulted in consumer losses.

## Forms and Documentation: What You Actually Need to Use

The primary form is the Michigan Realtors Wire Fraud Warning Advisory, form number MR-7040. This document explicitly warns buyers and sellers that wire instructions can be intercepted, advises them to verify all wiring details by phone before sending funds, and provides a signature line confirming the client received the warning. You should have this signed at the same time you execute the purchase agreement—not three days before closing when it is too late to matter.

Some brokerages require an additional internal form, often called a Wire Verification Confirmation or similar. This second document records that the agent independently verified the title company's wire instructions through a known phone number—not the number listed in an email. If your brokerage does not have this form, you should still document the verification call in your transaction file with a date, time, and the name of the person you spoke to.

| Document | Form Number | When to Use | Who Signs |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| Wire Fraud Warning Advisory | MR-7040 | At purchase agreement execution | Buyer and Seller |
| Wire Verification Confirmation | Brokerage-specific | 48 hours before closing | Agent (internal record) |
| Title Company Wire Instructions | Title-issued | Received and verified before closing | Agent verifies, client acts |

Do not confuse the MR-7040 with the older Fraud Warning Addendum some brokerages used internally before 2023. The older versions lacked specific language about email compromise and verification callbacks that LARA expects to see in current practice. If you are still using a 2021 or 2022 version, update immediately.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: What Actually Happens

**Failing to comply does not just risk a slap on the wrist—it can end your involvement in a transaction and trigger cascading liability.** Here is how enforcement and consequences play out in Michigan.

LARA can impose fines up to $2,500 per violation under the Occupational Code and can suspend or revoke your license for a pattern of negligence. A single incident where a client loses funds and you failed to provide the MR-7040 creates a strong case for disciplinary action. Beyond LARA, your client's attorney will likely name you in a civil suit for negligence and breach of fiduciary duty.

Your Errors and Omissions insurance may not save you. Most E&O policies contain exclusions or coverage limitations for claims arising from failure to follow industry-standard practices. If your brokerage had a wire fraud policy in writing and you ignored it, your carrier has grounds to deny the claim. The result: personal financial exposure for six-figure wire losses.

| Consequence | Trigger | Potential Outcome |
|-------------|---------|-------------------|
| LARA disciplinary action | Failure to disclose wire fraud risk | Fine up to $2,500, license suspension |
| Civil liability | Client loss without proper warning | Negligence judgment, damages |
| E&O coverage denial | Non-compliance with brokerage policy | Personal financial exposure |

Deal cancellation is the most common real-world outcome. A buyer whose funds get intercepted typically cannot close on time. The seller may invoke contract deadlines, retain earnest money, and relist. The agent who failed to educate and verify gets blamed by everyone in the room—and rightly so. ## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Wire Fraud Prevention

**Error one: Delivering the MR-7040 at the wrong time.** The warning is most effective when signed at purchase agreement execution. Many agents wait until the closing disclosure review or even the day before closing to bring up wire fraud. By that point, your buyer may have already received and acted on fraudulent instructions. The form is designed to prime the client's awareness before any wire communication occurs.

Error two: Forwarding title company wire instructions via email without a verbal disclaimer. Even if you add a footer warning about verifying instructions, forwarding wire routing numbers by email creates an additional attack surface. If your email is compromised, you become the vector. Best practice is to never forward wire instructions electronically—direct clients to call the title company using the number on the title company's official website, not from any email.

Error three: Failing to document the verification callback. You called the title company and confirmed the routing number. Great. But if you did not write down the date, time, and representative's name, you have no evidence you performed due diligence. In a dispute, LARA and the courts rely on documentation. A transaction file without a verification note is a transaction file that suggests you did nothing.

Error four: Assuming the title company handles everything. Title companies do have their own verification protocols under Michigan's Title Insurance Act, but their compliance does not substitute for yours. You have an independent fiduciary duty to your client. If the title company's email was spoofed and your client acted on it without your warning, both parties share liability—but your client will point at you first.

Error five: Using outdated contact information for title company verification. Agents who rely on phone numbers embedded in emails—even seemingly legitimate ones—can be calling the fraudster's spoofed line. Always verify through independently sourced contact information: the title company's website, your prior records, or a direct office visit.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers in Michigan carry supervisory liability under MCL 339.2512e. If an agent in your office fails to deliver wire fraud warnings and a client suffers a loss, LARA will examine whether you had policies in place and whether you enforced them. Having a policy on a shared drive that nobody reads does not constitute supervision.

**Start by auditing a random sample of closed transaction files every quarter.** Check for the signed MR-7040, the verification callback documentation, and evidence that the form was delivered at or before purchase agreement execution—not at closing. If your agents are consistently delivering the warning late, your training needs to change.

Require your agents to complete annual wire fraud prevention training and document it. LARA has not mandated specific CE credits for wire fraud as of 2026, but demonstrating proactive education strengthens your defense in any enforcement action. Some Michigan brokerages now include wire fraud protocol acknowledgment in their agent onboarding packet, alongside their agency disclosure policies.

Build your audit checklist around these questions: Was MR-7040 signed before or at purchase agreement execution? Is there a verification callback record in the file? Did the agent forward wire instructions by email at any point? Did the agent use a known phone number for verification? Tracking these four data points per file takes minutes but provides significant liability protection. Agents who consistently miss [deadlines and compliance steps](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) need additional oversight before they cost you a license.
