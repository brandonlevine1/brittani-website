## Why Pennsylvania Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026 Demand Your Attention

Every year, agents across the Commonwealth lose deals—and sometimes licenses—because they treat earnest money like a routine clerical step. Pennsylvania earnest money & escrow laws 2026 carry real enforcement teeth, and the Pennsylvania Real Estate Commission (PREC) has shown no signs of relaxing its auditing posture. If you handle even one deposit incorrectly, you expose your brokerage to regulatory action and your client to financial harm.

The governing framework sits primarily in the Real Estate Licensing and Registration Act (RELRA), 63 P.S. Sections 455.101–455.902, and its implementing regulations at 49 Pa. Code Chapter 35. These aren't suggestions. They are the rules that dictate how you receive, hold, disburse, and account for every dollar of deposit money that flows through your office.

This post breaks down the statutes, the forms, the deadlines, and the consequences that matter most in 2026. Agents who internalize these rules protect their clients and their own careers; those who wing it become cautionary tales on [PREC disciplinary action reports](https://www.dos.pa.gov/ProfessionalLicensing/BoardsCommissions/RealEstateCommission/Pages/default.aspx).

## The Statutory Foundation: RELRA and 49 Pa. Code Chapter 35

**Section 604(a)(5) of RELRA makes it a violation to fail to deposit money received on behalf of another person into a proper escrow account.** The statute is broad by design—any money received "in trust" must hit an escrow or trust account held in a federally insured banking institution in Pennsylvania. No exceptions for "the buyer said I could hold the check until next week."

49 Pa. Code Section 35.327 provides the operational details. It requires that escrow accounts bear the name of the broker, be clearly designated as escrow or trust accounts, and that records be maintained showing the date of deposit, the source, the property address, and the disbursement date. The regulation also prohibits commingling—broker operating funds cannot reside in the escrow account except for a nominal amount required by the bank to keep the account open.

Pennsylvania does not mandate interest-bearing escrow accounts by default. If the parties agree to an interest-bearing account, the agreement must specify who earns the interest and who bears any tax liability. This is documented in the PAR Agreement of Sale under Paragraph 24 (Escrow).

## Key PAR Forms Agents Must Know

The Pennsylvania Association of Realtors (PAR) publishes the standard forms that govern most residential transactions in the state. Understanding which forms control earnest money and escrow obligations prevents disputes before they start.

| Form | Purpose | Escrow-Related Clause |
|------|---------|----------------------|
| PAR Form ASR (Agreement of Sale — Residential) | Standard purchase contract | Paragraph 3 (Deposits), Paragraph 24 (Escrow) |
| PAR Form DE (Deposit Escrow Notice) | Confirms receipt of deposit into escrow | Entire form |
| PAR Form RR (Release of Deposit) | Mutual release when deal falls apart | Authorizes escrow holder disbursement |
| PAR Form DL (Deposit Dispute Letter) | Used when parties disagree on deposit release | Triggers statutory interpleader timeline |

**Paragraph 3 of Form ASR specifies the deposit amounts, the timing, and the identity of the escrow holder.** Agents filling this paragraph carelessly—leaving blanks or writing vague delivery dates—create ambiguity that leads to disputes later. The form allows for initial and additional deposits with separate deadlines, and each must be tracked independently.

Form DE deserves special attention. Once the deposit lands in escrow, the escrow holder should issue this notice to confirm receipt. While not a PREC regulatory mandate by letter, failure to provide written confirmation of deposit receipt creates a factual vacuum that becomes a liability issue if the deal goes sideways. Brokerages that skip this step are the ones who end up in arbitration explaining why nobody can document the deposit timeline.

## Deposit Timing: The Clock That Agents Keep Missing

Pennsylvania's standard of care—reinforced by PAR Form ASR—requires the deposit to be delivered to the escrow holder by the end of the next business day following full execution of the agreement. Agents who [miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) on deposit delivery put transactions at risk of termination.

The buyer can hand you a check at 9 PM on a Friday. That means you must deliver it to the escrow holder by close of business Monday—assuming Monday is not a holiday. "I forgot it in my car over the weekend" is not a defense recognized by PREC or by a buyer's attorney drafting a notice of default.

Some agents confuse delivery to escrow holder with deposit into the bank. These are two separate events. Delivery to the escrow holder (your broker's office or the designated attorney) satisfies the contractual obligation. The escrow holder then bears the regulatory duty to deposit into the trust account promptly—PREC expects this within one business day of receipt, consistent with 49 Pa. Code Section 35.327's recordkeeping requirements.

## What Happens When an Agent Fails to Comply

Noncompliance triggers three categories of risk: regulatory, contractual, and civil. They can stack on top of each other in a single transaction.

**Regulatory risk** means PREC can take action against your license. Under Section 604 of RELRA, violations involving escrow funds can result in formal reprimand, fines up to $10,000 per violation, license suspension, or license revocation. PREC publishes adjudication orders quarterly—read them. Agents are routinely disciplined for late deposits, unreported escrow shortages, and failure to maintain records.

**Contractual risk** means the deal itself may be voidable. If the deposit is not delivered by the deadline in Paragraph 3 of the Agreement of Sale, the seller may have grounds to declare the buyer in default. Alternatively, a buyer who discovers mishandled escrow funds may walk and demand the deposit back, citing the agent's breach of fiduciary duty.

**Civil risk** means a lawsuit. Conversion of escrow funds—even temporarily redirecting them—can expose an agent and broker to civil claims for damages, emotional distress, and attorney fees. In Pennsylvania, the broker is vicariously liable for agent mishandling of escrow under RELRA Section 604(a)(5), which means the brokerage insurance policy is on the line.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Earnest Money in Pennsylvania

Five errors surface repeatedly in PREC disciplinary actions and brokerage compliance reviews. Knowing them is different from avoiding them—especially under the pressure of a fast-moving transaction.

**Mistake 1: Accepting deposits made payable to the agent personally.** The check must be payable to the escrow holder identified in the agreement—typically the brokerage or the buyer's attorney. An agent who accepts a check in their own name and deposits it into a personal account has committed commingling regardless of intent. The fix is simple: instruct buyers at signing to make checks payable to the brokerage escrow account.

**Mistake 2: Holding a deposit "pending inspection" without written authorization.** Some agents tell buyers they will hold the deposit check uncashed until the inspection contingency passes. Unless the Agreement of Sale explicitly authorizes delayed deposit, this violates the delivery timeline. If you want a delayed deposit, write it into Paragraph 3 with a specific date and get all parties to initial.

**Mistake 3: Disbursing escrow without mutual written release.** When a deal falls apart, the emotional pressure to "just give the money back" leads agents to disburse without Form RR or an equivalent mutual release. Pennsylvania law does not permit unilateral disbursement by the escrow holder unless 30 days have passed since written notice to the non-consenting party under Section 604(a)(5.1) of RELRA. Disbursing prematurely exposes the broker to claims from both sides.

**Mistake 4: Failing to disclose escrow account details to buyers.** Buyers have the right to know where their money is held. Agents who cannot immediately identify the bank name, account type, and whether it is interest-bearing are failing a basic transparency obligation. The [transaction transparency for clients](/blog/transaction-transparency-for-clients) standard applies doubly to escrow.

**Mistake 5: Not reconciling escrow records monthly.** This is a broker-level failure that agents contribute to by delivering deposits late or without proper documentation. 49 Pa. Code Section 35.327 requires ongoing recordkeeping that effectively mandates monthly reconciliation. Agents who delay forwarding deposit receipts make reconciliation impossible.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry the ultimate regulatory burden for escrow compliance in Pennsylvania. PREC does not accept "my agent handled it" as a defense. The broker's name is on the escrow account, and the broker's license is on the line.

| Audit Area | Frequency | What to Check |
|------------|-----------|---------------|
| Deposit timing | Every transaction | Was deposit delivered within one business day of execution? |
| Account reconciliation | Monthly | Do ledger balances match bank statements for every open deposit? |
| Form DE issuance | Every transaction | Was deposit receipt confirmation sent to all parties? |
| Disbursement authorization | Every closed or canceled deal | Is there a signed Form RR or court order on file? |
| Commingling check | Monthly | Are any broker operating funds present in the escrow account beyond the bank minimum? |

**Brokers who do not conduct monthly escrow audits are playing Russian roulette with their license.** A single unreported shortage—even $50—can trigger a PREC investigation if discovered during a routine office inspection. PREC conducts random and complaint-driven audits of brokerage offices, and escrow records are the first thing investigators review.

Beyond regulatory compliance, brokers should enforce internal policies that go further than the statutory minimum. Require agents to submit copies of deposit checks within two hours of receipt. Mandate same-day notification to the transaction coordinator when a deposit is in hand. Build [checklists that beat memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) into your compliance workflow so that no deposit slips through the cracks.

Brokers should also maintain a written escrow policy manual, updated annually, and require every agent to sign an acknowledgment. When PREC investigators arrive, having a documented policy plus agent signatures demonstrates good-faith compliance efforts—even if an individual agent deviated.

## Dispute Resolution: When the Deposit Becomes a Battleground

Pennsylvania created a statutory framework for escrow disputes that agents must understand before the fight begins. Under RELRA Section 604(a)(5.1), when parties cannot agree on disbursement, the escrow holder must send written notice to both parties stating that funds will be disbursed to the claiming party in 30 days unless the other party objects in writing.

If the other party objects within 30 days, the escrow holder must retain the funds and may interplead—file the funds with the court and let a judge decide. The escrow holder is not an arbitrator. Agents and brokers who try to "split the difference" or negotiate deposit disputes without mutual written consent are exceeding their authority and risking liability.

The cost of interpleader falls on the brokerage unless the agreement states otherwise. This is why Paragraph 24 of PAR Form ASR matters—it can allocate interpleader costs to the defaulting party. Agents who leave this paragraph unmodified should at least understand what it says so they can explain it to clients before a dispute arises.
