The closing table as we know it is dying—not metaphorically, but literally. Remote Online Notarization (RON) is now legal in 47 states, title companies are embedding AI into commitment reviews, and Fannie Mae's Day 1 Certainty program has already eliminated the need for paper income verification on qualifying loans. The question brokers need to answer right now is not whether digital closings will become the norm, but whether agents will still be in the room when they do.

## RON Adoption Has Hit an Inflection Point

Between 2020 and 2025, RON transaction volume grew at a compound annual rate of over 40 percent, according to data published by the Mortgage Industry Standards Maintenance Organization (MISMO). Virginia was the first state to authorize RON back in 2012, and for years adoption crawled. The pandemic changed that calculus overnight, and the momentum never reversed.

**The legal infrastructure is now nearly universal.** The Revised Uniform Law Commission's 2023 model act on electronic notarization has been adopted or introduced in 38 state legislatures to date. Even states historically resistant to electronic recording—looking at you, New York—have moved to permanent authorization after temporary pandemic measures proved workable.

What this means for brokers: your closings will increasingly happen without a physical signing appointment. Title companies like Qualia and Snapdocs are building end-to-end digital closing platforms that route documents, verify identity, and record in one workflow. The signing appointment itself is becoming a 15-minute video call instead of a 90-minute ordeal in a conference room.

## Speed Is the New Differentiator

Freddie Mac reported in early 2025 that the average time from contract to close dropped to 38 days for conventional loans—down from 43 days just two years prior. That compression is not accidental. Automated underwriting, digital verification of assets and employment, and e-closing technology are all contributing to a pipeline that moves faster with fewer human touchpoints.

For teams that still treat the contract-to-close period as a passive waiting game, this is a wake-up call. Faster closings mean less time to catch errors, less runway to resolve title issues, and tighter windows for client communication. The agents who thrive will be the ones whose [transaction coordination processes](/blog/transaction-coordination-best-practices) are already systematized—not the ones scrambling to adapt after a deal blows up at hour eleven.

## The "Agent-Optional" Question

Here is where the conversation gets uncomfortable. Several iBuyer and fintech-forward brokerages—Opendoor, Offerpad, and the relaunched Redfin Direct program—have tested transaction models where the buyer closes without a traditional agent. The [NAR 2025 Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers](https://www.nar.realtor/research-and-statistics/research-reports/highlights-from-the-profile-of-home-buyers-and-sellers) showed that 11 percent of buyers under age 35 reported completing their purchase without a buyer's agent, up from 7 percent in 2022.

**That 11 percent number should alarm you—not because of its size, but because of its trajectory.** Post-settlement reforms from the Burnett v. NAR case have decoupled buyer-agent compensation from MLS offers, and the cultural assumption that you need an agent to close is eroding, particularly among younger, digitally fluent buyers.

Does this mean agents are irrelevant? No. But it does mean the closing itself—once a stage where agents demonstrated clear value by quarterbacking paperwork, resolving last-minute issues, and calming nervous clients—is being automated in ways that reduce the visible need for a human intermediary.

## The Strongest Counterargument

Critics of the "agent-optional" thesis correctly point out that complex transactions—contingent sales, estate properties, multi-party negotiations, 1031 exchanges—still demand experienced professionals. They are right. A first-time buyer purchasing a straightforward condo through a digital platform is categorically different from a family selling a deceased parent's home with unclear title, deferred maintenance, and three siblings who disagree on price.

The mistake is thinking those complex transactions represent the majority. They do not. Roughly 60 percent of residential transactions are relatively clean: single-family, owner-occupied, conventional financing, clear title. That is the segment most vulnerable to disintermediation—and it is the segment where most agents earn most of their living.

The answer is not to pretend the threat does not exist. The answer is to move your value upstream—into pricing strategy, negotiation, market intelligence, and relationship management—while ensuring the transactional mechanics [run on autopilot](/blog/automating-real-estate-back-office) rather than consuming agent hours.

## What Title Companies Are Building

Pay attention to what the title industry is doing, because it signals where the closing experience is headed. Doma Holdings (formerly States Title) has deployed machine learning models that can underwrite title insurance in under a minute for qualifying properties. Their "instant close" product eliminates the traditional title search for properties with clean chain-of-title data.

Qualia's platform now integrates directly with lender LOS systems to auto-populate closing documents, reducing preparation time from hours to minutes. These are not pilot programs. They are production systems handling tens of thousands of transactions per month.

**For brokers, the implication is stark: the back-office cost structure of a closing is falling toward zero, and the companies capturing that margin are technology vendors, not real estate brokerages.** If your revenue model depends on charging fees for coordination that software now handles, your margin is on borrowed time.

## Where Agents Still Win

The closings where agents remain indispensable share common traits: emotional complexity, negotiation leverage, or regulatory ambiguity. A divorce sale with competing interests. A buyer competing in a multiple-offer situation who needs their agent to craft terms that win without overpaying. A new-construction closing where the builder's contract is 40 pages of one-sided language.

These scenarios require judgment, empathy, and pattern recognition built over hundreds of transactions. No platform replaces that. But agents who want to remain relevant in these high-value moments need to stop spending 60 percent of their time on low-value coordination tasks that machines handle better and faster.

## Repositioning for 2027 and Beyond

The brokerages that will dominate the next cycle are the ones building two capabilities simultaneously: a frictionless digital closing experience for clean transactions, and a high-touch advisory service for complex ones. Trying to deliver both with the same manual process is how you end up overworked on simple deals and underresourced on hard ones.

This is where [intelligent automation](/blog/ai-real-estate-operations) earns its keep—not by replacing agents, but by handling the repetitive document chasing, deadline tracking, and status communication that currently eats 10-15 hours per transaction. Tools like [brittani.ai](/pricing) exist precisely for this gap: absorbing the coordination burden so that agents can focus on the advisory work that no RON platform or iBuyer algorithm will replicate anytime soon.

The future of closings is faster, digital, and yes—for a growing share of transactions—agent-optional. Your job is not to fight that reality. Your job is to make sure your team is so good at the parts that cannot be automated that clients choose you anyway.
