## Michigan Building Code Updates 2026: The Regulatory Shift Agents Cannot Ignore

The Michigan building code updates 2026 represent the most significant revision to the state's residential construction standards in nearly a decade. If you hold an active license under the Michigan Department of Licensing and Regulatory Affairs (LARA), these changes directly affect how you list, disclose, and close transactions on properties built or renovated under previous code cycles. Ignoring them is not a strategy—it is a liability event waiting to happen.

Michigan's Bureau of Construction Codes, housed within LARA, formally adopted amendments to the Michigan Residential Code (Part 10 of the State Construction Code, Act 230 of 1972) effective January 2026. The update aligns Michigan with portions of the 2021 International Residential Code (IRC) and the 2021 International Energy Conservation Code (IECC), while retaining state-specific amendments found in the Michigan Administrative Code, Rules 408.30501 through 408.30599.

For agents, the practical impact lands in three places: disclosure obligations, inspection contingencies, and post-closing liability. Each of these deserves attention—and each carries real financial exposure if you get it wrong.

## What Actually Changed in the 2026 Code Cycle

**The 2026 amendments tighten requirements in energy efficiency, electrical safety, and structural egress—three categories that show up constantly in residential resale inspections.** Specifically, the energy provisions now require R-49 ceiling insulation in Climate Zones 5 and above (which covers most of Michigan's Lower Peninsula and all of the Upper Peninsula), up from R-38 in prior cycles.

Electrical panel requirements were updated to mandate arc-fault circuit interrupter (AFCI) protection in all habitable rooms, not just bedrooms. Egress window well dimensions for below-grade bedrooms now match the 2021 IRC minimum of 9 square feet of clear opening area. These are not cosmetic changes; they directly affect whether a property is "code-compliant" at the time of inspection.

| Code Area | Previous Standard (2015 IRC Basis) | 2026 Standard (2021 IRC Basis) | Impact on Resale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ceiling Insulation (Zone 5+) | R-38 | R-49 | Sellers may need to upgrade or disclose |
| AFCI Protection | Bedrooms only | All habitable rooms | Older panels flagged on inspections |
| Egress Window Wells | 5.7 sq ft clear opening | 9 sq ft clear opening | Below-grade bedrooms may lose conformance |
| Exterior Deck Ledger Bolts | 1/2-inch lag screws allowed | 1/2-inch through-bolts required | Deck safety concerns on pre-2026 builds |
| Radon Mitigation Rough-In | Encouraged, not required | Required in new construction | Disclosure relevance for new builds |

The radon provision is particularly notable. New construction permits issued after January 1, 2026 must include passive radon mitigation rough-in systems. This does not retroactively apply to existing homes, but it changes the disclosure conversation for agents selling newly built inventory.

## How the Updates Affect Michigan Seller Disclosures

Michigan's Seller Disclosure Act (MCL 565.951 through 565.966) requires sellers to complete the Seller's Disclosure Statement before a purchase agreement is signed. The statute mandates disclosure of "known material defects," and a building code violation qualifies as a material defect under case law established in Roberts v. Saffell (Michigan Court of Appeals, 2003).

**Agents representing sellers must understand that the 2026 code changes expand the universe of what counts as "non-conforming" on a home inspection.** A property that was code-compliant when built in 2018 may now be flagged for insufficient AFCI coverage or undersized egress windows. The seller is not required to upgrade the home to current code—Michigan does not impose retroactive compliance on existing structures—but the seller is required to disclose known conditions that a reasonable buyer would consider material.

The form itself (commonly the Michigan Association of Realtors Form SR-0, Seller's Disclosure Statement) includes sections on structural, mechanical, and electrical systems. When a pre-listing inspection reveals conditions that conflict with current code, agents must ensure the seller accurately completes these sections. Leaving a field blank when the agent has actual knowledge of the condition is a violation of Article 26 of Michigan's Occupational Code (MCL 339.2512).

## Michigan Building Code Updates 2026: Liability Exposure for Agents and Brokers

The consequences of non-compliance are not hypothetical. LARA's Bureau of Professional Licensing investigates complaints against licensees under MCL 339.2512, which defines unprofessional conduct to include "failing to disclose to a purchaser material facts known to the licensee." A substantiated complaint can result in a formal hearing, license suspension, probation, or fines up to $10,000 per violation.

Beyond regulatory sanctions, agents face civil liability. Michigan courts have held that buyers can sue listing agents for negligent misrepresentation when the agent knew or should have known about a material defect. The measure of damages typically includes the cost to cure the defect, diminution in property value, or in extreme cases, rescission of the sale.

**Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk.** Buyer agents who discover undisclosed code issues during the inspection period will exercise contingency rights under the purchase agreement (Michigan Association of Realtors Form PA-2 or similar). If the issue surfaces after closing, the buyer's attorney will look at the disclosure statement, the inspection report, and the listing agent's marketing materials. Any inconsistency becomes Exhibit A in a complaint to LARA or a civil action.

Errors and Omissions (E&O) insurance may cover some exposure, but most policies exclude claims arising from "known or intentional" failures to disclose. An agent who saw the inspection report and said nothing has no E&O safety net.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Code-Related Disclosures

Three to five specific errors recur in LARA disciplinary actions and civil claims related to building code issues. These are not edge cases—they happen in ordinary residential transactions across Michigan every month.

**First, agents confuse "grandfathered" with "no disclosure needed."** A home built in 2005 is not required to meet 2026 code standards, but that does not eliminate the disclosure obligation. If the property has a condition that a reasonable buyer would want to know about—such as an electrical panel without AFCI protection in the living room—the seller must disclose it once the condition is known. The grandfathering concept protects the owner from mandatory upgrades, not from disclosure.

Second, agents rely on the home inspector to "handle" code issues. The inspector's job is to report findings to the buyer. The listing agent's obligation is independent. If the listing agent obtains a pre-listing inspection and sees code-related findings, the agent cannot simply wait and hope the buyer's inspector misses them.

Third, agents list properties with unpermitted additions or renovations and fail to disclose the lack of permits. Under MCL 125.1510, construction work requiring a permit that was performed without one is a violation of the State Construction Code. This includes finished basements, added bathrooms, and deck attachments—common value-adds that sellers complete without pulling permits.

Fourth, agents misstate the year of construction or renovation on MLS entries, which creates an implied representation about which code cycle applies. If a listing says "updated kitchen 2024" but the electrical work was done without a permit in 2019, the agent has created a misleading record.

Fifth, agents fail to recommend that sellers consult the local enforcing agency when code questions arise. Michigan has approximately 200 local enforcing agencies. Each can adopt amendments stricter than the state minimum. An agent who advises based on state code alone may miss local ordinance requirements. The [Bureau of Construction Codes](https://www.michigan.gov/lara/bureau-list/bcc) maintains a directory of local agencies.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers bear supervisory liability under MCL 339.2512(j) for the acts of their licensees. When an agent in the brokerage fails to disclose a known code violation, the broker's license is exposed alongside the agent's. This is not optional oversight—it is statutory.

**Brokers should audit three areas immediately.** First, confirm that every listing file contains a completed Seller's Disclosure Statement (Form SR-0 or equivalent) signed before the purchase agreement was executed. Second, review pre-listing inspection reports in the file and verify that any code-related findings are reflected in the disclosure. Third, confirm that marketing materials (MLS remarks, flyers, social media posts) do not make claims inconsistent with the property's actual condition or permit history.

| Broker Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency | Risk if Missed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Seller Disclosure completeness | All sections filled, no blanks on known conditions | Every listing at intake | LARA complaint, civil liability |
| Pre-listing inspection reconciliation | Inspection findings match disclosure entries | Within 48 hours of inspection | Negligent misrepresentation claim |
| Permit verification on renovations | Local enforcing agency records match MLS claims | Before listing goes active | Fraud allegation, MLS fine |
| Agent training on 2026 code changes | Documented CE or internal training completion | Annually | Supervisory liability under MCL 339.2512(j) |
| Post-closing file retention | Files retained 3+ years per LARA rule | Ongoing | Inability to defend against late-filed complaints |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-audit disclosure accuracy are accepting risk they cannot control. A structured file review process—ideally at listing intake and again before the inspection contingency deadline expires—reduces exposure meaningfully. ## How Agents Should Prepare Their Practice Now

The practical steps are straightforward. First, obtain a copy of the 2026 Michigan Residential Code amendments from LARA's Bureau of Construction Codes. Read the summary of changes, which LARA publishes alongside each code cycle adoption. Second, attend a continuing education course specifically covering the 2026 code cycle. Multiple Michigan real estate schools offer 2-hour electives on construction code updates that count toward the 18-hour CE renewal requirement.

Third, update your pre-listing process. If you order pre-listing inspections (and you should), instruct the inspector to flag conditions that conflict with current code, even if the home is not required to conform. This gives the seller the opportunity to disclose proactively rather than react defensively during the buyer's inspection period.

Fourth, build a relationship with the local enforcing agency in your primary market area. Know their permit search process. Most Michigan municipalities now offer online permit search tools, but some still require in-person or phone requests. When in doubt about whether an addition or renovation was permitted, verify before the listing goes live.
