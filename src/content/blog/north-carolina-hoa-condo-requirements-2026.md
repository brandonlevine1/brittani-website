## Understanding North Carolina HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026

Agents working subdivisions and condominium sales across North Carolina face a disclosure framework that is deceptively complex. The North Carolina hoa & condo disclosure rules 2026 split authority between two separate statutes, impose different cancellation windows depending on property type, and place affirmative duties on listing agents that go beyond simply ordering paperwork. Missing a single deadline can hand buyers a cost-free exit or expose brokerages to liability that no errors-and-omissions policy will cover cheerfully.

North Carolina governs planned community disclosures under the Planned Community Act (N.C.G.S. Chapter 47F) and condominium disclosures under the Condominium Act (N.C.G.S. Chapter 47C). These are not interchangeable statutes, and confusing them is one of the most common compliance failures in the state. The North Carolina Real Estate Commission (NCREC) expects every licensee to know which statute applies to a given listing before the property hits the MLS.

## The Two Statutes That Control Everything

**N.C.G.S. 47F-3-102 applies to properties in homeowners associations that are not condominiums.** This includes single-family subdivisions, townhome communities with HOA governance, and any development organized under the Planned Community Act. The statute requires that a resale disclosure packet be delivered to the buyer before or at the time of contract execution, or within a reasonable time thereafter as provided in the contract.

N.C.G.S. 47C-4-109 governs condominium resales specifically. It mandates a resale certificate from the unit owners' association and grants buyers a seven-day cancellation right after receipt. The content requirements differ from the HOA packet—condo certificates must include the declaration, bylaws, rules, current budget, pending special assessments, and the association's financial statements.

| Feature | HOA (47F) Planned Community | Condo (47C) |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------|
| Governing Statute | N.C.G.S. 47F-3-102 | N.C.G.S. 47C-4-109 |
| Cancellation Period | Per contract terms (typically 5 days in NCAR 2A) | 7 calendar days from receipt |
| Required Documents | Resale disclosure packet, governing docs, budget | Resale certificate, declaration, bylaws, budget, financials |
| Who Prepares | HOA or its management company | Unit owners' association |
| Fee Authority | Reasonable fee per statute | Reasonable fee per statute |

Agents often assume a single disclosure process covers both property types. It does not. The listing agent must verify whether the property is governed by Chapter 47F, Chapter 47C, or both—yes, some communities have layered governance where a master HOA sits over individual condominium regimes.

## Forms and Contract Provisions Agents Must Use

The North Carolina Association of Realtors (NCAR) Standard Form 2A, the Offer to Purchase and Contract, contains specific provisions addressing HOA and condominium disclosures. Paragraph 8 of Form 2A addresses the property disclosure obligations, and the [addenda that attach](https://www.ncrec.gov/) to the offer handle association-specific terms.

**NCAR Form 2A1-T, the Additional Provisions Addendum, is frequently used to specify who orders and pays for the resale certificate or disclosure packet.** Agents should also be aware of NCAR Form 2A12-T, the Owners' Association Disclosure Addendum, which specifically addresses the buyer's rights regarding association documents and establishes delivery deadlines and cancellation mechanics within the contract framework.

When representing a buyer in a condominium transaction, agents must confirm that the contract incorporates the statutory seven-day cancellation right from 47C-4-109. A contract that attempts to waive this right is unenforceable on that point. The NCREC has issued guidance confirming that agents who draft contracts omitting mandatory statutory protections face disciplinary action.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Liability, Cancellation, and Discipline

A buyer who never receives the required resale certificate under 47C-4-109 retains the right to cancel indefinitely—the seven-day clock never starts. This means a deal that closed six months ago can theoretically be unwound if the buyer can demonstrate non-delivery. While courts have applied reasonableness standards, the legal exposure is real and documented in North Carolina appellate decisions.

For HOA properties under 47F, failure to deliver the disclosure packet triggers whatever remedies the contract provides. Under the standard NCAR 2A language, buyers typically receive a five-day review period, and non-delivery extends the due diligence window or grants termination rights. Listing agents who fail to initiate the disclosure packet order promptly are creating a ticking clock that can blow up [at the worst possible moment](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

| Violation | Potential Consequence |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Failure to deliver HOA packet | Buyer termination right, extended due diligence, earnest money refund |
| Failure to deliver condo resale certificate | Indefinite cancellation right for buyer |
| Misrepresenting HOA fees or assessments | NCREC disciplinary action, civil liability |
| Omitting 47C cancellation right from contract | NCREC license discipline, contract reformation |
| Late delivery without buyer acknowledgment | Closing delay, potential breach of contract claim |

The NCREC treats disclosure failures as violations of Commission Rule 21 NCAC 58A.0104, which requires licensees to discover and disclose material facts. An association's financial distress, pending litigation, or upcoming special assessment all qualify as material facts that agents cannot ignore even if the formal packet is "in process."

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With HOA and Condo Disclosures

**The first and most frequent error is ordering the wrong document type.** Agents request an "HOA disclosure" for a condominium property and receive a packet that does not satisfy the 47C-4-109 requirements. Management companies will often produce whatever is ordered without flagging the statutory mismatch. The agent bears responsibility for knowing which statute applies.

The second mistake involves timing assumptions. Many agents believe that ordering the packet at listing satisfies their obligation. It does not. The statute requires delivery to the buyer, and the buyer's review period does not begin until actual receipt. Ordering early is smart logistics, but delivery must be documented with a date-stamped acknowledgment from the buyer or buyer's agent.

Third, agents routinely fail to review the packet contents before forwarding to buyers. A disclosure packet missing the current year's budget or omitting pending special assessments is an incomplete delivery under the statute. Agents who blindly forward documents from management companies without verifying completeness [are making the same mistake](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) that derails transactions repeatedly.

Fourth, dual-governance properties—where a master community association and a sub-association both have authority—require separate disclosures from each entity. Agents in large developments like those in the Charlotte metro or Triangle area frequently miss the second layer of documents.

Fifth, agents sometimes allow the due diligence period to expire before the buyer receives and reviews the HOA or condo documents. Under NCAR Form 2A, once due diligence expires, the buyer's termination leverage changes dramatically. Proper sequencing requires that association documents arrive early enough in due diligence for meaningful review.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers-in-charge carry supervisory liability under N.C.G.S. 93A-6(b)(3) for the acts of their provisional and full brokers. A brokerage that lacks a system for tracking HOA and condo disclosure compliance is accepting risk that scales with every transaction.

**The first audit point is verifying that every listing file identifies whether the property falls under 47F, 47C, or both.** This determination should happen at listing intake, not after an offer arrives. Brokers should require agents to document the governance structure in the listing file before the property goes active in the MLS.

Second, brokers must confirm that delivery of association documents to buyers is tracked with date-stamped proof. An email timestamp, a signed receipt, or a transaction management system entry all work—but verbal confirmations do not. The [transaction handoff process](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) between agent and transaction coordinator must include a specific checkpoint for this item.

Third, brokers should audit whether agents are reviewing packet contents rather than merely forwarding them. A quarterly file review that checks for budget inclusion, assessment disclosure, and completeness of governing documents will catch gaps before they become claims.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Risk If Missed |
|-------------------|-----------|----------------|
| Property governance classification (47F vs. 47C) | Every listing | Wrong disclosure type delivered |
| Delivery date documentation | Every transaction | Cancellation clock dispute |
| Packet completeness verification | Every transaction | Incomplete delivery, extended buyer rights |
| Dual-governance identification | Every listing in master-planned communities | Missing second disclosure set |
| Due diligence timeline alignment | Every buyer transaction | Documents arrive after DD expiration |
