## Understanding Washington Earnest Money & Escrow Laws 2026

Handling earnest money incorrectly in Washington can end your deal, trigger a Department of Licensing investigation, or expose your brokerage to six-figure liability. The rules governing Washington earnest money & escrow laws 2026 are scattered across multiple statutes, administrative codes, and standardized forms—and "I didn't know" has never been accepted as a defense. This article breaks down the specific legal framework, form requirements, common agent errors, and broker-level audit obligations that licensed practitioners must follow right now.

Washington's escrow and trust account rules are rooted in the Real Estate Brokerage Relationships Act (RCW 18.86), the Escrow Agent Registration Act (RCW 18.44), and the administrative rules in WAC 308-124C. The Department of Licensing (DOL) enforces these provisions, and the consequences for violations range from fines to permanent license revocation. Agents who treat earnest money handling as an afterthought are playing a game with career-ending stakes.

## Statutory Framework: Where the Rules Live

**RCW 18.86.050 outlines a licensee's duty to account for all funds received on behalf of a principal.** This duty is non-delegable, meaning the designated broker carries ultimate responsibility even when a managing broker or agent physically deposits the check. The statute requires prompt delivery of all funds to the party entitled to receive them or to an escrow agent as agreed in writing.

RCW 18.44 governs licensed escrow agents separately from real estate licensees. If your transaction uses a title company or independent escrow company to hold funds, that entity must be registered under this chapter. Agents who casually accept cashier's checks and "hold them until closing" without an agreement specifying custody are violating both statutes simultaneously.

WAC 308-124C-125 provides the operational details for trust accounts. Brokerages that hold earnest money must maintain a pooled trust account at a Washington-state financial institution, keep detailed records reconciled monthly, and never commingle funds with operating accounts. Even temporary commingling—depositing a commission check and an earnest money check into the same account by accident—constitutes a violation.

## Forms That Control the Transaction

Washington agents working through the Northwest Multiple Listing Service (NWMLS) rely on a suite of standardized forms that dictate earnest money terms. The most critical ones are listed below.

| Form | Purpose | Earnest Money Relevance |
|------|---------|------------------------|
| Form 21 (Residential Purchase and Sale Agreement) | Primary offer contract | Sets deposit amount, holder, deposit deadline, and liquidated damages clause |
| Form 22A (Financing Addendum) | Defines loan contingency | Impacts conditions under which earnest money is refundable |
| Form 22D (Optional Clauses Addendum) | Modifies standard terms | Can alter deposit timelines or add forfeiture conditions |
| Form 35 (Earnest Money Release) | Mutual release of deposit | Required for any disbursement when parties dispute entitlement |
| Form 21A (Amendment to Purchase and Sale Agreement) | Modifies existing terms | Used to extend deposit deadlines or change holder |

Form 21, Section 2 specifies that earnest money must be delivered to the holder within two business days of mutual acceptance unless the parties agree otherwise. The holder—identified by name in the agreement—is responsible for depositing funds into a trust or escrow account within three business days of receipt. Missing either deadline is a material breach that can give the other party grounds to terminate.

Form 35 deserves special attention. When a transaction fails and the parties disagree about who gets the deposit, neither the escrow holder nor the brokerage can unilaterally disburse funds. Both buyer and seller must sign Form 35, or the holder must interplead the funds into court under [RCW 4.08.160](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=4.08.160). Agents who pressure a party to "just sign the release" are skating dangerously close to practicing law.

## Deposit Deadlines and Refund Triggers

**Timing errors are the single fastest way for an agent to blow up a transaction over earnest money.** Washington's framework is strict about when funds must move and under what circumstances they can be returned.

Refundability hinges on contingencies. Under Form 22A, if the buyer cannot obtain financing and provides timely written notice before the financing contingency deadline, the earnest money is refundable. If the buyer waives contingencies or fails to give notice by the contractual deadline, the seller may claim the deposit as liquidated damages under Form 21's default provision. There is no grace period built into the form language.

The inspection contingency under Form 35 (Inspection Addendum, not to be confused with Form 35 Earnest Money Release) creates another refund trigger. A buyer who disapproves of inspection results within the contingency window and provides proper notice is entitled to a full refund. Once that window closes without written disapproval, the buyer's right to recover the deposit on inspection grounds evaporates.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Earnest Money

Experienced agents still trip over these errors, and newer licensees fall into them constantly. Here are five specific failures that generate DOL complaints and E&O claims in Washington.

**First, holding the check instead of delivering it.** Some listing agents physically hold an earnest money check in their desk drawer "until inspections clear," believing they are protecting the seller. This violates WAC 308-124C-125 and the delivery timeline in Form 21. The check must go to the designated holder within two business days of mutual acceptance, period.

Second, failing to identify the holder clearly in the purchase agreement. Form 21 requires the holder's name. Writing "escrow" without specifying which company creates ambiguity that delays deposit and gives either party a potential out. If the holder field is incomplete or incorrect, the offer may not even constitute a binding contract under Washington law.

Third, disbursing earnest money without mutual written authorization. When a deal falls apart, the pressure to "just release the funds" is immense. But a brokerage that releases earnest money to one party without the other's written consent—or a court order—faces liability to the non-consenting party for the full amount plus potential DOL sanctions.

Fourth, accepting personal checks without disclosing risk. While Form 21 does not prohibit personal checks, agents who fail to explain that a personal check can bounce—leaving no funds on deposit—expose their clients to a false sense of security. Best practice is to require cashier's checks or wired funds for any earnest money exceeding a nominal amount.

Fifth, miscalculating business days. The Form 21 deadline runs in "business days," which excludes Saturdays, Sundays, and legal holidays under NWMLS definitions. Agents who count calendar days instead of business days routinely miss deadlines, creating breach situations that did not need to exist. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Designated and managing brokers carry direct liability for trust account compliance under RCW 18.85.361. The DOL does not accept "my agent handled it" as an excuse during an audit or investigation. Brokers need systematic oversight, not occasional spot-checks.

| Audit Area | Frequency | What to Verify |
|------------|-----------|----------------|
| Trust account reconciliation | Monthly | Bank balance matches ledger; no negative balances on individual accounts |
| Deposit timeliness | Per transaction | Funds deposited within three business days of receipt by holder |
| Form 35 documentation | Per failed transaction | Mutual signatures before any disbursement; interpleader filed if disputed |
| Agent delivery compliance | Per transaction | Earnest money delivered to holder within two business days of mutual acceptance |
| Commingling checks | Monthly | No operating funds in trust account; no trust funds in operating account |

**Brokers who do not reconcile monthly are already in violation of WAC 308-124C-130.** The rule requires that reconciliation occur no later than the last day of the month following the month in which the funds were deposited. Documentation of each reconciliation must be retained for at least three years.

Beyond trust accounts, brokers should audit their agents' use of Form 21 to ensure the earnest money holder field is completed correctly and that amendments modifying deposit terms use Form 21A rather than informal email agreements. Informal modifications that lack proper form documentation create enforceability problems that can expose the brokerage to claims from both parties. Establishing a [transaction coordinator role](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) is one way to build consistent oversight into every file.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

The DOL's enforcement powers under RCW 18.85.361 include license suspension, license revocation, and civil fines up to $5,000 per violation. A single trust account violation can trigger all three simultaneously if the DOL determines the violation was willful or part of a pattern.

Beyond regulatory penalties, agents and brokers face civil liability. A buyer whose earnest money is improperly disbursed can sue the brokerage for conversion—an intentional tort that may not be covered by standard E&O insurance. Sellers who lose a deal because an agent failed to timely deposit funds can claim damages for the difference between the failed contract price and the eventual sale price.

Criminal exposure also exists in extreme cases. Misappropriation of trust funds constitutes theft under RCW 9A.56, and prosecutors have charged agents who "borrowed" from trust accounts with felony theft. Even short-term use of earnest money for personal expenses—replaced before anyone notices—meets the statutory definition of theft if done without authorization.

## Protecting Yourself in Every Transaction

The simplest protection is procedural discipline. Deliver funds on day one, deposit them by day three, document everything in writing, and never disburse without Form 35 signatures or a court order.
