## Ohio Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026: The Ground Has Shifted

The Ohio commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 have rewritten how agents earn, disclose, and negotiate their pay on every single transaction. If you hold an active license governed by Ohio Revised Code Chapter 4735, the post-settlement era demands changes to your paperwork, your client conversations, and your brokerage's compliance infrastructure. Ignoring these requirements does not just risk a slap on the wrist — it risks license discipline, deal cancellations, and civil liability.

Ohio's real estate market processed over 150,000 residential transactions in 2025. Every one of those deals in 2026 now flows through a fundamentally different compensation framework. The old shorthand of "seller pays both sides" as a default MLS offering is gone, replaced by mandatory written agreements, transparent fee disclosures, and buyer-side compensation that must be negotiated deal by deal.

## How the Settlement Changed Commission Mechanics in Ohio

The NAR settlement, finalized in late 2024, eliminated blanket offers of buyer-agent compensation through any NAR-affiliated MLS. Ohio's major MLS systems — including MLS Now (formerly NORMLS and CABOR MLS), the Dayton REALTORS MLS, and the Cincinnati Area Board of REALTORS MLS — all adopted compliant rules by August 2024. The compensation field that once displayed a percentage or flat fee to buyer agents is now either removed or restricted to a zero-value placeholder.

**What replaced it is a system built entirely on written, pre-showing agreements between buyer agents and their clients.** The Ohio Association of REALTORS updated its standard forms library to reflect these changes. Ohio REALTORS Form 101 (Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement) and Form 102 (Non-Exclusive Buyer Agency Agreement) now require a specific, disclosed compensation amount — stated as either a flat dollar figure or a percentage — that cannot be open-ended or contingent on what the seller may offer.

This means the buyer's agent fee is locked in before a showing occurs. If a seller offers compensation that exceeds the buyer-agent's agreed fee, the excess cannot be pocketed without additional written authorization. If the seller offers less or nothing, the buyer is contractually responsible for the gap.

## Required Forms and Documentation for Ohio Agents

Ohio agents must now maintain a paper trail that would have seemed excessive five years ago. The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, operating under the Ohio Department of Commerce, expects licensees to have these documents executed and retained:

| Form / Document | When Required | Key Compliance Element |
|---|---|---|
| Ohio REALTORS Form 101 (Exclusive Buyer Agency) | Before first showing | Must state specific compensation amount |
| Ohio REALTORS Form 102 (Non-Exclusive Buyer Agency) | Before first showing | Same compensation disclosure requirement |
| Ohio REALTORS Form 200 (Residential Purchase Agreement) | At offer submission | Compensation terms cannot conflict with buyer agreement |
| Agency Disclosure Statement (ORC 4735.56) | At first substantive contact | Must be provided before discussing confidential info |
| Commission Disbursement Authorization | At closing | Must match signed buyer agreement terms |

The Agency Disclosure Statement required under ORC Section 4735.56 remains mandatory and unchanged, but its timing matters more now. Agents who skip the disclosure and jump straight to a buyer agreement risk the agreement being voidable. The Ohio Division has issued [guidance through its licensing FAQ](https://com.ohio.gov/divisions-and-programs/real-estate-and-professional-licensing) confirming that agency disclosure must precede any compensation discussion.

Form 200, Ohio's standard purchase contract, has been updated to include a section where buyer-agent compensation is addressed in the offer itself. This creates a negotiation point — sellers can accept, counter, or reject the request for compensation contribution. The field must align with what was agreed upon in the buyer representation agreement, or supplemental documentation explaining the variance must be signed.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

**Non-compliance carries consequences that range from inconvenient to career-ending.** The Ohio Division of Real Estate can impose formal disciplinary action under ORC 4735.18, which lists grounds for license suspension or revocation. Failing to disclose material facts about compensation, operating without a required written agreement, or misrepresenting fee structures all fall under this statute.

MLS-level violations carry their own penalties. MLS Now, for example, can fine participating agents up to $5,000 per violation for inputting compensation data into restricted fields. Repeated violations trigger suspension from MLS access — which, for most agents, is functionally equivalent to being unable to work.

On the civil side, a buyer who was never presented with a compliant buyer agreement could argue in court that any compensation paid was unauthorized. This opens the agent and brokerage to disgorgement claims — being forced to return the commission. Title companies and closing attorneys in Ohio are now trained to flag disbursements that lack supporting buyer representation documentation, which means deals can stall at the closing table over missing paperwork.

Deal cancellation is also a live risk. If a seller's attorney discovers that the buyer's agent compensation request in the purchase agreement conflicts with what the buyer actually signed, the seller can use that inconsistency to challenge the contract's validity. This has already happened in at least two reported arbitration cases in Cuyahoga County in early 2026.

## Common Mistakes Ohio Agents Are Making Right Now

After eighteen months under the new rules, patterns of non-compliance have emerged. These are the errors that Ohio brokers and the Division are flagging most frequently.

**First: using vague language in buyer agreements.** Writing "compensation to be determined" or "up to X%" does not satisfy the settlement's specificity requirement. The amount must be concrete. An agent who writes a range rather than a fixed number is exposing both themselves and their client to disputes at closing.

Second: obtaining the buyer agreement after the showing has already occurred. The rule is not "before an offer" — it is before touring any property. Agents who show a home first and then ask for a signature are out of compliance from the moment they unlock the lockbox. The MLS participation agreements in Ohio explicitly require the agreement to predate property access.

Third: failing to reconcile the buyer agreement with the purchase contract. If Form 101 says the buyer owes 2.5% and Form 200 requests the seller contribute 3%, that mismatch creates a compliance problem. The buyer cannot be asked to authorize a higher amount flowing to the agent without a formal amendment to the buyer agreement.

Fourth: not explaining to buyers what happens when a seller refuses to contribute. Many agents gloss over this scenario during the signing conversation. When the buyer later discovers they owe the full commission out of pocket, the resulting disputes often escalate to formal complaints with the Division. Documenting the explanation — even with a simple initialed acknowledgment — protects the agent.

Fifth: continuing to discuss compensation amounts in MLS agent-only remarks. Several Ohio MLS systems have flagged listings where agents wrote things like "seller willing to offer 2.5% to buyer's agent" in private remarks fields. This is treated the same as putting it in the compensation field. The information must be communicated through separate channels — direct agent-to-agent contact, listing broker websites, or written seller instructions provided upon request.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under ORC 4735.18(A)(6) for the actions of their agents. A broker who fails to implement compliance systems is not merely negligent — they are potentially subject to the same discipline as the offending agent. The Ohio Division has made clear that "I didn't know my agent was doing that" is not a defense.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | What to Check |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer representation agreements on file | Every transaction | Signed before first showing, specific dollar/percentage amount stated |
| Form 200 compensation section | Every transaction | Must align with buyer agreement or include signed amendment |
| MLS listing data | Weekly | No compensation language in any field or remarks |
| Agency disclosure timing | Every new client | ORC 4735.56 disclosure must predate buyer agreement |
| Closing disbursement records | Monthly | Commission paid matches signed agreements exactly |

Brokers should also audit their agents' marketing materials and social media. Any public-facing content that implies a fixed commission structure — like "our buyers pay nothing" — is misleading under the new rules and can trigger a Division investigation. The post-settlement reality is that buyers may or may not pay directly depending on negotiation outcomes, and agents cannot guarantee otherwise.

Training frequency matters too. The [best systems for onboarding agents to new compliance timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) involve documented quarterly reviews, not annual seminars. Ohio's continuing education requirements under ORC 4735.141 mandate 30 hours per three-year cycle, and brokerages that incorporate settlement-specific training into that requirement are better positioned to demonstrate good-faith compliance efforts.
