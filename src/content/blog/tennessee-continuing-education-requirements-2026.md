## Understanding Tennessee Continuing Education Requirements 2026

Every licensed real estate professional in Tennessee operates under the authority of the Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC), housed within the Department of Commerce and Insurance. The Tennessee continuing education requirements 2026 cycle carries the same 16-hour mandate that has governed licensees for years — but the specific courses, deadlines, and enforcement actions demand close attention. Failing to meet these requirements does not merely create an administrative headache; it exposes agents, brokers, and their clients to real financial and legal risk.

Tennessee's licensing framework is codified primarily in the Tennessee Real Estate Broker License Act of 1973, found at [T.C.A. Title 62, Chapter 13](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regboards/trec.html). The statute grants TREC broad authority to set CE standards, approve course providers, and discipline licensees who fall short. For the 2025-2026 renewal cycle, the commission has maintained its historical structure: 8 hours of mandatory TREC Core content plus 8 hours of elective coursework from approved providers.

The renewal deadline depends on your license expiration date, which TREC staggers throughout the year. Your specific date is printed on your license and available through your online account on the Tennessee CORE system. Missing it triggers automatic status changes that can halt your business overnight.

## The 16-Hour Breakdown: Mandatory vs. Elective

**Not all 16 hours carry equal weight in the eyes of the commission.** The 8-hour TREC Core course is non-negotiable — no substitution, no waiver, no exception. This course is developed or approved directly by TREC staff and covers legislative changes from the most recent General Assembly session, rule amendments, disciplinary case studies, and emerging compliance issues specific to Tennessee practice.

The remaining 8 elective hours give agents more flexibility. Courses must be offered by a TREC-approved provider and can cover topics ranging from contracts and ethics to fair housing and risk management. However, agents cannot apply the same elective course twice within the same cycle, and courses taken before the cycle start date do not carry forward.

| Requirement | Hours | Provider | Substitution Allowed? |
|---|---|---|---|
| TREC Core Course | 8 | TREC-approved only | No |
| Elective Courses | 8 | Any TREC-approved provider | Yes, within approved catalog |
| Total Per Cycle | 16 | — | — |

One detail that trips up agents who hold licenses in multiple states: Tennessee does not accept CE credits from other jurisdictions unless the specific course has been independently approved by TREC. If you also hold a license in a state with reciprocity provisions, you still must complete Tennessee-specific coursework. ## Forms and Filings That Tie Into CE Compliance

Tennessee agents interact with several TREC forms during the renewal process. The most relevant is the license renewal application submitted through the online CORE (Comprehensive Online Regulatory and Enforcement) system. There is no separate paper CE completion certificate you mail to the commission — approved providers report completion electronically, and TREC's system updates your record accordingly.

However, agents should retain personal copies of their course completion certificates for a minimum of four years. If a provider fails to report properly, or if TREC's records show a discrepancy, the burden falls on the licensee to prove compliance. Form TR-1, the initial license application, is not involved at renewal, but agents reactivating from retired or expired status will encounter Form TR-3 (Application for Reactivation), which requires proof of all delinquent CE plus current cycle completion.

**Brokers filing annual firm renewal documents must also confirm that all affiliate brokers under their supervision hold active, CE-compliant licenses.** This is not a mere checkbox exercise. TREC has historically initiated investigations when a firm renewal triggers a mismatch between reported agents and their license statuses.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of missed CE in Tennessee escalate quickly. Under T.C.A. 62-13-312, TREC can impose civil penalties of up to $1,000 per violation. Practicing on an expired or retired license constitutes unlicensed activity — a Class B misdemeanor under Tennessee law that carries additional fines and potential criminal liability.

From a transactional standpoint, the fallout is equally severe. A deal closed by an agent whose license was lapsed at the time of execution creates a cloud over the commission payment. Brokers may be forced to return commissions, and the transaction itself could face legal challenge from a buyer or seller who later discovers their agent was not properly licensed. Title insurance underwriters have flagged these situations as grounds for claim denial.

TREC publishes disciplinary actions in its quarterly newsletter and online database. A CE-related violation becomes part of your permanent licensing record, visible to future brokers, clients, and cooperating agents. The reputational damage alone — appearing on a public enforcement list — can cost an agent referrals for years.

> "A licensee who engages in the business of real estate while their license is expired or retired is subject to disciplinary action including revocation, suspension, or civil penalty." — T.C.A. 62-13-312(b)(1)

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Tennessee CE

Five errors surface repeatedly in TREC enforcement actions and broker audit findings. Each one is avoidable, and each one has ended careers or cost agents thousands in penalties.

**First, agents confuse "enrolled" with "completed."** Signing up for a course on the last day before your deadline does not satisfy the requirement. TREC's system only recognizes completed courses with a final exam score meeting the provider's passing threshold. If your expiration date is March 31 and you finish the course on April 1, your license has already lapsed.

Second, agents take courses from non-approved providers. National real estate education platforms sometimes offer CE that is approved in other states but not in Tennessee. Always verify the provider's TREC approval number before enrolling. The commission maintains a searchable list of approved courses and providers on its website.

Third, some agents complete the elective hours but skip TREC Core, assuming any 16 hours of approved content will suffice. This results in an incomplete renewal and an automatic license status change. The Core course is tracked separately in TREC's system and cannot be replaced by additional electives.

Fourth, agents who let their license expire and then attempt to "backfill" CE assume they can simply pay a late fee and resume practice. Tennessee requires completion of all CE that would have been required during the lapsed period, plus the current cycle's hours, plus reactivation fees. For an agent who let two cycles pass, this means up to 32 hours of catch-up coursework.

Fifth, agents rely solely on their broker's reminder system rather than tracking their own deadline. This is a personal licensing obligation. Your broker's failure to remind you is not a defense before the commission. ## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Principal brokers in Tennessee carry supervisory liability that extends to their agents' licensing compliance. Under T.C.A. 62-13-312 and TREC Rule 1260-02-.12, the principal broker must ensure that no affiliate broker conducts real estate activity under the firm's name without a current, active license. This is not optional oversight — it is a statutory duty.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Source Document | Risk if Missed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Agent license expiration date | Quarterly minimum | CORE system lookup | Firm disciplinary action |
| TREC Core completion status | Before renewal deadline | Provider completion report | Agent license lapse |
| Elective hour total | Before renewal deadline | Agent's CE transcript | Incomplete renewal |
| Firm renewal agent roster accuracy | Annual | Firm renewal application | TREC investigation trigger |
| New agent CE timeline (first renewal) | 90 days post-licensure | Initial license date | Shortened first cycle miss |

**Brokers who discover an agent practicing on a lapsed license must immediately suspend that agent's activity and report the situation to TREC.** Failing to act once aware compounds the broker's own liability. TREC has sanctioned principal brokers with fines and temporary suspensions specifically for allowing known-expired agents to continue writing contracts.

A practical approach: run a quarterly audit of every affiliate broker's license status through the CORE system. Cross-reference their reported CE completion against the hours required for their specific renewal date. For firms with more than ten agents, this becomes a meaningful administrative burden — one reason many brokerages assign this task to their transaction coordinator or compliance officer. ## New Agent Requirements: The Shortened First Cycle

Agents licensed for the first time in Tennessee face a compressed CE timeline that catches many off guard. If you received your license fewer than 24 months before your first renewal date, you still owe the full 16 hours — including TREC Core. There is no prorated reduction for a shortened first cycle.

This means an agent licensed in January 2026 with a renewal date of December 2026 has roughly 11 months to complete 16 hours of CE. TREC does not grant extensions for new licensees, and the "I didn't know" defense has been explicitly rejected in published disciplinary decisions. Your sponsoring broker should communicate this timeline during onboarding, but again, the obligation rests with the individual licensee.

The first renewal is also where many agents make their initial administrative errors — wrong address on file, failure to update name changes through the proper TREC form, or using a personal email that no longer receives commission correspondence. Keep your CORE account information current. TREC sends renewal reminders electronically, but they go to the email address on file — nowhere else.

## Planning Ahead: How to Stay Compliant Without the Last-Minute Scramble

The most reliable agents treat CE completion like a closing deadline — non-negotiable and calendared well in advance. Completing your TREC Core course within the first quarter of your renewal cycle eliminates the single biggest compliance risk. Spreading elective hours across the remaining months prevents the compressed scheduling that leads to missed deadlines.

Tennessee's approved provider list includes both in-person and online options. Online courses offer more scheduling flexibility, but verify that the provider's platform reports completion to TREC electronically. Some smaller providers still require manual reporting, which can delay your transcript update by weeks — dangerously close to a deadline if you are finishing last-minute.
