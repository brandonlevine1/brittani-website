## Montana Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026: The Federal Mandate That Catches State Agents Off Guard

Montana does not have a standalone state statute governing lead-based paint disclosures in residential real estate transactions. That single fact trips up more agents than almost any other compliance issue in the state. Instead, **Montana lead paint disclosure requirements 2026** flow entirely from federal law—specifically the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, 42 U.S.C. 4852d) and its implementing regulations at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart F.

Because Montana defers to federal authority rather than layering its own rules on top, agents sometimes assume the obligation is less serious. The opposite is true. The enforcement teeth come from HUD and the EPA, both of which have increased per-violation civil penalties in recent years. And the Montana Board of Realty Regulation can pursue license discipline under Montana Code Annotated 37-51-321 for conduct that violates applicable law—including federal disclosure requirements—during a real estate transaction.

## Which Properties Trigger the Disclosure Obligation

The rule is binary: any "target housing" built before 1978 requires the lead paint disclosure before the buyer becomes obligated under a contract. Target housing means any residential dwelling except housing for the elderly or persons with disabilities (unless a child under age six resides there) and zero-bedroom units like studios or dormitories.

Montana's housing stock makes this relevant to a significant share of listings. According to U.S. Census data, roughly 28 percent of Montana's occupied housing units were built before 1978. In cities like Butte, Helena, Great Falls, and Missoula, that percentage climbs much higher due to their mining-era and early-twentieth-century architectural history.

The exemption list is short: sales of target housing at foreclosure, lease renewals where the disclosure was previously provided, and properties that have been certified lead-free by an accredited inspector. If none of those apply and the home predates 1978, the disclosure must happen.

## The Specific Forms Montana Agents Should Use

**The Montana Association of Realtors (MAR) provides Form 37-LBP, which serves as the state-specific version of the federally mandated lead paint disclosure.** This form tracks the language required by 24 CFR 35.92 and includes signature lines for the seller, buyer, and their respective agents. Some brokerages instead attach the standalone EPA form titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" directly to the purchase agreement.

Either approach satisfies federal law, but the form must contain three elements: the seller's disclosure of any known lead-based paint or hazards, a statement confirming the buyer received the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home," and confirmation that the buyer received a 10-day opportunity to conduct an independent lead-based paint inspection.

| Form Component | What It Covers | Who Signs |
|---|---|---|
| Seller disclosure section | Known lead paint, reports, or test results | Seller |
| Buyer acknowledgment section | Receipt of EPA pamphlet and inspection opportunity | Buyer |
| Agent certification | Confirmation that agent informed seller of obligations and ensured delivery | Listing agent and buyer's agent |
| 10-day inspection waiver (optional) | Buyer's election to waive or shorten the inspection period | Buyer |

Both agents—listing and buyer's—must sign the form. This is not a technicality. It reflects the independent duty placed on each licensee by 24 CFR 35.92(c).

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: What Agents Actually Risk

The consequences for violating lead paint disclosure requirements divide into three categories: federal civil penalties, private litigation exposure, and state license discipline.

HUD and the EPA can impose civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation as of the most recent inflation adjustment under the Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act. These penalties apply to sellers, landlords, and their agents independently. A single transaction with a missing disclosure form can generate multiple violations—one for failure to disclose known hazards, another for failure to provide the pamphlet, and a third for failure to offer the inspection period.

In private litigation, buyers can pursue treble damages under 42 U.S.C. 4852d(b)(3). That means if a buyer suffers $40,000 in lead remediation costs and can prove the disclosure was never provided, the court can award $120,000 plus attorney fees. Montana district courts have subject matter jurisdiction over these federal claims, and the three-year statute of limitations runs from the date the buyer discovers the violation.

On the state level, the Montana Board of Realty Regulation can initiate disciplinary proceedings under MCA 37-51-321(1)(a), which authorizes action for "any conduct that constitutes dishonest or fraudulent dealing." Failure to provide a required federal disclosure fits squarely within that language. Sanctions range from formal reprimand to license revocation.

## Common Mistakes Montana Agents Make With Lead Paint Disclosures

**Even experienced agents in Montana fall into patterns that create liability.** The following five errors appear repeatedly in Board complaints and litigation files.

First, agents assume the disclosure is only needed when the seller knows lead paint exists. Wrong. The obligation to disclose is triggered by the property's age, not the seller's knowledge. Even if the seller has zero awareness of any lead paint, the form must be completed with the seller checking the box indicating no knowledge—and the buyer must still receive the pamphlet and the inspection opportunity.

Second, agents deliver the EPA pamphlet but forget to document it. The form requires a buyer signature confirming receipt. Handing someone a pamphlet at a showing without getting a signature creates no proof of compliance. If a dispute arises two years later, the agent has nothing to show.

Third, listing agents fail to provide the disclosure before the buyer becomes obligated. The regulatory language in 24 CFR 35.86 is specific: disclosure must occur "before the purchaser is obligated under any contract to purchase the housing." In Montana, that typically means before the buyer signs the purchase agreement or, at latest, as part of the signed offer. Delivering the form at closing is too late and constitutes a violation even if no harm results.

Fourth, agents confuse the 10-day inspection opportunity with the standard home inspection contingency. These are separate rights. The buyer can waive the lead inspection entirely, but the waiver must be documented on the disclosure form. Relying on a general inspection contingency does not satisfy the federal requirement to affirmatively offer the lead-specific opportunity.

Fifth, dual agents assume one signature line covers both roles. It does not. When an agent represents both parties, that agent must sign in both the listing-agent and buyer-agent certification sections to confirm independent compliance duties were met for each party.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability under both [Montana agency law](/blog/south-dakota-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) and federal regulation. A broker who fails to implement systems for lead paint compliance is exposed to the same penalties as the individual agent.

**Every designated broker in Montana should build a compliance checkpoint that fires before any pre-1978 listing goes active on MLS.** This checkpoint should confirm three things: the seller has completed the disclosure form, the listing file contains a copy of the EPA pamphlet designated for buyer delivery, and the listing agent has documented the property's construction year from county records—not just the seller's verbal representation.

| Broker Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure form completed and signed by seller | Before listing activation | Missing seller signature or unchecked boxes |
| EPA pamphlet in file | Before listing activation | No pamphlet copy; reliance on buyer's agent to supply |
| Buyer signature on disclosure | Before contract ratification | Form unsigned or dated after contract execution |
| 10-day inspection documentation | At contract ratification | No waiver signed and no inspection period noted in timeline |
| Both agent signatures present | Before closing | One agent line blank, especially in dual agency |

Brokers should also periodically audit closed files. A random quarterly review of five to ten transactions involving pre-1978 homes provides early detection of systemic gaps. The Montana Board has noted in past enforcement actions that brokers who demonstrate active supervision receive more favorable outcomes in disciplinary proceedings.

## The EPA Pamphlet Requirement Is Not Optional

Some agents treat the pamphlet as a nice-to-have supplement rather than a regulatory requirement with its own penalty exposure. Under 24 CFR 35.88(a), the seller or seller's agent must provide the buyer with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" before the buyer becomes obligated under the contract.

The pamphlet was last revised by the EPA in 2021 and remains current as of 2026. Agents should verify they are distributing the most recent version. Outdated pamphlets—particularly pre-2010 versions that lack current renovation safety information—do not technically satisfy the regulation's requirement to provide "the pamphlet developed under section 406 of TSCA."

> "Each seller of a residential property that is target housing shall provide the purchaser with the lead hazard information pamphlet." — 24 CFR 35.88(a)

The [EPA's lead paint program page](https://www.epa.gov/lead/protect-your-family-lead-your-home-real-estate-disclosure) hosts the current downloadable version. Brokers should keep printed copies in their offices and embed the PDF link in listing-side transaction checklists.

## Montana-Specific Considerations That Affect Compliance

While the disclosure obligation is federal, Montana's real estate practice environment creates unique compliance dynamics. The state's reliance on the Montana Realtors Form Library means that many agents encounter the lead paint disclosure as Form 37-LBP integrated into their standard transaction packet. Agents who use non-MAR forms or work with FSBO sellers adapting their own paperwork must ensure the federal minimum content requirements are met regardless of format.

Montana's rural markets also present challenges. Properties in counties with limited building-permit records may lack clear documentation of construction year. In those situations, agents should err on the side of providing the disclosure. There is no penalty for providing a lead paint disclosure on a post-1978 home, but there are severe penalties for failing to provide one on a pre-1978 property.

The Montana Department of Environmental Quality (DEQ) administers the state's lead-based paint activities program under the federal authorization granted by EPA Region 8. While DEQ's primary role involves certification of lead inspectors, risk assessors, and abatement contractors, agents should know that any lead inspection report generated during the buyer's 10-day window will come from a DEQ-certified professional. Referring buyers to DEQ's list of certified inspectors demonstrates competence and [helps agents avoid the kind of timeline failures](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) that can torpedo deals.

## How This Fits Into the Broader Transaction Workflow

Lead paint disclosure is not an isolated compliance task—it connects to your listing intake process, your buyer consultation, and your contract-to-close timeline. Treating it as a standalone checkbox rather than an integrated workflow step is where most errors originate.

At listing intake, the agent should verify the property's construction year through Montana Cadastral or county GIS records and immediately flag any pre-1978 property for lead disclosure. At buyer consultation, the buyer's agent should explain the 10-day inspection right before offers are submitted so buyers can make informed decisions about waivers. At contract execution, both agents should confirm signatures are present on the disclosure form before submitting to their respective brokers.
