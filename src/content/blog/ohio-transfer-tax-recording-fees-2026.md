## Understanding Ohio Transfer Tax & Recording Fee Schedules 2026

Getting the numbers wrong on a settlement statement is one of the fastest ways to delay a closing or lose a client's trust. Ohio transfer tax & recording fee schedules 2026 carry specific statutory requirements that differ from county to county, and agents who estimate instead of verify are gambling with their deals. This guide breaks down the current fee structure, the forms you must file, and the penalties you face when something goes sideways.

Ohio's conveyance fee framework is governed primarily by Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Sections 319.54 and 322.01 through 322.02. The Ohio Department of Taxation and each county auditor's office administer these fees at the local level. The county recorder's office handles the actual recording of instruments and sets its own schedule for page fees, indexing, and other charges under ORC 317.32.

Real estate agents do not physically pay these fees in most transactions—that falls to the closing attorney or title company. But agents are responsible for accurately disclosing estimated costs to buyers and sellers, and for flagging exemptions or local surcharges that affect the bottom line on net sheets and purchase agreements.

## The Two-Layer Fee Structure: State and County

**Ohio operates a two-tier conveyance fee system that catches agents off guard when they move between counties.** The mandatory state conveyance fee is fixed at $1.00 per $1,000 of the sale price (or fraction thereof) under ORC 322.02. On top of that, counties may impose a permissive real property transfer tax of up to $3.00 per $1,000 under ORC 322.02(C).

Not every county charges the full permissive amount. Some levy $1.00 per $1,000, others charge $2.00, and many max out at $3.00. The result is a combined conveyance fee ranging from $1.00 to $4.00 per $1,000 depending on where the property sits. Agents working border counties—think Butler and Warren, or Lorain and Cuyahoga—must verify each county's current rate rather than assuming uniformity.

The fee is calculated on the full consideration, including assumed mortgage balances. Agents who forget to include assumed debt in the calculation will understate the fee on the settlement statement, which the county auditor will catch and reject.

## Recording Fees Under ORC 317.32

Recording fees are separate from conveyance fees and are paid directly to the county recorder when the deed, mortgage, or other instrument is filed. Under ORC 317.32, recorders charge a base fee per document plus additional charges per page. Most counties have adopted a schedule that includes $28 for the first two pages and $8 for each additional page, though this can vary slightly by county based on local fiscal needs and prior legislative updates.

Additional instruments recorded at the same closing—mortgage documents, affidavits of title, power of attorney filings—each carry their own fee. A standard residential transaction involving a warranty deed, a new mortgage, and a release of the seller's old mortgage can easily generate $150 to $250 in recording costs before you factor in the conveyance fee.

**Agents must distinguish between recording fees (paid to the recorder) and conveyance fees (paid to the auditor), because they appear as separate line items on the Closing Disclosure.** Combining them into a single estimate creates confusion and can trigger RESPA tolerance violations if the lender's Loan Estimate was based on incorrect breakdowns.

## Key Forms and Filing Requirements

Every conveyance in Ohio requires a completed [DTE 100 form](https://tax.ohio.gov/real-property/forms) (Statement of Conveyance of Homestead Property) or DTE 100(EX) (Statement of Reason for Exemption from Real Property Conveyance Fee). These forms are submitted to the county auditor before the deed can be recorded. Without an accepted DTE 100 or DTE 100(EX), the recorder will refuse the instrument.

The DTE 100 requires the full sale price, the conveyance fee calculation, and the signatures of both the grantor and grantee (or their authorized agents). The form also asks for the property's parcel number, school district, and tax mailing address. Errors on any of these fields—particularly a mismatched parcel number—will bounce the filing back.

For exempt transfers (such as transfers between spouses, transfers to or from government entities, or transfers pursuant to court order), Form DTE 100(EX) must list the specific exemption code from ORC 322.02(D). There are over 20 enumerated exemptions, and agents involved in transactions like estate transfers or LLC restructures need to confirm which code applies before closing day. If you are handling transactions that cross multiple compliance areas, a [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) can prevent exemption codes from falling through the cracks.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Ohio Transfer Taxes and Recording Fees

**Mistake one: using a flat estimate across counties.** An agent who closes primarily in Franklin County may quote $4.00 per $1,000 on a deal in Licking County, where the permissive fee is lower. The reverse is worse—underestimating the fee forces the seller to bring additional funds to closing or delays the transaction until the shortage is resolved.

Mistake two: forgetting that the conveyance fee applies to the total consideration, not just the cash portion. When a buyer assumes an existing mortgage or pays part of the price by taking property "subject to" a lien, that assumed balance counts toward the fee calculation under ORC 319.54(G)(3). Agents who net out the mortgage balance from their fee estimate will come up short at the auditor's window.

Mistake three: failing to identify exempt transactions early in the process. Family transfers, divorce decrees, and trust-to-beneficiary conveyances often qualify for full exemption from the conveyance fee. If the agent does not flag the exemption during contract preparation, the title company may calculate fees on the full value, surprising the client on the settlement statement. Worse, if the correct DTE 100(EX) is not prepared, the closing gets pushed while paperwork is corrected.

Mistake four: not verifying recording fee schedules before preparing the seller's net sheet. Recording fees change, and county recorders are not required to give advance notice of schedule updates beyond posting the new rates. An agent relying on last year's numbers may misquote costs by $50 to $100 per document—a small amount that becomes a trust issue when the client sees a different number at closing.

Mistake five: listing the conveyance fee as a "buyer's cost" without contractual support. Ohio statute places the fee on the grantor by default. If the parties intend for the buyer to pay, that must be stated explicitly in the purchase agreement. Agents who insert the fee on the wrong side of the ledger without contract language risk the seller refusing to close or the auditor questioning the filing. This kind of deadline-related breakdown is exactly [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) under pressure—assumptions replace verification.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under ORC 4735.18 for the acts of their affiliated licensees. When an agent consistently miscalculates transfer fees or fails to disclose them properly, the broker faces potential disciplinary action from the Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency | Risk if Missed |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|----------------|
| Net sheet accuracy | Conveyance fee matches county rate | Every transaction | Client disputes, E&O claims |
| DTE 100 / DTE 100(EX) completion | Correct form selected, all fields accurate | Every transaction | Recording rejection, closing delay |
| County rate verification | Agent confirmed current permissive rate | Quarterly or per-county | Underquoted fees, seller shortfalls |
| Exemption documentation | Proper exemption code cited with supporting docs | Every exempt transfer | Auditor rejection, potential tax liability |
| Recording fee estimates | Matched to current county recorder schedule | Semi-annually | RESPA tolerance violations |

Brokers should maintain a reference sheet of current conveyance rates and recording fees for every county in their market area. This is not optional due diligence—it is the baseline for supervision. If you are running a brokerage with agents across multiple Ohio counties, a [weekly pipeline review system](/blog/weekly-pipeline-review-system) can surface fee discrepancies before they reach the closing table.

The Ohio Division of Real Estate can impose fines, require continuing education, or suspend a license for repeated negligence in fee disclosures. Under ORC 4735.18(A)(6), misrepresentation of costs—even through carelessness—constitutes grounds for disciplinary proceedings. Brokers who do not audit net sheets and closing documents are exposed to vicarious liability.

## Penalties and Liability for Non-Compliance

If the conveyance fee is not paid or is underpaid, the county auditor will refuse to accept the DTE 100, and the recorder will not record the deed. Title does not legally transfer until the deed is recorded. This means the buyer lacks record title, the lender's mortgage is unrecorded and potentially unenforceable against third parties, and the entire transaction sits in limbo.

Beyond the immediate recording failure, underpayment can trigger interest and penalties assessed by the county auditor's office. The seller remains the record owner until the deed is properly filed, which creates insurance gaps, tax billing confusion, and potential fraud exposure. Agents who caused the error through negligent advice may face E&O claims from their clients or the title company.

In extreme cases—particularly where an agent intentionally misrepresented a transaction's value to reduce the conveyance fee—criminal penalties under ORC 322.99 apply. The statute classifies willful evasion of the conveyance fee as a misdemeanor of the first degree, carrying up to six months in jail and a $1,000 fine. That scenario is rare, but it underscores why accuracy matters.

## Staying Current as Rates and Rules Shift

County commissioners can adjust the permissive transfer tax rate with proper legislative action, and recorder fee schedules update periodically. There is no single statewide notification system that pushes changes to agents. The responsibility falls on each licensee—and their broker—to verify rates before preparing cost disclosures.

The Ohio County Auditors' Association and the Ohio Recorders' Association both publish rate information, but neither maintains a guaranteed real-time database. Agents should call or check the website of the specific county recorder and auditor before each transaction, particularly in counties where they close infrequently.
