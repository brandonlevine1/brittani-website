## Washington 1031 Exchange State Tax Implications 2026: The State-Level Layer Most Agents Miss

Federal Section 1031 gets plenty of attention in continuing education courses, but the state-level tax obligations in Washington are where deals quietly hemorrhage money or stall at recording. Understanding Washington 1031 exchange state tax implications 2026 is not optional knowledge for licensed agents advising investment property clients—it is a practice-risk issue. Washington imposes its own Real Estate Excise Tax and a relatively new capital gains tax, both of which interact with 1031 transactions in ways that surprise even experienced practitioners.

The disconnect between federal deferral and state-level payment obligations has generated thousands of dollars in penalties for sellers who assumed a like-kind exchange meant a tax-free closing day. Agents who fail to flag these obligations risk more than an unhappy client—they risk regulatory complaints and E&O claims.

## How Washington's REET Applies to 1031 Exchanges

**Washington's Real Estate Excise Tax does not contain a blanket exemption for 1031 exchanges.** Under RCW 82.45.010, REET is triggered by any "sale" of real property, defined broadly as any transfer of ownership for consideration. A 1031 exchange still involves a sale of the relinquished property, and that sale is subject to REET at closing.

The graduated REET rates for 2026 apply based on the selling price of the property. For most investment properties in the Puget Sound corridor, agents are looking at combined state and local rates that range from 1.10% to 3.00% depending on price tier and jurisdiction. The tax is due before or at the time the deed is recorded with the county auditor.

There is one narrow exception worth knowing: RCW 82.45.010(3) excludes certain transfers between affiliated entities that qualify as mere changes in form. This is not the same as a standard 1031 exchange between unrelated parties. Agents should never conflate the two.

## The Capital Gains Tax Wrinkle: SB 5096 and Federal Recognition

Washington's 7% tax on long-term capital gains—enacted through SB 5096 and codified at RCW 82.87—applies to gains exceeding $270,000 (the 2026 threshold, adjusted for inflation). The good news for 1031 exchange clients: Washington's capital gains tax piggybacks on federal recognition principles. If a gain is not recognized at the federal level because of a valid 1031 exchange, it is also not recognized for purposes of Washington's capital gains tax.

**This means a properly structured 1031 exchange can defer the state capital gains tax, but only if every federal requirement is met.** A botched identification period, a constructive receipt issue, or a failed reverse exchange does not just blow up the federal deferral—it simultaneously triggers a 7% Washington capital gains liability on any gain above the threshold.

Agents need to understand that their role is not to structure the exchange (that belongs to qualified intermediaries and tax counsel), but to ensure the transaction timeline does not inadvertently torpedo compliance. Missing the [buyer contingency deadlines](/blog/washington-buyer-contingency-deadlines-2026) on the replacement property side can cascade into a federal identification failure, which then triggers both federal and state tax consequences for the seller.

## Filing Requirements and Form Numbers Agents Should Know

The primary filing obligation at closing is the Real Estate Excise Tax Affidavit, submitted through the Washington Department of Revenue's myDOR system or on paper Form REV 84-0001A. This affidavit must be filed and the tax paid before the county auditor will record the deed. No exceptions for pending 1031 exchanges.

For the capital gains tax, sellers file annually using the Washington Department of Revenue's excise tax return. If the exchange is completed within the same calendar year, no gain is reported. If the exchange spans calendar years (the relinquished property closes in December and the replacement property closes the following February, for example), the taxpayer may need to file and then amend—or request an extension.

| Filing Obligation | Form/System | Due Date | Who Files |
|---|---|---|---|
| Real Estate Excise Tax | REV 84-0001A / myDOR | At or before deed recording | Seller (escrow typically handles) |
| Capital Gains Tax Return | DOR Annual Return | April 15 of following year | Taxpayer (seller) |
| Federal 1031 Reporting | IRS Form 8824 | With annual tax return | Taxpayer (seller) |
| Qualified Intermediary Report | QI provides to taxpayer | Varies by QI agreement | QI |

Escrow and title companies typically handle the REET affidavit submission, but agents must verify this is happening—especially in FSBO-adjacent situations or when working with out-of-state escrow companies unfamiliar with Washington's system.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make on Washington 1031 Transactions

**The first and most dangerous mistake is telling clients that REET is waived for 1031 exchanges.** This advice is flatly wrong, leads to last-minute cash shortfalls at closing, and opens the agent to misrepresentation liability under WAC 308-124-020(2), which prohibits licensees from making misleading statements about material transaction facts.

The second common error is failing to coordinate timing between the sale of the relinquished property and the purchase of the replacement property. Agents representing the seller on the relinquished side often treat their job as done at closing, but the 45-day identification window and 180-day exchange period are running from that close date. If the same agent or a referral partner is involved on the buy side, poor communication about [earnest money rules](/blog/washington-earnest-money-rules-2026) and contract contingencies can destroy the exchange timeline.

Third, agents frequently forget that Washington's REET applies again when the replacement property is eventually sold—there is no step-up or credit for the REET paid on the relinquished property. Clients who execute serial 1031 exchanges across multiple Washington properties pay REET every single time.

Fourth, agents mishandle the [seller disclosure requirements](/blog/washington-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026) on the relinquished property, leading to post-closing disputes that delay the exchange period or result in rescission. A rescinded sale within the 180-day window can destroy the entire exchange.

Fifth, agents neglect to document their advice limitations in writing. The Washington Department of Licensing expects licensees to refer tax questions to qualified professionals. An agent who verbally assures a client about tax treatment without a written referral disclaimer is exposed if anything goes wrong.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers and managing brokers carry supervisory liability under RCW 18.85.275. When affiliated agents handle 1031 exchange transactions, the brokerage bears responsibility for ensuring compliant practices. This is not abstract—the Washington Department of Licensing can and does investigate brokerages where agents provide unlicensed tax advice.

| Broker Audit Point | What to Look For | Risk If Missing |
|---|---|---|
| Written tax referral disclaimer | In transaction file, signed by client | Unauthorized practice of law/accounting claim |
| REET affidavit confirmation | Copy of filed REV 84-0001A in file | Recording failure, deal delay |
| Timeline documentation | 45-day and 180-day deadlines noted in file | Malpractice exposure if deadlines missed |
| QI engagement letter | Confirmation client has retained QI | Constructive receipt issues, blown exchange |
| Commission disbursement timing | Commission paid from relinquished sale proceeds, not exchange funds | QI commingling concerns |

**Brokers should require a 1031 exchange checklist in every transaction file where the seller indicates exchange intent.** This checklist should confirm that the agent did not provide tax advice, that a QI was engaged before closing, that REET was paid, and that timeline deadlines were communicated in writing. Quarterly file audits should flag any exchange transaction missing these elements.

The brokerage's E&O policy may also contain exclusions for tax-related claims. Brokers who do not verify their coverage parameters are gambling with firm assets every time an agent touches an exchange deal.

## Liability Exposure: What Happens When Agents Get It Wrong

Under Washington law, an agent who provides incorrect tax information is not merely embarrassed—they face multiple exposure vectors. The Department of Licensing can impose fines up to $5,000 per violation under RCW 18.85.361 and suspend or revoke licenses. Civil liability for negligent misrepresentation can dwarf those fines, particularly when a client loses a six-figure tax deferral because of bad agent advice.

> "A licensee shall not make, print, publish, distribute, or cause to be made, printed, published, or distributed any material that is misleading or inaccurate in any material particular." — [WAC 308-124-020(2)](https://apps.leg.wa.gov/WAC/default.aspx?cite=308-124-020)

Deal cancellation is also a real risk. If a seller discovers mid-transaction that REET was not budgeted or that the exchange timeline is impossible given the contract dates, they may refuse to close. The resulting fallout—earnest money disputes, buyer claims, potential commission loss—lands on the agent who failed to flag the issue early.

The Washington Real Estate Commission does not treat "I thought my client's CPA was handling it" as an adequate defense when the agent made affirmative representations about tax treatment in emails or text messages. Documentation discipline is everything.

## Practical Guidance for 2026 Transactions

The most protective posture for Washington agents in 2026 is straightforward: flag, refer, and document. When a client mentions a 1031 exchange, flag it immediately in your transaction management system. Refer all tax questions to a CPA or tax attorney in writing. Document that referral in the transaction file.

On the transactional mechanics side, coordinate with the escrow officer to confirm REET payment, verify the qualified intermediary is engaged and has provided exchange documents to escrow, and ensure your contract timelines (closing date, possession date, contingency deadlines) align with the 45-day and 180-day federal windows.
