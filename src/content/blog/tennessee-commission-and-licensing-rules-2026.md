## Understanding Tennessee Commission & Licensing Rules 2026

The Tennessee Real Estate Commission (TREC) governs every licensee in the state, and the regulatory framework heading into 2026 carries meaningful enforcement weight that agents cannot afford to ignore. Tennessee commission & licensing rules 2026 reflect both long-standing state statutes and newer requirements prompted by national settlement agreements around buyer compensation transparency. If you hold a license in Tennessee—whether as an affiliate broker, broker, or principal broker—the rules below affect how you earn, disclose, and collect every dollar of commission.

TREC operates under the authority of the Tennessee Real Estate Broker License Act, codified at Tennessee Code Annotated (T.C.A.) Title 62, Chapter 13. The [Tennessee Real Estate Commission's official site](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regboards/trec.html) publishes rule updates, disciplinary actions, and form changes that every licensee should monitor quarterly.

## Commission Structures and Disclosure Obligations

**Tennessee law requires that all commission arrangements flow through the principal broker's office.** An affiliate broker cannot independently negotiate, receive, or hold commission payments. T.C.A. 62-13-312 makes this explicit: compensation for real estate services must be paid to the licensee's supervising broker, who then disburses it to the affiliate broker according to their internal agreement.

Since the implementation of buyer agency compensation changes, Tennessee agents must disclose their compensation terms in writing before providing substantive services. The Tennessee Association of Realtors (TAR) updated several forms to address this, including RF401 (Exclusive Buyer Representation Agreement) and RF141 (Compensation Disclosure and Consent). These forms require agents to state the exact amount or percentage of compensation and identify who is expected to pay it.

Commission splits offered through the MLS are no longer guaranteed to appear in listing data in the same manner as before. Agents must confirm compensation terms independently, and any offer of cooperative compensation must be documented outside of MLS fields if the local MLS has adopted new display rules. This shift places a heavier documentation burden on both listing and buyer agents.

## Licensing Tiers and Renewal Requirements

Tennessee maintains a tiered licensing system that distinguishes between affiliate brokers, brokers, and principal brokers. Each tier carries different supervisory responsibilities and commission-handling authority. The table below outlines the key differences relevant to commission activity.

| License Type | Minimum Experience | Can Hold Escrow Funds | Commission Paid Directly | Supervisory Authority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Affiliate Broker | None (pass exam) | No | No — must go through broker | None |
| Broker | 3 years as affiliate | Yes (if principal broker) | No — unless also principal broker | Can supervise with firm |
| Principal Broker | Broker license + designated | Yes | Yes — receives for firm | Full supervisory duty |

Renewal occurs on a biennial cycle. Affiliate brokers and brokers must complete 16 hours of continuing education, which includes a mandatory TREC core course that changes each cycle. Failure to renew by your expiration date triggers automatic license expiration—not suspension—which means you are practicing illegally if you continue working past that date.

TREC Rule 1260-02-.02 specifies that a licensee who allows their license to expire for more than one year must retake the state exam. This catches more agents than you might expect, particularly those who let licenses go inactive during career pauses and attempt to reactivate without understanding the timeline restrictions.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences for commission and licensing violations in Tennessee range from financial penalties to criminal prosecution, depending on the severity. T.C.A. 62-13-313 authorizes TREC to impose civil penalties of up to $1,000 per violation per day. For repeat offenders or egregious conduct, the Commission can revoke or suspend a license entirely.

**If an agent receives commission directly from a client or cooperating broker without routing it through their principal broker, that single act violates T.C.A. 62-13-312 and can result in license suspension.** The agent's broker also faces liability for failure to supervise under TREC Rule 1260-02-.12, which holds principal brokers accountable for the actions of their affiliated licensees.

Deal cancellation is a real risk when licensing defects surface. If a buyer or seller discovers that their agent was operating on an expired license, the transaction may be voidable, and the agent has no legal standing to claim their commission. Tennessee courts have upheld this principle in disputes where unlicensed activity was proven, leaving agents with zero recourse to recover fees for work already performed.

Beyond TREC enforcement, agents face exposure to private lawsuits. A client who suffers financial harm due to an agent's licensing or commission violation can pursue damages in civil court, and TREC disciplinary findings are admissible as evidence.

## Common Mistakes Tennessee Agents Make

Three to five errors account for the majority of TREC complaints related to commission and licensing. Each one is preventable with proper systems.

**First, agents fail to execute written buyer representation agreements before showing property.** Tennessee requires this documentation under both TREC rules and the current NAR settlement framework. Operating without RF401 or an equivalent signed agreement exposes the agent to forfeiting their compensation entirely if a dispute arises. The form must specify the compensation amount—vague language like "customary commission" is insufficient.

Second, agents accept referral fees from or pay referral fees to unlicensed individuals. T.C.A. 62-13-301 explicitly prohibits compensation to anyone not holding an active license for real estate brokerage activity. This includes paying a "finder's fee" to a friend, former client, or influencer who sends you business. TREC treats this as a serious violation regardless of the dollar amount.

Third, agents neglect to update their license when changing brokerages. TREC Rule 1260-02-.09 requires that any change of broker affiliation be reported within five days. During the gap between leaving one brokerage and officially affiliating with another, the agent has no authority to practice. Transactions initiated during that gap are legally compromised, and any commission earned is subject to forfeiture.

Fourth, agents misrepresent their license status on marketing materials. Advertising as a "broker" when you hold an affiliate broker license violates TREC advertising rules under 1260-02-.12(3). This seems minor until a complaint is filed—TREC issues citations and fines for exactly this kind of misrepresentation, and it appears on your permanent disciplinary record.

Fifth, agents fail to disclose dual agency or designated agency status using the correct Tennessee forms. The [proper handling of agency disclosure](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) is not optional—TREC requires the Confirmation of Agency Status form (RF 751) to be signed at or before the writing of an offer. Omitting this step is one of the most frequently cited violations in TREC disciplinary actions.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Principal brokers in Tennessee carry personal liability for the compliance failures of their affiliated licensees. TREC Rule 1260-02-.12 does not offer a "I didn't know" defense—the principal broker is expected to maintain systems that prevent violations before they occur.

| Audit Area | What to Check | Frequency | Relevant Rule |
|---|---|---|---|
| License status of all affiliates | Active status, CE completion, expiration dates | Monthly | T.C.A. 62-13-312 |
| Buyer representation agreements | RF401 signed before substantive services | Every transaction | TREC Rule 1260-02-.12 |
| Commission disbursement records | All payments flow through brokerage | Every closing | T.C.A. 62-13-312 |
| Agency disclosure forms | RF751 executed before offer submission | Every transaction | T.C.A. 62-13-403 |
| Advertising compliance | Correct license type, brokerage name displayed | Quarterly | TREC Rule 1260-02-.12(3) |

**Brokers who rely on agents to self-report compliance are inviting regulatory action.** TREC audits firms on a random basis, and complaints from consumers or cooperating agents can trigger targeted investigations. The Commission reviews transaction files and expects to find completed RF401, RF751, and RF141 forms in every applicable deal.

A practical approach involves implementing a [transaction coordination system](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) that flags missing documents before closing. Many Tennessee brokerages have adopted digital compliance checks that prevent files from advancing to closing without required signatures. This is not optional oversight—it is the legal standard TREC holds principal brokers to when violations surface.

Brokers should also conduct exit audits when an affiliate broker leaves the firm. T.C.A. 62-13-312(b) requires that any pending commission disputes be resolved through the principal broker's office, and the departing agent has no independent claim to funds held by the brokerage. Documenting the terms of commission splits in the independent contractor agreement—before disputes arise—protects both parties.
