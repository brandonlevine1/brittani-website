AI will not replace transaction coordinators. It will replace the parts of transaction coordination that should never have required a human in the first place — and the TCs who remain will command higher fees, carry larger portfolios, and function more like compliance officers than document chasers. Brokers who understand this distinction will retain talent and scale faster than those waiting for a magic button that handles an entire closing autonomously.

## The Automation Ceiling Is Lower Than Vendors Claim

Most AI tools marketed to real estate operations handle document parsing, deadline calculation, and status notifications. These are meaningful time savings — NAR's 2025 Member Profile reported that agents spend an average of 18 hours per transaction on administrative tasks — but they represent perhaps 40 percent of what a skilled TC actually does. The remaining 60 percent involves judgment calls that no current model reliably executes: interpreting ambiguous contract language, negotiating extension requests between agents who refuse to call each other back, and flagging liability exposure before it becomes a broker's problem.

Consider what happened in early 2025 when the Texas Real Estate Commission updated its [One to Four Family Residential Contract (TREC 20-17)](https://www.trec.texas.gov/forms/standard-contract-forms) with revised buyer representation language post-settlement. TCs across the state had to re-read addenda in progress, determine which pipeline transactions required updated disclosures, and advise agents on timing. No API call handles that kind of contextual triage.

## What Actually Changes: The Role Splits in Two

**The TC role is bifurcating into a lower tier that AI absorbs and an upper tier that becomes indispensable.** The lower tier — chasing signatures, confirming appointment times, sending reminder emails at day 7, 14, and 21 — will be automated within most brokerages by the end of 2026. The upper tier — exception handling, compliance interpretation, and relationship management during disputes — will grow in value precisely because fewer humans will be doing the routine work that used to justify a full headcount.

This mirrors what happened in accounting when QuickBooks and later cloud platforms eliminated manual bookkeeping. The Bureau of Labor Statistics shows bookkeeper employment fell 27 percent between 2004 and 2024, while accountant and auditor roles grew 9 percent in the same window. The grunt work disappeared; the advisory work expanded.

## Brokers Should Watch Headcount Ratios, Not Headlines

If you manage a team of four TCs handling 50 transactions each per month, the realistic near-term outcome is not firing three of them. It is restructuring so two TCs handle 100 files each at the administrative layer (with AI doing the scheduling, document tracking, and status updates) while one senior TC focuses exclusively on exception resolution and compliance review. The fourth role may indeed phase out — or transition into a client experience function that drives referrals.

**The brokerage that panics and eliminates TC positions wholesale will rediscover why they hired TCs in the first place around the time their E&O carrier sends a reservation-of-rights letter.** Missed contingency deadlines, improperly executed amendments, and disclosure timing errors do not fix themselves. They metastasize into lawsuits.

## The Strongest Counterargument — And Why It Falls Short

The most credible case for full replacement goes like this: large language models improve at reasoning tasks every six months, document understanding is already near-human accuracy for structured forms, and within three years the exception-handling gap will close. Proponents point to tools that already flag conflicting dates, missing initials, and inconsistent party names with better precision than a junior TC on file number 47 of the month.

I take this seriously. The trajectory is real. But it misses a social layer that no model addresses: TCs operate inside a web of human relationships where tone, timing, and trust determine outcomes. When a listing agent's TC calls the buyer's lender to push a clear-to-close forward by 48 hours, that call works because of a relationship built over dozens of prior closings. An automated email requesting the same accommodation gets deprioritized or ignored. Until AI can build and maintain professional relationships that produce preferential cooperation, the human TC retains a structural advantage on any transaction where something goes sideways — which, per the National Association of Realtors' 2025 data, includes roughly [30 percent of contracts that encounter at least one material obstacle before closing](/blog/common-transaction-delays).

## Regulation Will Slow the Transition Further

State licensing frameworks have not caught up. The California Department of Real Estate still requires a licensed individual to review and approve disclosure packages in brokerage-supervised transactions. Colorado's Commission has proposed rules around AI-generated consumer communications that would mandate human review of any automated message containing contract terms. These regulatory gates are not going away; they are expanding.

Brokers operating in multiple states face a patchwork of compliance obligations that effectively mandate human oversight at the TC level. Even if your AI can draft a perfect wire fraud warning email, someone with a license number attached to their name needs to approve it in at least 14 states as of this writing. The [compliance burden grows when you scale across jurisdictions](/blog/multi-state-compliance-challenges), and that burden lands on people, not software.

## What Smart Brokerages Are Doing Right Now

**The firms getting this right treat AI as infrastructure beneath the TC, not a replacement for the TC.** They are investing in platforms that handle the 40 percent — document intake, deadline math, automated status updates to all parties — so their human coordinators spend their hours on the 60 percent that protects the brokerage and closes deals that would otherwise fall apart.

This looks like: a senior TC who reviews 150 files per month instead of 50, because she is not manually tracking 11 different deadlines per file. It looks like a broker who can offer white-glove service to top-producing agents without tripling payroll. It looks like an operations team that catches a missing HOA resale certificate on day two instead of day 28.

## The Actionable Shift for Your Brokerage

Stop asking whether AI will replace your TCs. Start asking which tasks your TCs perform that require no judgment, no relationship, and no license — and automate those immediately. Then reinvest the recovered hours into compliance review and agent support, where every hour of TC time has measurable downstream value in reduced risk and higher close rates.
