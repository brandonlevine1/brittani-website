## Why Illinois Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026 Demand Your Attention

Disclosure liability remains the single most common source of post-closing litigation against Illinois residential agents. If you are listing or selling residential property in the state this year, Illinois property condition & environmental disclosures 2026 requirements should be front of mind on every transaction file. One missed form, one unchecked box, or one late delivery can expose you, your seller, and your brokerage to rescission claims or monetary damages.

Illinois disclosure law sits across multiple statutes rather than a single omnibus code section. The Residential Real Property Disclosure Act (765 ILCS 77), the Radon Awareness Act (420 ILCS 46), federal lead-based paint rules under 42 U.S.C. 4852d, and local municipal ordinances each impose separate obligations. Agents who treat disclosure as one form and one signature miss the full picture—and miss it at their peril.

## The Core Statute: 765 ILCS 77 and the Illinois Disclosure Report

**The Residential Real Property Disclosure Act requires sellers of residential real property (one to four dwelling units) to complete and deliver a written disclosure report to prospective buyers before the buyer is obligated under a contract.** The statutory form is set out directly in the statute at 765 ILCS 77/35. It covers 23 categories of property conditions including structural elements, mechanical systems, water and sewer, flooding history, environmental hazards, and code violations.

Sellers must answer "yes," "no," or "not applicable" to each item. They are not required to perform inspections—only to disclose what they actually know. This distinction matters because agents sometimes pressure sellers to investigate conditions, which can actually expand the seller's knowledge (and therefore liability).

Delivery must occur before the buyer is bound. If the disclosure arrives late or not at all, the buyer has the right to terminate the contract within three business days of receipt without penalty under 765 ILCS 77/25.

## Environmental Disclosures: Radon, Lead Paint, and Beyond

Illinois layers several environmental disclosure obligations on top of the general property condition report. The two most frequent are radon and lead-based paint, but agents working near former industrial sites or agricultural land should also watch for IEPA brownfield notices and underground storage tank records.

### Radon Awareness Act (420 ILCS 46)

The Illinois Emergency Management Agency (IEMA) publishes the "Radon Testing Guidelines for Real Estate Transactions" pamphlet. Sellers must provide this pamphlet to buyers and disclose any known radon test results, mitigation measures, or radon concentrations. The act does not require testing—it requires disclosure of what is already known and delivery of the state pamphlet.

### Federal Lead-Based Paint Disclosure (42 U.S.C. 4852d)

For any dwelling built before 1978, sellers and their agents must provide buyers with the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home," disclose known lead-based paint or hazards, and allow a 10-day inspection opportunity (unless waived). The standard federal form used in Illinois practice is HUD's "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form. Both listing and buyer agents must sign as confirmation that requirements were met.

| Disclosure Type | Governing Law | Required Form/Pamphlet | Delivery Deadline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Property Condition | 765 ILCS 77 | Statutory Disclosure Report (765 ILCS 77/35) | Before buyer is obligated |
| Radon | 420 ILCS 46 | IEMA Radon Pamphlet + known results | Before buyer is obligated |
| Lead-Based Paint (pre-1978) | 42 U.S.C. 4852d / 24 CFR 35 | EPA Pamphlet + HUD Disclosure Form | Before contract is binding |
| IEPA Environmental Liens | 765 ILCS 77/35 Item 8 | Addressed in statutory disclosure | Before buyer is obligated |

Agents working transactions similar to those in other states may find parallels in how [Texas handles property condition disclosures](/blog/texas-property-condition-disclosures-2026) or how [New York structures its requirements](/blog/new-york-property-condition-disclosures-2026), though Illinois's statutory form is notably more prescriptive.

## Consequences of Noncompliance: Liability, Rescission, and Fines

**Failing to deliver the required disclosures exposes agents and sellers to actual damages, contract rescission, and potential license discipline from the Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation (IDFPR).** Under 765 ILCS 77/55, a seller who willfully or negligently fails to disclose a known material defect is liable to the buyer for actual damages plus court costs and attorney fees.

For agents specifically, IDFPR can pursue disciplinary action under the Real Estate License Act of 2000 (225 ILCS 454) for conduct that demonstrates incompetence or failure to follow statutory requirements. Penalties range from fines to license suspension. Federal lead-paint violations carry their own enforcement teeth—HUD can impose civil penalties up to $21,410 per violation (adjusted for inflation) under 24 CFR 30.

Contract cancellation is the most immediate risk. If a buyer does not receive the property disclosure before becoming obligated, the three-business-day termination window opens upon receipt. In a competitive market, having a deal unwound over a late disclosure is an avoidable disaster.

> "A person who knowingly violates or fails to perform any duty prescribed by any provision of this Act... shall be liable in the amount of actual damages and court costs, and the court may award reasonable attorney fees." — 765 ILCS 77/55

## Common Mistakes Illinois Agents Make With Disclosures

Every year, the same errors generate complaints, rescissions, and E&O claims. Here are the five most frequent.

**First: relying on a prior listing's disclosure report.** When a property was listed previously and the deal fell through, agents often recycle the old disclosure form. Illinois law requires a disclosure reflecting the seller's knowledge at the time of the current transaction. Conditions change—water intrusion may have occurred, repairs may have been done, or new code violations may have been issued. Always secure a fresh form.

Second: treating the radon pamphlet as optional. Some agents forget that the Radon Awareness Act is a separate statutory requirement from the general property disclosure. The IEMA pamphlet must be physically delivered (or electronically with proper consent). Checking a box on the general disclosure form about radon does not satisfy 420 ILCS 46.

Third: failing to document the date of delivery. The three-business-day termination clock runs from the date the buyer receives the disclosure. Without a signed acknowledgment showing the delivery date, you cannot prove when the clock started. If a dispute arises, a missing date stamp becomes your problem.

Fourth: confusing "not applicable" with "unknown." The statutory form distinguishes between items that do not apply to the property and items the seller simply does not know about. Coaching sellers to mark "N/A" when they actually mean "I don't know" misrepresents their knowledge and can constitute negligent nondisclosure.

Fifth: ignoring municipal disclosure requirements. Chicago, for example, requires a separate Energy Cost Disclosure Ordinance (Municipal Code 5-16) for residential buildings. Evanston has its own radon ordinance. Agents working in municipalities with local add-ons must identify those requirements independently of state law.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability for their agents' disclosure failures under 225 ILCS 454/15-15. A supervising broker who does not implement systems to verify disclosure compliance is inviting regulatory scrutiny and E&O exposure.

| Audit Checkpoint | What to Verify | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| File opening | Disclosure packet sent to seller within 48 hours of listing agreement | Disclosure not in file 5+ days post-listing |
| Pre-contract | Signed disclosure delivered to buyer with date stamp | Missing buyer signature or date |
| Lead paint (pre-1978) | HUD form + EPA pamphlet in file with all parties' signatures | Only seller's signature present |
| Radon pamphlet | IEMA pamphlet delivery confirmation | No separate radon acknowledgment |
| Municipal add-ons | Chicago energy, local radon, or local transfer stamps | Agent working outside home market without local checklist |

Brokers should run disclosure audits at two points: within 72 hours of listing activation and again before attorney review expiration. Waiting until closing to check for missing forms is too late—by then, the buyer's termination right may have been triggered or waived improperly. If your brokerage struggles to [track multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without a systemized approach, that gap shows up most painfully in disclosure compliance.

## Exemptions You Should Know Cold

Not every transfer triggers the Residential Real Property Disclosure Act. Section 765 ILCS 77/15 carves out specific exemptions. Transfers pursuant to court order (foreclosure, probate, bankruptcy), transfers by a fiduciary in the course of estate administration, transfers from one co-owner to another, transfers by a government entity, and transfers involving new construction that has never been occupied are all exempt.

However—and this catches agents regularly—the lead-based paint disclosure applies regardless of these state-law exemptions if the home was built before 1978 and does not fall within one of the federal carve-outs (e.g., foreclosure sales, lease renewals under 100 days, housing designated exclusively for elderly or disabled persons). You can be exempt from the state property disclosure and still fully obligated on lead paint.

The radon pamphlet requirement under 420 ILCS 46 similarly does not mirror the property disclosure exemptions. Its applicability turns on whether the transaction involves "residential real property" broadly, and the exemptions listed are narrower than those in 765 ILCS 77.
