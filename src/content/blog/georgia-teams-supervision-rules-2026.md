## Why Georgia Teams & Supervision Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

The Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) has tightened enforcement around team structures, advertising, and supervisory obligations in ways that directly affect how you run your business. If you operate within a team—or broker one—understanding Georgia teams & supervision rules 2026 is not optional. It is the difference between a clean license renewal and an appearance before the Commission.

Georgia's regulatory framework for teams lives primarily in O.C.G.A. Title 43, Chapter 40, and the GREC administrative rules found in Chapter 520-1 of the Georgia Compilation of Rules and Regulations. These statutes set the boundaries for how teams form, how they advertise, and how brokers must supervise every licensee underneath them. The 2026 enforcement posture reflects post-NAR settlement realities and increased consumer complaints about team-level confusion.

This post breaks down the statutory requirements, the forms you must file, the penalties for non-compliance, and the specific mistakes agents keep making year after year.

## The Statutory Foundation: O.C.G.A. 43-40 and GREC Rule 520-1-.09

**Georgia law does not formally define a "team" as a separate legal entity.** Instead, every team operates as a marketing arrangement under a qualifying broker's license. O.C.G.A. 43-40-18 places direct supervisory responsibility on the broker for all licensees, regardless of whether those licensees call themselves a team, a group, or a partnership.

GREC Rule 520-1-.09 addresses advertising specifically. It requires that any team name used in advertising must include the licensed brokerage name or be registered as a trade name with GREC. The rule also mandates that team advertising cannot mislead the public into believing the team is an independent brokerage.

The practical effect: your team's Instagram handle, yard signs, and email signatures all fall under GREC jurisdiction. A team name like "The Johnson Group" without the brokerage identifier is a violation waiting to generate a complaint.

## Advertising Compliance: What GREC Actually Requires

GREC's advertising rules under 520-1-.09 apply to every medium—print, digital, social, and broadcast. The qualifying broker's name or the brokerage trade name must appear in a clear and conspicuous manner in all team advertising. This requirement extends to individual agent advertising when that agent is part of a team.

The Commission uses a "reasonable consumer" standard when evaluating complaints. If a reasonable consumer could mistake a team advertisement for a standalone brokerage, the ad violates the rule. GREC has specifically flagged oversized team logos paired with undersized brokerage names as a recurring violation pattern.

| Advertising Element | Compliant Example | Non-Compliant Example |
|---|---|---|
| Yard Sign | "Smith Team - Keller Williams Peachtree" | "Smith Team" (no brokerage) |
| Social Media Bio | "Smith Team at ABC Realty - Licensed in GA" | "Smith Team - Your Atlanta Experts" |
| Email Signature | "Jane Smith, Smith Team, ABC Realty" | "Jane Smith, Smith Team CEO" |
| Website Domain | smithteam-abcrealty.com (with brokerage on homepage) | smithteamrealty.com (implies independent brokerage) |
| Business Card | Team name with brokerage name same font or larger | Team name three times the size of brokerage name |

## Broker Supervision Duties Under Georgia Law

O.C.G.A. 43-40-18(a) states that the qualifying broker is responsible for "the supervision of all licensed activities performed by affiliated licensees." This is not aspirational language. It is the statutory hook GREC uses to discipline brokers when team members violate rules.

**Supervision in Georgia means active, documented oversight—not passive availability.** GREC has made clear through disciplinary actions that a broker cannot claim ignorance of a team member's misconduct if the broker failed to implement reasonable supervisory systems. The Commission looks for written policies, regular file reviews, and documented training.

For teams specifically, the broker must verify that each team member holds an active Georgia license, that agency disclosures are properly delivered using GREC Form [GAR F201](https://www.grec.state.ga.us/) (the Georgia Brokerage Relationships in Real Estate Transactions Act disclosure), and that trust account procedures are followed. The qualifying broker—not the team leader—bears statutory responsibility for trust account compliance under O.C.G.A. 43-40-20.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: Fines, Suspensions, and Deal Fallout

GREC's disciplinary authority under O.C.G.A. 43-40-25 includes license revocation, suspension, reprimand, fines up to $5,000 per violation, and required completion of additional education. For advertising violations, the typical first offense results in a citation carrying a $500 to $1,000 fine. Repeated violations escalate quickly.

**Beyond GREC penalties, non-compliant team structures create civil liability exposure.** If a consumer can demonstrate they were confused about who represented them because of team advertising or disclosure failures, the resulting claims often target both the individual agent and the qualifying broker. Georgia courts have allowed negligence and fraud claims to proceed where agency disclosures were missing or misleading.

Deal cancellation is a real risk. Under GAR Form F20 (the Georgia Purchase and Sale Agreement), buyers retain rights to terminate if material misrepresentations—including undisclosed agency relationships—are discovered. A team member who fails to deliver the BRRETA disclosure form before substantive discussions could hand the other side grounds for termination and damages.

| Violation Type | Typical GREC Penalty | Civil Liability Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Missing brokerage name in advertising | $500-$1,000 fine, cease-and-desist | Low (unless consumer confusion proven) |
| Failure to deliver BRRETA disclosure | $1,000-$2,500 fine, mandatory education | High (agency confusion, fraud claims) |
| Unlicensed team assistant performing licensed activities | $2,500-$5,000 fine, possible suspension | Very high (unauthorized practice) |
| Trust account commingling by team | Broker license suspension or revocation | Very high (theft, conversion claims) |
| Dual agency without written consent | $1,000-$5,000 fine | High (breach of fiduciary duty) |

## Common Mistakes Georgia Teams Make

Three to five errors appear repeatedly in GREC disciplinary records and consumer complaints. These are not edge cases—they represent the most frequent team-level compliance failures in the state.

**First, team leaders acting as de facto brokers without a broker's license.** Georgia requires a broker's license (O.C.G.A. 43-40-10) to supervise other licensees. A team leader with only a salesperson's license cannot conduct broker-level supervision, hold earnest money, or make compliance decisions for other team members. GREC has disciplined agents for this exact arrangement.

Second, unlicensed assistants performing activities that require a license. Georgia law requires a license for anyone who negotiates, lists, sells, or represents parties in a real estate transaction. When a team's unlicensed transaction coordinator drafts counteroffers, discusses pricing with clients, or shows property independently, the entire team and broker face exposure. Understanding [what a transaction coordinator can and cannot do](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) matters here.

Third, failing to update GREC when team members join or leave. Brokers must file transfer paperwork with GREC within 30 days of any affiliation change. Teams with revolving membership often fall behind, leaving agents technically unaffiliated—and therefore practicing without proper brokerage coverage.

Fourth, dual agency situations within a team that go undisclosed. When one team member represents the buyer and another represents the seller in the same transaction, Georgia's BRRETA requires written, informed consent from both parties. Teams frequently assume that because different people are involved, no dual agency exists. GREC disagrees.

Fifth, using personal trust accounts or team-specific accounts instead of the brokerage trust account. O.C.G.A. 43-40-20 requires all earnest money to flow through the qualifying broker's designated trust account. Team-level accounts without broker authorization violate the statute.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers who oversee teams in Georgia should build audit systems around three pillars: advertising compliance, agency disclosure delivery, and trust account integrity.

For advertising, conduct a quarterly review of all team marketing materials—including social media profiles, paid ads, websites, and printed collateral. Document the review with dates and screenshots. GREC investigators look for evidence that brokers took reasonable steps; documentation is your defense.

For agency disclosures, implement a system that confirms BRRETA Form F201 delivery before any substantive discussion with a prospective client. Many brokerages now require the signed disclosure to be uploaded to the transaction file within 24 hours of first client contact. Electronic timestamping strengthens your compliance record.

> "The qualifying broker shall exercise reasonable supervision over the brokerage activities of all affiliated licensees." — O.C.G.A. 43-40-18(a)

For trust accounts, reconcile monthly and cross-reference deposits against transaction files. Any earnest money received by a team member must reach the brokerage trust account within the timeframe specified in the purchase agreement (typically five banking days under GAR contract terms). Brokers should audit whether team members are directing clients to wire funds correctly—reviewing [wire verification protocols](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) is a starting point.

## Structuring Your Team for Compliance in 2026

The safest team structure in Georgia mirrors what GREC expects: one qualifying broker at the top, clear written agreements defining each team member's role, and all client-facing activity channeled through the brokerage's compliance infrastructure. Team operating agreements should address advertising approval workflows, lead routing disclosures, commission split documentation, and departure protocols.

Written team policies should specify who delivers the BRRETA disclosure, who handles earnest money logistics, and who communicates with the closing attorney. Georgia is an attorney-close state, which adds another layer—team members must understand they cannot provide legal advice or direct clients on title matters.

Brokers should also maintain a roster of every team operating under their license, updated in real time, with copies of each team's internal agreements on file. GREC's investigators have requested these documents during audits, and producing them promptly demonstrates the "reasonable supervision" standard the statute requires.
