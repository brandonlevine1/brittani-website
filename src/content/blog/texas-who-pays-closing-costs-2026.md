## Understanding Texas Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026

The question of Texas who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is not answered by statute alone. It is answered by contract terms, local custom, lender requirements, and the specific language an agent selects in TREC promulgated forms. Getting this wrong does not just frustrate clients — it creates real liability exposure for the agent and the brokerage.

Texas closing costs typically range from 2% to 5% of the purchase price for buyers and 6% to 10% for sellers (when commissions are included). The allocation between the parties is negotiated during the contract phase and memorialized on specific lines of the TREC One to Four Family Residential Contract (Form 20-17). Unlike some states where attorneys draft bespoke purchase agreements, Texas agents work within a rigid promulgated-form framework overseen by the [Texas Real Estate Commission (TREC)](https://www.trec.texas.gov/).

That framework means every dollar of closing cost allocation traces back to a box an agent checked or a blank an agent filled. Agents who treat these fields as afterthoughts are the ones who end up in [deals that break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) at the settlement table.

## The Standard Buyer vs. Seller Split in Texas

**Texas has no statute that mandates which party pays specific closing costs.** Everything is negotiable. However, strong local customs have calcified over decades, and deviating from them without explanation invites client confusion and potential disputes.

| Cost Item | Customary Payor | Negotiable? |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Owner's title insurance policy | Seller | Yes |
| Lender's title insurance policy | Buyer | Yes |
| Survey | Varies by county | Yes |
| Escrow fees | Split 50/50 or varies | Yes |
| Recording fees | Buyer | Yes |
| Loan origination and discount points | Buyer | Yes |
| Property tax prorations | Both (prorated) | Contract-dependent |
| Home warranty | Negotiated | Yes |

In Bexar, Harris, and Travis counties, sellers almost always pay for the owner's title policy. In some rural counties, custom flips and the buyer pays. An agent who assumes one county's custom applies statewide is making a mistake that shows up at the closing table when the title company's settlement statement does not match the contract.

## TREC Forms That Control the Allocation

The primary document governing closing cost allocation is the TREC One to Four Family Residential Contract (Form 20-17, revised 2024). Paragraph 6 ("Title Policy") and Paragraph 12 ("Settlement and Other Expenses") are where agents lock in who pays what.

Paragraph 12 contains sub-sections that address the seller's contribution to buyer's closing costs (often called "seller concessions"), title company selection, and specific expense assignments. If an agent leaves Paragraph 12 blank or enters an ambiguous figure, the title company has no contractual authority to charge one party over the other — and the deal stalls.

**Agents must also account for the TREC Third Party Financing Addendum (Form 40-9) when seller concessions are involved.** This addendum specifies the maximum seller contribution as a percentage of the sales price, which must align with the buyer's lender guidelines. A seller concession that exceeds FHA, VA, or conventional limits voids the financing condition and can collapse the transaction.

Beyond these, the TREC Amendment to Contract (Form 39-8) is the proper vehicle for changing cost allocations after execution. Agents who attempt to renegotiate closing costs via email or verbal agreement — without executing an amendment — are operating outside TREC's promulgated form requirements under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.155.

## Liability When Agents Get This Wrong

Section 1101.652 of the Texas Occupations Code lists grounds for TREC disciplinary action against licensees. Subsection (b)(1) covers negligence or incompetence, and subsection (b)(2) addresses making a material misrepresentation. Incorrectly filling in closing cost allocations — or failing to fill them in at all — can trigger either.

The consequences escalate quickly. TREC can issue a reprimand, suspend a license, impose an administrative penalty up to $5,000 per violation, or revoke a license entirely. Beyond regulatory action, the agent and brokerage face civil liability if a party suffers financial harm because costs were misallocated.

Consider a scenario where an agent writes $8,000 in seller concessions into Paragraph 12 but the buyer's FHA loan only permits 6% of the sales price — which on a $120,000 home caps at $7,200. The lender flags the discrepancy, the closing is delayed, the buyer's rate lock expires, and the deal costs both parties money. The listing agent's brokerage may face a [blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) claim, and the buyer's agent may face a TREC complaint for incompetent handling of the financing addendum.

## Common Mistakes Texas Agents Make on Closing Costs

**The first mistake is leaving Paragraph 12 partially blank and assuming the title company will "figure it out."** Title companies settle based on contract language. They are not authorized to interpret intent or fill gaps an agent left open.

The second mistake is writing seller concessions as a flat dollar amount that exceeds the buyer's loan program limits. Conventional loans cap seller concessions at 3% for buyers putting less than 10% down, 6% for 10-25% down, and 9% for more than 25% down. VA loans cap at 4% for certain costs. Agents who write "$15,000 seller contribution" on a $280,000 VA purchase have just written an impossible contract term.

Third, agents confuse the owner's title policy with the lender's title policy. These are separate products with separate payors. Writing "seller pays title policy" without specifying which one creates ambiguity that the title company must resolve — usually by calling both agents and delaying closing.

Fourth, some agents fail to execute a formal amendment (TREC 39-8) when closing cost terms change during negotiations. A text message saying "seller agreed to pay $3K toward buyer's costs" has no contractual force without an executed amendment. This error is especially common under [deadline pressure](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines).

Fifth, agents sometimes forget that property tax prorations are not a fixed cost — they change daily and depend on the closing date. Agents who quote a specific proration number during negotiations without noting it will be recalculated at closing create client expectations that do not match reality.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry vicarious liability for their agents' contract errors under Texas Occupations Code Section 1101.803. A broker who fails to maintain adequate supervision over how agents complete TREC forms risks disciplinary action at the brokerage level — not just against the individual agent.

**Brokers should audit every executed contract for three specific compliance points.** First, confirm that Paragraph 12 is fully completed with no blank fields that should contain a dollar amount or party designation. Second, verify that any seller concession amount in the contract does not exceed the limits imposed by the buyer's loan type as stated in the Third Party Financing Addendum (Form 40-9). Third, confirm that any post-execution changes to closing cost terms are documented via TREC Amendment (Form 39-8) and not merely referenced in email threads.

| Audit Point | Form to Check | Red Flag |
|------------|---------------|----------|
| Closing cost allocation complete | TREC 20-17, Paragraph 12 | Blank fields or "TBD" notations |
| Seller concession within loan limits | TREC 40-9 | Concession exceeds FHA/VA/conventional caps |
| Amendments executed for changes | TREC 39-8 | Email-only renegotiations without signed amendment |
| Title policy payor specified | TREC 20-17, Paragraph 6 | Ambiguous "title policy" without owner/lender distinction |
| Tax proration method noted | TREC 20-17, Paragraph 12 | No indication of proration calculation basis |

Brokers who rely on agents to self-audit are playing a losing game. A weekly file review — ideally before the option period expires — catches these errors when they are still fixable. ## How Seller Concessions Interact With Appraisals and Lender Rules

A seller concession inflates the effective purchase price from the lender's perspective. If a seller agrees to pay $10,000 toward the buyer's closing costs on a $300,000 home, the lender evaluates whether the property appraises at $300,000 — not at the "net" price of $290,000. But if the appraisal comes in low, the seller concession may need to be reduced, which triggers an amendment.

Agents must understand that excessive concessions can signal to appraisers and underwriters that the sales price is artificially inflated. Fannie Mae's Selling Guide (B3-4.1-02) explicitly instructs underwriters to scrutinize transactions where seller concessions are at or near maximum limits. This is not a TREC compliance issue — it is a practical deal-survival issue.

The interplay between contract terms and lender guidelines is exactly where experienced transaction coordinators earn their keep. Agents who try to manage this alone — especially across [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) — frequently miss the moment when a concession becomes untenable and an amendment becomes necessary.

## Protecting Yourself When Texas Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026 Gets Contested

Disputes over closing cost allocation most often arise when one party claims they did not understand what they agreed to. This is a disclosure and documentation problem, not a legal ambiguity problem. The TREC forms are clear — the issue is whether the agent explained them clearly and the client's acknowledgment is documented.

**Texas agents should keep written records of any closing cost discussion that occurs outside the four corners of the contract.** An email confirming "Per our conversation, the seller is contributing $5,000 toward your closing costs as reflected in Paragraph 12" is cheap insurance against a future complaint. TREC's complaint process under 22 TAC Section 535.71 examines whether the agent acted competently and in the client's interest — documentation of client communication is the agent's primary defense.

The 2026 market in Texas — with shifting inventory levels and rate volatility — means closing cost negotiations are more aggressive than they were in 2021 or 2022. Sellers are again being asked to contribute, and buyers are pushing for concessions that test loan-program ceilings. Agents who understand the boundaries, complete the forms correctly, and document their communications will close more deals with fewer complaints. That is the bottom line on Texas who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026: the answer is always "whatever the properly executed contract says," and the agent's job is to make sure that contract says it clearly.
