## Why Georgia HOA & Condo Disclosure Rules 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Georgia agents handling properties governed by a homeowners association or condominium regime face a disclosure framework that blends statutory mandates with contract-driven obligations. Understanding the Georgia HOA & condo disclosure rules 2026 is not optional — it is a direct liability question that determines whether a deal closes cleanly or blows up two days before the wire transfer. The Georgia Real Estate Commission (GREC) has made clear that agents who mishandle association disclosures risk disciplinary action under their license law.

Unlike states that consolidate everything into a single seller disclosure form, Georgia splits the duties across the Georgia Condominium Act, the Georgia Property Owners' Association Act, and the GAR (Georgia Association of Realtors) contract forms that control timing and contingencies. Agents who treat HOA disclosure as an afterthought are the same ones scrambling when a buyer exercises a cancellation right nobody warned the seller about. This post breaks down the statutes, the forms, the penalties, and the operational errors that get agents in trouble.

## The Two Statutes That Control Everything

**Georgia draws a hard line between condominiums and non-condo HOA communities, and agents must know which statute applies to the property they are selling.** Condominiums fall under the Georgia Condominium Act, codified at O.C.G.A. Title 44, Chapter 3, Article 3. Non-condo HOA properties — single-family subdivisions, townhome communities without a condominium declaration — fall under the Georgia Property Owners' Association Act at O.C.G.A. Section 44-3-220 through 44-3-235.

The condominium resale certificate requirement lives at O.C.G.A. Section 44-3-111. This section mandates that a seller furnish a resale certificate containing financial statements, pending assessments, insurance coverage, and any known violations attributed to the unit. The buyer's right to cancel after receiving the certificate makes it a deal-critical document rather than a courtesy item.

For non-condo HOA communities, the Property Owners' Association Act (O.C.G.A. Section 44-3-223) requires disclosure of association obligations, assessments, and governing documents. However, the enforcement mechanism differs — there is no automatic statutory cancellation right built into the POA Act the way there is for condominiums. This distinction trips up agents who assume the same rules apply to both property types.

## GAR Forms: The Contracts That Do the Heavy Lifting

Georgia agents overwhelmingly use GAR contract forms, and the HOA-related provisions live in specific exhibits and stipulations that agents must actively incorporate. The GAR Purchase and Sale Agreement (Form F20) contains baseline references to association disclosures, but the real machinery sits in the Community Association Disclosure Exhibit.

| Form | Purpose | When to Use |
|------|---------|-------------|
| GAR F20 | Purchase and Sale Agreement | Every residential transaction |
| GAR F313 | Community Association Disclosure Exhibit | Any property subject to an HOA or condo association |
| GAR F314 | Seller's Disclosure of Association Information | Seller completes with known association details |
| GAR CB20 | Condo Purchase and Sale Agreement | Condominium-specific transactions |

**Form F313 is where agents set the contractual deadline for the buyer to receive and review association documents.** If you leave the deadline blank or fail to attach F313 altogether, you create ambiguity about when the buyer's review period ends — and ambiguity in Georgia real estate contracts tends to resolve against the party who drafted the terms. The Georgia Court of Appeals has consistently held that unclear contingency deadlines favor the non-drafting party.

Form CB20 is the condominium-specific purchase agreement that incorporates the resale certificate requirement by reference to O.C.G.A. Section 44-3-111. Agents listing condominiums who use the standard F20 instead of CB20 are missing statutory protections built into the condo-specific form. This is one of the most common errors that [causes deals to fall apart late in the process](/blog/where-deals-break-down).

## What the Resale Certificate Must Contain

The condominium resale certificate under O.C.G.A. Section 44-3-111 is not a one-page summary. It must include specific categories of information, and an incomplete certificate does not satisfy the seller's statutory obligation.

Required contents include: the monthly common expense assessment and any special assessments approved but not yet due; the association's current operating budget and most recent financial statement; a statement of any pending lawsuits involving the association; the amount of reserves allocated for capital expenditures; any known alterations or improvements to the unit that violate the declaration; and the association's insurance coverage summary.

**If the certificate is incomplete or contains material misstatements, the buyer retains cancellation rights even after the contractual review period.** Georgia courts have treated an incomplete certificate as the functional equivalent of non-delivery, meaning the buyer's cancellation window never began running. Agents representing sellers must verify completeness rather than simply forwarding whatever the management company produces.

The association typically charges a fee for preparing the resale certificate — often between $150 and $400 in the Atlanta metro area. The [true financial impact of a failed closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) dwarfs this fee, yet agents frequently delay ordering the certificate to save the seller money upfront, only to face a last-minute cancellation.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance: Liability, Cancellation, and Discipline

Georgia does not treat disclosure failures as minor paperwork issues. The consequences flow through three separate channels: contractual, statutory, and regulatory.

On the contractual side, failure to deliver required association documents within the GAR F313 timeline gives the buyer a termination right. The earnest money returns to the buyer, and the seller loses market time with no recourse against their own agent unless they can prove the agent's negligence caused the failure.

On the statutory side, O.C.G.A. Section 44-3-111 explicitly states that a buyer who does not receive the resale certificate may cancel the contract at any time prior to closing. There is no cure period. The seller cannot rush the certificate to the buyer at the closing table and claim compliance — the statute contemplates meaningful review time.

> "A purchaser may cancel the contract of purchase at any time before the receipt of the certificate or within the time period stated in the contract." — O.C.G.A. Section 44-3-111(d)

On the regulatory side, GREC Rule 520-1-.05 requires licensees to exercise reasonable skill and care in handling transaction documents. An agent who fails to advise a seller about the resale certificate obligation, or who neglects to attach Form F313 to a transaction involving a community association, faces potential disciplinary action including citation, fine, or license suspension. GREC's [disciplinary actions database](https://grec.state.ga.us/information-research/disciplinary-actions/) shows multiple cases where disclosure failures contributed to formal complaints.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With HOA and Condo Disclosures

**First: Using the wrong purchase agreement form for condominiums.** Agents who list a condo unit and draft the offer on GAR F20 instead of CB20 omit the statutory resale certificate framework. The deal still closes sometimes, but the seller has technically failed to comply with O.C.G.A. Section 44-3-111, leaving both parties exposed to post-closing claims.

Second: Failing to order the resale certificate early enough. Management companies in metro Atlanta routinely take 10 to 15 business days to produce a certificate. Agents who wait until the due diligence period is half over find themselves requesting deadline extensions or watching the buyer terminate. The smart move is to order the certificate within 48 hours of binding agreement.

Third: Treating the HOA disclosure as optional for non-condo communities. While the Property Owners' Association Act has weaker enforcement teeth than the Condominium Act, the GAR F313 exhibit still creates contractual obligations. An agent who skips F313 because "it's just a subdivision" leaves the buyer without a structured review period and the seller without a clear termination cutoff date.

Fourth: Not verifying the accuracy of seller-provided association information on Form F314. Sellers frequently understate monthly dues, forget about pending special assessments, or misidentify the management company. Agents are not required to independently verify every detail, but passing along information you know to be inaccurate creates license law violations under GREC Rule 520-1-.06 regarding misrepresentation.

Fifth: Confusing the buyer's statutory cancellation right with the inspection contingency. These are separate rights with separate timelines. A buyer can waive the inspection contingency and still retain the right to cancel based on the resale certificate under O.C.G.A. Section 44-3-111. Agents who tell their seller clients "the buyer waived inspections, so we're safe" are giving dangerously incomplete advice.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokerage compliance in Georgia starts with file review, and HOA disclosure documents are among the most frequently missing items in transaction files. Brokers should build a checklist that flags any property located in a community association and verifies that the correct forms are attached before the file moves past the binding agreement stage.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Risk if Missing |
|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Property type classification | Condo vs. non-condo HOA | Wrong contract form used |
| GAR F313 attached | Exhibit present with completed deadlines | Ambiguous buyer termination rights |
| GAR F314 completed by seller | All fields answered, no blanks | Misrepresentation exposure |
| Resale certificate ordered | Ordered within 48 hours of binding | Missed contractual deadline |
| Certificate completeness | All O.C.G.A. 44-3-111 categories present | Buyer cancellation right stays open |
| Buyer acknowledgment of receipt | Signed and dated | Dispute over when review period began |

Brokers should also train agents on the distinction between the two governing statutes. A quarterly compliance session covering [proper transaction timelines](/blog/training-new-agents-timelines) and form selection prevents the kind of errors that generate GREC complaints. The brokerage's E&O carrier will also appreciate documented training — it demonstrates reasonable supervision if a claim arises.

Transaction management platforms can flag HOA-related tasks automatically, but only if the property is correctly tagged at intake. Brokers who rely on agents to self-identify association properties will inevitably miss some. A better approach ties MLS data fields to automated [deadline tracking across active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals), ensuring nothing slips through during high-volume months.
