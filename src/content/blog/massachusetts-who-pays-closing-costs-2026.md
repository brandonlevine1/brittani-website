# Massachusetts Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026: What Every Agent Must Know

The question of Massachusetts who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026 is not purely academic. It determines how you draft offers, how you advise clients during negotiations, and where your liability exposure sits if something goes sideways at the closing table. Massachusetts handles cost allocation differently from most states because of its attorney-closing requirement, its unique deed excise tax structure, and the specific forms mandated by state regulators.

This article breaks down what agents and brokers need to know heading into the second half of 2026, including the statutes that govern cost allocation, the forms you should be using, and the errors that most commonly trigger complaints to the Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons.

## The Statutory Framework Governing Closing Costs in Massachusetts

Massachusetts does not have a single statute that says "the buyer pays X and the seller pays Y." Instead, cost allocation is governed by a combination of state law, custom, and contract terms negotiated in the Standard Form Purchase and Sale Agreement (the Greater Boston Real Estate Board form, commonly called the "P&S").

The deed excise tax is the clearest statutory assignment. Under M.G.L. Chapter 64D, Section 1, the seller is responsible for the excise stamp on the deed at a rate of $4.56 per $1,000 of consideration. The only exception is Barnstable County, which imposes an additional county surcharge under the Cape Cod and Islands Water Protection Fund Act.

Recording fees, governed by [M.G.L. Chapter 36, Section 13](https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleIV/Chapter36/Section13), are typically split based on which party benefits from the recorded document. The deed recording fee falls on the buyer (who receives title), while the discharge of the seller's mortgage is at the seller's expense.

## Standard Cost Allocation: Buyer vs. Seller Breakdown

**Most disputes arise not from the big-ticket items but from the gray-area costs that Massachusetts custom—rather than statute—assigns to one party.** The table below reflects the prevailing allocation for a standard residential transaction in 2026.

| Cost Item | Typically Paid By | Governing Authority |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|
| Deed excise tax ($4.56/thousand) | Seller | M.G.L. c. 64D, s. 1 |
| Owner's title insurance premium | Buyer | Custom / P&S terms |
| Lender's title insurance premium | Buyer | Lender requirement |
| Attorney fee (own counsel) | Each party | Custom |
| Recording fee — deed | Buyer | M.G.L. c. 36, s. 13 |
| Recording fee — mortgage discharge | Seller | M.G.L. c. 36, s. 13 |
| Municipal lien certificate | Seller | M.G.L. c. 60, s. 23 |
| Smoke/CO detector certificate | Seller | M.G.L. c. 148, s. 26F |

The municipal lien certificate (MLC) deserves special attention. Under M.G.L. Chapter 60, Section 23, the municipality must issue this certificate, and custom places the cost on the seller since it confirms the property's tax and lien status. In 2026, MLC fees range from $25 to $150 depending on the municipality, but some towns are now charging rush fees exceeding $200.

## Forms and Disclosures That Agents Must Use

The Massachusetts Board of Registration of Real Estate Brokers and Salespersons, operating under the Division of Professional Licensure, requires agents to provide clear disclosure of their agency relationships and compensation structures. The relevant forms include the Mandatory Licensee-Consumer Relationship Disclosure form prescribed by 254 CMR 3.00.

**When it comes to closing cost estimates, agents should direct clients to the Loan Estimate (LE) and Closing Disclosure (CD) forms mandated by TRID under federal law, but the Massachusetts-specific P&S agreement is where the actual allocation gets locked in.** The Greater Boston Real Estate Board Standard Form Purchase and Sale Agreement, Section 7 ("Adjustments") and Section 11 ("Closing"), define how and when costs are apportioned between the parties.

Agents representing buyers must also be aware of 254 CMR 2.00(8), which prohibits licensees from making material misrepresentations about costs a buyer will incur. If you tell a buyer that the seller "always" pays for a particular cost that is actually negotiable, you are potentially violating this regulation.

For agents working transactions involving [wire transfers and escrow disbursements](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process), the closing attorney typically handles the HUD-1 replacement (the Closing Disclosure), but the agent's role in setting client expectations about cost allocation begins at the offer stage.

## What Happens When Agents Get It Wrong

Failure to properly disclose or accurately represent closing cost responsibilities carries real consequences in Massachusetts. The Board of Registration has the authority under M.G.L. Chapter 112, Section 87AAA to impose fines up to $1,000 per violation, suspend or revoke licenses, and require additional education hours.

Beyond regulatory action, agents face civil liability. A buyer who pays costs they were told the seller would cover has a potential claim for misrepresentation under M.G.L. Chapter 93A (the consumer protection statute), which allows for treble damages. This is not hypothetical—Massachusetts courts have awarded damages against agents who inaccurately communicated cost responsibilities, particularly in cases where the misrepresentation induced the buyer to proceed with a transaction they otherwise would not have.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk. If the Closing Disclosure reveals a materially different cost allocation than what was communicated during negotiations, either party's attorney can raise the issue at or before closing. In a tight market, a blown closing over $3,000 in disputed costs creates cascading liability across the entire transaction chain.

## Five Common Mistakes Massachusetts Agents Make on Closing Costs

**The first and most frequent error is assuming that "custom" equals "rule."** An agent who tells a seller they must pay for the buyer's title insurance because "that's how it's done" is confusing market practice with legal obligation. In Massachusetts, the buyer customarily pays for owner's title insurance—but even that can be negotiated.

The second mistake is failing to account for the Barnstable County deed excise surcharge. Agents listing properties on Cape Cod or the Islands must disclose the additional 2% Community Preservation Act surcharge (where adopted) on top of the standard $4.56 per thousand. Omitting this from a net sheet can mean a five-figure surprise for sellers.

Third, agents routinely forget about the smoke and carbon monoxide detector certificate requirement under M.G.L. Chapter 148, Section 26F. The seller must provide this certificate at closing, and the inspection and any necessary detector upgrades are the seller's expense. Agents who fail to calendar this requirement risk closing delays.

Fourth, many agents do not properly explain how seller concessions interact with lender caps. A conventional loan with less than 10% down typically limits seller-paid closing costs to 3% of the purchase price. Writing a seller concession of 4% into the offer results in a lender rejection that [creates the exact kind of deadline pressure](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) that derails transactions.

The fifth error involves the municipal lien certificate timeline. Agents who submit the MLC request too late discover that some Massachusetts municipalities take 10 to 14 business days to issue the certificate. Missing this window can push closing beyond the P&S deadline, triggering default provisions.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers operating under 254 CMR 2.00 have supervisory obligations that extend to how their agents communicate closing cost information. The regulation requires brokers to maintain systems that ensure agents provide accurate disclosures and comply with the Mandatory Licensee-Consumer Relationship Disclosure requirements.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Risk if Missed |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Net sheets provided to sellers | Accuracy of deed excise calculation, MLC fees, attorney fees | Consumer complaint, 93A claim |
| Buyer cost estimates at offer stage | Alignment with Loan Estimate, proper seller concession caps | Lender rejection, blown deal |
| P&S review before execution | Cost allocation in Sections 7 and 11 matches what was negotiated | Closing table dispute |
| Agency disclosure timing | 254 CMR 3.00 form delivered at first substantive contact | Board fine, license action |
| Wire fraud protocols | Verification of closing attorney wire instructions | Financial loss, E&O claim |

Brokers should implement quarterly file audits that specifically examine whether agents' pre-closing communications to clients match the final Closing Disclosure figures. A pattern of discrepancies—even small ones—indicates either sloppy practice or a training gap that needs immediate correction.

The supervisory obligation is not passive. Under M.G.L. Chapter 112, Section 87AAA, the Board can hold a broker personally responsible for an agent's violations if the broker failed to maintain adequate oversight. This means brokers who [rely on memory instead of systematic checklists](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) are exposing themselves to direct regulatory action.

## Negotiating Cost Allocation in the Current Market

The 2026 Massachusetts market has shifted some traditional cost allocations. In competitive situations, buyers are increasingly offering to pay costs that were historically seller expenses—including the deed excise tax in some extreme cases. While unusual, this is perfectly legal as long as both parties agree in the P&S.

Seller concessions remain the most common negotiation point. In buyer-favorable conditions, agents should structure concession requests with specificity: "Seller to contribute $8,000 toward buyer's closing costs" is enforceable and clear. Vague language like "seller to pay buyer's costs" invites dispute.

Agents should also be aware that the Massachusetts attorney-closing requirement means that both parties' attorneys will scrutinize cost allocation before the closing date. Unlike states where a title company simply processes the transaction, Massachusetts closing attorneys have a professional obligation to ensure their client is not bearing costs they did not agree to.
