# Florida Property Condition & Environmental Disclosures 2026: What Every Agent Must Get Right

Florida's disclosure framework confuses agents more than almost any other state's because it blends common-law obligations with scattered statutory mandates rather than offering a single unified disclosure form. Understanding **Florida property condition & environmental disclosures 2026** requirements is not optional — it is the difference between a clean closing and a post-sale lawsuit that drags on for years. This guide breaks down the specific statutes, form numbers, liability exposure, and enforcement gaps that every Florida licensee and broker should know heading into the second half of 2026.

## The Legal Foundation: Johnson v. Davis and Florida Statute 689

**Florida is a "caveat emptor with exceptions" state, not a statutory disclosure state.** Unlike Texas or Tennessee, where a specific state-mandated seller disclosure form exists, Florida relies on case law — primarily the 1985 Florida Supreme Court decision *Johnson v. Davis* (480 So. 2d 625) — to establish that sellers must disclose material facts affecting property value that are not readily observable by the buyer.

Florida Statute Chapter 689 governs conveyances of land and includes Section 689.25, which addresses disclosure requirements for certain properties in community development districts. Section 689.261 mandates coastal property erosion disclosures. Section 689.26 requires energy-efficiency rating disclosures, though this provision is rarely enforced in practice.

The practical effect is that Florida agents cannot rely on a single statutory checklist the way agents in [Texas can with their TREC form](/blog/texas-property-condition-disclosures-2026). Instead, Florida agents must assemble multiple disclosures from different sources and verify that sellers have addressed each applicable category.

## Required Forms and Where They Come From

Florida Realtors (the statewide trade association) publishes the forms most agents use to satisfy disclosure obligations. These are contractual tools, not state-mandated instruments, but they carry legal weight when incorporated into the purchase agreement.

| Form Number | Form Name | Purpose | When Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| SPDR | Seller's Property Disclosure - Residential | Documents known material defects | Best practice for all residential sales |
| CRSP | Comprehensive Rider to Sale and Purchase | Includes environmental and condition riders | When additional disclosures apply |
| Lead Paint Disclosure (federal) | Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | Federal requirement for pre-1978 homes | All pre-1978 residential sales |
| Radon Gas Disclosure | Radon Disclosure | Florida Statute 404.056(5) requirement | Every real estate sale contract |
| CDD Disclosure | Community Development District Addendum | Section 689.25 requirement | Properties within a CDD |

The radon disclosure language is specifically prescribed by statute — agents cannot paraphrase it. Florida Statute 404.056(5) requires the following statement appear in the sale contract:

> "RADON GAS: Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive gas that, when it has accumulated in a building in sufficient quantities, may present health risks to persons who are exposed to it over time. Levels of radon that exceed federal and state guidelines have been found in buildings in Florida. Additional information regarding radon and radon testing may be obtained from your county health department."

Failure to include this language does not void the contract, but it exposes the licensee to disciplinary action by the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) under Chapter 475, Florida Statutes.

## Environmental Disclosures Specific to Florida

Florida's geography and development patterns create environmental disclosure obligations that agents in landlocked states never encounter. Coastal erosion under Section 689.261 requires sellers of property seaward of the coastal construction control line to disclose that the property may be subject to erosion and government regulation.

**Sinkholes represent one of the highest-liability environmental issues in Florida residential transactions.** While no single statute mandates a "sinkhole disclosure form," sellers who have filed insurance claims related to sinkhole activity or who have knowledge of geological testing must disclose this under *Johnson v. Davis*. Section 627.7073 of the Florida Insurance Code governs sinkhole claims and creates documentation that listing agents should ask sellers about directly.

Chinese drywall (defective drywall imported between 2001 and 2009) remains a disclosure issue in parts of South and Southwest Florida. Sellers who performed remediation or who know of affected drywall must disclose it. The Florida legislature addressed this in Section 558.442 regarding construction defect notice requirements, and agents should confirm whether any prior remediation was performed per manufacturer recall standards.

Additionally, properties near or within former agricultural zones may require disclosure of environmental contamination from pesticides, fertilizers, or petroleum storage. The Florida Department of Environmental Protection maintains a contamination tracking database that agents can reference during due diligence.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of disclosure failures in Florida fall into three buckets: civil liability, regulatory discipline, and deal collapse.

Civil liability under *Johnson v. Davis* allows buyers to sue for rescission (unwinding the sale entirely) or damages (the cost of repair plus diminution in value). Listing agents who knew about or willfully ignored defects face personal liability alongside the seller. The brokerage can be dragged in under vicarious liability theories if the broker failed to supervise.

Regulatory discipline comes through the DBPR's Division of Real Estate, which enforces [Chapter 475, Florida Statutes](https://www.flsenate.gov/Laws/Statutes/2024/Chapter475). Violations of disclosure duties constitute a breach of Section 475.25(1)(b), which prohibits fraud, misrepresentation, and failure to disclose material facts. Penalties range from fines up to $5,000 per violation, mandatory education, probation, suspension, or license revocation.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk. Under the Florida Realtors/Florida Bar "AS IS" Residential Contract (Form CR-6), buyers who discover undisclosed material defects during the inspection period can cancel and receive their deposit back. Even after closing, discovery of concealed defects triggers potential rescission claims within the statute of limitations period.

## Five Common Mistakes Florida Agents Make

**First, relying solely on the "AS IS" contract language to eliminate disclosure duties.** The "AS IS" designation in Form CR-6 shifts the burden of discovery to the buyer during inspection — it does not eliminate the seller's obligation to disclose known material defects. Agents who tell sellers "you don't have to disclose anything because we're selling AS IS" are giving dangerously incorrect legal advice.

Second, omitting the radon disclosure from the purchase contract. This happens more often than it should, especially when agents draft addenda or use non-standard contract templates. The statutory language must appear verbatim in the contract, not in a separate document that gets lost in the file.

Third, failing to ask sellers about prior insurance claims related to sinkholes, Chinese drywall, or water intrusion. Sellers will not always volunteer this information. Listing agents should include specific questions about claims history in their pre-listing consultation and document the answers.

Fourth, ignoring the CDD disclosure requirement for properties in community development districts. This is especially common in new-construction communities in Central Florida where CDDs fund infrastructure through special assessments. Section 689.25 requires written disclosure before the contract is executed — not at closing.

Fifth, skipping federal lead-based paint disclosures for pre-1978 homes. Florida agents sometimes assume this only applies in northern states with older housing stock, but Florida has significant pre-1978 inventory, particularly in Jacksonville, Tampa, and Miami's historic neighborhoods. The federal requirement under 42 U.S.C. 4852d carries penalties up to $19,507 per violation.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers who treat disclosure compliance as an agent-by-agent honor system are accepting unnecessary risk. The DBPR holds brokers responsible for supervisory failures under Section 475.25(1)(u), and Florida courts have found brokerages liable when their agents' disclosure failures reflect systemic training gaps.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Radon language in contracts | Verbatim statutory text present | Every transaction |
| SPDR form completion | All fields addressed, no blanks | Every listing file |
| Lead paint disclosure (pre-1978) | Signed by all parties, pamphlet receipt confirmed | Every applicable transaction |
| CDD disclosure timing | Delivered before contract execution | Every CDD property |
| Sinkhole/claims inquiry | Documented in listing file | Every listing intake |

Brokers should maintain a disclosure checklist that transaction coordinators or office managers verify before a file moves from "pending" to "closed." If your team handles high volume, tracking these deadlines and form completions manually [is where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down). Automated compliance tracking — whether through a TC service or software — prevents the kind of file gaps that trigger DBPR audits.

Quarterly file audits should specifically pull a random sample of closed transactions and verify that every required disclosure document is present, properly signed, and dated appropriately. Brokers who only audit when a complaint arrives are already behind.

## Staying Current: 2026 Regulatory Developments

The Florida legislature continues to refine disclosure obligations. Bills introduced in the 2025 and 2026 sessions have proposed expanding flood disclosure requirements beyond what FEMA maps currently show, reflecting growing pressure from insurers and consumer advocacy groups. Agents should monitor the Florida Realtors legislative tracker for any mid-year statutory changes that affect form updates.

The DBPR has also increased its focus on environmental disclosures tied to climate-related property risks. While no new statute has passed as of this writing, the regulatory posture suggests that agents operating in flood-prone and coastal zones should err on the side of over-disclosure rather than under-disclosure.
