# Mississippi Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026: What Every Agent Must Get Right

If you handle any residential transaction involving a pre-1978 property in Mississippi, you are personally exposed to federal liability every time a lead paint disclosure is missed or mishandled. The Mississippi lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 have not changed substantively from prior years at the state regulatory level, but enforcement attention from HUD and the EPA has increased, and the Mississippi Real Estate Commission (MREC) continues to treat disclosure failures as grounds for disciplinary action. This guide breaks down exactly what you owe your clients, your broker, and yourself.

## The Federal Foundation That Governs Mississippi

Mississippi does not have a standalone state lead paint disclosure statute. Instead, the obligation flows entirely from the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulation at [24 CFR Part 35, Subpart H](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-24/subtitle-A/part-35/subpart-H). The regulation applies uniformly across all 50 states, and Mississippi agents are bound by it without any state-level carve-out or modification.

The rule applies to every sale or lease of "target housing," defined as residential dwellings constructed before January 1, 1978. Exemptions exist for housing exclusively for the elderly where no child under six resides, zero-bedroom units, and properties already certified lead-free by a licensed inspector. Everything else built before 1978 triggers the full disclosure protocol.

Because Mississippi lacks a separate state statute, agents sometimes assume the obligation is optional or loosely enforced. That assumption is wrong. MREC Rule 1502 establishes that licensees must comply with all applicable federal laws in conducting real estate transactions, and a federal disclosure violation can serve as the basis for a license complaint under Miss. Code Ann. Section 73-35-21.

## The Required Forms and Documents

**There is no Mississippi-specific lead paint disclosure form number issued by MREC.** Instead, agents must use the standard EPA/HUD disclosure form titled "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards." This two-page document is the legally operative instrument, and no substitute or abbreviated version satisfies the requirement.

The form contains three components that must be completed before the buyer becomes contractually obligated. First, the seller's disclosure section where the seller indicates known lead-based paint or hazards, or checks that they have no knowledge. Second, the buyer's acknowledgment section confirming receipt of the disclosure and the EPA pamphlet. Third, the agent certification section where both the listing agent and buyer's agent sign confirming that they have informed their respective clients of their obligations.

In addition to the form, the seller must provide the buyer with a copy of the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." The current version was last updated in 2021, and the EPA has indicated no revision is planned for 2026. Agents should verify they are distributing the most current version, as older editions from the 1990s still circulate in some office filing cabinets.

| Document | Who Provides It | When It Must Be Delivered |
|----------|----------------|--------------------------|
| EPA/HUD Lead Paint Disclosure Form | Listing agent prepares; both parties sign | Before buyer is obligated under contract |
| EPA Pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" | Seller (via listing agent) | Same as above |
| Known lead inspection reports | Seller | With or before disclosure form |
| 10-day inspection contingency notice | Buyer's agent | Written in contract or waived in writing |

## The 10-Day Inspection Window Agents Forget

Federal regulation grants the buyer a 10-day period to conduct a lead-based paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense. This is not a suggestion. The seller must allow it unless the buyer affirmatively waives the opportunity in writing. The parties may mutually agree to a different time period, but the seller cannot unilaterally shorten or eliminate it.

In Mississippi's fast-moving markets, particularly in the DeSoto County corridor and the Jackson metro area, agents sometimes treat this 10-day window as an inconvenience that can be glossed over. That is a compliance failure. If the purchase agreement does not explicitly address the lead inspection period, either by providing it or documenting the buyer's written waiver, the transaction is technically out of compliance from day one.

The waiver must be a separate written acknowledgment, not a buried clause in a 20-page contract addendum. HUD enforcement guidance has specifically flagged boilerplate waiver language hidden in general inspection contingency clauses as insufficient. ## Penalties and Liability When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of noncompliance operate on three separate tracks, and they can all hit simultaneously.

**Federal civil penalties under Title X can reach $21,018 per violation as of the 2025 HUD inflation adjustment, with no indication of a reduction for 2026.** Criminal penalties for knowing and willful violations carry fines up to $21,018 per day plus potential imprisonment. HUD's Office of Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity investigates complaints, and the EPA retains independent enforcement authority.

Private litigation represents the second track. Under 42 U.S.C. Section 4852d(b)(3), a buyer who was not provided the required disclosure may bring a civil action and recover treble damages. Mississippi courts have not produced a published appellate opinion on a lead paint disclosure claim as of this writing, but federal district courts in the Southern and Northern Districts of Mississippi have jurisdiction, and the treble damages provision makes these cases attractive to plaintiff's attorneys.

The third track is MREC disciplinary action. Under Miss. Code Ann. Section 73-35-21, the Commission may suspend or revoke a license for any conduct demonstrating incompetency, untrustworthiness, or dishonest dealing. A failure to comply with a known federal disclosure obligation fits squarely within that standard. MREC disciplinary orders are public records and can affect E&O insurance renewability.

## Common Mistakes Mississippi Agents Make

Five errors account for the majority of compliance failures observed in MREC complaint files and HUD enforcement actions across the Southeast.

**First, agents assume the disclosure is only required when they "know" about lead paint.** The obligation to disclose is triggered by the property's age, not by confirmed knowledge of lead hazards. Even if the seller checks the box indicating no knowledge of lead-based paint, the form itself and the pamphlet must still be delivered and signed.

Second, agents fail to retain the signed disclosure form for the required period. Federal regulation mandates that sellers and agents retain a copy of the completed disclosure for no less than three years from the date of sale. Many agents rely on their brokerage's transaction management system but never verify that the lead paint form was actually uploaded. If the file is incomplete and a complaint surfaces two years later, the agent cannot demonstrate compliance.

Third, agents use outdated pamphlets. The EPA pamphlet has been revised multiple times, and distributing a superseded version does not satisfy the regulatory requirement. The operative version as of 2026 is the one bearing revision date December 2012, with a subsequent reprint in 2021. Agents pulling copies from old listing packets are risking a technical violation.

Fourth, agents treat the disclosure as a listing-side-only responsibility. Both the listing agent and the buyer's agent must sign the certification section of the disclosure form. A buyer's agent who fails to ensure their client received the pamphlet and signed the acknowledgment is independently liable, regardless of what the listing agent did or did not do.

Fifth, agents confuse the lead paint disclosure with the Mississippi property condition disclosure required under Miss. Code Ann. Section 89-1-501 et seq. These are separate obligations with separate forms. Completing one does not satisfy the other. ## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under both MREC rules and federal regulation. 24 CFR 35.88(a)(2) explicitly states that "each agent" is responsible for compliance, and MREC Rule 1601 places affirmative supervisory duties on brokers for all transactions conducted under their license.

A broker's audit protocol should verify five things in every pre-1978 transaction file. The signed EPA/HUD disclosure form is present with all three sections completed. The EPA pamphlet delivery is documented, either through a signed receipt line on the disclosure form or a separate acknowledgment. The 10-day inspection opportunity was addressed in the contract, either by inclusion or written waiver. The disclosure form was signed before or simultaneously with the purchase agreement, not after. And the file retention clock is tracked so forms are not purged before the three-year minimum.

**Brokers who rely solely on agents self-reporting compliance are exposing the entire firm.** A single HUD complaint can trigger an audit of all transactions handled by that office within the relevant period. If systemic noncompliance is discovered, penalties multiply rapidly. The brokerage could face aggregate fines exceeding $100,000 from a single enforcement sweep.

Transaction coordination systems that automatically flag pre-1978 properties and generate disclosure checklists reduce this risk substantially. ## Mississippi Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026: Staying Ahead of Enforcement

HUD's enforcement priorities for 2026 emphasize rental housing, but sales transactions are not exempt from random audit or complaint-driven investigation. The Mississippi Department of Environmental Quality (MDEQ) handles lead abatement certification but does not directly enforce the real estate disclosure requirement. That enforcement lane belongs to HUD and EPA at the federal level, with MREC handling license discipline.

Agents who build the disclosure into their standard workflow for every listing appointment involving a pre-1978 home will never face a compliance gap. The form takes five minutes to complete. The pamphlet is free to download from EPA.gov. The 10-day inspection language is a single clause in the purchase agreement. None of this is burdensome unless it is forgotten.
