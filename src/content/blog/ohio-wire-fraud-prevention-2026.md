## Understanding Ohio Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026

Wire fraud losses in real estate topped $446 million nationally in 2023, according to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center. Ohio has not been spared. The Ohio wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 framework places specific obligations on licensed agents and brokers to warn consumers and document that warning before any funds change hands.

The Ohio Division of Real Estate and Professional Licensing, operating under the Ohio Department of Commerce, enforces these rules through its existing licensee conduct standards and the Ohio Revised Code (ORC) Chapter 4735. Agents who treat wire fraud advisories as optional paperwork are gambling with their license and their clients' life savings.

This is not a theoretical risk. Franklin County alone saw over $3.8 million in real estate wire fraud losses reported to law enforcement in 2024. The question is not whether your transaction will be targeted, but whether you documented the warning when it was.

## The Legal Framework: ORC Chapter 4735 and Division Rules

Ohio does not have a single standalone wire fraud statute aimed exclusively at real estate licensees. Instead, the obligation arises from ORC 4735.18(A)(6), which prohibits licensees from failing to disclose material facts, and ORC 4735.67, which imposes duties of disclosure and good faith on all licensees acting in a fiduciary or ministerial capacity.

**The Ohio Division of Real Estate interprets wire fraud risk as a known material fact that agents must disclose.** This interpretation was reinforced in a 2024 Division bulletin reminding licensees that failure to warn consumers about wire fraud schemes constitutes a violation of their statutory duties. The bulletin specifically referenced the obligation to provide written warnings.

Ohio REALTORS (the state trade association) developed Form 525, the Wire Fraud Advisory and Warning, to give agents a standardized disclosure document. While Form 525 is not mandated by statute, using it creates an evidentiary record that the agent fulfilled their disclosure duty under ORC 4735.18(A)(6).

## What Forms Ohio Agents Must Use

The primary document is Ohio REALTORS Form 525 (Wire Fraud Advisory and Warning). This form explains the mechanics of business email compromise, instructs consumers to verify wire instructions by phone using a trusted number, and requires a dated signature from the recipient.

Some brokerages in Ohio also require agents to use a supplemental Wire Instruction Verification Acknowledgment that specifies the exact callback procedures clients should follow. This is not a numbered Ohio REALTORS form but rather a brokerage-level compliance document.

| Form | Source | When to Deliver | Who Signs |
|------|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Ohio REALTORS Form 525 | Ohio REALTORS | At or before contract signing | Buyer and/or seller |
| Brokerage Wire Verification Acknowledgment | Individual brokerage | When wire instructions are first discussed | Buyer (typically) |
| Consumer Guide on Wire Fraud | Ohio Division of Real Estate | First substantive contact | Client receiving the guide |
| Title Company Wire Procedures Disclosure | Title/escrow company | At title commitment delivery | Buyer |

Agents should deliver Form 525 at the earliest moment funds or financing will be discussed. Waiting until closing week defeats the purpose because most wire fraud attacks occur in the days leading up to closing when urgency is highest and vigilance is lowest.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of skipping wire fraud disclosures break into three categories: regulatory action, civil liability, and deal collapse. None of them are abstract.

**Regulatory penalties from the Ohio Division of Real Estate range from formal reprimand to license revocation.** Under ORC 4735.18, the Division can impose fines up to $5,000 per violation, suspend a license for up to two years, or permanently revoke it. A pattern of noncompliance or a single incident resulting in consumer harm dramatically increases the severity of sanctions.

Civil liability exposes agents and their brokerages to lawsuits from defrauded buyers or sellers. Ohio courts have allowed negligence claims to proceed against licensees who failed to warn clients about foreseeable risks. If a buyer wires $80,000 to a fraudster and the agent never provided Form 525 or any written warning, the agent's errors and omissions insurance carrier will likely face a claim, and the agent's deductible and future premiums will reflect the loss.

Deal cancellation is the third consequence. When funds are intercepted, closings collapse. The buyer may lack the resources to wire again. The seller may refuse to extend the closing date. The entire transaction falls apart, and the agent earns zero commission on months of work. Understanding [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) starts with recognizing that compliance failures are often the invisible trigger.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Wire Fraud Compliance

Five errors appear repeatedly in Division complaint files and brokerage audits.

**First, agents deliver Form 525 at the wrong time.** Handing the wire fraud advisory to a buyer at the closing table provides zero protective value. The form must be delivered early enough for the client to absorb the information and implement verification procedures before they receive wire instructions.

Second, agents assume the title company handles everything. Title companies do provide their own wire verification disclosures, but this does not relieve the agent of their independent duty under ORC 4735.18(A)(6). The agent's obligation is separate and distinct from whatever the title company does.

Third, agents send wire fraud warnings via email without obtaining a signed acknowledgment. An email without a signature is difficult to prove was received and read. The Division expects documentation, which means a signed and dated form in the transaction file.

Fourth, agents fail to warn sellers. Wire fraud does not exclusively target buyers. Seller impersonation schemes have surged, where fraudsters pose as sellers and redirect proceeds. Both sides of the transaction need the advisory.

Fifth, agents use outdated forms. Ohio REALTORS updates its forms periodically, and using a superseded version of Form 525 can undermine the agent's compliance defense. Always pull the current version from the Ohio REALTORS forms library before each transaction.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory liability under ORC 4735.18(A)(22) for failing to adequately supervise their agents. A single agent's wire fraud compliance failure can trigger Division action against the brokerage itself.

| Audit Item | Frequency | What to Check |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| Form 525 in every transaction file | Per-transaction | Signed, dated, delivered before or at contract |
| Agent training documentation | Annually | Wire fraud CE credits or brokerage training logs |
| Email security policies | Quarterly | SPF/DKIM/DMARC on brokerage domain, agent compliance |
| Incident response protocol | Semi-annually | Written plan for what to do if a client reports fraud |
| Form version currency | Annually | Confirm agents are using current Ohio REALTORS forms |

**Brokers should build wire fraud compliance into their standard transaction checklist rather than treating it as a separate initiative.** A [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) that includes wire fraud advisory delivery as a gated step ensures nothing slips through during agent-to-TC handoffs.

Brokers also need to verify that agents are not transmitting wire instructions via email. Ohio REALTORS and the Ohio Division have both issued guidance that agents should never send wire instructions electronically. If an agent's email is compromised, the brokerage becomes the conduit for the fraud.

## Email Security and the Agent's Role

Most wire fraud begins with a compromised email account. Agents who use personal Gmail or Yahoo accounts for transaction communications create massive vulnerability. The Ohio Division has not mandated specific email platforms, but brokerage policies increasingly require domain-verified email with multi-factor authentication.

Agents should never forward wire instructions received from title companies. Even if the instructions are legitimate, the act of forwarding creates another potential interception point. Instead, direct clients to contact the title company using a phone number verified from an independent source, not a number found in an email.

This procedural discipline is part of the broader [wire instructions verification process](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) that protects both the client and the agent's license.

## Ohio-Specific Enforcement Trends in 2025-2026

The Ohio Division of Real Estate issued 14 formal complaints in 2024 related to disclosure failures in transactions where wire fraud occurred. Not all resulted in license suspension, but several led to consent agreements requiring additional continuing education and monitored practice periods.

The Division has signaled through its [advisory bulletins](https://com.ohio.gov/divisions-and-programs/real-estate-and-professional-licensing) that wire fraud prevention will remain a priority enforcement area through 2026. Licensees should expect that any consumer complaint involving wire fraud will trigger a file review that specifically examines whether Form 525 or an equivalent advisory was properly delivered and documented.

**Ohio agents approaching license renewal should verify they have met any wire fraud continuing education recommendations issued by their brokerage or the Division.** While the Division has not yet mandated specific wire fraud CE hours, voluntary completion of approved courses strengthens a licensee's defense if a complaint is filed.
