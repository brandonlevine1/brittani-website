# The TC Industry Is Consolidating — What That Means for Agents

The transaction coordination space is undergoing a wave of consolidation that will reshape how agents manage closings within the next 18 months. Three of the five largest independent TC firms in the U.S. have either merged or been acquired since early 2025, and private equity money is flowing into what was once a fragmented cottage industry. If you run a team or brokerage and haven't thought about what happens when your TC provider gets swallowed by a larger entity, you're already behind.

## The Acquisitions Nobody Is Talking About

**In Q4 2025, Transactly acquired two regional TC shops — one in the Mountain West and another operating across the Southeast — bringing its active agent count above 10,000.** That same quarter, a PE-backed roll-up strategy consolidated at least four small TC operations under a single holding company in Texas. These aren't splashy headlines because TC has always been treated as back-office infrastructure, not a product category worth watching.

But the economics tell a different story. The average independent TC charges between $350 and $495 per file. At scale, with standardized workflows and offshore labor, margins on that fee can exceed 60%. Investors noticed. According to data from [T3 Sixty's 2025 Swanepoel Trends Report](https://www.t3sixty.com/research/), transaction management services ranked among the top five growth categories in real estate ancillary services for the second consecutive year.

## Why Consolidation Is Accelerating Now

Three forces are driving this compression. First, NAR's settlement terms — finalized in late 2024 — created widespread commission uncertainty, pushing agents to cut overhead. That made outsourced TC services more attractive to a broader pool of agents who previously handled coordination in-house. Demand spiked, and larger firms had the capital to absorb that demand.

Second, the compliance burden increased. State regulators in California, Colorado, and Texas all expanded disclosure and documentation requirements between 2024 and 2026. The [California Department of Real Estate](https://www.dre.ca.gov/) alone added three new mandatory disclosure forms to residential transactions effective January 2026. Small TC operators without tech infrastructure struggle to keep up with changing forms and timelines. Larger firms with engineering teams can update templates across thousands of transactions overnight.

Third, the technology gap widened. Modern TC platforms now integrate directly with MLS systems, e-signature tools, and CRM databases. Building and maintaining those integrations costs money that solo TCs and micro-firms simply don't have.

## What Agents Lose When Their TC Gets Acquired

**The most immediate risk is service degradation masked by corporate efficiency language.** When a two-person TC shop gets absorbed into a 50-person operation, the agent who chose that shop for its responsiveness and local knowledge doesn't keep those qualities. They get reassigned to a queue. Response times go from hours to days. The TC who knew your preferred title company and understood your state's wet-signature requirements is replaced by a generalist working 40 files simultaneously.

I've spoken with team leads in Arizona and Georgia who experienced exactly this after their TC firms were acquired in mid-2025. One described it as "going from a concierge to a call center overnight." The acquiring company kept the brand name and the pricing — but gutted the service model to hit margin targets.

There's also a data question. When your transaction history, client contact lists, and workflow preferences live inside a platform owned by a firm that also serves your competitors, you've handed over operational intelligence without negotiating for it. [Understanding who controls your transaction data](/blog/transaction-data-ownership) is no longer a theoretical concern.

## The Counterargument: Scale Brings Benefits

The strongest case for consolidation is that professionalized, well-capitalized TC firms can invest in better technology, offer more consistent compliance coverage, and provide redundancy that solo operators cannot. If your independent TC gets sick, your closings stall. A firm with 30 coordinators on staff can reassign files instantly.

That's a real advantage, and I don't dismiss it. But the argument assumes that scale automatically translates into better outcomes for the agent. It doesn't. Scale translates into better outcomes for the firm's investors. The agent benefits only when the firm's incentives remain aligned with service quality rather than margin expansion — and post-acquisition, that alignment almost always deteriorates. The playbook from every other rolled-up service industry (think: veterinary clinics, dental practices, home services) confirms this pattern.

## What Smart Brokers Are Doing Right Now

**Forward-thinking brokerages are treating TC as a core operational function rather than a commodity to be outsourced to the lowest bidder.** Some are bringing coordination back in-house with dedicated staff. Others are building hybrid models — internal systems for workflow management paired with external TCs for overflow volume only.

The brokerages I respect most are auditing their vendor dependencies right now. They're asking: if our TC provider gets acquired tomorrow, what breaks? How quickly can we migrate? Do we own our templates, checklists, and timelines, or do those live in someone else's system?

These aren't paranoid questions. They're operational due diligence. The same discipline you'd apply to evaluating a title company or lender relationship should apply to your transaction coordination stack. [Building redundancy into your closing workflow](/blog/tc-workflow-redundancy) is table stakes in a consolidating market.

## The Technology Layer Changes Everything

The real disruption isn't one TC firm buying another. It's that AI-driven coordination tools are making the traditional TC model — a human managing 25-35 files using checklists and calendar reminders — look antiquated. When software can track deadlines, flag missing documents, send compliant communications, and adapt to state-specific requirements without human intervention, the value proposition of a mid-tier TC firm erodes fast.

This doesn't mean human TCs disappear. It means the work they do becomes higher-order: resolving exceptions, managing difficult lender relationships, handling title disputes. The routine 80% of coordination work — deadline tracking, document chasing, status updates — is being absorbed by technology at a pace that makes the consolidation wave feel almost secondary.

## Where This Leaves You

The agents and brokers who will maintain control over their transaction operations are those building systems they own rather than renting access to someone else's. That means investing in technology that sits within your operation, adapts to your workflows, and doesn't become leverage for a vendor to raise prices after locking you into their ecosystem.
