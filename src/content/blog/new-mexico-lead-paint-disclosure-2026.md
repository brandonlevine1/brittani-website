## Understanding New Mexico Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026

The New Mexico lead paint disclosure requirements 2026 apply to every residential real estate transaction involving a property built before 1978. This is not optional, negotiable, or something you can backfill after closing. Federal law—specifically the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018) and its implementing regulations at 24 CFR Part 35, Subpart F—mandates that sellers and their agents disclose known lead-based paint hazards before a buyer becomes obligated under a contract.

New Mexico does not have a separate state-level lead paint disclosure statute that supersedes the federal requirement. Instead, the New Mexico Real Estate Commission (NMREC) enforces compliance through its licensing standards and disciplinary authority under the Real Estate License Law, NMSA 1978, Sections 61-29-1 through 61-29-32. An agent who fails to deliver the required disclosure faces both federal penalties from the EPA and HUD, and state-level license discipline from the NMREC.

The practical effect is straightforward: if you list or sell a pre-1978 home in Albuquerque, Las Cruces, Santa Fe, or anywhere else in the state, you must ensure the lead paint disclosure is executed correctly before the buyer signs the purchase agreement—or at minimum, as an attachment to it.

## The Specific Forms Agents Must Use in New Mexico

**The primary document is the federally prescribed "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" form, commonly referred to as the EPA/HUD Lead Paint Disclosure form.** There is no separate New Mexico state form for this purpose. The EPA form is the controlling document, and it must be completed in full with signatures from the seller, the buyer, and their respective agents.

In practice, most New Mexico agents working through the New Mexico Association of Realtors (NMAR) will encounter this disclosure bundled into the NMAR Form 2105—the Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Addendum used alongside the standard Purchase Agreement (NMAR Form 2101). The addendum incorporates the federally required language, the seller's disclosure of known hazards, the buyer's acknowledgment of receipt of the EPA pamphlet "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home," and the buyer's election regarding the 10-day inspection period.

Agents must also provide the buyer with a copy of the EPA pamphlet itself. Simply referencing it on the form is insufficient. The pamphlet delivery must occur before the buyer is obligated, and the form must reflect that it was received. Failure to attach the pamphlet has triggered enforcement actions in other states, and there is no reason to believe New Mexico agents are insulated from the same scrutiny.

## Buyer vs. Seller Responsibilities: A Side-by-Side Breakdown

The division of duties under the lead paint disclosure framework often confuses newer agents who assume it falls entirely on the listing side. Both parties—and both agents—carry distinct obligations.

| Responsibility | Seller / Listing Agent | Buyer / Buyer's Agent |
|---|---|---|
| Disclose known lead paint or hazards | Required before contract execution | N/A |
| Provide copies of existing lead reports | Required—must attach all available records | Must receive and acknowledge receipt |
| Deliver EPA pamphlet | Must provide to buyer | Must confirm receipt on disclosure form |
| Offer 10-day inspection period | Must include in contract language | May accept, modify, or waive in writing |
| Sign the disclosure form | Both seller and listing agent sign | Both buyer and buyer's agent sign |
| Retain signed copies for 3 years | Listing agent must retain | Buyer's agent must retain |

This table matters because enforcement actions often target the agent who failed to countersign or retain records—not just the principal. Your signature on that form is a personal attestation of compliance.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

Federal penalties for violating the lead paint disclosure rule are not theoretical. Under 42 U.S.C. 4852d and the Toxic Substances Control Act (15 U.S.C. 2615), the EPA and HUD can impose civil penalties of up to $25,584 per violation (as adjusted for inflation in 2026). Criminal penalties, including imprisonment, are possible for knowing and willful violations.

**At the state level, the NMREC can take disciplinary action under NMSA 1978, Section 61-29-12, which authorizes license suspension, revocation, or fines for violations of federal or state law connected to real estate practice.** A failure to disclose lead paint does not need to result in actual harm to trigger discipline; the violation itself is sufficient.

Beyond regulatory penalties, buyers have a private right of action. A buyer who discovers undisclosed lead paint hazards can sue for treble damages (three times actual damages) under Section 1018 of Title X. They can also seek rescission of the contract, meaning the deal unwinds entirely—and you may be named personally in the suit alongside the seller. The statute of limitations for such claims is generally two years from discovery of the violation, not from closing.

Similar enforcement frameworks apply in neighboring states. Agents who also practice in Texas should review [Texas lead paint disclosure obligations](/blog/texas-lead-paint-disclosure-2026) to understand how the same federal mandate plays out under a different state commission's oversight.

## Common Mistakes New Mexico Agents Make

Five errors surface repeatedly in lead paint disclosure compliance failures across New Mexico brokerages. Each one is avoidable with proper systems.

**First, agents frequently fail to confirm the property's year of construction before listing.** County assessor records in Bernalillo, Santa Fe, and Dona Ana counties sometimes show conflicting dates. If there is any ambiguity about whether the home predates 1978, the safest course is to complete the disclosure. Relying on a seller's verbal statement that the home was "built in the early '80s" without verifying against public records is a compliance risk.

Second, listing agents present the disclosure form at closing rather than before contract execution. The federal rule is explicit: the disclosure must be made before the purchaser is obligated under a contract. Delivering it at the closing table—after the buyer has already committed—violates the timing requirement even if the content is accurate.

Third, agents fail to document the buyer's election regarding the 10-day inspection period. The form requires the buyer to affirmatively check a box indicating they will conduct an inspection, have already done so, or are waiving the opportunity. A blank checkbox creates ambiguity that can be weaponized in litigation.

Fourth, agents neglect to retain their signed copies for the full three-year period mandated by 24 CFR 35.92. Transaction files get purged, offices change hands, and digital records get lost in email migrations. If HUD audits your file 30 months post-closing and you cannot produce the signed disclosure, you are in violation.

Fifth, agents representing buyers in "as-is" transactions assume the lead paint disclosure is unnecessary because the buyer has accepted the property's condition. The "as-is" designation does not override the federal disclosure requirement. Period. An as-is sale of a pre-1978 home still demands full lead paint disclosure compliance. This misconception also appears in [discussions about where deals commonly break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) due to compliance gaps.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Qualifying brokers in New Mexico bear supervisory responsibility under NMSA 1978, Section 61-29-2(H), which defines the broker's duty to supervise associate licensees. A broker who fails to implement systems ensuring lead paint disclosure compliance can face personal disciplinary action—even if the broker was not directly involved in the transaction.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Verify disclosure present in all pre-1978 files | Every transaction at review | Signed by all four parties |
| Confirm EPA pamphlet delivery documented | Every transaction at review | Buyer acknowledgment on form |
| Check 10-day inspection election completed | Every transaction at review | No blank checkboxes |
| File retention verification | Quarterly | Minimum 3-year digital backup |
| Agent training on disclosure timing | Annually or at onboarding | Before contract execution, not at closing |

Brokers should not rely on agents self-certifying compliance. A second set of eyes—whether a transaction coordinator, compliance officer, or automated system—must verify that the disclosure was executed properly before the file moves to closing. The cost of a missed disclosure far exceeds the cost of a review step.

For brokerages managing high transaction volumes, [tracking multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) without a systematic approach almost guarantees that a lead paint disclosure will eventually slip through the cracks.

## The 10-Day Inspection Period and Contract Implications

The default 10-day lead paint inspection window is a federal right granted to buyers, not a suggestion embedded in state forms. Under 24 CFR 35.90, the seller must permit the buyer a 10-day period to conduct a risk assessment or inspection for the presence of lead-based paint or lead-based paint hazards. The parties can mutually agree to a different time frame, but the buyer cannot be forced to waive this right without their informed written consent.

In New Mexico purchase agreements using NMAR forms, the 10-day period is addressed within the Lead-Based Paint Disclosure Addendum (Form 2105). If the buyer elects to conduct an inspection and discovers lead hazards, the contractual consequences depend on how contingencies are structured in the main purchase agreement. A buyer who discovers significant lead contamination during this period can typically withdraw without forfeiting earnest money, assuming the contingency language supports it.

Agents should treat the 10-day window with the same seriousness as a home inspection contingency deadline. Missing it—or miscalculating when it starts—can eliminate the buyer's ability to exit the contract cleanly.
