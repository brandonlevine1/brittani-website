## Florida Buyer Agency Agreement Requirements 2026: The Statutory and Practical Framework

The post-settlement era has made Florida buyer agency agreement requirements 2026 a top compliance priority for every brokerage in the state. Since August 2024, when the National Association of Realtors settlement went into effect, Florida agents have operated under new expectations about when, how, and in what form they must secure written buyer representation agreements. The Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) and Florida Realtors have both updated guidance, and agents who treat these agreements as optional paperwork are exposing themselves and their brokers to real financial risk.

Florida statute has long required written agency disclosures under [Chapter 475, Part I, Florida Statutes](https://www.flsenate.gov/Laws/Statutes/2024/Chapter475/Part_I/). The 2024-2026 regulatory environment layered the NAR settlement mandates on top of existing state law, creating a dual-compliance obligation that catches many agents off guard. This article breaks down the specific forms, statutory requirements, enforcement mechanisms, and operational pitfalls that Florida agents and brokers need to address right now.

## The Statutory Foundation: Chapter 475 and FREC Rules

**Florida's agency disclosure requirements predate the NAR settlement by decades, but the settlement changed what "disclosure" alone can accomplish.** Under Section 475.278, Florida Statutes, a licensee must disclose the nature of the brokerage relationship at or before the time of entering into a listing agreement, before showing property, or before the earliest meaningful contact with a buyer. The statute recognizes three relationship types: transaction broker (the default), single agent, and no brokerage relationship.

Prior to the settlement, many agents operated as transaction brokers without a written buyer agreement, relying on the statutory default and a disclosure form. That approach no longer satisfies MLS participation rules or NAR membership obligations. A written buyer agency agreement is now required before an agent can tour a property with a buyer through any Realtor-affiliated MLS in Florida.

FREC Rule 61J2-10.028, Florida Administrative Code, governs the timing and delivery of brokerage relationship disclosures. Agents must still provide the appropriate disclosure form, but the buyer agency agreement is a separate, additional document that establishes the scope of services, compensation terms, and duration of the relationship.

## Which Forms Florida Agents Must Use

Florida Realtors publishes standardized forms that most brokerages adopt. The two primary buyer agency agreement forms are:

| Form | Name | Use Case | Exclusivity |
|------|------|----------|-------------|
| BBA-19 | Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement | Buyer works with one agent/brokerage exclusively | Exclusive |
| NBBA-5 | Non-Exclusive Buyer Brokerage Agreement | Buyer may work with multiple agents simultaneously | Non-exclusive |
| CRSP-16 | Consent to Transition to Transaction Broker | Used when agent transitions from single agent to transaction broker mid-deal | N/A |
| BRD-6 | Brokerage Relationship Disclosure | Required disclosure of relationship type under 475.278 | N/A |

The BBA-19 is the workhorse form for most residential transactions. It specifies the property type, geographic area, agreement duration, compensation structure (including whether the buyer or seller pays and what happens if a cooperating compensation offer is less than the agreed amount), and early termination provisions. The NBBA-5 serves agents working with less-committed buyer prospects who want flexibility.

**Every brokerage should verify that its agents are using the most current version of these forms, since Florida Realtors updates them regularly in response to regulatory changes.** Using an outdated version with incorrect compensation language can void commission claims or trigger FREC complaints.

## Compensation Disclosure: The Settlement's Biggest Change

The NAR settlement's most disruptive requirement for Florida agents is the prohibition on communicating offers of compensation through the MLS. Compensation can still be offered by sellers, but it must be negotiated outside the MLS system. This makes the buyer agency agreement's compensation section the single most important clause in the document.

Under the current framework, the buyer agency agreement must state the agent's compensation in a clear, specific amount — either a flat fee, a percentage of the purchase price, or an hourly rate. Blanket language like "whatever the seller offers" is no longer permitted. The agreement must also disclose that the compensation is negotiable and that the agent cannot receive compensation exceeding the amount specified in the agreement without additional written consent.

Florida Realtors Form BBA-19, Section 6, addresses compensation with specific blanks for the amount and a checkbox structure for how shortfalls between seller-offered compensation and the agreed rate will be handled. Agents who leave this section incomplete or use ambiguous language face commission disputes that brokers will have to mediate — or litigate.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Florida Buyer Agreements

Five errors appear with alarming frequency in FREC complaints and brokerage audits related to buyer agency agreements in Florida.

**First, agents present the agreement after already showing property.** The timing requirement is explicit: before touring. An agent who shows three homes, then asks the buyer to sign at the fourth, has already violated the standard. If the buyer subsequently works with another agent and purchases one of those first three homes, the original agent has a weak commission claim and a potential FREC violation.

Second, agents fail to specify a definite termination date. Section 475.25(1)(e), Florida Statutes, prohibits licensees from having "indefinite" agreements that bind consumers without a clear end date. An agreement without a termination date is voidable and can result in disciplinary action including fines up to $5,000 per violation.

Third, agents use the wrong relationship disclosure alongside the buyer agreement. An agent operating as a single agent must provide the single agent disclosure (Form SA-13) before or simultaneously with the buyer agreement. Providing a transaction broker disclosure while the buyer agreement implies single-agent fiduciary duties creates a conflict that FREC examiners flag during audits. This kind of paperwork mismatch is exactly [why agents miss deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) and compliance checkpoints.

Fourth, agents neglect to explain the compensation shortfall provision. When a seller offers less cooperative compensation than the buyer agreement specifies, the buyer may owe the difference. Agents who fail to explain this upfront face claims of misrepresentation under Section 475.25(1)(b) and potential rescission of the agreement.

Fifth, agents allow verbal modifications to the agreement's scope or term without written amendments. Florida's Statute of Frauds (Chapter 725) requires real estate agreements to be in writing. A verbal promise to extend the agreement or reduce the commission amount is unenforceable and creates liability.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences for non-compliance operate on three levels: regulatory, civil, and transactional.

At the regulatory level, FREC can impose penalties under Section 475.25, Florida Statutes. Violations include operating without proper written agreements, misrepresenting the agency relationship, and failing to deliver required disclosures. Penalties range from reprimands and mandatory education to fines of $1,000 to $5,000 per count, license suspension, and revocation for repeat or egregious offenders.

At the civil level, agents without valid buyer agreements lose standing to claim compensation. Florida courts have consistently held that an agent cannot recover a commission without a written agreement establishing the right to that commission. In disputes between cooperating brokers, the absence of a buyer agreement is a disqualifying deficiency. Errors and omissions insurance policies may also deny coverage for claims arising from transactions where the agent failed to secure proper agreements, as the failure constitutes a known procedural violation rather than a covered professional error.

At the transactional level, a missing or defective buyer agreement can blow up a deal. Title companies and closing attorneys increasingly request copies of buyer agency agreements before disbursing commission checks. If the agreement is unsigned, expired, or inconsistent with the compensation shown on the closing disclosure, the closing agent may hold funds in escrow pending resolution. This is one of the core scenarios [where deals break down](/blog/where-deals-break-down) in ways that damage client relationships permanently.

## What Brokers Must Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under Section 475.25(1)(u), Florida Statutes, for the actions of their sales associates. A broker who fails to implement systems for monitoring buyer agency agreement compliance is jointly liable for violations committed by agents under their license.

| Audit Item | Frequency | What to Check |
|------------|-----------|---------------|
| Agreement execution date vs. first showing date | Per transaction | Agreement must predate first property tour |
| Termination date present and reasonable | Per transaction | Must be a specific calendar date, not "until closing" |
| Compensation amount specified | Per transaction | Must be a definite number or percentage, not open-ended |
| Correct relationship disclosure attached | Per transaction | Must match the relationship type claimed in the agreement |
| Current form version used | Quarterly | Florida Realtors updates forms; outdated versions create liability |
| Agent training documentation | Semi-annually | FREC may request proof that agents received compliance training |

**Brokers should build buyer agreement verification into their transaction intake process, not rely on agents to self-report compliance.** The moment a new buyer transaction enters the brokerage's system, someone — whether an office manager, transaction coordinator, or automated compliance tool — needs to confirm the agreement exists, is properly executed, and matches the deal terms.

This is precisely the kind of repetitive compliance checkpoint that [transaction coordination systems](/blog/what-is-a-transaction-coordinator) exist to handle. Without a structured review process, brokerages discover gaps only when a commission dispute or FREC complaint forces the issue.

## Duration, Termination, and Renewal Pitfalls

Florida law does not prescribe a maximum duration for buyer agency agreements, but practical and regulatory considerations push toward shorter terms. FREC has signaled through informal guidance and continuing education materials that excessively long agreements — those exceeding six months without justification — may be scrutinized as potentially unconscionable, particularly when paired with liquidated damages clauses.

The agreement must include a mechanism for early termination. Form BBA-19 includes a protection period (sometimes called a "tail" or "override" clause) that entitles the agent to compensation if the buyer purchases a property the agent introduced during the agreement term, even after the agreement expires. This protection period must also have a definite end date.

Renewal provisions require separate written consent. An auto-renewal clause without affirmative buyer action is risky under Florida consumer protection statutes and may not survive a legal challenge. Best practice is to execute a new agreement rather than rely on renewal language.
