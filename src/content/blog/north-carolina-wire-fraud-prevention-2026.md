## Why North Carolina Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026 Demand Your Attention Now

Wire fraud losses in real estate topped $446 million nationally in 2024, according to the FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center. North Carolina was not spared—the state ranked among the top fifteen for reported business email compromise schemes targeting closings. Understanding North Carolina wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 is no longer optional education; it is a compliance obligation tied directly to your license.

The North Carolina Real Estate Commission (NCREC) has steadily increased its expectations around agent conduct regarding wire fraud warnings. While the Commission has not passed a standalone wire fraud statute, it enforces these duties through existing license law, specifically N.C. General Statute 93A-6(a)(1), which authorizes discipline for any act that demonstrates "unworthiness or incompetency" in protecting clients. Failing to warn a buyer about wire fraud now falls squarely in that category.

This post covers the exact forms you need, the mistakes that trigger liability, and what your broker-in-charge should be checking in every file.

## The Regulatory Framework Behind the Requirement

North Carolina handles real estate closings through licensed attorneys, not escrow companies. That distinction matters because the [wire instructions verification process](/blog/wire-instructions-verification-process) involves coordination between your office, the closing attorney's firm, and the lender. Each party carries independent responsibility to protect funds.

**The NCREC's position, clarified in multiple continuing education bulletins since 2022, is that agents must take affirmative steps to educate clients about wire fraud risks before closing.** The Commission references Rule 58A.0104, which obligates brokers to account for and safeguard trust funds and client property. While wire fraud typically targets funds before they reach the attorney's trust account, the duty of care begins the moment you establish a fiduciary relationship.

The North Carolina Association of REALTORS (NCAR) responded by creating standardized forms specifically designed to document that you met this obligation. These forms are your primary compliance tool.

## Required Forms and When to Present Them

NCAR Form 920, titled "Wire Fraud Warning," is the document most agents should be using. It is a standalone advisory that explains common wire fraud schemes, warns clients never to trust emailed wiring instructions without verbal verification, and includes a signature line confirming the client received and understood the warning.

**You should present Form 920 at the same time you present your agency agreement—not at closing.** Waiting until the closing table defeats the purpose. By then, funds have already been wired. The form must be in the file before any money moves.

Some brokerages also incorporate wire fraud language into their brokerage-specific addenda or buyer consultation packets. That practice is acceptable as long as the language is at least as protective as Form 920 and the client signs it separately. Burying a wire fraud paragraph on page seven of a fifteen-page buyer representation agreement does not demonstrate meaningful disclosure.

| Form | Purpose | When to Present | Who Signs |
|------|---------|-----------------|-----------|
| NCAR Form 920 (Wire Fraud Warning) | Warns client about wire fraud schemes | At initial representation agreement | Buyer or seller |
| NCAR Form 800 Series (Offer/Contract) | Contains general advisory language | At offer submission | All parties |
| Closing Attorney's Wire Verification Letter | Confirms legitimate wire details | 48-72 hours before closing | Buyer |
| Brokerage-Specific Wire Fraud Addendum | Supplements Form 920 per office policy | Per broker policy | Buyer or seller |

The closing attorney's office typically sends its own wire verification letter or secure portal link with legitimate wiring details. Your job is to make sure your client knows—well in advance—that this is the only legitimate source for those instructions.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make with Wire Fraud Compliance

Five errors appear repeatedly in disciplinary files and E&O claims across North Carolina. Any one of them can expose you to Commission action or civil liability.

**First, agents email wire instructions directly to clients.** Even if the closing attorney's office asked you to forward them, doing so puts you in the chain of custody for that information. If a hacker intercepts the email from your account, you become a defendant. Never forward wire instructions. Direct your client to call the closing attorney's office using a phone number from a verified source—not the one in the email.

Second, agents present the Wire Fraud Warning at closing instead of at the start of representation. The Commission evaluates whether you gave the client a meaningful opportunity to protect themselves. A form signed five minutes before wiring accomplishes nothing. Document the date and confirm it predates any fund transfer.

Third, agents fail to retain a signed copy in the transaction file. If a dispute arises eighteen months later, your memory is not evidence. The signed form is. Many agents who gave verbal warnings found themselves unable to prove it during Commission inquiries.

Fourth, agents assume the closing attorney handles everything. Attorneys have their own obligations, but yours are independent. The NCREC does not accept "the attorney warned them" as a defense for your failure to warn them yourself.

Fifth, agents use outdated versions of Form 920. NCAR updates forms periodically. Using a 2021 version in 2026 may omit references to newer fraud techniques like AI-generated voice impersonation or deepfake video calls, which are now referenced in current wire fraud education materials. Check the NCAR forms library at least annually.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers-in-charge carry heightened responsibility under [Rule 58A.0110](https://www.ncrec.gov/Commission/Rules), which requires them to supervise affiliated brokers and ensure compliance with Commission rules. A wire fraud loss tied to an agent's negligence reflects directly on the supervising broker's license.

**Every broker-in-charge should implement a file review protocol that specifically checks for a signed Wire Fraud Warning form.** This is not a suggestion—it is a risk management requirement that most errors and omissions insurers now mandate as a condition of coverage. If your E&O application asks whether you have a wire fraud prevention policy and you check "yes" without actually auditing compliance, you risk a coverage denial on claims.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Who Reviews | Consequence of Gap |
|------------|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Signed Form 920 in file | Every transaction | TC or broker | Disciplinary exposure under G.S. 93A-6 |
| Agent training on wire fraud | Annually minimum | Broker-in-charge | E&O coverage risk |
| Email policy compliance (no forwarding wire info) | Quarterly spot check | Office manager | Potential negligence liability |
| Form version currency | Annually | Compliance lead | Inadequate disclosure claim |
| Client confirmation of attorney contact | Pre-closing checklist | Transaction coordinator or agent | Breakdown in verification chain |

Brokers should also document their office wire fraud policy in writing and have each agent sign an acknowledgment. The NCREC has indicated in continuing education materials that a written office policy demonstrates the "systems and procedures" component the Commission looks for when evaluating broker supervision.

If you are tracking multiple deals simultaneously, this is exactly the kind of compliance checkpoint that gets missed under pressure. ## Liability Exposure: What Happens When You Fail to Comply

The consequences for noncompliance fall into three categories: regulatory, civil, and contractual.

On the regulatory side, the NCREC can pursue discipline under N.C. General Statute 93A-6(a)(1) for incompetency or unworthiness. Sanctions range from a letter of reprimand to license suspension or revocation. The Commission has historically been more aggressive with repeat offenders or cases involving actual client financial loss.

Civil liability is the more immediate financial threat. A buyer who loses $300,000 to wire fraud will name every professional in the transaction—agent, broker, closing attorney, and lender—in a negligence suit. Your defense rests almost entirely on whether you can prove you warned the client and took reasonable steps to prevent the loss. Without a signed Form 920 and documentation of verbal warnings, your position is weak.

Contractually, some purchase agreements now include wire fraud acknowledgment language. If a deal falls apart because stolen funds cannot be recovered in time, the failure to close can trigger earnest money disputes and potential breach of contract claims. Understanding [the true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) means recognizing that wire fraud does not just steal money—it kills transactions entirely.

## Practical Steps for Every Transaction in 2026

Build wire fraud prevention into your workflow at four specific points. At the agency agreement signing, present Form 920 and have the client sign it. At contract ratification, verbally remind the client that wire instructions will come only from the closing attorney's secure channel. Forty-eight hours before closing, confirm the client received legitimate wire details directly from the attorney's office. On closing day, verify that funds arrived as expected.

This four-touch approach creates a documented trail that satisfies both Commission expectations and E&O carrier requirements. It takes less than ten minutes across the life of a transaction but eliminates hours of liability exposure.
