## Understanding Vermont Agency Disclosure Requirements 2026

Vermont's agency disclosure framework is deceptively simple on paper but consistently trips up agents who treat it as a mere checkbox. The Vermont agency disclosure requirements 2026 remain governed by 26 V.S.A. Chapter 41 and the rules promulgated by the Vermont Real Estate Commission (VREC), which operates under the Office of Professional Regulation. If you hold an active license in the state, you are personally responsible for compliance regardless of whether your brokerage has internal systems to catch errors.

The core obligation is straightforward: before any substantive discussion takes place, you must present a written disclosure explaining the types of agency relationships permitted under Vermont law. "Substantive" is the operative word, and it means the moment a conversation moves beyond casual pleasantries into anything touching price, motivation, property condition, or negotiation strategy. Miss that window, and you have already created a compliance gap that no after-the-fact signature can fully cure.

Vermont's relatively small market size does not insulate agents from enforcement. The VREC reviews complaints actively, and neighboring state commissions have increased cross-border referral of licensee conduct issues—relevant for agents working along the New Hampshire and Massachusetts borders.

## The Statutory Foundation and Required Form

**Vermont's agency disclosure mandate originates in 26 V.S.A. Section 2296, which requires licensees to provide a written disclosure of agency relationships to all parties in a transaction.** The statute does not prescribe a single numbered form the way some states do. Instead, the VREC publishes a mandatory template titled "Disclosure of Agency Relationships" that agents must use without material alteration.

The form itself covers four relationship types that Vermont recognizes: seller's agent, buyer's agent, dual agent, and transaction facilitator (sometimes called a non-agent facilitator). Each relationship type carries different duties regarding confidentiality, loyalty, and disclosure of material facts. Your obligation is not just to hand over the form but to explain each option clearly enough that the consumer can make an informed choice.

You can find the current version of the mandated form on the [Vermont Office of Professional Regulation website](https://sos.vermont.gov/opr/real-estate/). Any time the Commission updates the form language, licensees are expected to adopt the new version immediately—ignorance of a revision is not a valid defense.

## Timing Rules That Create the Most Exposure

The timing requirement is where most liability originates. Vermont requires disclosure at "first substantive contact," which the Commission has interpreted broadly in disciplinary proceedings. An open house conversation that turns into a discussion about what a buyer can afford, a text exchange where a seller reveals their bottom-line number, or a phone call where an agent offers a pricing opinion all qualify.

| Scenario | Disclosure Required? | Why |
|----------|---------------------|-----|
| Buyer asks for an MLS listing link | No | Ministerial act, no substantive advice |
| Buyer asks "Is this a good price for the area?" | Yes | Agent providing market opinion |
| Seller asks "Can you sell my house?" at a social event | Yes | Solicitation of agency relationship |
| Open house visitor signs in and takes a flyer | No | No substantive discussion yet |
| Open house visitor asks about seller motivation | Yes | Confidential information territory |

The distinction between ministerial acts and substantive contact is not academic—it is the line that determines whether a complaint has teeth. If you are working [multiple active deals](/blog/track-multiple-active-deals) simultaneously, the risk of forgetting disclosure on one of them compounds quickly.

## Dual Agency and Designated Agency in Vermont

**Vermont permits dual agency but imposes strict informed-consent requirements that go beyond a simple signature.** Under 26 V.S.A. Section 2297, an agent or brokerage seeking to act as a dual agent must obtain written consent from both the buyer and the seller after providing a clear explanation of how dual agency limits the duties owed to each party.

The explanation must cover, at minimum, that the dual agent cannot advocate for one party's interests over the other, cannot disclose confidential negotiation information from either side without permission, and cannot advise either party on price or terms in a way that disadvantages the other. A bare signature on a form without this explanation leaves the agent exposed.

Vermont also recognizes designated agency within a brokerage, where two licensees within the same firm represent opposing sides. The brokerage must have a written policy on file with the VREC outlining how it manages designated agency, and both clients must consent in writing. This is distinct from dual agency because each party retains a dedicated advocate—but the broker still bears supervisory liability for ensuring the firewall holds.

## What Happens When Agents Fail to Comply

The consequences of non-compliance fall into three categories: regulatory discipline, civil liability, and transactional fallout. None of them are theoretical.

On the regulatory side, the VREC can impose fines of up to $1,000 per violation, issue a formal reprimand, suspend a license for a defined period, or revoke a license entirely for repeat or egregious violations. The Commission's disciplinary orders are public record, which means a single lapse can follow your reputation for years.

Civil liability is harder to quantify but potentially far more expensive. A party who can demonstrate they were harmed by an undisclosed conflict—say a dual agency situation they never consented to—can pursue damages in court. Vermont courts have recognized causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty, and the absence of a signed disclosure form makes it extremely difficult for the agent to prove the relationship was properly established. Deal cancellation is also on the table; a buyer or seller who discovers a disclosure failure before closing may have grounds to void the contract entirely, triggering [the kind of cascading deadline failures](/blog/real-cost-of-missed-deadlines) that damage everyone involved.

## Common Mistakes Vermont Agents Make

Five errors appear repeatedly in VREC disciplinary files and in the closing-day scrambles that brokers have to clean up.

**First, agents delay disclosure until the purchase agreement stage.** By then, weeks of substantive communication have already occurred without the required form in place. The statute says first substantive contact, not first written offer. Retroactive disclosure does not cure the gap.

Second, agents treat the disclosure form as a one-time obligation and fail to re-disclose when the relationship changes. If you start as a buyer's agent and the client wants to see one of your own listings, the shift to potential dual agency triggers a new disclosure obligation. The original form does not cover this scenario.

Third, agents allow clients to initial the form without verbally explaining the options. Vermont's standard expects that the consumer understands what they are consenting to, and a rushed initialing at a kitchen table does not satisfy that standard if the consumer later claims they had no idea what dual agency meant.

Fourth, agents working with unrepresented parties—such as a listing agent communicating directly with a buyer who has no agent—fail to disclose that they represent the seller and owe no duties to the buyer. The form must still be provided to the unrepresented party, and the agent must clearly state their role.

Fifth, teams and assistants handle initial client contact without providing the disclosure because "the lead agent will do it later." Vermont holds the licensee responsible regardless of internal delegation. If your unlicensed assistant takes a call that becomes substantive, you have already missed the window.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under 26 V.S.A. Section 2299 for the acts of their affiliated licensees. This is not a passive obligation—it requires affirmative systems.

| Audit Point | Frequency | What to Look For |
|-------------|-----------|------------------|
| Signed disclosure forms in transaction files | Every transaction | Form present, dated before or at first substantive contact |
| Dual agency consent documentation | Every dual agency deal | Separate signed consent from both parties, explanation documented |
| Designated agency policy on file with VREC | Annually | Current version, reflecting actual brokerage practice |
| Agent training records on disclosure | Annually | Documented training, not just a verbal reminder |
| Team and assistant protocols | Quarterly | Written procedures for who delivers disclosure at first contact |

A broker who discovers a missing disclosure mid-transaction faces an uncomfortable choice: disclose the error to the parties (risking deal disruption) or hope nobody notices (risking far worse consequences later). The right answer is always the first option, paired with immediate corrective action and documentation that the broker took steps to remedy the situation.

Brokers should also audit how their agents handle [seller disclosure obligations](/blog/vermont-seller-disclosure-requirements-2026), since agency disclosure and property condition disclosure are often confused or combined in ways that leave gaps in both.

## How Other New England States Compare

Vermont's approach sits between the more prescriptive frameworks of states like Rhode Island and the lighter-touch models further south. Understanding where Vermont falls on this spectrum helps agents who work across state lines or refer clients regionally.

| Requirement | Vermont | Rhode Island | South Carolina |
|-------------|---------|--------------|----------------|
| Timing trigger | First substantive contact | First personal meeting | First substantive contact |
| Dual agency permitted | Yes, with written consent | Yes, with written consent | Yes, with written consent |
| State-published form required | Yes (VREC template) | Yes (numbered form) | Yes (SCR form) |
| Penalty for non-compliance | Up to $1,000/violation + license action | Fine + license action | Fine + license action |
| Transaction facilitator recognized | Yes | No | No (but limited dual agent role exists) |

Agents licensed in multiple New England states should review [Rhode Island's agency disclosure rules](/blog/rhode-island-agency-disclosure-requirements-2026) alongside Vermont's, because the timing triggers differ subtly and the forms are not interchangeable.
