## Washington Commission Structure Rules After the NAR Settlement 2026: The New Baseline

The days of assuming a seller's listing agent would simply split a predetermined commission with a buyer's agent are over in Washington. Understanding Washington commission structure rules after the NAR settlement 2026 is no longer optional knowledge—it is the operational backbone of every residential transaction in the state. Agents who treat these changes as minor paperwork tweaks risk deal cancellations, license complaints, and personal liability.

Washington's real estate market operates under the oversight of the Washington State Department of Licensing (DOL), which regulates licensees through RCW 18.85 and WAC 308-124. The Northwest Multiple Listing Service (NWMLS) layered its own practice changes on top of the national settlement requirements, creating a state-specific compliance environment that differs from neighboring Oregon and Idaho. If you closed transactions before August 2024 and haven't retooled your workflow, this post maps out exactly what changed, what forms you need, and where agents are getting tripped up.

## How the NAR Settlement Changed Commission Mechanics in Washington

**The core structural shift is simple: buyer-agent compensation can no longer be advertised, offered, or negotiated through the MLS.** NWMLS removed the buyer-agent compensation field from its listing input sheets effective August 17, 2024. That field is gone—not hidden, not optional, gone.

Sellers can still agree to pay a buyer's agent, but the mechanism changed. Any such agreement must happen outside the MLS, typically through direct negotiation memorialized in the purchase and sale agreement or an addendum. NWMLS Form 21 (Residential Purchase and Sale Agreement) now includes updated sections addressing seller concessions that could include compensation to the buyer's agent, but only when requested in the offer.

This means every buyer's agent must enter a transaction with a clear, signed compensation agreement with their own client before writing an offer. The compensation terms live in the buyer agency agreement, not in the listing.

## Required Forms and Where to Find Them

Washington agents working through NWMLS rely on specific form numbers that have been updated to reflect post-settlement compliance. Here is a breakdown of the forms most directly affected:

| Form | Name | Key Change Post-Settlement |
|------|------|---------------------------|
| NWMLS Form 41 | Buyer Representation Agreement | Now required before touring; must state compensation amount or rate; sets maximum agent can earn |
| NWMLS Form 21 | Residential Purchase and Sale Agreement | Seller concession language updated; no pre-filled buyer-agent commission line |
| NWMLS Form 22D | Optional Clauses Addendum | Used to request seller-paid buyer-agent compensation as part of offer terms |
| NWMLS Form 1A | Listing Agreement (Exclusive) | Seller's obligation to pay listing broker only; no cooperative compensation commitment |

Agents operating outside NWMLS territory or using brokerage-proprietary forms must ensure those forms meet DOL requirements under WAC 308-124-020, which mandates that compensation disclosures be "clear, conspicuous, and provided in writing prior to the performance of licensed activities." The DOL has not issued a state-specific rule superseding the NAR settlement practices, but it enforces existing statutory requirements that align with them—particularly RCW 18.86.030, which governs the duties of a buyer's agent.

## What Happens If an Agent Fails to Comply

Non-compliance doesn't just create awkward conversations. It exposes agents and brokers to layered consequences:

**The DOL can initiate disciplinary action under RCW 18.85.361 for violations including failure to disclose material facts, acting without written authorization, or failing to account for funds.** A buyer's agent who tours homes without a signed Form 41, then later attempts to collect compensation from a seller concession, has arguably performed licensed activity without written authorization for their fee.

From a transactional standpoint, a missing or defective buyer agency agreement can give a buyer grounds to cancel. If the buyer discovers mid-transaction that they owe their agent a fee not properly disclosed upfront, they may invoke rescission rights or refuse to close. That means a dead deal and potential earnest money disputes.

NWMLS itself can issue fines for rule violations and suspend MLS access. The fine schedule ranges from $1,000 for a first offense to $5,000 for repeat violations, plus mandatory re-education. And these infractions feed into DOL complaint files if a consumer files a grievance.

Civil liability adds another layer. If a buyer pays compensation they didn't understand they owed, the agent faces exposure under the Washington Consumer Protection Act (RCW 19.86), which allows treble damages for unfair or deceptive practices. The settlement was designed to prevent exactly this kind of opacity—agents who recreate it invite lawsuits.

## Common Mistakes Agents Are Making Right Now

Even agents who attended the mandatory settlement-related trainings are stumbling. Here are the five most frequent errors showing up in transaction reviews and DOL complaints:

**First, agents are using outdated forms.** Some brokerages still have pre-August 2024 versions of Form 41 circulating in their template libraries. These older versions lack the required compensation ceiling language and do not include the NWMLS-mandated disclosure about MLS non-involvement in compensation. Using an outdated form is functionally the same as not having one.

Second, agents are verbally agreeing to compensation adjustments without amending the written agreement. A buyer's agent whose Form 41 says 2.5% cannot accept 3% from a seller concession without a written amendment. The agreement sets a maximum. Exceeding it without documentation violates DOL requirements and creates audit problems.

Third, listing agents are still signaling cooperative compensation through private remarks or showing instructions. NWMLS rules explicitly prohibit any reference to buyer-agent compensation in listing remarks, photos, supplements, or any MLS field. Agents who type "seller will contribute to buyer agent fee—call for details" in private remarks face immediate fines.

Fourth, buyer's agents are presenting the compensation request in the offer without explaining it to the buyer. The buyer must understand that requesting seller-paid compensation reduces their negotiating power on price or other concessions. Failing to explain this trade-off is a breach of the duty of loyalty under RCW 18.86.050.

Fifth, agents are treating the buyer agreement as a one-time checkbox rather than a living document. If terms change—if the buyer decides to look at higher-priced homes, if you agree to reduce your rate to make an offer work—the agreement must be amended in writing each time. Verbal modifications are unenforceable and create liability exposure.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Designated brokers in Washington carry supervisory liability under RCW 18.85.285. That statute makes the designated broker responsible for ensuring every licensee under their supervision complies with real estate law and rules. Post-settlement, that responsibility has grown heavier.

**Every brokerage should be running a quarterly audit of buyer agency agreements to verify they are signed, dated before first showing activity, and contain compliant compensation language.** The audit should cross-reference showing dates in showing management platforms against Form 41 execution dates. A signed agreement dated after the first showing is a red flag.

| Audit Item | What to Check | Red Flag |
|------------|---------------|----------|
| Form 41 execution date | Must predate first showing or touring activity | Agreement signed same day or after first tour |
| Compensation ceiling | Must state specific amount or percentage | Blank, "TBD," or range without maximum |
| MLS listing remarks | No reference to buyer-agent compensation | Keywords like "bonus," "co-op," or "buyer agent paid" |
| Form 21 concession requests | Compensation request matches Form 41 terms | Requested amount exceeds Form 41 ceiling |
| Amendment documentation | Written amendment for any compensation changes | Email-only or verbal confirmation in file |

Brokers should also review their office policy manuals and update them to explicitly prohibit compensation signaling in MLS entries. A written policy protects the brokerage if an individual agent violates the rule—without one, the designated broker may share liability.

Training frequency matters too. The DOL expects [ongoing education](https://www.dol.wa.gov/business/realestate/) aligned with current rules, not just the one-time settlement webinars from 2024. Brokers who can demonstrate regular training—documented with sign-in sheets and agendas—stand on stronger ground if a complaint arises.

## The Practical Impact on Offer Strategy

These rule changes don't just affect paperwork—they reshape how agents write competitive offers. In a multiple-offer scenario, a buyer requesting seller-paid agent compensation is asking the seller to net less. That request competes against buyers who either pay their own agent or whose agent agreed to a lower fee.

Washington's market conditions in 2026 vary by county, but in high-demand areas like King, Snohomish, and Pierce counties, sellers have little incentive to agree to buyer-agent compensation unless the offer price or terms compensate. Buyer's agents who refuse to discuss fee flexibility with their clients are losing offers—and losing clients.

This doesn't mean agents should race to the bottom on fees. It means the conversation about compensation must happen early, honestly, and in writing. The Form 41 is where that conversation lives. Agents who treat it as an administrative afterthought rather than a strategic document are handicapping their buyers before the first offer goes out.
