## Understanding New Jersey Who Pays Closing Costs — Buyer vs. Seller 2026

The question of **New Jersey who pays closing costs — buyer vs. seller 2026** is not purely academic for licensed agents. Misallocating even a single fee on an estimated net sheet can collapse a transaction, trigger a complaint to the New Jersey Real Estate Commission (NJREC), or expose your brokerage to E&O claims. New Jersey's attorney-review custom and its unique Realty Transfer Fee structure make cost allocation more nuanced here than in most states.

Every residential deal in the Garden State involves closing costs that typically range from 2% to 5% of the purchase price for buyers and 6% to 8% for sellers (once commissions are included). Those percentages shift based on local custom, negotiation strength, and statutory mandates. Agents who cannot articulate exactly which fees are fixed by law, which are customary, and which are purely negotiable will lose credibility at the table and risk regulatory exposure.

## Statutory Framework Governing Closing Cost Allocation

New Jersey does not have a single statute that assigns every closing cost to one party. Instead, allocations derive from a patchwork of statutes, administrative code provisions, and longstanding title company custom. The primary laws agents must know are N.J.S.A. 46:15-5 through 46:15-10 (the Realty Transfer Fee Act), N.J.S.A. 54A:8-8 (the Gross Income Tax withholding for sellers), and N.J.A.C. 11:5-6 (the NJREC's standards of agent conduct).

The [New Jersey Division of Revenue](https://www.nj.gov/treasury/taxation/rttgeninfo.shtml) administers the Realty Transfer Fee, which is calculated on a sliding scale and paid at recording. Under N.J.S.A. 46:15-7, the seller bears this fee unless the contract states otherwise. That statutory default matters because many out-of-state agents wrongly assume transfer taxes are always split.

**N.J.S.A. 54A:8-8 requires that sellers who are non-residents of New Jersey or who will become non-residents upon sale must pay an estimated gross income tax of 8.97% of the gain or 2% of the consideration (whichever is greater) at closing.** This is not optional. The title company withholds it, and an agent who fails to flag the obligation early can blow the seller's net proceeds calculation entirely.

Custom varies by county. In Bergen and Essex counties, sellers sometimes pay for the buyer's owner's title policy as a negotiation incentive in competitive markets. In Ocean and Cape May counties, buyers nearly always cover their own title work. Agents must know local norms or risk drafting offers that read as out of touch.

## Key Forms and Documents Agents Must Use

New Jersey's standard residential contract is the NJ REALTORS Form 118 (Statewide), which contains specific provisions in Section 16 addressing cost allocation for items like title insurance, survey, and inspections. Agents listing cost responsibilities in the Additional Terms section of Form 118 must be precise because attorney-review modifications frequently alter these provisions.

**The NJREC requires that all estimated net sheets provided to sellers comply with N.J.A.C. 11:5-6.4(a)(2), which mandates that agents present reasonably accurate financial information.** A net sheet that omits the Realty Transfer Fee or the GIT withholding violates this standard. Agents should also be familiar with the TRID-compliant Closing Disclosure (CFPB Form H-25), which the lender issues and which agents should cross-reference against their own estimates.

For buyer representation, agents often prepare a cost estimate using their brokerage's proprietary worksheet or the NJ REALTORS Buyer Estimate of Costs (informally tracked as a companion to Form 118). While not a regulated form, inaccurate estimates undermine client trust and may constitute [a deal breakdown](/blog/where-deals-break-down) that was entirely avoidable.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Closing Costs in NJ

**Mistake 1: Forgetting the mansion tax exists.** Agents listing properties at $975,000 sometimes encourage sellers to price at $1,010,000 without warning the buyer that a 1% mansion tax (approximately $10,100) now applies. That single oversight changes the buyer's cash-to-close figure by five figures and routinely kills deals during attorney review.

**Mistake 2: Treating the Realty Transfer Fee as a split cost.** Because some states split transfer taxes, agents relocating to NJ or working with out-of-state referral partners often draft contracts proposing a 50/50 split. This contradicts the statutory default and confuses title companies. If the contract is silent, the seller pays. If the contract says "split," both attorneys will flag it, and the ensuing negotiation delays closing.

**Mistake 3: Ignoring the GIT withholding for sellers moving out of state.** A seller who is currently a New Jersey resident but plans to relocate to Florida before closing becomes a non-resident seller under the statute. Agents who do not ask about post-closing residency plans leave sellers blindsided by a 2% withholding that the title company will not waive without a properly filed GIT/REP-3 exemption form.

**Mistake 4: Failing to update cost estimates after attorney-review changes.** Attorney review in NJ (typically a three-business-day period per contract) often results in modifications to who pays for inspections, repairs, and even title charges. Agents who do not revise their net sheets post-review are working off stale numbers. This is where [missed deadlines](/blog/why-agents-miss-deadlines) compound into blown closings.

**Mistake 5: Verbally promising seller credits without documenting them.** NJREC prohibits agents from making representations that are not reflected in writing. A verbal assurance that "the seller will cover your closing costs up to $10,000" means nothing if it does not appear in the contract or an amendment. The buyer's lender will not honor it, and the agent faces a potential N.J.A.C. 11:5-6.1 violation.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers of record in New Jersey carry supervisory liability under N.J.A.C. 11:5-1.24. If an agent in your office consistently misquotes closing cost allocations, the NJREC can hold your brokerage accountable even if you were unaware of the specific error. Proactive auditing is not optional — it is a regulatory expectation.

First, brokers should audit estimated net sheets at listing intake. Compare the agent's figures against a standardized template that includes the Realty Transfer Fee, GIT withholding (with a checkbox for non-resident status), and county-specific recording fees. A [transaction handoff checklist](/blog/transaction-handoff-checklist) that includes a cost-verification step catches errors before they reach the client.

Second, brokers must confirm that every contract submitted includes explicit cost allocation language in Section 16 or the Additional Terms of Form 118. A contract silent on cost allocation is not inherently defective, but it invites attorney-review disputes that slow closings and generate client complaints.

Third, track post-attorney-review amendments. If an amendment shifts a cost from seller to buyer (or vice versa), the agent must produce a revised net sheet within 48 hours. Brokers who build this into their compliance workflow reduce E&O exposure significantly.

| Audit Point | Frequency | Responsible Party | Consequence of Failure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Net sheet accuracy at listing | Every listing | Listing agent + broker review | NJREC complaint, E&O claim |
| Form 118 cost allocation language | Every contract | Transaction coordinator or broker | Attorney-review delays, deal collapse |
| Post-review amendment cost updates | Within 48 hours of amendment | Listing and buyer's agent | Client trust breach, potential misrepresentation |
| GIT withholding flag for non-resident sellers | At listing and at contract | Listing agent | Seller shortfall at closing, possible rescission |

## Liability When Agents Get It Wrong

The consequences of misrepresenting closing cost responsibilities extend beyond unhappy clients. Under N.J.A.C. 11:5-6.4, an agent who provides materially inaccurate financial information to a consumer can face disciplinary action including license suspension (up to two years), fines of up to $10,000 per violation, and mandatory continuing education requirements.

Deal cancellation is the most immediate risk. If a buyer discovers at the closing table that their cash-to-close is $8,000 higher than the agent represented, they may refuse to close. The seller then faces carrying costs, potential re-listing, and a possible claim against the buyer's agent's brokerage. These are exactly the scenarios described in our analysis of [the true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing).

Beyond regulatory action, civil liability looms. New Jersey courts have held that real estate agents owe a duty of care when providing financial estimates, even if those estimates carry disclaimers. A disclaimer on a net sheet stating "for informational purposes only" does not immunize an agent from a negligence claim if the numbers were recklessly wrong.

## Negotiation Realities in the 2026 Market

Market conditions in 2026 affect who actually ends up paying what, regardless of statutory defaults. In seller's markets (still prevalent in Hudson, Bergen, and Morris counties), buyers routinely waive requests for seller credits. In buyer's markets (parts of South Jersey and some inland Warren County communities), sellers are offering 3% to 6% in closing cost credits to attract offers.

Agents must separate statutory obligations from negotiable items in client conversations. A seller cannot negotiate away the GIT withholding — the title company withholds it regardless of contract language. But a seller can agree to pay for the buyer's title insurance, the survey, or a portion of prepaid taxes. The contract must specify these concessions clearly, and the buyer's lender must approve them within TRID tolerances.
