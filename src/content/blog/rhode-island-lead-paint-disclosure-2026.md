## Why Rhode Island Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements 2026 Demand Your Attention

Rhode Island has one of the oldest housing stocks in the country—roughly 80 percent of residential structures were built before 1978. That statistic alone makes understanding **Rhode Island lead paint disclosure requirements 2026** a non-negotiable part of your practice. Get this wrong and you face federal fines, license complaints, and deal-killing litigation.

The state layers its own regulations on top of the federal Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act of 1992 (Title X, Section 1018). Rhode Island's Lead Poisoning Prevention Act (R.I. Gen. Laws Chapter 23-24.6) and the rules enforced by the Rhode Island Department of Health (RIDOH) create a disclosure environment that is more aggressive than what most agents encounter in neighboring states. Understanding both the federal floor and the state ceiling is what separates compliant agents from those writing checks to attorneys.

## The Federal Foundation: EPA Rule 40 CFR Part 745

Every agent licensed in Rhode Island must comply with the EPA's Rule 40 CFR Part 745, Subpart F. This regulation requires sellers and landlords of pre-1978 housing to disclose known lead-based paint hazards, provide buyers with available records, and furnish the EPA pamphlet titled "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home." The rule also mandates a 10-day inspection opportunity for buyers.

**The specific federal disclosure form used in most Rhode Island transactions is the "Disclosure of Information on Lead-Based Paint and/or Lead-Based Paint Hazards" (sometimes referred to as the "EPA/HUD Disclosure Form").** Both the seller and buyer—along with their respective agents—must sign this form before or at the time a purchase and sale agreement is executed. The form is not optional; it is a condition of lawful transfer.

Agents acting as dual agents or transaction brokers carry the same obligation. There is no exemption based on agency relationship. If you represent any party in the sale of a pre-1978 dwelling, you must confirm disclosure compliance.

## Rhode Island's Additional Layer: Chapter 23-24.6 and RIDOH

Where Rhode Island diverges from the federal baseline is in its enforcement posture and its interplay with the state's lead hazard mitigation laws. Under R.I. Gen. Laws Section 23-24.6-4, property owners have affirmative duties to maintain lead-safe conditions in pre-1978 housing, particularly rental units. While this section targets landlords more directly, it affects sales agents because any known lead hazard mitigation history—including Lead Hazard Reduction Certificates and Lead-Safe Certificates issued by RIDOH—must be disclosed.

The Rhode Island Association of Realtors (RIAR) provides standardized transaction forms that incorporate the federal disclosure. The RIAR Purchase and Sale Agreement includes a lead paint disclosure addendum, and agents should verify they are using the most current version of that addendum for 2026 transactions. RIDOH also maintains a [searchable database of lead-safe housing](https://health.ri.gov/programs/detail.php?pgm_id=1053) that agents can reference when verifying a seller's claims about prior inspections or abatements.

Rhode Island courts have been clear: ignorance of a property's lead history does not shield an agent from liability when records were publicly available. The duty of inquiry is real here. If a seller states "no known hazards" but the RIDOH database shows a prior lead order on the property, the listing agent has a problem.

## Form Requirements and Timing Rules

| Requirement | Federal (EPA/HUD) | Rhode Island (State) |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure form signed | Before or at P&S execution | Same timing; RIAR addendum |
| EPA pamphlet provided | Before or at P&S execution | Same; current 2024 edition |
| Buyer inspection period | Minimum 10 days (waivable) | Same; must be documented in writing |
| Known hazard records | All available reports disclosed | Includes RIDOH certificates and orders |
| Agent signature | Required on disclosure form | Required on RIAR addendum |

The timing issue is where many agents stumble. The disclosure must be delivered before or simultaneously with the execution of the purchase and sale agreement—not at closing, not during the attorney review period, not when the buyer "gets around to it." A signed offer without a completed lead disclosure attached is a compliance failure from the moment ink hits paper.

If a buyer waives the 10-day inspection period, that waiver must be in writing on the disclosure form itself. A verbal waiver or a text message does not satisfy the regulatory requirement. Similar timing concerns arise in [other states with aggressive disclosure regimes](/blog/tennessee-lead-paint-disclosure-2026), but Rhode Island's older housing stock makes the stakes higher.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance: What Agents Actually Risk

**Failing to comply with lead paint disclosure rules can trigger consequences at three levels: federal, state, and civil.** Understanding each is essential for agents who think this is just a form to check off.

At the federal level, the EPA can impose civil penalties of up to $19,507 per violation (adjusted for inflation under 40 CFR Part 19). Criminal penalties can reach $19,507 per day in cases of knowing or willful violations, plus potential imprisonment. The EPA has increased enforcement actions in New England in recent years, partly because the region's older housing creates elevated public health risk.

At the state level, RIDOH can refer non-compliant parties to the Rhode Island Attorney General's office. Violations of R.I. Gen. Laws Chapter 23-24.6 can result in fines, injunctive relief, and orders requiring lead hazard mitigation at the violator's expense. The Rhode Island Department of Business Regulation, which oversees real estate licensing, can also take disciplinary action against an agent's license for failure to meet disclosure obligations.

At the civil level, buyers who discover undisclosed lead hazards can sue for rescission of the contract, actual damages, treble damages under some circumstances, and attorney's fees. Rhode Island courts have awarded six-figure judgments against sellers and their agents in cases where children were exposed to lead hazards in properties where disclosures were incomplete or fabricated.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make in Rhode Island

The five errors below account for the majority of compliance failures in Rhode Island lead paint transactions. Each one is preventable with basic process discipline.

**First, agents treat the disclosure form as a closing document rather than a pre-contract document.** The form must accompany or precede the purchase and sale agreement. Filing it in the closing package means the transaction operated out of compliance for its entire duration. This is the single most common violation agents commit.

Second, listing agents accept a seller's "I don't know" without verifying the RIDOH database. Rhode Island maintains public records of lead inspections, orders, and certificates. An agent who fails to cross-reference available public data is exposed to claims that they facilitated a misleading disclosure. The duty of inquiry exists even if it is not explicitly statutory—courts have imposed it through common-law negligence standards.

Third, agents allow buyers to waive the 10-day inspection period verbally or through informal communication. The waiver must appear on the disclosure form itself, signed by the buyer. A text saying "we're good, skip the lead test" is legally meaningless and leaves the agent unprotected.

Fourth, agents fail to retain copies of the signed disclosure for the required three-year period. Under 40 CFR 745.113, sellers and agents must keep the disclosure and all acknowledgments for at least three years from the date of sale. Agents who purge files annually or rely solely on their brokerage's retention system without verifying compliance are gambling with their license.

Fifth, agents in dual-agency or assigned-agency transactions assume the "other side" handled it. Both agents sign the disclosure form. Both are liable if it is incomplete. There is no pass-through of responsibility in a dual-agency situation—each agent has an independent obligation to confirm compliance.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers carry supervisory liability under both Rhode Island licensing law and federal disclosure regulations. If an agent in your office fails to deliver proper lead paint disclosures, the brokerage is exposed to the same penalties the agent faces—plus additional claims of negligent supervision.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Documentation |
|---|---|---|
| Disclosure form present in file | Every pre-1978 transaction | Signed by all parties and both agents |
| EPA pamphlet delivery confirmed | Every pre-1978 transaction | Buyer acknowledgment on form |
| Inspection waiver properly documented | When buyer waives 10-day period | Written waiver on disclosure form |
| Retention of records (3-year minimum) | Annual file audit | Physical or electronic copy verified |
| RIDOH database cross-reference | Listing intake for pre-1978 | Screenshot or printout in file |

**Brokers should build lead paint compliance into their transaction checklist at the listing agreement stage, not at closing.** The moment a listing is taken on a pre-1978 property, the disclosure obligation begins. Waiting until an offer arrives creates gaps that are difficult to remediate retroactively.

Training is the other half of the equation. Rhode Island does not currently require a separate lead-paint-specific continuing education course for licensees, but brokers who build a 30-minute annual training on disclosure compliance reduce their E&O exposure significantly. ## Rental Transactions: A Brief Note for Agents Handling Leases

Rhode Island's Lead Poisoning Prevention Act places heightened duties on landlords, and agents who handle lease transactions must ensure the same EPA/HUD disclosure form is completed for pre-1978 rental properties. The 10-day inspection opportunity applies to tenants just as it does to buyers. RIDOH's enforcement has historically focused on rental properties due to the elevated risk to children, so agents managing rental portfolios face even more scrutiny than those handling sales alone.

The Lead-Safe Certificate and Lead Hazard Reduction Certificate programs administered by RIDOH are particularly relevant here. A property with a current Lead-Safe Certificate has documented compliance with the state's lead hazard standards. Agents should request copies of these certificates during listing intake and include them in the disclosure package.
