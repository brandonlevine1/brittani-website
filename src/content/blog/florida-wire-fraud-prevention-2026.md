## Florida Wire Fraud Prevention Requirements 2026: The Rules Agents Cannot Afford to Ignore

Wire fraud losses in real estate topped $446 million nationally in 2023, and Florida consistently ranks among the top three states for reported incidents. Understanding the Florida wire fraud prevention requirements 2026 is not optional for licensed agents—it is a baseline condition for staying compliant and keeping clients protected. The combination of high transaction volume, international buyer activity, and large wire amounts makes Florida closings a prime target for business email compromise schemes.

The Florida Real Estate Commission (FREC) and the Florida Bar have both responded with tightened standards around disclosure and verification. Agents who treat wire fraud warnings as an afterthought are exposing themselves to disciplinary action, civil liability, and brokerage termination.

## What Florida Law Actually Requires From Agents

Florida does not have a single standalone statute titled "Wire Fraud Prevention Act" the way some agents assume. Instead, the obligations are derived from multiple sources: Chapter 475 of the Florida Statutes (governing real estate licensees), the Florida Bar's rules on trust accounting, and contractual language embedded in standard transaction forms.

**Section 475.25(1)(b), Florida Statutes, makes it a violation for any licensee to be guilty of fraud, misrepresentation, concealment, dishonest dealing, or culpable negligence in any business transaction.** FREC has interpreted "culpable negligence" to include failure to warn clients about known risks—and wire fraud in 2026 is a known risk that no licensee can credibly claim ignorance of. The standard of care has shifted.

The [FAR/BAR AS IS Contract](https://www.floridabar.org/the-florida-bar-news/revised-far-bar-residential-contracts-approved/) includes specific wire fraud advisory language in Paragraph 18(N), warning parties that wiring instructions received via email should be independently verified by phone using a known number. This language creates a contractual expectation that agents will at minimum direct clients to read and understand it.

## Forms and Documents Agents Must Use

The primary contract forms carrying wire fraud advisory language include the FAR/BAR "AS IS" Residential Contract for Sale and Purchase and the FAR/BAR Standard Residential Contract. Both incorporate cautionary language about email-based wire instructions.

Beyond the contract itself, most Florida brokerages now require a standalone Wire Fraud Advisory or Wire Fraud Disclosure signed at or before contract execution. While no single FREC-issued form number exists for this purpose, the widely adopted versions come from title underwriters like Old Republic, Fidelity, and First American. Your brokerage likely has a designated version—if you cannot locate it, that is a compliance gap you need to close today.

| Document | When It Must Be Signed | Who Signs | Purpose |
|----------|----------------------|-----------|---------|
| FAR/BAR AS IS Contract (Para 18N) | At contract execution | Buyer and Seller | Contractual wire fraud warning |
| Standalone Wire Fraud Advisory | At or before contract execution | Buyer (minimum) | Dedicated written warning |
| Title Company Wire Verification Form | Before wire is sent | Buyer | Confirms callback verification protocol |
| Brokerage Compliance Acknowledgment | At onboarding or annually | Agent | Confirms agent training on wire fraud |

Agents working with international buyers or all-cash transactions should double-check that the wire fraud advisory is translated or clearly communicated. FREC expects the duty of disclosure to be meaningful, not just a signature on a page a client cannot read.

## Common Mistakes Agents Make With Wire Fraud Compliance

Most agents believe they are covered simply because the FAR/BAR contract contains advisory language. That assumption fails under scrutiny. Here are the specific errors that create liability.

**First, agents routinely forward wire instructions from the title company to clients via email without any verbal warning or callback verification reminder.** The moment you forward wiring details in an unencrypted email, you have created the exact vector that criminals exploit. Best practice is to never send wire instructions—direct the client to obtain them directly from the closing agent by phone.

Second, agents fail to get the standalone Wire Fraud Advisory signed at contract. They assume the contract language is sufficient. When a fraud event occurs and litigation follows, the plaintiff's attorney will ask for a signed, standalone acknowledgment—and its absence looks like negligence.

Third, agents do not document the verbal warnings they provide. A quick "I told them to call and verify" has zero evidentiary value without a file note, timestamped email confirmation, or CRM entry. Document every conversation about wire safety in writing, even if it is a brief follow-up email to the client saying "As we discussed, please call the title company at this number before wiring any funds."

Fourth, agents use personal email accounts with no two-factor authentication. When your email account is compromised, the criminal monitors your transactions and sends spoofed wire instructions to your clients. FREC has flagged agents under Section 475.25 for failing to maintain reasonable security over client communications.

Fifth, agents wait until the week of closing to address wire safety. By then, the buyer may have already received fraudulent instructions and acted on them. The advisory must come early—ideally at contract, with a verbal reinforcement 72 hours before the scheduled wire.

## What Brokers Need to Audit and Enforce

Brokers bear supervisory responsibility under Section 475.25(1)(u), which holds brokers accountable for failure to properly supervise their associates. If an agent in your office fails to deliver wire fraud disclosures and a client suffers a loss, the broker faces discipline alongside the agent.

**Every Florida brokerage should have a written wire fraud prevention policy that specifies exactly which forms must be used, when they must be signed, and how they are stored.** This is not a suggestion—it is the minimum standard that FREC auditors and E&O carriers expect in 2026.

Brokers should audit for these items quarterly at minimum: confirmation that every closed file contains a signed Wire Fraud Advisory, verification that agents are not forwarding wire instructions via email, documentation that agents completed annual wire fraud training, and evidence that the brokerage email system uses two-factor authentication and encryption.

| Audit Item | Frequency | Red Flag If Missing |
|------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Signed Wire Fraud Advisory in file | Every transaction | Immediate compliance gap |
| Agent wire fraud training certificate | Annually | Disciplinary exposure |
| Email security (2FA enabled) | Quarterly | Cyber liability risk |
| Policy acknowledgment on file | At onboarding + annually | Supervisory failure under 475.25(1)(u) |
| No wire instructions forwarded via agent email | Random spot check | Direct liability trigger |

Brokerages that rely on [manual checklists and memory](/blog/why-checklists-beat-memory) to track these requirements are playing a dangerous game. The volume of transactions in a typical Florida office makes it inevitable that files will slip through without proper documentation unless a system enforces compliance automatically.

## Liability When Compliance Fails

The consequences of failing to meet wire fraud prevention requirements in Florida are multi-layered. Civil liability comes first—buyers who lose six or seven figures to wire fraud will name everyone in the transaction chain, including the listing and buyer's agents, in a negligence lawsuit.

FREC disciplinary action can result in fines up to $5,000 per violation, license suspension, or revocation under Section 475.25. The "culpable negligence" standard does not require intent—it requires only that the agent should have known better given the state of the industry.

E&O insurance carriers have increasingly added wire fraud exclusions or coverage conditions that require proof of compliance with disclosure requirements. If your file lacks the signed advisory, your carrier may deny the claim entirely. That leaves the agent and broker personally exposed. Understanding [the true cost of a blown closing](/blog/true-cost-of-a-blown-closing) goes beyond lost commission—it extends to personal financial ruin when wire fraud enters the picture.

## How to Build a Bulletproof Wire Fraud Prevention Workflow

Start by establishing a non-negotiable checklist item at contract execution: the Wire Fraud Advisory must be signed before the file moves forward. No exceptions. No "I'll get it later." The file does not advance to title until that document is in.

At the midpoint of the transaction, send a written reminder to the buyer about wire verification procedures. Reference the title company's name, their verified phone number, and a clear instruction: "Do not send any wire without calling this number first to confirm instructions." Keep a copy of that communication in the file.

Seventy-two hours before the scheduled wire date, make a phone call to the buyer. Confirm they have the correct title company number. Remind them that any last-minute changes to wiring instructions received by email are almost certainly fraudulent. Document the call with a file note and a follow-up email.
